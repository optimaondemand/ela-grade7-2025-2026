The Odyssey — Module Project

Module 1 Project — Is Odysseus a Hero?

A written analytical essay with a VR spatial argument recording

The Assignment

Write a 400–600 word analytical essay that takes a position on the following question:

Is Odysseus a hero? What does the evidence of the poem — not the narrator’s claims about him, but what he actually does — say about his virtue?

You have been building this argument since Week 1. Your VR compositions, your discussion posts, your quick writes, and your comprehension checks have all been preparing you to write this essay. It should be the most developed analytical writing you have done yet.

What Your Essay Must Include
1

A parallel-structure thesis in your introduction

Your thesis must state your position clearly and use parallel structure to name at least two qualities. It should do the work the module taught: make a claim that is specific enough to argue, not so broad it proves nothing. See the Lesson 5.2 examples for models.

2

At least three pieces of specific textual evidence

Name the scene, describe what happens, and connect it explicitly to your thesis. Evidence from at least two different parts of the poem (early voyage, Books 9–12, homecoming) is required. Do not summarize — analyze: say what the scene shows, not just what happens in it.

3

One concessive sentence that acknowledges the strongest objection

Use the structure from Lesson 3.3: grant something real, then answer it. The concession should be the best argument the other side could make — not a weak objection you can dismiss easily.

4

Sentence variety throughout

Your essay should include at least one periodic or cumulative sentence (labeled in a comment or note to your teacher), and should not consist entirely of short declarative sentences. Read your draft aloud before submitting — if the rhythm sounds flat, vary the sentence lengths.

The VR Component

Your spatial recording from VR 5.5 is the VR component of this project. Submit it alongside your written essay. Your teacher will review both together. The recording does not need to be word-for-word identical to your essay — it is the spoken, spatial version of the same argument. If the recording helped you think more clearly about your claim, you may revise the essay to match.

If technical issues prevented you from completing the spatial recording, submit a written transcript (300–400 words) describing your VR composition and the argument you would have made walking through it.

Rubric
Criterion Full (25 pts) Partial (15 pts) Minimal (5 pts)
Claim & Thesis Clear, specific parallel-structure thesis; answers the question with a position that can be argued Thesis present but vague or lacks parallel structure No clear thesis; essay summarizes rather than argues
Evidence & Analysis Three or more specific scenes named and connected to thesis; analysis says what each scene shows, not just what happens Two scenes with partial analysis; some summary without connection to claim One scene or scenes described without analysis
Concession & Reasoning Concessive sentence grants a real objection and answers it; reasoning holds together across the essay Concession present but weak (trivial objection) or unanswered No concession; reasoning does not address opposing view
Writing & Craft Sentence variety present; at least one periodic or cumulative sentence; prose is clear and controlled; grammar errors do not impede reading Some sentence variety; occasional unclear phrasing; few grammar errors Monotonous sentence structure; grammar errors impede reading
Total: 100 points. Each criterion is worth 25 points maximum. The VR component (spatial recording or transcript) is evaluated as part of Evidence & Analysis — a strong recording that shows a well-organized spatial argument can raise that criterion’s score; a missing recording without explanation will lower it.
