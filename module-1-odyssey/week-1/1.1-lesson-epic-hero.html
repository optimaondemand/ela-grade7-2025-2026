Lesson

The Odyssey is an epic — a long narrative poem celebrating a hero’s journey through trial and transformation — but it is a strange epic, and the strangeness is the point. Most ancient heroes are celebrated for power: Achilles is the greatest warrior, Beowulf tears monsters apart with his bare hands, Aeneas carries a whole civilization on his back. Odysseus is celebrated for something quieter and more unsettling. His Greek epithet is polytropos — literally “of many turnings” — and the Fitzgerald translation renders it as “the man of twists and turns.” His great gift is mētis: cunning intelligence, the ability to read a situation, adapt, deceive when necessary, and survive when sheer force would only get him killed. Homer opens the poem not with a battle but with Odysseus weeping on a beach, marooned on Calypso’s island, unable to go home — which tells you immediately that this is a story about endurance, not conquest. As you read Books 1 and 5 this week, you are meeting a hero whose defining strength is not what he can do to his enemies but how he thinks, adapts, and holds himself together when everything around him is trying to unmake him. That is worth paying careful attention to — because Homer is asking whether that kind of excellence counts as genuine heroism, and the question is harder than it looks.

Historical Context

The Odyssey was composed in ancient Greece, most likely in the 8th century BCE, and attributed to a poet named Homer — though scholars debate whether a single person composed it or whether it grew from a long oral tradition of sung poetry. For centuries before it was written down, the poem was performed aloud by professional singers called aoidoi, who memorized and recombined traditional phrases, stories, and formulas to compose new versions in performance. This is why Homer uses repeated phrases called epithets — “rosy-fingered Dawn,” “the wine-dark sea,” “bright-eyed Athena” — they were the building blocks of oral composition, helping the singer maintain the poem’s rhythm while searching for the next line.

The poem is set in the aftermath of the Trojan War, which ancient Greeks believed had taken place roughly in the 12th century BCE. The war itself was caused by the abduction of Helen, wife of the Greek king Menelaus, by the Trojan prince Paris — and it lasted ten years. Odysseus fought at Troy and helped end the war with the famous wooden horse. The Odyssey begins ten years after that — making Odysseus twenty years from home, a man whose son has grown up without him and whose wife has been holding off a hundred suitors who assume he is dead.

Vocabulary
EPIC

A long narrative poem celebrating a hero of great cultural significance, involving extraordinary trials, divine interference, and elevated language. The hero’s journey in an epic typically reflects the values of the civilization that produced it. Homer’s Odyssey celebrates not a warrior’s strength but a man’s cleverness, endurance, and loyalty — which tells you something important about what ancient Greeks valued beyond battle.

“Sing in me, Muse, and through me tell the story / of that man skilled in all ways of contending, / the wanderer, harried for years on end...” — Book 1, opening lines (Fitzgerald translation)

Copia practice: Rewrite the opening lines above two different ways — once shortening them into a single plain sentence, and once expanding them by adding two more specific details about Odysseus’s trials. What do you gain or lose in each version?

HUBRIS

Dangerous overconfidence — the belief that one is exempt from the laws that govern everyone else. In Greek tragedy and epic, hubris is the character flaw that causes heroes to overstep, offend the gods, and bring catastrophe on themselves. Odysseus’s journey is partly a consequence of his own hubris: after blinding the Cyclops, he shouts his real name as he sails away — allowing Poseidon to know exactly who to punish. The ten-year journey home is, in part, the cost of that one moment of pride.

“Cyclops, if ever mortal man inquire / how you were put to shame and blinded, tell him / Odysseus, raider of cities, took your eye: / Laertës’ son, whose home’s on Ithaca!” — Book 9

Copia practice: The sentence above is a boast that costs Odysseus dearly. Rewrite it as a version that does NOT display hubris — what would a cunning man say instead?

MĒTIS (cunning intelligence)

The Greek word for the kind of intelligence Odysseus possesses: not raw intelligence, but practical, adaptive, situational cleverness — the ability to read a situation and find the hidden move no one else sees. The goddess Athena, Odysseus’s divine patron, is herself a goddess of mētis. This module’s central question is whether mētis is a genuine virtue — something admirable and good — or whether it is merely useful, and whether being useful and being virtuous are the same thing.

Copia practice: Write one sentence describing a character from any story you know (a book, a film, a game) who possesses mētis — without using the words “smart” or “clever.”

Comprehension Check

Write your answer to each question, then open the sample response to check your thinking. This is ungraded — it is for your own understanding.

1

What does the opening invocation of the Muse tell us about how Homer positions his poem?

Think about who is supposedly “singing” this poem if the Muse is speaking through Homer.

▶ Sample response
By asking the Muse to “sing in me,” Homer positions the poem not as his own invention but as divinely inspired truth — the Muse is the actual author, and Homer is her instrument. This is an epic convention, but it also does something real: it claims that the story of Odysseus is not just a story but a record of something that happened, preserved and transmitted by divine authority. It gives the poem the weight of truth before it begins.
2

Why does Odysseus refuse Calypso’s offer of immortality?

What does he choose instead, and what does that choice cost him?

▶ Sample response
Odysseus refuses because he wants to go home to Ithaca and to Penelope. He chooses the mortal life, with all its grief and uncertainty, over an immortal existence on a beautiful island. What this choice costs him is the offer itself — he gives up eternal life and ease for the chance at a home he might not reach. Homer presents this not as tragedy but as a defining choice: Odysseus values his human identity — his name, his family, his place — more than any divine gift.
3

What does polytropos (man of many turnings / man of twists and turns) suggest about Homer’s view of Odysseus?

Is this a compliment, a warning, or both?

▶ Sample response
It is deliberately ambiguous — Homer’s opening word for Odysseus contains both admiration and a hint of unease. A man who turns many ways is adaptable and resourceful, which is admirable. But a man of many turnings is also a man who cannot be pinned down, whose loyalty and honesty are always situational. Homer is introducing a hero whose greatest gift is also the thing that makes him hard to entirely trust. The ambiguity is the point: this is not a simple celebration.
4

Athena is Odysseus’s divine patron. What does it mean for an epic hero to have divine support — does it undercut his achievement?

Think about what Athena actually does vs. what Odysseus still has to do himself.

▶ Sample response
Athena opens doors and persuades other gods, but she does not do Odysseus’s work for him. He still has to sail the sea, survive the Cyclops, endure Circe, descend to the underworld, and fight a hundred suitors. What divine favor gives the hero is opportunity — a chance that would otherwise be cut off. Whether he takes that chance, and how, is still up to him. This is how Greek epic understands destiny and agency together: the gods set the stage; the hero still has to perform.
