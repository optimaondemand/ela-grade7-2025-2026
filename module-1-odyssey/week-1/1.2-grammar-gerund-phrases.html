7th Grade ELA — Module 1
The Odyssey — Week 1 — Grammar Lesson

Lesson 1.2 — Gerund Phrases

Turning verbs into nouns — and why good writers do it

Objectives
1

Identify gerunds and gerund phrases in sentences and name their function (subject, object, complement).

2

Write original sentences using gerund phrases, drawing examples from The Odyssey.

Warm-Up 1 min
From Book 1: “Kneeling down at the water’s edge...” and “...weeping through his years on the island shore.” Both of those -ing words are doing different grammatical jobs. What do you notice?

One -ing word is part of a verb; one is acting like a noun. That difference is what this lesson is about. Thirty seconds of noticing before we name it.

Lesson

A gerund is a verb that has been transformed into a noun by adding -ing. When a gerund takes its own objects, modifiers, or complements, the whole cluster is called a gerund phrase. Like any noun, a gerund phrase can function as the subject of a sentence (“Weeping on the beach did not solve Odysseus’s problem”), as the object of a verb (“He hated waiting on Calypso’s island”), or as the complement of a linking verb (“His only skill was outwitting his enemies”). What makes gerund phrases valuable to writers is that they let you name an action — which is inherently dynamic, full of motion — without making it the main verb of the sentence. You can then say something about that action, qualify it, place it, or compare it, which gives your sentences more weight and precision than a simple verb alone can supply. Homer does this in the Greek original (and Fitzgerald preserves it in English) whenever he wants to isolate and contemplate what a character is doing rather than just showing the action rushing past.

Examples from The Odyssey

Example 1 — gerund phrase as subject

“Surviving the sea after Poseidon’s wrath was the hardest thing Odysseus had ever done.”

The gerund phrase Surviving the sea after Poseidon’s wrath is the subject of the verb was. Notice how much information it packs in before the main verb even arrives.

Example 2 — gerund phrase as object

“Calypso offered him living forever at her side, but he refused.”

The gerund phrase living forever at her side is the direct object of offered. The word living here is a gerund — not a verb, because it is the thing being offered, not the action of offering.

Example 3 — gerund phrase as complement

“Odysseus’s greatest weapon was knowing what his enemy did not know.”

The gerund phrase knowing what his enemy did not know completes the subject by coming after the linking verb was. It defines what “greatest weapon” is.

Practice
1

Identify the gerund phrase and its function: “Tricking the Cyclops required both courage and quick thinking.”

▶ Answer
Gerund phrase: Tricking the Cyclops — it is the subject of the verb required.
2

Identify the gerund phrase and its function: “Penelope spent years resisting the suitors’ pressure.”

▶ Answer
Gerund phrase: resisting the suitors’ pressure — it is the object of the verb spent. (She spent time doing what? Resisting.)
3

Fix the sentence: “To sail through the night without stars was terrifying.” — Rewrite it with a gerund phrase as the subject instead of an infinitive phrase.

▶ Sample answer
Sailing through the night without stars was terrifying. (Both are grammatically acceptable; gerund phrasing often feels more active and immediate.)
Application 5 min

Write two original sentences about Odysseus or a character from Books 1–5. Each sentence must contain a gerund phrase. In one sentence, the gerund phrase must be the subject. In the other, it must be the object.
