7th Grade ELA — Module 1
The Odyssey — Week 1 — Grammar Lesson

Lesson 1.3 — Infinitive Phrases

The difference between a purpose and an action

Objectives
1

Identify infinitive phrases and explain their grammatical function (noun, adjective, adverb).

2

Distinguish an infinitive phrase from a gerund phrase and choose deliberately between them in your own writing.

Warm-Up 1 min
“He sailed for years to reach Ithaca.” vs. “He sailed for years reaching Ithaca.” — Do these two sentences mean the same thing? Read them aloud and notice the difference.

The first uses an infinitive; the second uses a gerund. They are not identical. Today you learn exactly what makes them different — and why that difference matters when you write.

Lesson

An infinitive is the base form of a verb preceded by to: to sail, to deceive, to survive. An infinitive phrase is an infinitive plus everything attached to it: objects, modifiers, complements. Like gerund phrases, infinitive phrases can function as nouns (“To deceive the Cyclops was his only option”), but they can also function as adjectives (“Odysseus needed a plan to escape the cave”) or as adverbs expressing purpose (“He sharpened the stake to blind the Cyclops”). That last function — expressing purpose or intention — is something gerund phrases cannot do, and it is where infinitive phrases are most powerful. When Homer describes Odysseus acting, he almost always implies purpose: Odysseus does not merely do things; he does them in order to achieve something. The infinitive phrase captures this forward-reaching intentionality that defines the epic hero’s mind. A man who acts toward a goal is different from a man who simply acts — and infinitive phrases are how writers mark that difference on the page.

Examples from The Odyssey

Noun function (subject)

“To reach Ithaca was the only thing Odysseus thought about on Calypso’s island.”

The infinitive phrase To reach Ithaca is the subject of was. Compare with the gerund: “Reaching Ithaca was all he thought about.” Both are grammatically correct; the infinitive emphasizes the goal-state, the gerund the ongoing process.

Adjective function (modifying a noun)

“Odysseus needed a trick to escape the Cyclops’s cave.”

The infinitive phrase to escape the Cyclops’s cave modifies the noun trick — it tells us what kind of trick. Adjective infinitives often follow nouns like plan, reason, way, trick, attempt.

Adverb function (expressing purpose)

“He hardened the stake in the fire to blind the Cyclops.”

The infinitive phrase to blind the Cyclops modifies the verb hardened — it tells us why he hardened it. Adverb infinitives expressing purpose can almost always be paraphrased with “in order to.”

Practice
1

Identify the infinitive phrase and its function: “Penelope unraveled her weaving each night to delay choosing a new husband.”

▶ Answer
to delay choosing a new husband — adverb function, expressing purpose (she unraveled in order to delay).
2

Identify the infinitive phrase and its function: “To endure ten years of wandering required more than physical strength.”

▶ Answer
To endure ten years of wandering — noun function, subject of required.
3

Decide: which word is better here, and why? “Odysseus’s only desire was [returning / to return] to Ithaca.”

▶ Discussion
Both are grammatically correct. Returning (gerund) emphasizes the process — the ongoing act of traveling home. To return (infinitive) emphasizes the goal-state — the destination, the moment of arrival. For Odysseus specifically, to return is more accurate: what he desires is not the journey but the homecoming. Choose the one that better fits the meaning you intend.
Application 5 min

Write one sentence about Odysseus using an infinitive phrase that expresses purpose (adverb function). Then write one sentence using an infinitive phrase as a noun (subject or object). Label each one.
