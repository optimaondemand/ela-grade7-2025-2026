## Week 1 Reading Quiz — The Odyssey (Books 1 and 5)
**10 questions | 10 points | 15 min | Canvas Quiz — auto-graded**

---

**Q1.** What does the word *polytropos* (translated by Fitzgerald as "the man of twists and turns") suggest about Odysseus?

- A) He is physically powerful and impossible to defeat in battle
- B) He is a man of many strategies and adaptations, clever rather than simply strong
- C) He has traveled so many roads that he has lost his sense of direction
- D) He is dishonest and cannot be trusted by gods or mortals

**Correct: B**

---

**Q2.** At the opening of Book 1, where is Odysseus, and what is his situation?

- A) He is fighting his way through the underworld, trying to find the path home
- B) He is at sea in a storm, clinging to a piece of wreckage
- C) He is on Calypso's island, marooned and longing for home
- D) He is disguised as a beggar in his own palace in Ithaca

**Correct: C**

---

**Q3.** Why is Poseidon hostile to Odysseus throughout the poem?

- A) Odysseus stole sacred cattle belonging to Poseidon
- B) Odysseus blinded Poseidon's son, the Cyclops Polyphemus
- C) Odysseus refused to offer sacrifice at Poseidon's altar before leaving Troy
- D) Poseidon is jealous of Odysseus's favor with Athena

**Correct: B**

---

**Q4.** What does Calypso offer Odysseus, and what is his response?

- A) She offers him a fleet of ships; he accepts but then sails the wrong direction
- B) She offers him immortality and eternal youth; he refuses and chooses to go home
- C) She offers him knowledge of the future; he accepts in exchange for staying one more year
- D) She offers him safe passage home; he refuses because he doesn't trust her

**Correct: B**

---

**Q5.** Which goddess is Odysseus's divine patron and advocate throughout the poem?

- A) Aphrodite, goddess of love
- B) Hera, queen of the gods
- C) Athena, goddess of wisdom and craft
- D) Persephone, queen of the underworld

**Correct: C**

---

**Q6.** In Book 1, what is happening at Odysseus's palace in Ithaca while he is away?

- A) Penelope has remarried and the palace is prosperous and orderly
- B) Telemachus has taken over as king and rules the island firmly
- C) A crowd of suitors has taken over the palace, eating Odysseus's food and pressuring Penelope to choose a new husband
- D) The palace has been abandoned and fallen into ruin

**Correct: C**

---

**Q7.** What does Odysseus's refusal of immortality reveal about his values?

- A) He is too proud to accept a gift from a lesser goddess
- B) He values his human identity, his family, and his home more than endless life and ease
- C) He does not believe Calypso has the power to actually grant immortality
- D) He is afraid of what the other gods would do if they found out

**Correct: B**

---

**Q8.** The *Odyssey* begins *in medias res* — in the middle of things. What does this mean for the reader?

- A) The story starts at the very beginning of Odysseus's journey home from Troy
- B) We enter the story ten years into Odysseus's journey, with much already having happened
- C) The poem skips back and forth randomly through time with no clear order
- D) The story is told from the perspective of Odysseus's son Telemachus, not Odysseus himself

**Correct: B**

---

**Q9.** What literary device is Homer using when he calls the sea "wine-dark" and Dawn "rosy-fingered"?

- A) Simile — a comparison using "like" or "as"
- B) Alliteration — repetition of consonant sounds at the start of words
- C) Epic epithet — a repeated descriptive phrase attached to a person or thing
- D) Hyperbole — deliberate exaggeration for effect

**Correct: C**

---

**Q10.** Based on Books 1 and 5, which statement best describes Homer's presentation of Odysseus as a hero?

- A) Odysseus is celebrated primarily for his physical strength and his ability to defeat enemies in open combat
- B) Odysseus is presented as deeply flawed and ultimately unworthy of heroic status
- C) Odysseus is celebrated for his endurance, cunning, and attachment to his human identity rather than for military might
- D) Odysseus is presented as a passive figure whose fate is entirely controlled by the gods

**Correct: C**
