The Odyssey — Week 2

Lesson 2.1 — Cunning, Hubris, and the Cyclops

When cleverness becomes cruelty — and what that costs a hero

Objectives
1

Analyze the Cyclops episode (Book 9) as a demonstration of both Odysseus’s cunning and his hubris, identifying where the two conflict.

2

Explain how Odysseus’s boast after escaping the Cyclops functions as a structural turning point in the poem.

Warm-Up 2 min
Have you ever done something clever to get out of a problem — and then ruined it by showing off? Think of a time when pride undid something skill had built.

That experience is at the heart of Book 9. Odysseus wins through cleverness, then nearly loses everything through pride. Keep that pattern in mind as you read.

Lesson

Book 9 is the most famous episode of the Odyssey, and it is carefully designed to show two things about Odysseus simultaneously: his mētis at its most brilliant, and his hubris at its most destructive. Inside the Cyclops’s cave, Odysseus is all calculation: he identifies that Polyphemus is the son of Poseidon before he acts, he names himself “Nobody” so that when Polyphemus cries out his neighbors cannot help, he uses the sharpened stake and the rams with precise timing. Every step is planned, measured, and executed without waste. But the moment the raft clears the harbor and the Cyclops cannot reach him, something breaks in Odysseus: he shouts his real name. Not because the plan required it — the plan was already complete — but because he cannot stand for Polyphemus not to know who defeated him. That single moment of vanity gives Poseidon a name and a target, and it extends Odysseus’s journey by years. Homer is making a precise argument: the same man whose cunning can solve any puzzle is also the man whose pride can undo it. These two traits are not separate; they come from the same source, and that makes them impossible to untangle.

Historical Context

The Cyclops in Greek mythology represents a class of beings who exist outside civilization: they have no laws, no councils, no agriculture, no hospitality. They are a contrast to what the Greeks valued in their own culture. When Odysseus enters the Cyclops’s cave, he is entering a world with no rules — which means his only resource is his own intelligence.

The episode also participates in a Greek debate about whether the ends justify the means. Odysseus’s crew members urge him to take the cheese and leave immediately — which would have been safer. Odysseus wants to stay and see what the Cyclops is like, and that curiosity gets six of his men killed. His intelligence saves the rest, but his curiosity caused the problem. Ancient Greek audiences would have noticed this tension.

Vocabulary
HUBRIS (in action)

Last week you learned the definition of hubris. This week you see it. Hubris is not simply confidence; it is the specific overconfidence that makes a person believe they are above consequences — that they can shout their name into the face of a god’s son without reprisal. The Greek concept carries a religious weight: to display hubris is to offend the gods by forgetting that you are mortal.

“Cyclops, if ever mortal man inquire / how you were put to shame and blinded, tell him / Odysseus, raider of cities, took your eye: / Laertës’ son, whose home’s on Ithaca!” — Book 9

Copia practice: Rewrite this boast so that it communicates the same defeat of the Cyclops WITHOUT displaying hubris. What does Odysseus lose if he stays silent?

XENIA (guest-friendship)

In Greek culture, xenia was the sacred duty of hospitality owed to strangers. A host was required to feed a guest, shelter them, and give them gifts before asking who they were or where they came from. The Cyclops violates every rule of xenia by eating Odysseus’s men rather than hosting them. This violation makes the Cyclops not just physically monstrous but morally monstrous in Greek terms — which is part of what makes Odysseus’s revenge feel justified to a Greek audience, even though it also displays hubris.

Copia practice: Write a sentence using xenia (or “guest-friendship”) to describe what the Cyclops fails to provide and what that failure means.

Comprehension Check
1

Why does Odysseus tell the Cyclops his name is “Nobody”?

▶ Sample response
So that when Polyphemus cries out in pain after being blinded, his neighboring Cyclopes ask who is hurting him, and he must answer “Nobody” — causing them to conclude no one is harming him and to go back to sleep. It is a brilliant trap built into the naming itself, turning Polyphemus’s own cry against him.
2

After Odysseus escapes, what does he shout back to the Cyclops, and what are the consequences?

▶ Sample response
Odysseus reveals his true name and homeland, boasting that he blinded the Cyclops. Polyphemus then prays to his father Poseidon to curse Odysseus — either to die at sea, or to reach home late, alone, and in misery. This prayer is answered. By shouting his name, Odysseus gives Poseidon a target and causes years of additional suffering for himself and his crew.
3

Is Odysseus’s cunning in the Cyclops episode admirable, troubling, or both? Explain in 2–3 sentences.

▶ Discussion (no single right answer)
Both readings have textual support. The cunning saves his men's lives and demonstrates extraordinary quick thinking under pressure — that is admirable. But his decision to stay in the cave rather than flee immediately, driven by curiosity, caused the deaths of six men. And his boast at the end shows that the same pride that made him capable of the plan also made him unable to leave it anonymous. Homer presents this as a tragedy of character, not a simple triumph.
