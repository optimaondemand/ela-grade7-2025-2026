7th Grade ELA — Module 1
The Odyssey — Week 2 — Grammar Lesson

Lesson 2.3 — Relative Clauses

Who, which, that — and the difference between them

Objectives
1

Identify relative clauses introduced by who, which, and that, and distinguish restrictive from non-restrictive clauses.

2

Use commas correctly with relative clauses and choose between who, which, and that deliberately.

Warm-Up 1 min
“The Cyclops who lives on the island has no laws.” vs. “Polyphemus, who lives on the island, has no laws.” — Why does the first sentence have no commas and the second does?

The answer is about whether the clause is essential to identifying who you mean. That distinction — restrictive vs. non-restrictive — is what this lesson teaches.

Lesson

A relative clause is a dependent clause introduced by a relative pronoun (who, whom, which, that) that modifies a noun. The crucial distinction is between restrictive and non-restrictive relative clauses. A restrictive clause defines or limits the noun — it is essential to identifying which person or thing you mean, so it takes no commas: “The man who blinded the Cyclops was Odysseus” (not just any man — specifically the one who blinded the Cyclops). A non-restrictive clause adds information about a noun already fully identified — it could be removed without changing the core meaning, so it is set off by commas: “Odysseus, who blinded the Cyclops, was cursed by Poseidon” (we already know which Odysseus; the clause adds information). The pronouns follow a convention: who refers to people, which to things and animals (non-restrictive), and that to things and animals (restrictive). Using these correctly signals to a reader exactly how much the clause matters to the noun it modifies.

Examples from The Odyssey

Restrictive (no commas — essential to identification)

“The suitor who insulted Penelope most openly was Antinous.”

The clause tells us which suitor — so it is restrictive, no commas.

Non-restrictive (commas — adds information)

“Antinous, who insulted Penelope most openly, was the first suitor Odysseus killed.”

We already know which Antinous; the clause adds a fact about him. Commas are required.

That vs. which

“The stake that Odysseus sharpened blinded Polyphemus.” (restrictive — that)
“The olive stake, which Odysseus had sharpened in the fire, was their only weapon.” (non-restrictive — which)

That for essential, which for additional.

Practice
1

Add commas if needed: “Circe who turned men into animals was also capable of reversing the spell.”

▶ Answer
Circe, who turned men into animals, was also capable of reversing the spell. (Non-restrictive — we already know which Circe; the clause adds information.)
2

Write two sentences about Odysseus: one with a restrictive relative clause (who/that, no commas) and one with a non-restrictive clause (who/which, commas). Label each.
