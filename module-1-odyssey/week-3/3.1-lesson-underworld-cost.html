Lesson 3.1 — The Underworld and the Cost of the Journey

What the dead know — and what it costs Odysseus to hear it

Objectives

2

Warm-Up 2 min
Imagine you could speak to someone who had died — someone you loved or admired. What is the one question you would ask? What do you think they would say to you?

Odysseus travels to the Underworld to ask the prophet Tiresias how to get home. But what he actually learns there is much larger than directions.

Lesson

Book 11 of The Odyssey, called the Nekuia — the journey to the dead — is one of the strangest and most important episodes in the entire poem. Odysseus does not go into the Underworld in the way a reader might imagine; he stands at its threshold, pours a trench of blood, and the shades of the dead come to him, drinking the blood so they can briefly speak. What he hears is not a simple travel itinerary. Tiresias warns him that the journey home will exact a cost proportional to the mistakes already made, especially the blinding of Polyphemus. His mother Anticleia, who died while he was away, tells him that it was grief — longing for her son — that killed her, and Odysseus, reaching to embrace her, finds his arms close on air: the dead cannot be held. The encounter that strikes readers hardest, though, is with Achilles — the greatest warrior of the Trojan War, now simply a shade in the dark. Achilles tells Odysseus he would rather be the poorest living man than king among the dead. This is Homer pressing directly on the central tension of the poem: Odysseus has been offered immortality and refused it; Achilles had the choice of a short glorious life or a long obscure one and chose glory, and now he regrets it. The Underworld is not just a plot device. It is the place where Homer asks what a life is worth — and how much of it a hero can spend on mētis and ambition before he loses himself.

Historical & Literary Context

The Nekuia belongs to an ancient pattern: the hero’s descent to the world of the dead (called katabasis in Greek) is one of the oldest narrative forms in world literature — Gilgamesh, Aeneas, and Dante all make the same journey. In Greek religion, the dead were not fully gone; they existed in a diminished form in Hades’s realm, and the living could consult them through ritual. For Homer’s audience, a hero who could descend and return was a hero of extraordinary power. But Homer uses the convention critically: rather than returning with power, Odysseus returns with a more painful understanding of what his journey costs the people who love him.

Vocabulary

NEKUIA (neh-KOO-ee-ah) — noun

A ritual calling up of the dead; from Greek nekus, corpse. In literary terms, a descent to or consultation with the underworld.

Copia practice:

Original: “Odysseus performed a nekuia at the threshold of Hades.”

KATABASIS (kah-TAB-ah-sis) — noun

A descent into the underworld made by a living hero; from Greek katabainō, to go down. A defining pattern of epic literature across many cultures.

Copia practice:

Original: “The katabasis is a test no ordinary mortal could survive.”

Comprehension Check
1

In your own words, explain what Achilles tells Odysseus in the Underworld — and why it matters for understanding the poem’s central question.

▶ Sample answer
Achilles says he would rather be a poor living man than a king among the dead — meaning his heroic death, which he chose for glory, now feels like a loss. This matters because Odysseus has already refused immortality from Calypso; the poem asks what a life is actually for, and Achilles’s answer complicates the idea that glory is worth any cost.
2

Why does Odysseus choose Scylla over Charybdis? Is this an example of mētis, or is it simply the lesser of two evils?

▶ Sample answer
Odysseus chooses Scylla because losing six men is survivable, while Charybdis could destroy everyone. This could be called mētis in that it is a rational calculation under impossible conditions — he is thinking ahead, accepting a guaranteed partial loss to prevent a total loss. But it also shows the terrible burden of his leadership: he knows six men will die and rows past anyway.
