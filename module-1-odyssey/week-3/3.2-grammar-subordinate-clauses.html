7th Grade ELA — Module 1
The Odyssey — Week 3 — Grammar Lesson

Lesson 3.2 — Subordinate Clauses

Showing cause, condition, and concession — the grammar of complex thought

Objectives
1

Identify subordinate clauses and classify them by their function (cause, condition, concession, time).

2

Use subordinate clauses to write more precise sentences that show how ideas relate to each other.

Warm-Up 1 min
“Odysseus wept.” That sentence is true but tells us almost nothing. Now try: “Odysseus wept because he could not embrace his mother’s shade.” What did adding one clause do?

It added a reason — a relationship between two ideas. That’s what subordinate clauses do: they show how things connect.

Lesson

A subordinate clause is a group of words containing a subject and verb that cannot stand alone as a sentence — it depends on the main clause to complete its meaning. Subordinate clauses are introduced by subordinating conjunctions, and the conjunction you choose determines the logical relationship you are expressing. Because and since introduce causal clauses (one thing causes another): “Odysseus could not embrace his mother because she was only a shade.” If and unless introduce conditional clauses (one thing depends on another): “If Odysseus had stayed silent, Poseidon would not have known who blinded his son.” Although, though, and even though introduce concessive clauses (one thing is true despite another): “Although Odysseus was cunning, his pride undid him at the moment of escape.” The concessive clause is the most sophisticated of the three because it holds two truths in tension — it is also the clause type that appears most often in strong analytical writing. When you need to acknowledge what the other side of an argument gets right, you reach for although.

Examples from The Odyssey

Causal (because / since)

“Poseidon hounded Odysseus because the hero had blinded his son without remorse.”

The causal clause explains the origin of a situation.

Conditional (if / unless)

“Unless Odysseus appeases Poseidon, Tiresias warns, he will not reach home safely.”

The conditional clause sets up a dependency between events.

Concessive (although / though / even though)

“Although Achilles was the greatest warrior of his generation, he told Odysseus he would trade all his glory to be alive.”

The concessive clause acknowledges a contradiction — the most analytically powerful of the three types.

Practice
1

Identify the type of subordinate clause: “Even though Circe warned him, Odysseus did not fully prepare his men for what lay ahead.”

▶ Answer
Concessive clause. “Even though” signals that the warning happened but did not produce the expected result. The clause holds two facts in tension: the warning existed, and the preparation was still insufficient.
2

Write one sentence about Odysseus’s cunning using a concessive clause (although / though / even though). Your sentence should acknowledge something true about his cleverness while also naming a limit or cost of it.
