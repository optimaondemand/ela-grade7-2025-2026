The Odyssey — Week 3 — Grammar Lesson

Lesson 3.3 — Adverbial Concession in Argument

How to grant a point without losing your argument

Objectives
1

Use concessive adverbial phrases and clauses to acknowledge an opposing point while maintaining your own argument.

2

Recognize how professional writers use concession to make their arguments stronger, not weaker.

Warm-Up 1 min
Two sentences: (A) “Odysseus was cruel to his men.” (B) “Odysseus was a great leader.” Can both be true at once? How would you write a single sentence that says yes to both?

The answer is concession — the grammatical move of holding two truths in the same sentence without erasing either one.

Lesson

Adverbial concession is the move of granting something to the other side while keeping your main claim. You saw the concessive clause in Lesson 3.2 (although, though, even though). Now the focus is on how concession works as an argumentative strategy — not just as a grammar form. A writer who never concedes anything sounds like they haven’t really thought about the problem; a writer who concedes the right things and then answers them sounds like they have. The pattern is: grant → pivot → claim. The grant names what is true about the opposing view. The pivot turns away from it (but, yet, still, nevertheless, nonetheless). The claim restates or sharpens your own position in light of what you just granted. Example: “Although Odysseus’s plan in the Cyclops cave was brilliantly designed, his decision to reveal his name as he sailed away transformed a successful escape into the origin of ten more years of suffering.” The first clause grants the cleverness; the pivot is implied in the contrast; the main clause argues that the boast undoes what the plan accomplished. This structure is the engine of literary argument, and you will use it in every paragraph of your module project essay.

Grant → Pivot → Claim in Action

Weak (no concession)

“Odysseus’s cunning shows he is a great leader.”

Stronger (concession added)

“Although Odysseus repeatedly sacrifices individual men for the group’s survival, his decisions consistently preserve the mission when pure force would destroy it — suggesting that cunning, even when it costs something, is the quality that keeps the crew alive.”

The concessive pivot words

To begin the grant: although, though, even though, while, granted that, admittedly
To make the pivot: but, yet, still, nevertheless, nonetheless, however

Practice
1

Revise this weak sentence using the grant → pivot → claim structure: “Odysseus is not a good leader because he loses all his men.”

▶ Sample answer
Although Odysseus returns alone while every one of his men perishes, the losses trace not to his cunning but to moments when that cunning breaks down — the boast after the Cyclops, the men’s own disobedience with the cattle of the sun — suggesting that mētis itself is not what fails him.
2

Write your own grant → pivot → claim sentence arguing either that Odysseus’s cunning is a virtue or that it is not. Grant the strongest point on the other side before making your claim.
