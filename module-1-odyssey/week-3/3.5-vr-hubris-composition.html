The Odyssey — Week 3 — VR Activity 2

VR 3.5 — Hubris Composition

Building a visual argument about what pride costs a hero

What You’re Doing

You are composing a single spatial scene that represents a moment of hubris from Books 9–12 — a moment where Odysseus’s pride overrides his judgment. Your composition should show not just the moment itself but its consequence: the IFX arrangement must make visible the before and after, or the cause and effect, of the prideful act. This visual argument is the spatial equivalent of the concessive clause you practiced in Lesson 3.3.

What You’ll Need
  • Device: VR headset or desktop ENGAGE client
  • Books 9–12 for reference
  • Estimated time: 30 minutes
How to Join
Session type:Persistent
Session ID:[Teacher: insert Session ID]
Location:Ancient Greece Coastal or Open Landscape Location
On arrival:One composition zone is marked. A sticky note at the spawn point lists the three required annotation questions.
What to Do
1

Choose your moment. Pick one act of hubris from Books 9–12: the boast after the Cyclops, the decision to tell his men about Scylla before sailing past her, or another moment you identify in the reading. If you are unsure, the boast after the Cyclops is the clearest example.

2

Build the composition. Use 3–4 IFX to represent the moment and its consequence. There is no single right arrangement — but the objects you choose and where you place them should make the cause-and-effect relationship visible to someone walking through the scene.

3

Annotate with three sticky notes:
(a) What act of pride does this scene represent?
(b) What does it cost? (Name one specific consequence from the text.)
(c) Was the cost avoidable? Write your answer as a concessive sentence: “Although…”

4

Submit one snapshot showing the full composition and all three sticky notes. Paste the snapshot as an image upload in the Canvas assignment for VR 3.5.

What to Submit / Time / Grading

One snapshot image. 30 minutes. Completion-graded (10 pts).

Facilitator Note

Watch for sticky note (c) — the concessive annotation. Students often write flat answers (“yes it was avoidable” or “no it wasn’t”) rather than holding the tension. Prompt: “Can you write that sentence so it grants something to the other side before making its claim?” This is the grammar lesson and the literary argument lesson working at the same moment.
