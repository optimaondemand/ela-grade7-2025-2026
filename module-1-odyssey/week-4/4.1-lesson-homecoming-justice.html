7th Grade ELA — Module 1
The Odyssey — Week 4 — Lesson

Lesson 4.1 — Homecoming and Justice

Disguise, recognition, and whether the slaughter of the suitors is deserved

Objectives
1

Analyze how Homer uses disguise and recognition as literary devices in Books 13–22, and explain what each recognition scene reveals about the relationship between identity and loyalty.

2

Evaluate whether the slaughter of the suitors constitutes justice, and connect your evaluation to the module’s central question about Odysseus’s virtue.

Warm-Up 2 min
If someone has wronged you or your family while you were away and could not defend yourself, what do you think is a fair response when you return? Does the answer change depending on how badly they wronged you?

That question is exactly what Homer puts before his audience in the final third of The Odyssey — and he does not give a simple answer.

Lesson

Odysseus returns to Ithaca in disguise, and the Books from 13 through 22 are structured around a series of recognition scenes: moments when a character who has known Odysseus sees through the disguise, or is tested to see if they will. His old nurse Eurycleia recognizes him by a scar on his thigh before he silences her. His dog Argos recognizes him and dies. Eumaeus the swineherd, loyal for twenty years without knowing if his master would return, passes the test without even knowing he’s being tested. Penelope is the last and most complex: she devises the contest of the bow, which no one but Odysseus could win, suggesting she may half-know who the disguised beggar is before she officially recognizes him. These scenes do something important: they answer the question of who has been loyal and who has not, sorting the household before Odysseus acts. The slaughter of the suitors in Book 22 is one of the most violent scenes in ancient literature — 108 men killed in a locked hall, with servants who collaborated also executed afterward. Homer presents it as just: the suitors violated xenia, consumed Odysseus’s household, and threatened his family. But readers across centuries have asked whether the violence is proportionate, and whether a man capable of this degree of premeditated killing is fully admirable. The question of Odysseus’s virtue, which opened in the first books, reaches its most difficult test here.

Historical & Literary Context

In Homeric Greece, the concept of dike (justice) was not primarily about legal process; it was about the proper order of things being restored. When Odysseus kills the suitors, he is not acting as a judge in a court — he is restoring the order they violated. Greek audiences would have understood the slaughter as the inevitable and correct culmination of the plot. Modern readers often find it harder to accept. This gap is itself worth examining: it reveals something about the difference between Homeric values and contemporary ones, and it puts real interpretive pressure on the poem.

Vocabulary

DIKE (DEE-kay) — noun

The Greek concept of justice as the proper order of things; right relationships maintained or restored. Not primarily about punishment, but about balance.

Copia practice:

Original: “Odysseus believed the slaughter of the suitors was an act of dike.”

ANAGNORISIS (an-ag-NOR-ih-sis) — noun

A moment of recognition or discovery in a narrative, especially when a character’s true identity is revealed. Aristotle named this one of the essential elements of tragedy.

Copia practice:

Original: “The scene with Eurycleia is the first anagnorisis in Odysseus’s homecoming.”

Comprehension Check
1

Name two recognition scenes from Books 13–22 and explain what each one reveals about loyalty.

▶ Sample answer
Eurycleia recognizes Odysseus by his scar while washing his feet; her recognition shows that faithful servants carry the knowledge of their masters' bodies across decades of absence. Argos the dog recognizes Odysseus and dies immediately after — suggesting that loyalty in its most basic form needs no language and survives longer than the body that holds it.
2

Is the slaughter of the suitors justice or revenge? Write one concessive sentence that grants the strongest point on the other side of whatever position you take.
