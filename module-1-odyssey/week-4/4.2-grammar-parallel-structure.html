7th Grade ELA — Module 1
The Odyssey — Week 4 — Grammar Lesson

Lesson 4.2 — Parallel Structure

Making a list feel inevitable — the grammar of pattern and rhythm

Objectives
1

Identify faulty parallelism in sentences and explain why it disrupts the reader.

2

Write sentences with deliberate parallel structure to create rhythm, emphasis, and clarity.

Warm-Up 1 min
Something is wrong with this sentence: “Odysseus was cunning, brave, and showed patience.” Can you feel it before you can name it? Rewrite the sentence so it sounds right.

The problem is faulty parallelism: the first two items in the list are adjectives (cunning, brave), but the third is a verb phrase (showed patience). When a list breaks its own pattern, the reader stumbles. Parallel structure fixes this.

Lesson

Parallel structure means that items in a list or series use the same grammatical form. If you begin a list with a noun, all items should be nouns. If you begin with an infinitive phrase, all items should be infinitive phrases. If you begin with a subordinate clause, all items should be clauses. Breaking this pattern creates faulty parallelism — the sentence still makes logical sense, but it creates friction, a small cognitive hiccup that interrupts the reader’s experience. Good writers use parallel structure not just to avoid errors, but as a deliberate tool: a list of three perfectly parallel items has a momentum and a rhythm that no other sentence structure quite matches. Homer’s oral poetry relied on this effect constantly — the same grammatical pattern, repeated, gives the listener’s ear something to hold onto and builds toward a conclusion. In your own analytical writing, parallel structure is especially useful in thesis statements and topic sentences, where you need to make a claim that accounts for multiple things at once without losing control of the sentence.

Examples

Faulty (adjective / adjective / verb phrase)

“Penelope was patient, loyal, and showed great intelligence.”

Parallel (adjective / adjective / adjective)

“Penelope was patient, loyal, and intelligent.”

Parallel (infinitive / infinitive / infinitive) — Homeric style

“Odysseus had to survive the Cyclops, to resist Circe’s magic, and to navigate past Scylla and Charybdis.”

Deliberate parallel structure in a claim

“Homer’s Odysseus is admirable for his intelligence, troubling for his pride, and fascinating for the way neither quality can exist without the other.”

Practice
1

Fix the faulty parallelism: “The suitors were arrogant, they were greedy, and showed no respect for Penelope.”

▶ Sample answer
The suitors were arrogant, greedy, and contemptuous of Penelope. (Three adjectives, parallel.) Or: The suitors showed arrogance toward their host, greed toward his household, and contempt toward his wife. (Three noun phrases, parallel.)
2

Write a parallel-structure sentence summarizing what Odysseus’s cunning requires. Use three items in your list, all the same grammatical form. Label the form you used.
