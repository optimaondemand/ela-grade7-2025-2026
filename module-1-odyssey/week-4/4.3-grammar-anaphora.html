The Odyssey — Week 4 — Grammar Lesson

Lesson 4.3 — Anaphora

Repetition as rhetoric — how returning to the same word builds power

Objectives
1

Identify anaphora in literary and persuasive texts and explain what effect the repetition creates.

2

Use anaphora deliberately in a short passage to create emphasis and rhythm.

Warm-Up 1 min
“He suffered on the sea. He suffered in the hall of Circe. He suffered at the edge of the world.” What does the repetition of “He suffered” do that a single sentence (“He suffered in many places”) does not?

The repeated phrase accumulates — each instance adds weight, and by the third, the reader feels the relentlessness of the suffering in a way a summary cannot convey. That technique is anaphora.

Lesson

Anaphora is the repetition of a word or phrase at the beginning of successive clauses, sentences, or lines. It is one of the oldest rhetorical figures in any language — Homer used it, the Hebrew Psalms use it, the King James Bible uses it, Dr. King used it. It works because repetition in language does the same thing repetition does in music: it establishes a pattern, and once the pattern is established, every arrival of the repeated element hits harder than the one before. The repeated phrase functions as a drumbeat, and the new information that follows each repetition rides that beat. Anaphora differs from mere repetition in that it is deliberate and structural — the same word or phrase, at the same position in each unit, building toward a conclusion. It also pairs naturally with parallel structure (Lesson 4.2): the repeated opening is the pattern, and everything that follows it must be grammatically consistent for the figure to work. The most important thing to know about anaphora is when to stop: three or four instances is usually the right number. More than that and the effect tips from powerful into tedious.

Examples

From an Odyssey paraphrase

“He wept for his homeland. He wept for his father, who grew old without him. He wept for the years the sea had taken.”

Each “He wept for” adds an object of grief; by the third, the loss feels total.

In an analytical claim

“Odysseus is cunning when the plan works. He is cunning when the plan fails but he survives. He is cunning even in the moment of boasting — because the boast, too, is a performance, a claim on the story that will be told about him.”

“He is cunning” repeats; the third instance arrives with a twist that reframes everything that came before it.

Practice
1

Identify the anaphora in this passage and explain what effect the repetition creates: “He waited twenty years. He waited while his house was consumed. He waited while his wife wove and unwove the same shroud. He waited — and when he returned, he did not wait any longer.”

▶ Answer
“He waited” is the anaphora. Repeated three times, it establishes patience as Odysseus’s defining quality during the absence; the fourth sentence breaks the pattern deliberately (“he did not wait any longer”), and the break lands with finality because the reader expects one more “He waited.”
2
