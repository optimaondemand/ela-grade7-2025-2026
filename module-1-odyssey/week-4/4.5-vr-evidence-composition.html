7th Grade ELA — Module 1
The Odyssey — Week 4 — VR Activity 2

VR 4.5 — The Evidence Composition

Build your module argument spatially — arrange evidence for your claim about Odysseus’s virtue

What You’re Doing

The module project asks you to take a position on Odysseus’s virtue: is he admirable, troubling, or both? This VR session is where you draft your argument spatially before you write it in prose. You will arrange evidence from three episodes — one from early in the poem, one from the middle, one from the homecoming — into a three-zone composition, write a claim on a Whiteboard IFX that your evidence supports, and record a short spatial recording in which you walk through your argument. The spatial recording becomes the first draft of your module essay.

What You’ll Need
  • Device: VR headset (spatial recording is headset-only)
  • Session type: Persistent Session
  • Session ID: ELA7-M1-W4
  • Location: Ancient Greek Villa (same Location as VR 4.4 — your tableau is still there)
  • Pre-work: Decide before you enter VR which three episodes you will use as evidence and what claim they support. Jot a rough claim on paper first — you’ll refine it on the Whiteboard IFX inside.
How to Join
1. Open ENGAGE on your headset.
2. Select Join a Session and enter Session ID: ELA7-M1-W4.
3. Location: Ancient Greek Villa.
4. Spawn at the main courtyard. Your Zone A/B/C tableau from VR 4.4 will be there. Move past it to the open area near the inner wall — a Whiteboard IFX and a sticky note labeled “Evidence Work Area” mark your workspace.
What to Do
1

Write your claim on the Whiteboard IFX

At the Evidence Work Area, open the Whiteboard IFX and write your claim about Odysseus’s virtue at the top. Your claim should be specific — not “Odysseus is complicated” but something like “Odysseus is admirable for his intelligence but troubling for the way his cunning erases the line between strategy and cruelty.” Use parallel structure (Lesson 4.2) in your claim.

2

Place Evidence Zone 1 — early poem

Choose an episode from Books 1–8 (for example: Odysseus weeping on Calypso’s island, or his introduction in Book 1). Place one or two IFX that represent the scene. Add a sticky note labeled Evidence 1 that states what this episode shows about Odysseus — and how it supports your claim. One to two sentences.

3

Place Evidence Zone 2 — middle poem

Choose an episode from Books 9–12 (the Cyclops, Circe, the Underworld, Scylla and Charybdis, the Sirens). Place IFX and add a sticky note labeled Evidence 2 with one to two sentences connecting this episode to your claim. If this episode complicates your claim rather than confirms it, say so — that makes your argument stronger, not weaker.

4

Place Evidence Zone 3 — homecoming

Choose an episode from Books 13–24 (the recognition scenes, the slaughter of the suitors, the reunion with Penelope). Place IFX and add a sticky note labeled Evidence 3 with one to two sentences. This is usually your strongest or most surprising piece of evidence — save it for last so your argument builds.

5

Record your argument walkthrough

Start a spatial recording and walk from your Whiteboard IFX through Evidence Zones 1, 2, and 3, explaining your claim and how each piece of evidence supports it. Speak clearly and in complete sentences. Aim for 90 seconds to 2 minutes. You do not need to be perfect — this is a draft, and you will refine the ideas in your written project.

What to Submit

Submit the link to your spatial recording to this Canvas assignment, or upload the spatial recording file if your headset exports it directly. If your headset does not support spatial recording export, take a snapshot showing your Whiteboard IFX claim and all three evidence sticky notes, and submit the snapshot instead — then type your spoken walkthrough (2–4 sentences per zone) in the text box.

Points: 10 pts — completion credit (Whiteboard claim + three evidence zones + recording or snapshot submitted).

Estimated Time

30 minutes in-headset. Drafting the Whiteboard claim takes the most time for most students — encourage them to write a rough version first and revise it after they have placed their evidence zones.

Facilitator Note

This session is the planning draft for the module project. Students who struggle with the written project often do so because they have not yet committed to a claim — this session forces the commitment spatially before they write. Common sticking point: students want to say “Odysseus is both admirable and troubling” without specifying what drives each quality or how they relate. Prompt them toward a claim with a hinge: “admirable for X but troubling for Y because they share the same root.” The Whiteboard IFX is already placed in the work area. Spatial recording instructions are on the sticky note at spawn. Students submitting a snapshot + text instead of a recording should be told to write the same ideas they would have spoken — the medium is secondary to the argument.
