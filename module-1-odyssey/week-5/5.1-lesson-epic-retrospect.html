The Odyssey — Week 5 — Lesson

Lesson 5.1 — The Poem Looks Back at Itself

What kind of man does the story say Odysseus is? What kind does it show?

Objectives
1

Explain the difference between what a narrator claims and what a narrative shows, and apply that distinction to Homer’s presentation of Odysseus.

2

Use the full poem’s arc to refine and finalize the claim you will argue in your module project.

Warm-Up 2 min
The opening lines of The Odyssey tell us Odysseus is “never at a loss” and that he “saw the townlands and learned the minds of many distant men.” After reading the whole poem, do you think that description is accurate? Is there a gap between what Homer says Odysseus is and what Homer shows him doing?

That gap — between what a narrator claims and what the story actually shows — is one of the most interesting things to look for in any narrative. Skilled readers learn to read both levels at once.

Lesson

Every narrative has two layers: what it tells and what it shows. The narrator of The Odyssey tells us in the first lines that Odysseus is cunning, experienced, and devoted to returning home. The story then shows us something more complicated. Odysseus weeps on Calypso’s island — but spends his nights with her. He boasts his name to the Cyclops, inviting a decade more of suffering. He loses every single one of his men. He keeps his identity secret from Penelope until she has already passed the test of the bow — which protects him strategically but also makes his homecoming look like a trap he has set for his own wife. None of this makes Odysseus uninteresting or unworthy of reading — it makes him richer and harder to settle. The gap between the hero the poem promises and the man the poem delivers is where the real argument lives.

There is a term for the difference between what a narrator claims is true and what the events of a story suggest: narrative irony. It is not sarcasm — it is the effect that arises when a reader can see something about a character or situation that the narrator either cannot or chooses not to state directly. Homer uses this throughout The Odyssey. The narrator calls Odysseus “godlike” and “great-hearted” even at moments when his behavior is anything but. Whether this is the narrator’s blind admiration, or Homer’s deliberate irony inviting us to question those epithets, is the interpretive question your module project will take up.

Historical & Literary Context

Oral epic poetry in the Homeric tradition uses formulaic epithets — fixed phrases attached to a character or object that fill a specific metrical slot in the line: “wily Odysseus,” “rosy-fingered Dawn,” “bright-eyed Athena.” Scholars once thought these epithets were purely mechanical — the singer reached for them when he needed a particular number of syllables. More recent scholarship has argued that the epithets are also meaningful: “wily” keeps Odysseus’s central quality in front of the audience at every appearance. This matters for interpretation: when Homer calls Odysseus “sacker of cities” in a line where Odysseus is weeping, is that ironic, or simply formulaic? The answer shapes how you read the character.

Vocabulary

NARRATIVE IRONY — noun

The gap between what a narrator claims and what the story’s events actually suggest — the effect that arises when a reader can see something the narrator does not name.

Copia practice:

Original: “The scene with the Cyclops is an example of narrative irony.”

EPITHET — noun

A descriptive word or phrase attached to a person’s name and repeated as a fixed formula. In oral poetry, epithets serve a metrical function but also characterize — “wily Odysseus,” “swift-footed Achilles.”

Copia practice:

Original: “Homer uses the epithet ‘wily’ for Odysseus throughout the poem.”

Comprehension Check
1

Identify one moment in The Odyssey where there is a gap between what the narrator claims about Odysseus and what his actions actually show. Describe the gap in two to three sentences.

▶ Sample answer
After the Cyclops encounter, the narrator calls Odysseus “great-hearted” even as Odysseus shouts his real name back across the water, gifting the Cyclops with the information he needs to invoke Poseidon’s curse. The narrator’s epithet praises Odysseus’s courage; the action shows recklessness that endangers his men. The gap invites us to ask whether “great-hearted” describes a virtue or an excess.
2

Based on the complete poem, write one sentence that states your final claim about Odysseus’s virtue. Use parallel structure and at least two specific qualities. This will be the thesis of your module project.
