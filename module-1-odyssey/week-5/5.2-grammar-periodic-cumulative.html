7th Grade ELA — Module 1
The Odyssey — Week 5 — Grammar Lesson

Lesson 5.2 — Periodic and Cumulative Sentences

Two ways to build a long sentence — and two different experiences they create for the reader

Objectives
1

Distinguish a periodic sentence from a cumulative sentence and explain the different effect each creates.

2

Write one of each type in the context of your argument about Odysseus.

Warm-Up 1 min
Compare these two sentences: (A) “Odysseus waited.” (B) “Although he had crossed the sea, descended to the Underworld, outwitted a Cyclops, and survived ten years of gods and monsters, Odysseus waited.” Which creates more suspense before the main idea arrives? Which gives you the main idea first and then enriches it?

Sentence B withholds the main verb until the very end — making you wait through everything Odysseus endured before you know what he does at the end of it. That is a periodic sentence. Now reverse it: “Odysseus waited, having crossed the sea, descended to the Underworld, outwitted a Cyclops, and survived ten years of gods and monsters.” Now the main idea comes first and the rest accumulates behind it. That is a cumulative sentence.

Lesson

A periodic sentence withholds the main clause until the end. Everything before the main clause — subordinate clauses, participial phrases, absolute phrases, appositive chains — builds suspense and makes the reader wait. The longer the build-up, the more weight the main clause carries when it arrives. Periodic sentences are the grammar of anticipation: they are especially powerful at the end of a paragraph, where the reader is already primed to expect a conclusion. A cumulative sentence states the main clause first and then accumulates detail after it. The reader knows where they are going from the first word, and everything that follows adds texture, nuance, or example. Cumulative sentences are the grammar of elaboration: they are especially useful mid-paragraph, where the writer is building out a claim that has already been stated. Both types appear constantly in literary and analytical prose; both are available to you as a writer. The choice between them is a choice about reader experience — suspense and arrival (periodic) versus confidence and accumulation (cumulative).

Examples

Periodic — suspense before the main clause

“Because he could not stop boasting, because he could not resist the satisfaction of letting his enemy know who had beaten him, Odysseus gave Polyphemus his real name.”

The two “because” clauses build up before the main action is named. The wait makes the moment of naming feel more significant.

Cumulative — main clause first, then accumulation

“Odysseus gave Polyphemus his real name, unable to resist the satisfaction of being known, unwilling to let his victory remain anonymous, too proud to let cunning be his only memorial.”

The main action is named immediately; the participial phrases that follow deepen the explanation of why without creating suspense.

Periodic — in an analytical claim

“If cunning without restraint is not a virtue, and if the evidence of Books 9 through 12 suggests that Odysseus’s cunning consistently exceeds the restraint necessary to protect his men, then Homer’s hero is not fully admirable on the terms the poem itself establishes.”

Practice
1

Identify whether this sentence is periodic or cumulative and explain your answer in one sentence: “Penelope was loyal, weaving and unweaving the same shroud for three years, deflecting suitors with a patience that looked passive but was actually a form of power, waiting for a husband she had no guarantee would return.”

▶ Answer
Cumulative: the main clause (“Penelope was loyal”) is stated first, and the three participial phrases that follow accumulate the evidence and texture that explain what that loyalty looked like in practice.
2

Write one periodic sentence and one cumulative sentence, each making a claim about Odysseus’s virtue. Label each. Your periodic sentence should use at least one subordinate clause before the main clause arrives.
