The Odyssey — Week 5 — VR Activity 2

VR 5.5 — The Final Argument Recording

Record your complete argument about Odysseus’s virtue — the spatial version of your module essay

What You’re Doing

You have a thesis, three pieces of evidence, and a draft argument from VR 4.5. You have read at least one other student’s composition and responded to it. Now you will record your final, polished argument as a spatial recording — walking from your thesis on the Whiteboard IFX through each evidence zone, giving your full argument in spoken sentences. This recording is the VR component of your module project. It should be 2–3 minutes, prepared in advance, and spoken in the same analytical voice you will use in your written essay. You may update your Whiteboard IFX thesis and evidence sticky notes before recording if the gallery walk changed your thinking.

What You’ll Need
  • Device: VR headset (spatial recording is headset-only)
  • Session type: Persistent Session
  • Session ID: ELA7-M1-W4
  • Location: Ancient Greek Villa (your Evidence Composition is still there)
  • Pre-work: Write out or rehearse your 2–3 minute argument before you enter. You can bring notes — the recording does not have to be memorized, but it should be fluent enough to hold together as an argument from start to finish.
How to Join
1. Open ENGAGE on your headset.
2. Select Join a Session and enter Session ID: ELA7-M1-W4.
3. Location: Ancient Greek Villa. Navigate to your Evidence Composition in the work area beyond the gallery.
What to Do
1

Update your composition if needed

If the gallery walk (VR 5.4) changed your thinking — refined your claim, added a counterargument you need to answer, or revealed a better piece of evidence — update your Whiteboard IFX thesis and/or evidence sticky notes before you record. You may add one additional IFX if the revision requires it.

2

Position yourself at the Whiteboard IFX

Stand in front of your Whiteboard IFX. Make sure all three evidence zones are visible behind you. This is your starting position for the recording.

3

Record your argument walkthrough — 2–3 minutes

Start a spatial recording. State your thesis clearly in the first 20 seconds. Then walk to Evidence Zone 1 and explain it. Walk to Zone 2 and explain it — including any complication it introduces. Walk to Zone 3 and explain your strongest or most surprising evidence. Return to the Whiteboard IFX and state your conclusion — what the evidence together shows about Odysseus that one piece alone could not. If you have a counterargument to answer (from the gallery walk), do it before the conclusion. Keep the recording under 3 minutes — a tight argument is a stronger argument.

What to Submit

Submit the spatial recording link to this Canvas assignment. If your headset exports the file directly, upload the file. This recording is the VR component of your module project and will be included with your written essay submission — your teacher will review both together. If technical issues prevent recording, submit a 300–400 word written transcript of what you would have said, typed into the text box.

Points: 10 pts — completion credit (recording 2–3 minutes submitted; all three evidence zones addressed).

Estimated Time

25–30 minutes. Most students need one or two attempts at the recording before they feel confident. Budget time for a restart if the first take falls apart mid-argument — that is normal and the spatial recording can be started again from the beginning.

Facilitator Note

This is the VR component of the module project. Students who have done VR 4.5 well will find this recording relatively natural — they are refining an argument they have already drafted spatially. Students who struggled with VR 4.5 may need encouragement to update their composition before recording rather than recording over a weak first draft. Remind students that the recording is not a separate assignment from the written essay — it is the same argument in a different medium, and the teacher will read both together. Common problem: students record for less than 90 seconds because they run out of things to say. This almost always means their evidence zones were not specific enough — prompt them to add a concrete detail from the text to each zone before recording.
