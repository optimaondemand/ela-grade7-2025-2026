7th Grade ELA — Module 2
The Hobbit — Week 1 — Lesson

Lesson 1.1 — The Reluctant Hero

Bilbo Baggins and the call he didn’t want

Objectives
1

Analyze how Tolkien establishes Bilbo as an anti-hero at the novel’s opening and trace how the first six chapters begin to change him.

2

Compare Bilbo’s call to adventure to Odysseus’s situation at the start of The Odyssey, noting what each hero gains and loses by leaving home.

Warm-Up 2 min
Think of a time when you were asked to do something you felt completely unqualified for. Did you do it? What happened? Now think about Bilbo Baggins: Gandalf chooses him not for any obvious heroic quality but because Gandalf “wants” him. What does it mean to be chosen for a hero’s role before you’ve done anything heroic?

That question — whether heroism is something you are or something you become — is the engine of this novel. Tolkien’s answer unfolds across nineteen chapters.

Lesson

The Hobbit opens with a hero who is explicitly unsuited for what lies ahead. Bilbo Baggins values routine, comfort, a well-stocked pantry, and the absence of anything unexpected — which means he values the absence of adventure. Tolkien builds this introductory portrait carefully: Bag End is described with loving detail, Bilbo’s domestic contentment is real and sympathetic, and his refusal of Gandalf’s invitation is entirely reasonable. The novel does not mock Bilbo for this; it takes his Baggins side seriously. But Tolkien also introduces the Tookish side — the inherited tendency toward curiosity and wandering that Bilbo has spent his life suppressing. The unexpected party does not create Bilbo’s adventurous impulse; it uncovers one that was already there. This is what makes the novel’s central question interesting: Bilbo does not change into someone he was not. He becomes more fully himself. Compare him to Odysseus, who is already a seasoned warrior and survivor when The Odyssey begins — his heroism is established before the poem opens; we watch him exercise it, not discover it. Bilbo’s heroism is entirely prospective: he has done nothing yet, and every step of his transformation is visible to the reader. By the end of Chapter 6 — the troll encounter, the goblin tunnels, the riddle game, the eagle rescue — he has survived things that would have been unthinkable in Chapter 1. The question the module will pursue is whether survival is the same thing as heroism, or whether Tolkien is asking for something more.

Historical & Literary Context

Tolkien published The Hobbit in 1937. He was a professor of Old English at Oxford, and the novel draws heavily on Norse and Anglo-Saxon mythology: the dwarves’ names come from the Old Norse poem Völuspá, and Gandalf means “wand-elf.” Tolkien was also a veteran of World War I, and some scholars read Bilbo’s journey through dangerous landscapes as shaped by that experience — the sense that ordinary people can be placed in extraordinary danger and must find reserves they did not know they had. Unlike the martial heroes of the myths he studied, Bilbo is explicitly un-martial: his weapons are his wits, his luck, and a magic ring he didn’t ask for.

Vocabulary

EUCATASTROPHE (yoo-KAT-uh-stroh-fee) — noun

Tolkien’s own coinage for the sudden, joyous turn of events that averts disaster at the last possible moment — the redemptive climax he believed all good fantasy must contain. The eagle rescue at the end of Chapter 6 is an early example.

Copia practice:

Original: “The arrival of the eagles at the end of Chapter 6 is an eucatastrophe.”

TOOKISH — adjective (Tolkien’s coinage)

Adventurous, impulsive, drawn to the unknown — from Bilbo’s maternal ancestors, the Tooks, who were considered slightly “odd” among Hobbits for their wandering tendencies. The opposite of Baggins-ish.

Copia practice:

Original: “Something Tookish woke up inside Bilbo when he heard the dwarves’ song.”

Comprehension Check
1

Gandalf tells Bilbo he is looking for someone to share in “an adventure.” Bilbo’s first response is politely horrified. What does this exchange reveal about what Tolkien values in a hero — and what he does not?

▶ Sample answer
Tolkien’s opening deliberately inverts the heroic convention: his hero begins by refusing the call entirely. This suggests that heroism, for Tolkien, is not a fixed identity but something that emerges under pressure — the hero is not chosen because he already is one, but so that he might become one. The comedy of Bilbo’s polite horror is also the novel’s first serious point.
2

By the end of Chapter 6, Bilbo has done several things he never thought he could do. Name two specific actions and explain what each reveals about his developing character.
