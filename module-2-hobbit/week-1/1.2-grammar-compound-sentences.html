The Hobbit — Week 1 — Grammar Lesson

Lesson 1.2 — Compound Sentences and Coordinating Conjunctions

Joining two complete thoughts — and why the joining word matters

Objectives
1

Identify and correctly punctuate compound sentences using coordinating conjunctions.

2

Choose the right coordinating conjunction to express the logical relationship between two clauses.

Warm-Up 1 min
Something is off about this sentence: “Bilbo wanted to stay home, and he went on the adventure anyway.” The word and suggests simple addition, but the two clauses are actually in tension. What word would better capture the relationship?

The problem is a mismatched conjunction. Choosing the wrong one creates a sentence that is grammatically correct but logically misleading. The coordinating conjunction is not decoration — it is the hinge that names the relationship between the two clauses.

Lesson

A compound sentence joins two independent clauses with a coordinating conjunction and a comma. The seven coordinating conjunctions are remembered with the acronym FANBOYS: For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So. Each signals a different logical relationship:

FOR
reason or cause (“He went, for Gandalf had asked him”)
AND
addition (“He packed his handkerchief, and he packed nothing else”)
NOR
neither alternative (“He had not fought a dragon, nor had he wanted to”)
BUT
contrast (“He was afraid, but he went in anyway”)
OR
alternative (“He could go forward, or he could go back”)
YET
contrast, stronger than but (“He was terrified, yet something Tookish kept him walking”)
SO
result or consequence (“Gandalf kept the trolls talking, so dawn came before they could act”)

Using the wrong conjunction creates friction — the sentence makes grammatical sense but the reader feels a mismatch between the logical relationship implied and the relationship stated. Tolkien’s prose uses compound sentences constantly, especially in action sequences where events pile up quickly.

Examples

“Bilbo had never left the Shire, yet something stirred in him when he heard the dwarves sing.”

YET — contrast with an unexpected quality; stronger than but because the contradiction feels more surprising.

“The trolls argued about how to cook the dwarves, so Gandalf kept them talking until dawn.”

SO — result; Gandalf’s action follows directly from the trolls’ distraction.

“Bilbo could go back to Bag End, or he could go forward into the dark.”

OR — alternative; exactly two options, mutually exclusive.

“He was afraid of the dark, but he went in anyway.” vs. “He was afraid of the dark, yet he went in anyway.”

BUT is the standard contrast conjunction; YET makes the contrast feel more significant — choose based on how much weight the contrast should carry.

Practice
1

Fix the conjunction: “Bilbo found the ring in the dark, and he didn’t know what it was.” What conjunction better captures the relationship? Rewrite the sentence.

▶ Sample answer
YET or BUT: “Bilbo found the ring in the dark, yet he had no idea what it was.” The two clauses are in contrast — finding something versus not understanding it — and and implies mere addition. YET captures the irony more precisely: he holds the most powerful object in the world and is completely unaware of it.
2

Write a compound sentence about Bilbo’s character using YET or SO. Label which conjunction you used and explain in one sentence why that conjunction fits the relationship between your two clauses.
