7th Grade ELA — Module 2
The Hobbit — Week 1 — Grammar Lesson

Lesson 1.3 — The Semicolon

A full stop that isn’t — joining closely related thoughts without a conjunction

Objectives
1

Use semicolons correctly to join two closely related independent clauses.

2

Distinguish when a semicolon is the right choice from when a period, comma, or coordinating conjunction would be better.

Warm-Up 1 min
Read these two versions: (A) “Bilbo was a homebody. He kept his pantry well-stocked.” (B) “Bilbo was a homebody; he kept his pantry well-stocked.” What does the semicolon do that the period does not?

The period treats the two sentences as separate. The semicolon pulls them together without naming the connection — it implies the relationship rather than stating it, and trusts the reader to feel it.

Lesson

A semicolon joins two independent clauses without a coordinating conjunction. Its effect differs from both a period and a comma+conjunction: a period creates a full stop and treats the clauses as separate; a conjunction + comma names the relationship explicitly; a semicolon implies the relationship without naming it, creating a tighter connection that asks the reader to infer how the two clauses relate. This implication is the semicolon’s power and its risk — it works best when the relationship is obvious enough that the reader can feel it without being told. If the relationship is not clear, use a conjunction instead.

Semicolons also appear before conjunctive adverbs (however, therefore, nevertheless, consequently, moreover) when connecting independent clauses: “Bilbo was frightened; nevertheless, he pressed on.” The comma after the conjunctive adverb is required. And one firm rule: never use a semicolon between a dependent clause and an independent clause.

Examples

“Bilbo had never burgled anything in his life; he had no idea where to begin.”

The second clause explains the first — the relationship (cause-effect) is clear enough that the reader feels it without being told.

“The dwarves were loud and demanding; however, Bilbo made them tea and scones anyway.”

Conjunctive adverb (however) + comma after — both required.

WRONG: “Although Bilbo was frightened; he went anyway.”

Never use a semicolon between a dependent clause (“Although Bilbo was frightened”) and an independent clause. Use a comma: “Although Bilbo was frightened, he went anyway.”

Practice
1

Identify the error and rewrite correctly: “Bilbo packed quickly; and ran out the door without his pocket-handkerchief.”

▶ Answer
Error: do not use a semicolon before a coordinating conjunction. Fix option 1 — use a comma: “Bilbo packed quickly, and he ran out the door without his pocket-handkerchief.” Fix option 2 — remove the conjunction: “Bilbo packed quickly; he ran out the door without his pocket-handkerchief.”
2

Write two sentences about The Hobbit and join them with a semicolon. Your two clauses must be genuinely related — the semicolon should feel inevitable, not random. Then write one sentence explaining what implied relationship the semicolon is relying on.
