# Week 1 Quiz — The Hobbit, Chapters 1–6
# 10 questions × 5 pts = 50 points
# Format: A) / B) / C) / D) with **Answer: X**

---

**Q1.** At the opening of *The Hobbit*, Tolkien describes Bilbo as someone who values "never doing anything unexpected." What does this detail establish about Bilbo's character before the adventure begins?

A) He is timid because of a childhood trauma involving the outside world.
B) He has deliberately suppressed his Tookish side and built an identity around predictability.
C) He is too old to be a convincing adventurer, and Gandalf has chosen the wrong hobbit.
D) He represents the typical hobbit — unremarkable, passive, and without any potential for growth.

**Answer: B**

---

**Q2.** Gandalf tells Bilbo he is looking for someone to share in "an adventure." Bilbo's first response is to offer Gandalf a "good morning" and back away. What does this exchange suggest about how Tolkien defines the start of a hero's journey?

A) Heroes always refuse the call at first — refusal is a sign of wisdom.
B) Adventure finds the unlikely person, not the one who seeks it out.
C) The hero must be tricked or deceived into beginning the journey.
D) Bilbo's refusal proves he is not truly the right choice for the quest.

**Answer: B**

---

**Q3.** The dwarves describe Bilbo to Gandalf as their "burglar." Bilbo has never burgled anything. What is the effect of giving Bilbo this title before he has done anything to earn it?

A) It creates a comic mismatch that Tolkien uses only for humor, not for theme.
B) It suggests that identity can precede action — that being named something can create pressure to become it.
C) It proves the dwarves are foolish judges of character who will regret their choice.
D) It establishes that Gandalf is manipulating everyone, including the dwarves.

**Answer: B**

---

**Q4.** In Chapter 2, Bilbo attempts to steal from one of the trolls. He is caught immediately. What does his failed burglary attempt reveal about his character at this early stage of the journey?

A) He is fundamentally incompetent and will need to be rescued throughout the novel.
B) He is willing to attempt something terrifying even when he has no idea how to do it.
C) He was overconfident, which is his primary character flaw throughout the novel.
D) The attempt shows he has already fully transformed into a hero by Chapter 2.

**Answer: B**

---

**Q5.** Gandalf saves the company from the trolls by tricking Bert, Tom, and William into arguing with each other until dawn turns them to stone. What does this rescue suggest about the kind of heroism Tolkien values?

A) Brute force is always necessary — Gandalf simply had more of it than the dwarves.
B) True heroism means never needing help; Bilbo's failure disqualifies him as the hero.
C) Cunning and intelligence can accomplish what strength cannot — and both matter.
D) The scene is primarily comic and has no bearing on the novel's treatment of heroism.

**Answer: C**

---

**Q6.** In the goblin tunnels of Chapter 4, the company is captured while Bilbo is separated from the group. He wakes alone in the dark with no weapon, no companions, and no plan. What does this moment represent in terms of Bilbo's development?

A) A punishment for leaving the Shire — Tolkien shows that adventuring has real costs.
B) The first time Bilbo must rely entirely on himself, with no Gandalf to rescue him.
C) Evidence that the quest has gone wrong and cannot be recovered.
D) A test designed by Gandalf to see whether Bilbo is worthy of the ring.

**Answer: B**

---

**Q7.** The riddle game between Bilbo and Gollum in Chapter 5 is governed by rules both characters honor — Gollum will show the way out if he loses; Bilbo will let himself be eaten if he loses. What does this scene suggest about the relationship between cleverness and ethics in the novel?

A) Rules exist only to be broken — Bilbo wins by cheating, and the novel approves.
B) Intelligence is morally neutral; what matters is how you use it under pressure.
C) The riddle game shows that Bilbo's Baggins side is more useful than his Tookish side.
D) Gollum is honorable and Bilbo is not — Bilbo's final "riddle" is an unfair trick.

**Answer: B**

---

**Q8.** When Bilbo finds the ring in the dark of the tunnel, he pockets it without fully understanding what it is. He uses it to escape Gollum. What is significant about the way Tolkien has Bilbo acquire the ring?

A) It proves Bilbo has a thieving instinct that will cause problems later.
B) The ring comes to Bilbo by chance, not by seeking — he does not pursue power.
C) Tolkien intends the reader to see this as a moral failing Bilbo must overcome.
D) Finding the ring transforms Bilbo immediately into a capable and confident hero.

**Answer: B**

---

**Q9.** By the end of Chapter 6, after the encounter with the Wargs and the eagle rescue, Bilbo is exhausted, injured, and mostly carried by an eagle. Thorin Oakenshield says he began to think Gandalf had made a poor choice. What does Thorin's comment reveal about the novel's central conflict?

A) Thorin is right — Bilbo has not yet done anything that justifies his place in the company.
B) The novel is building toward a moment when Bilbo will prove Thorin wrong through a specific act of courage or cunning.
C) Thorin's skepticism is the novel's way of suggesting the quest will ultimately fail.
D) Bilbo agrees with Thorin, which is why he considers turning back at the start of Chapter 7.

**Answer: B**

---

**Q10.** Tolkien coined the word *eucatastrophe* to describe a sudden joyful turn that averts disaster at the last moment. Which of the following events from Chapters 1–6 best fits this definition?

A) Bilbo's departure from Bag End without his pocket-handkerchief.
B) The riddle game ending with Bilbo's "What have I got in my pocket?" question.
C) The eagles arriving to rescue the company from the treetops just before the fire reaches them.
D) Gandalf's reappearance at the dwarves' unexpected party.

**Answer: C**
