7th Grade ELA — Module 2
The Hobbit — Week 2 — Lesson

Lesson 2.1 — Riddles in the Dark

Language as power — the riddle game, the ring, and what Bilbo finds in himself

Objectives
1

Analyze how Tolkien uses the riddle game as a test of intelligence, nerve, and moral character — and what Bilbo’s performance reveals about his development.

2

Trace how Bilbo’s acquisition of the ring and his first use of his own name (in Chapter 8) mark two distinct moments of self-definition.

Warm-Up 2 min
Think of a situation where you had to talk your way out of something — or where the right words made all the difference. Now consider: Bilbo Baggins, who has never fought a monster, never traveled more than a few miles from home, alone in the dark under a mountain with no weapon and no plan. His only asset is his mind and his mouth. Is that enough?

Chapter 5 answers that question. And the answer Tolkien gives — yes, sometimes the right words at the right moment are exactly enough — is one of the most important things the novel argues.

Lesson

Chapter 5 of The Hobbit is unlike anything that came before it. The company has vanished; the dwarves and Gandalf are somewhere else in the mountain. Bilbo is alone in absolute dark, with nothing but a ring he found by accident and the contents of his own mind. What happens next is not a fight — it is a conversation, governed by rules both parties agree to honor, in which the stakes are Bilbo’s life. The riddle game is an ancient tradition in Norse and Anglo-Saxon literature; Tolkien, a scholar of these traditions, knew that riddles were considered a form of wisdom contest — the one who could frame and solve riddles well was the one who understood the world more deeply. What makes Bilbo’s riddle game remarkable is not that he wins by superior intellect. He wins partly by luck (his “What have I got in my pocket?” question is not technically a riddle at all) and partly by the nerve to use what he has rather than freeze. By Chapters 8 and 9, something has shifted further: when Bilbo names himself to the spiders — “I am Barrel-rider” — and later, in full honesty, “I am Bilbo Baggins of the Shire” — Tolkien marks a moment of genuine self-possession. The hobbit who could not introduce himself to trolls without being caught now chooses his own name, on his own terms, as a declaration. Between Chapter 1 and Chapter 9, language has shifted from something that happens to Bilbo to something he wields. This is not yet heroism, but it is its precondition.

Historical & Literary Context

The riddle contest appears in some of the oldest literature in the English tradition. In Beowulf, the hero’s wisdom is tested through speech as much as combat. The Anglo-Saxon poem The Exeter Book contains dozens of riddles — some still unsolved — that were treated as serious intellectual exercises, not games. Tolkien taught these texts at Oxford and built the riddle game in Chapter 5 as a deliberate echo of that tradition. Riddles work by forcing the speaker to describe an object from the outside, without naming it — to hold two things in mind at once (what the thing is and how it appears). A reader who knows this context hears the riddle game as Bilbo entering a very old kind of test.

Vocabulary

KENNING (KEN-ing) — noun

A compressed poetic phrase that describes something without naming it directly — a feature of Old English and Norse poetry. “Whale-road” for the sea; “bone-house” for a body; “word-hoard” for a vocabulary. Tolkien’s riddles use kenning-like constructions: “Alive without breath, / as cold as death” (fish). Each riddle is a kenning extended into verse.

Copia practice:

Original: “Tolkien’s riddles use kennings to describe things without naming them.”

SELF-POSSESSION — noun

The quality of being in control of one’s own feelings, words, and actions under pressure — not the same as confidence or courage, but the steadiness that makes both possible. When Bilbo names himself to the spiders in Chapter 8, he is exercising self-possession: he chooses how to present himself rather than reacting to the situation blindly.

Copia practice:

Original: “Bilbo’s decision to name himself to the spiders shows a new self-possession.”

Comprehension Check
1

Bilbo’s final “riddle” — “What have I got in my pocket?” — is not technically a riddle at all. Gollum agrees to accept it anyway. Does this make Bilbo’s victory fair or unfair? What does your answer reveal about what the scene is really testing?

▶ Sample answer
The scene is testing nerve, not fairness. Bilbo uses what he has — a lucky guess that happens to be unanswerable — rather than waiting for a riddle he cannot produce under pressure. The real question is not whether it was technically fair but whether Bilbo had the presence of mind to recognize and use the moment. He did. Tolkien seems to value that kind of practical intelligence over formal rule-following.
2

In Chapter 8, Bilbo kills the giant spider with his sword — and then names the sword “Sting.” In traditional epic literature, naming a weapon is a significant act. What does it mean, in terms of character, that Bilbo names his weapon after doing something heroic rather than before?
