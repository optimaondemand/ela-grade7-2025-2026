7th Grade ELA — Module 2
The Hobbit — Week 2 — Grammar Lesson

Lesson 2.2 — Active and Passive Voice

Who acts, who is acted upon — and why it matters which one you put first

Objectives
1

Identify active and passive voice constructions and explain the difference in emphasis each creates.

2

Understand when passive voice is the stronger choice — and when it hides meaning the reader needs.

Warm-Up 1 min
Compare these two versions: (A) “Bilbo killed the spider.” (B) “The spider was killed by Bilbo.” Both sentences describe the same event. What changes between them? Which puts Bilbo at the center, and which puts the spider there?

Version A is active — Bilbo is the subject, the one doing the action. Version B is passive — the spider is the grammatical subject, and Bilbo is pushed to the end. The event is the same; the emphasis is completely different. In Chapter 8 of The Hobbit, Tolkien writes version A. That choice matters.

Lesson

In an active voice sentence, the subject performs the action: Bilbo slipped on the ring. In a passive voice sentence, the subject receives the action: The ring was slipped on by Bilbo — or, in its most stripped-down form, The ring was slipped on (agent unmentioned entirely). Passive voice is formed with a form of be (is, was, were, has been, will be) followed by a past participle (killed, caught, carried, found).

The case against passive voice is well-known: it can hide agency, weaken verbs, and make writing feel evasive. “Mistakes were made” is the classic example — passive voice with the agent deliberately omitted. But passive voice also has legitimate uses that writers should recognize rather than fear:

  • When the receiver of the action matters more than the doer: “The ring was found in the dark.” The emphasis is on the ring and the circumstances — who found it is less important than that it was found.
  • When the agent is unknown or irrelevant: “The cave had been carved centuries before.”
  • When you want to avoid repeating the subject too many times in a paragraph.

The key is intention: passive voice chosen deliberately for emphasis is a craft decision; passive voice used habitually because it sounds more formal is a mistake.

Examples from The Hobbit

“He stabbed the Mirkwood spider with his short sword.” — Active

Bilbo is doing. This is the first time in the novel that Bilbo kills something on purpose, without Gandalf, without the dwarves, without any external help. Active voice is the right choice: the emphasis belongs on Bilbo as the agent.

“The dwarves were captured by the spiders and hung in their webs.” — Passive

The dwarves are being acted upon — helpless, passive — which is exactly the point. Passive voice here mirrors their situation: they are not the agents of their own rescue; Bilbo is.

“A ring was found in the dark.” vs. “Bilbo found a ring in the dark.”

The passive version emphasizes the ring and the mystery of its discovery; the active version emphasizes Bilbo as finder. Both are defensible — the choice depends on what the writer wants the reader to feel.

Practice
1

Rewrite in active voice: “The riddle was answered by Bilbo just before the time ran out.” Then explain: does the active version feel stronger? Does anything get lost?

▶ Answer
Active: “Bilbo answered the riddle just before the time ran out.” This is stronger and clearer — Bilbo is the subject, which is where the emphasis belongs. The passive version buries him. Nothing of substance is lost by switching to active here; the passive was simply weaker.
2

Write one sentence about the Mirkwood spider encounter in active voice, and one sentence about the dwarves’ capture in passive voice. For each, write one sentence explaining why that voice is the right choice for that subject.
