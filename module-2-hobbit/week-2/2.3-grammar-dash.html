Lesson 2.3 — The Dash

The sharpest tool in punctuation — and how to wield it without overdoing it

Objectives
1

Use the em dash correctly for three purposes: emphasis, interruption, and appositive replacement.

2

Distinguish the em dash from the hyphen and the en dash, and understand why overuse of the dash weakens it.

Warm-Up 1 min
Read these two sentences: (A) “Bilbo reached into the darkness and felt something cold, smooth, and small.” (B) “Bilbo reached into the darkness and felt something cold, smooth, and small — a ring.” What does the dash do that the rest of the sentence cannot do alone?

The dash creates a beat — a tiny pause that sharpens what follows. In version B, “a ring” lands with more weight because the dash delays it and isolates it. The information is the same in both sentences; the experience of reading them is not.

Lesson

The em dash (—) is the longest of the three dash-related marks: the hyphen (-) joins compound words; the en dash (–) indicates ranges (pages 10–15); the em dash creates emphasis, signals interruption, or replaces a comma or parenthesis when you want a stronger pause. It is typed as two hyphens (--) in most word processors, which auto-convert. Three main uses:

1. Emphasis at the end of a sentence

Place the dash before the word or phrase you want the reader to feel: “He had found the one thing he needed most — a way out.” The information after the dash is already contained in the sentence logically; the dash makes it land harder.

2. Interruption or abrupt shift

Use a pair of dashes to interrupt the flow of a sentence with an inserted thought or detail: “Bilbo — hobbit, burglar, and reluctant hero — stood at the edge of the forest.” The dashes are stronger than commas: they signal that the interruption is worth noticing, not just a parenthetical. Do not use more than one pair of dashes per sentence.

3. In dialogue: interrupted speech

When a character is cut off mid-sentence: “I don’t want to go anywhere near —” began Bilbo, but Thorin had already walked away. The dash replaces a period; no closing quotation mark after it (the interruption is the end of the speech).

One rule: the dash loses its power when overused. If every other sentence has a dash, none of them feel emphatic. Reserve the dash for moments that genuinely need that extra weight.

Practice
1

Rewrite this sentence using a dash for emphasis: “Bilbo finally felt something he had not felt since leaving the Shire, and that thing was courage.”

▶ Answer
“Bilbo finally felt something he had not felt since leaving the Shire — courage.” The dash isolates “courage” and makes it arrive with more force than the original phrasing.
2

Write two sentences about The Hobbit using the dash: one with a pair of dashes for interruption (inserting a detail about a character), and one with a dash at the end for emphasis. Identify which use you used in each sentence.
