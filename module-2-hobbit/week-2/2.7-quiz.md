# Week 2 Quiz — The Hobbit, Chapters 5–10

# Format: A) / B) / C) / D) with **Answer: X**

**Q1.** In the riddle game, both Bilbo and Gollum agree to follow the rules — Gollum will show the way out if he loses; Bilbo is eaten if he loses. What makes this agreement significant, given that Gollum is a dangerous creature with no obligation to honor it?

A) It shows that Gollum is fundamentally good and can be trusted in any situation.
B) It reveals that even in extreme danger, Bilbo instinctively seeks a civilized framework rather than trying to run or fight.
C) It proves that Bilbo is naive — he should never trust Gollum's word.
D) The agreement is just a plot device and has no deeper meaning for characterization.

**Answer: B**

---

**Q2.** Bilbo's final "riddle" — "What have I got in my pocket?" — is not a true riddle because it has no logical solution Gollum could ever reach. Yet the scene is presented as Bilbo's moment of victory. What does this suggest about Tolkien's view of intelligence and heroism?

A) Cheating is acceptable as long as the cause is just.
B) The ability to improvise under pressure — to use what you have — is as valuable as formal cleverness.
C) Bilbo's victory is hollow because he did not win fairly.
D) Gollum is simply foolish for accepting an unfair riddle.

**Answer: B**

---

**Q3.** When Bilbo puts on the ring and becomes invisible, he discovers he can move through spaces and observe situations without being observed. How does this ability change his role in the company from this point forward?

A) It makes him lazy — he relies on the ring rather than developing his own skills.
B) It gives him a tool that transforms him from a burden into an asset — a scout and rescuer rather than a passenger.
C) It corrupts him immediately, as it does with Gollum.
D) It creates jealousy among the dwarves, which becomes the central conflict of the second half.

**Answer: B**

---

**Q4.** Tolkien describes the Mirkwood forest as "dangerously enchanting" — beautiful but disorienting, full of darkness and strange lights that lure travelers off the path. What literary purpose does Mirkwood serve in the novel?

A) It is a realistic description of northern European forests that Tolkien knew from his research.
B) It functions as an externalized landscape of confusion and temptation — the physical environment mirrors the company's psychological state.
C) It exists solely to introduce the Wood-elves as a new group of characters.
D) It is meant to show that nature is always a greater threat than monsters.

**Answer: B**

---

**Q5.** In Chapter 8, Bilbo kills a Mirkwood spider alone — without Gandalf, without the dwarves, without the ring. Which detail from the chapter most clearly signals this as a turning point in his development?

A) He feels relief that the spider is dead and immediately rejoins the dwarves.
B) He gives his short sword a name — "Sting" — after the kill, claiming an identity through the act.
C) He discovers that he is physically stronger than he realized.
D) Gandalf appears at the critical moment to guide him through the worst of it.

**Answer: B**

---

**Q6.** Consider this sentence: "The dwarves were bound in webs by the spiders and left hanging from the trees." This is in passive voice. If you rewrote it as "The spiders bound the dwarves in webs and left them hanging from the trees," what changes?

A) The rewritten version is objectively better — passive voice is always wrong.
B) The rewritten version puts the spiders in the active role; the original puts the dwarves' helplessness at the center. The better choice depends on what the writer wants the reader to feel.
C) Nothing meaningful changes — the two sentences say exactly the same thing.
D) The passive version hides who the agent is, so the active version is always clearer.

**Answer: B**

---

**Q7.** The Wood-elf king Thranduil imprisons the dwarves but cannot find Bilbo, who uses the ring to remain invisible. Tolkien presents Thranduil as both elegant and somewhat petty — proud of his kingdom, unwilling to help without gain. What does this portrait suggest about the novel's treatment of authority?

A) All authority figures in the novel are villains who must be defeated.
B) Even sympathetic or legitimate authorities can be obstacles, and cleverness is sometimes the right response to institutional power.
C) Thranduil is morally equivalent to Smaug — both are obstacles to the quest for the same reasons.
D) The scene exists primarily to show that elves are untrustworthy, reversing the positive portrayal of Rivendell.

**Answer: B**

---

**Q8.** Bilbo's plan to free the dwarves involves hiding them in barrels and floating them down the river. It is physically uncomfortable, undignified, and not guaranteed to work. What does this plan reveal about Bilbo's developing character?

A) He is desperate and has chosen the only option available, which reflects his lack of strategic thinking.
B) He is willing to accept that heroism sometimes looks ridiculous — practical solutions matter more than elegant ones.
C) He has become reckless and is risking the dwarves' lives unnecessarily.
D) The barrel plan shows he has the same cunning as Gandalf, whom he is consciously imitating.

**Answer: B**

---

**Q9.** By Chapter 10, Bilbo has rescued the dwarves, navigated Mirkwood, and arrived at Lake-town with the company intact — largely through his own initiative. Yet Thorin still introduces himself as the leader and receives the credit. How does Tolkien use this gap between Bilbo's contribution and his recognition?

A) It is meant to show that Thorin is ungrateful and will eventually be punished for it.
B) It establishes that Bilbo's heroism is not yet about recognition or status — his identity as a hero is developing privately, before the world acknowledges it.
C) It suggests that Bilbo is still seen as inferior by the dwarves and will never earn their respect.
D) The gap exists only because Tolkien needed to preserve Thorin's role as the formal leader for plot purposes.

**Answer: B**

---

**Q10.** Consider these two sentences: (A) "Bilbo crept through the darkness toward the glow of Gollum's eyes." (B) "The glow of Gollum's eyes was crept toward through the darkness by Bilbo." Which sentence is better, and why?

A) Sentence B is better because passive voice sounds more formal and sophisticated in literary writing.
B) Sentence A is better because the active construction keeps Bilbo as the agent, which is where the emphasis belongs — he is acting, not being acted upon.
C) Both sentences are equally effective because they describe the same event.
D) Sentence B is better because it puts Gollum at the center, which is more suspenseful.

**Answer: B**
