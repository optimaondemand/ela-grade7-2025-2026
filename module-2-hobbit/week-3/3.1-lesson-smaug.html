7th Grade ELA — Module 2
The Hobbit — Week 3 — Lesson

Lesson 3.1 — Smaug

The dragon who listens — and why that makes him more dangerous than one who just attacks

Objectives
1

Analyze how Tolkien uses Bilbo’s riddling conversation with Smaug to reveal both characters — and why Bilbo’s verbal strategy is more sophisticated here than in any earlier scene.

2

Explain how Smaug’s vanity and love of riddles ultimately become his vulnerability — and what this says about the relationship between intelligence and pride.

Warm-Up 2 min
Imagine you had to speak with someone much more powerful than you — someone who could destroy you if you said the wrong thing — and you could not let them know who you were or where you came from. How would you talk? What would you reveal? What would you hide? This is exactly the problem Bilbo faces in Chapter 12 when he descends into Smaug’s lair.

The interesting thing about Bilbo’s conversation with Smaug is not just that he survives it. It’s that he almost doesn’t — not because Smaug is violent, but because Smaug is intelligent and Bilbo makes one mistake.

Lesson

Smaug is the most intelligent villain in The Hobbit, and Tolkien is careful to show this through how the dragon speaks rather than through what he does. When Bilbo descends into the Lonely Mountain in Chapter 12, invisible and terrified, Smaug does not immediately try to kill him. He talks to him. He poses questions, listens to the answers, and parses them for hidden meaning. He is flattered by Bilbo’s riddling self-descriptions — “I am he that walks unseen... the clue-finder, the web-cutter, the stinging fly” — and delighted to play the guessing game. But Smaug is also doing something more subtle: he is trying to determine where Bilbo came from and who sent him, because that knowledge would tell him who his real enemy is. Bilbo’s riddle-names protect him for a while, but then Smaug asks about the ponies at Lake-town — a detail Bilbo never mentioned. Smaug already knows. The dragon has been three moves ahead the whole time. What saves Bilbo is not cleverness alone; it is knowing when to leave. He escapes before Smaug can act on what he has inferred. The lesson Tolkien embeds in this scene — that intelligence and pride together create a specific kind of blindness — is illustrated when Smaug’s own vanity leads him to show Bilbo his one weak spot: the bare patch on his chest. He cannot resist proving how invulnerable he is. That decision kills him, though not immediately, and not by Bilbo’s hand. The connection between Smaug’s pride and his death runs through Bard’s arrow — and through the thrush that carries the information. Tolkien builds a causal chain across four chapters that starts with Smaug’s inability to stop showing off.

Historical & Literary Context

Tolkien drew Smaug from the dragon of the Old English poem Beowulf and from the Norse figure Fafnir in the Volsunga saga. Both of those dragons are vast, ancient, and associated with hoarded gold that corrupts those who seek it. But Tolkien’s dragon is more sophisticated than his sources: he speaks, reasons, flatters, and infers. The conversation in Chapter 12 has no parallel in Beowulf, where the dragon simply attacks. Tolkien added the riddling dialogue to give Bilbo a scene that tested him intellectually rather than physically — and to establish that the real threat of a dragon is not fire but cunning.

Vocabulary

DRAGON-SICKNESS — noun (Tolkien’s coinage)

The corrupting influence of a dragon’s hoard — an obsessive greed for gold that takes hold of those who come too close to it. Thorin is eventually afflicted by dragon-sickness in the Lonely Mountain, and Tolkien uses the term to connect individual greed to the broader evil that Smaug represents. The sickness is not supernatural in a simple sense; it is the magnification of a flaw that was already there.

Copia practice:

Original: “Thorin’s dragon-sickness makes him increasingly unable to see past the treasure.”

HUBRIS (HYOO-bris) — noun

Excessive pride or arrogance that leads a character to overestimate themselves and underestimate others — often resulting in their downfall. A central concept in Greek tragedy; Tolkien applies it to Smaug. The dragon’s decision to show Bilbo his bare patch is an act of hubris: he is so confident in his own invulnerability that he cannot resist demonstrating it, and this demonstration provides the intelligence that eventually kills him.

Copia practice:

Original: “Smaug’s hubris — his need to prove his own invulnerability — is ultimately what destroys him.”

Comprehension Check
1

Bilbo introduces himself to Smaug using a series of kenning-like riddle-names: “the web-cutter,” “the clue-finder,” “Barrel-rider.” Why does Bilbo choose this strategy rather than simply refusing to answer? What does it reveal about his understanding of Smaug?

▶ Sample answer
Bilbo has understood something important about Smaug: the dragon enjoys the game. By offering riddles instead of a name, Bilbo engages Smaug’s intelligence and vanity rather than his aggression. Silence would be suspicious; a direct name would give Smaug information he could use. The riddle-names satisfy Smaug’s appetite for cleverness while protecting Bilbo’s identity — a sophisticated rhetorical strategy that Bilbo could not have managed at the start of the novel.
2

Smaug dies not from Bilbo’s plan but from Bard’s arrow — information Bilbo gathered and a thrush overheard and passed on. Is Bilbo responsible for Smaug’s death? Does responsibility for an outcome require direct action?
