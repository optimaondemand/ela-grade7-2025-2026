7th Grade ELA — Module 2
The Hobbit — Week 3 — Grammar Lesson

Lesson 3.2 — Introductory Phrases and Clauses

What you put before the main clause shapes how the reader arrives at it

Objectives
1

Identify and correctly punctuate three types of introductory elements: prepositional phrases, participial phrases, and adverbial clauses.

2

Use introductory elements deliberately to control how a sentence begins and what relationship the opening sets up with the main clause.

Warm-Up 1 min
Compare these two versions: (A) “Bilbo crept down the tunnel.” (B) “Moving as quietly as a shadow, Bilbo crept down the tunnel.” Both sentences end the same way. What does the introductory phrase in B add that A cannot?

The introductory phrase in B establishes how the action is happening before the action is named. It creates a cinematic effect: the reader sees the manner of movement, then the movement itself. The information could have gone at the end (“Bilbo crept down the tunnel, moving as quietly as a shadow”) — but leading with it changes the emphasis. What you put first is what the reader arrives at the sentence expecting.

Lesson

An introductory element is any word, phrase, or clause that comes before the main clause of a sentence. It always takes a comma after it. Three types:

1. Prepositional phrase

Begins with a preposition (in, on, at, through, beneath, after, beside, under). Sets up location, time, or circumstance: “In the deepest part of the mountain, Bilbo heard the slow breathing of the dragon.” Comma after the phrase.

Short phrases: the comma is optional if the phrase is only 2–3 words (“At dawn Bilbo woke.”) but is never wrong.

2. Participial phrase

Begins with a present participle (-ing verb) or past participle (-ed verb). Describes the subject of the main clause — the subject must be doing or having had done the participle action: “Holding his breath, Bilbo pressed against the tunnel wall.” Danger: the participial phrase must modify the subject of the main clause. “Holding his breath, the tunnel felt very narrow.” is a dangling modifier — the tunnel is not holding its breath. Comma required after the phrase.

3. Adverbial clause (subordinating conjunction)

Begins with a subordinating conjunction: although, because, when, while, after, before, if, since, unless, until. Creates a relationship (time, cause, condition) before the main clause: “Although Smaug was flattered by the riddles, he was not fooled for long.” Comma after the clause. Note: if the adverbial clause comes after the main clause, no comma: “Smaug was not fooled for long although he was flattered by the riddles.”

Practice
1

Rewrite this sentence so it begins with an introductory participial phrase: “Bilbo slipped on the ring and disappeared before Smaug could see him.”

▶ Sample answer
“Slipping the ring onto his finger, Bilbo disappeared before Smaug could see him.” The participial phrase puts the ring action first, which shifts the emphasis to the mechanism of his escape rather than the escape itself.
2

Identify and correct the dangling modifier: “Creeping through the dark tunnel, the smell of dragon-smoke grew stronger.”

▶ Answer
Error: the smell is not creeping. Fix: “Creeping through the dark tunnel, Bilbo noticed that the smell of dragon-smoke grew stronger.” The subject of the main clause must be the one doing the creeping.
3

Write three sentences about the Smaug encounter — one beginning with a prepositional phrase, one with a participial phrase, and one with an adverbial clause. Label each.
