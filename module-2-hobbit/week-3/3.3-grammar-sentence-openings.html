The Hobbit — Week 3 — Grammar Lesson

Lesson 3.3 — Varying Sentence Openings

The first word of every sentence tells the reader something — vary it or lose the reader

Objectives
1

Diagnose monotonous sentence openings in a passage and identify what pattern is causing the problem.

2

Revise a monotonous passage by varying sentence openings using the techniques from this week’s grammar lessons.

Warm-Up 1 min
Read this passage aloud: “Bilbo walked into the tunnel. Bilbo heard the breathing of the dragon. Bilbo felt the heat on his face. Bilbo stopped. Bilbo put on the ring. Bilbo crept forward.” What is wrong with it? What does it feel like to read?

Every sentence starts with “Bilbo” + verb. The structure is grammatically correct, but reading it feels like a list. The monotony of the opening hides the tension that the content should be building. Varying sentence openings is not decoration — it is a precision tool for controlling rhythm and emphasis.

Lesson

The most common writing problem at the sentence level is not bad grammar but monotonous structure: every sentence starts with the subject. The subject-first sentence is the default, and the default is fine — in moderation. When every sentence in a paragraph starts the same way, the reader’s attention slips. A varied opening pattern keeps the reader alert. Five moves that change a sentence opening without changing the content:

Prepositional phrase
“In the silence of the tunnel, Bilbo heard the dragon breathe.”
Participial phrase
“Holding his breath, Bilbo crept forward.”
Adverbial clause
“Although he could not see anything, Bilbo could feel the heat.”
Adverb
“Slowly, he put one foot in front of the other.”
Inverted syntax
“Deeper and deeper into the mountain he went.” (object/complement before subject)

The goal is not to use every technique in every paragraph — that would be its own kind of monotony. The goal is to read your own writing and notice when the openings have become mechanical, then change two or three of them deliberately. Tolkien’s prose naturally varies this way; his chapters use all five patterns within single pages.

Practice — Revision Exercise

Rewrite the following passage (based on Chapter 12) so that no two consecutive sentences begin the same way. You may add words, combine sentences, or split sentences, but the content must remain the same. Label each opening type you use.

Original: “Bilbo descended the tunnel. Bilbo could feel the air growing warmer. Bilbo could see a faint reddish glow ahead. Bilbo stopped and listened. Bilbo heard nothing except the deep, slow breathing of a vast creature in sleep. Bilbo stepped forward again. Bilbo rounded a corner and the glow became a furnace light.”
▶ Sample revision
Descending the tunnel, Bilbo felt the air growing warmer with every step. [participial] Ahead, a faint reddish glow had begun to appear. [adverb] He stopped and listened. [subject-first] Nothing reached his ears except the deep, slow breathing of a vast creature in sleep. [inverted] Although his legs wanted to turn back, he stepped forward again. [adverbial] Around the next corner, the glow became a furnace light. [prepositional] — Note: no two consecutive sentences begin the same way; the content is unchanged.
