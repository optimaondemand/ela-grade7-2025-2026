# Week 3 Quiz — The Hobbit, Chapters 11–14

**Q1.** When Bilbo descends into the Lonely Mountain alone in Chapter 12, he uses riddle-names to introduce himself to Smaug: "the web-cutter," "the clue-finder," "Barrel-rider." Why does this strategy work better than silence or a direct answer?

A) Smaug cannot understand riddles, so the names distract him long enough for Bilbo to escape.
B) Riddle-names satisfy Smaug's love of cleverness while withholding the information that could lead Smaug to the dwarves' location and allies.
C) Bilbo has been told by Gandalf to use this specific technique before entering the mountain.
D) The riddle-names make Smaug believe Bilbo is a much more powerful being than a hobbit.

**Answer: B**

---

**Q2.** Smaug boasts of his invulnerability by showing Bilbo his armored belly — and inadvertently reveals the bare patch on his left breast. What does this moment illustrate about the relationship between intelligence and pride?

A) Even the most intelligent beings make mistakes under pressure, and Smaug is no exception.
B) Smaug's hubris — his need to prove how invulnerable he is — creates the exact weakness his pride claims he doesn't have.
C) Intelligence always eventually defeats itself; Smaug is simply too clever for his own good.
D) The bare patch is not a real weakness — Tolkien includes it only to give Bard a plot device.

**Answer: B**

---

**Q3.** When Bilbo describes himself as "Barrel-rider," Smaug immediately connects this to Lake-town and the barrel-riders who float goods down the river. What does Smaug's inference reveal about his intelligence?

A) Smaug has spies in Lake-town who have already told him about Bilbo.
B) Even through Bilbo's riddle-names, Smaug is doing active detective work — each name is data he is using to triangulate Bilbo's origins.
C) Smaug already knew Bilbo was coming and is simply playing a game.
D) The inference shows Smaug has magical powers of knowledge, not ordinary intelligence.

**Answer: B**

---

**Q4.** Consider this sentence: "Creeping down the tunnel, the smell of smoke grew stronger." What is wrong with it, and how would you fix it?

A) Nothing is wrong — the participial phrase correctly modifies the tunnel.
B) It is a dangling modifier: the smell is not creeping. Fix: "Creeping down the tunnel, Bilbo noticed the smell of smoke growing stronger."
C) The comma after "tunnel" should be a semicolon.
D) The phrase should come after the main clause: "The smell of smoke grew stronger, creeping down the tunnel."

**Answer: B**

---

**Q5.** Tolkien describes Smaug's death — shot by Bard's arrow through the bare patch — as the result of a chain of events that began with the thrush overhearing Bilbo's report to the dwarves. What does this causal chain suggest about how Tolkien defines heroic action?

A) True heroism always requires physical combat — Bilbo falls short because he does not kill Smaug himself.
B) Heroic action can work indirectly: gathering and sharing information that eventually leads to a larger outcome is a legitimate form of contribution.
C) The chain of events proves that luck, not character, determines outcomes in the novel.
D) Tolkien uses the chain to show that Bard, not Bilbo, is the true hero of this section of the novel.

**Answer: B**

---

**Q6.** Which of the following sentences uses an introductory adverbial clause correctly?

A) "Although the dragon was asleep. Bilbo crept forward."
B) "Although the dragon was asleep, Bilbo crept forward."
C) "Although, the dragon was asleep, Bilbo crept forward."
D) "Although the dragon was asleep Bilbo crept forward."

**Answer: B**

---

**Q7.** In Chapter 13, after Smaug leaves the mountain to attack Lake-town, Bilbo and the dwarves explore the empty halls of Erebor. Bilbo finds the Arkenstone — the greatest treasure of the dwarven kingdom — and pockets it without telling Thorin. Why does this moment matter for the rest of the novel?

A) The Arkenstone gives Bilbo magical powers that he will use in the Battle of the Five Armies.
B) Bilbo is beginning to act on his own judgment rather than the group's interests — a sign of both his growth and the beginning of a moral tension the novel will explore.
C) Bilbo hides the Arkenstone out of greed, which proves dragon-sickness has already infected him.
D) The moment is primarily significant because it sets up a plot twist that has no deeper thematic meaning.

**Answer: B**

---

**Q8.** Thorin's behavior in Chapters 12–14 begins to change: he becomes suspicious, possessive, and increasingly unwilling to share the treasure or honor the agreement with the Lake-men. Tolkien names this condition "dragon-sickness." What does this term suggest about the nature of greed in the novel?

A) Greed is a supernatural curse that Smaug placed on the treasure before he died.
B) Greed is presented as a kind of contagion — a character weakness that the treasure magnifies rather than creates, and that spreads to those who come too close to the hoard.
C) Dragon-sickness affects only dwarves because of their racial love of gold; hobbits are immune.
D) Tolkien uses the term to excuse Thorin's behavior — it is not really his fault.

**Answer: B**

---

**Q9.** Rewrite this sentence with a participial phrase opening: "Bilbo looked at the hoard and felt a strange pull toward the Arkenstone." Which of the following rewrites is correct?

A) "The Arkenstone pulling at him, Bilbo looked at the hoard and felt a strange pull."
B) "Looking at the hoard, Bilbo felt a strange pull toward the Arkenstone."
C) "Looking at the hoard, the Arkenstone pulled strangely at Bilbo."
D) "Bilbo, looking at the hoard, and felt a strange pull toward the Arkenstone."

**Answer: B**

---

**Q10.** At the end of Chapter 14, Smaug is dead, the dwarves have reclaimed the Lonely Mountain, and Bilbo is holding the Arkenstone in secret. The quest's stated goal has been achieved — the dragon is dead and the treasure is available. Why does Tolkien not end the novel here?

A) The quest was never really about the dragon — it was about Thorin reclaiming the throne, which requires a battle.
B) The novel's real question — whether the quest produces wisdom or corruption, and whether Bilbo's growth holds under moral pressure — has not yet been tested.
C) Tolkien needed to introduce the Battle of the Five Armies as an action sequence to satisfy readers expecting a traditional adventure ending.
D) The novel cannot end until Gandalf returns to resolve the conflict, and Gandalf is still absent.

**Answer: B**
