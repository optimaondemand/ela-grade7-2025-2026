7th Grade ELA — Module 2
The Hobbit — Week 4 — Lesson

Lesson 4.1 — The Arkenstone Decision

Bilbo’s highest moment — and what it costs him

Objectives
1

Analyze Bilbo’s decision to give the Arkenstone to Bard and Thranduil as the moral climax of his character development — and distinguish moral courage from physical courage.

2

Evaluate whether Bilbo’s betrayal of Thorin is justified — and what Tolkien’s presentation of Thorin’s reaction suggests about the novel’s moral framework.

Warm-Up 2 min
Imagine you discovered that a friend — someone you had risked your life for — was about to do something that would hurt a lot of other people because of greed and pride. You had the power to stop it, but only by betraying them. What would you do? What would it cost you either way?

This is exactly Bilbo’s dilemma in Chapter 15. He has crossed a mountain with Thorin, survived dragons and spiders and goblins for Thorin, and grown genuinely fond of him. What he does in the night is not a small thing — and Tolkien presents it as Bilbo’s most courageous act.

Lesson

Bilbo’s decision to bring the Arkenstone to Bard and Thranduil in the middle of the night — against Thorin’s explicit wishes, at risk of being considered a traitor — is the moral peak of his journey. Everything before this was survival: trolls, goblins, Gollum, spiders, a dragon. Some of it required courage; all of it required Bilbo to keep himself alive. What he does in Chapter 15 requires something harder: he risks the friendships he has built, his reputation among the dwarves, and his fourteenth share of the treasure, because he believes the war that Thorin is drifting toward is wrong. The Arkenstone is not just a bargaining chip; it is Thorin’s most prized possession, the symbol of dwarven kingship. Bilbo has been carrying it secretly for days, knowing he should give it to Thorin, and choosing not to because he also knows what Thorin will do with it — use it to deepen his grip on the treasure rather than to make peace. Gandalf appears after the transaction to say a few clarifying words to Bilbo: “Well done, Mr. Baggins.” This is the closest Tolkien comes to explicitly endorsing Bilbo’s choice — and it is notable that the endorsement comes from the wisest character in the novel. Thorin’s reaction is fury: he throws Bilbo out of the mountain and calls him a traitor. Tolkien does not soften this. Bilbo has, by Thorin’s measure, broken faith with the company. The novel’s question is whether Bilbo’s measure of the situation — that a war over gold is wrong, and that averting it is worth the cost — is the right one. Thorin’s deathbed reconciliation, where he admits he had been wrong and that Bilbo was right, is Tolkien’s answer. But that answer is not cheap: Thorin has to die to get there.

Historical & Literary Context

The Battle of the Five Armies — dwarves, men, elves, eagles, and wargs/goblins — is Tolkien’s most explicit engagement with the theme that drives much of his fiction: war as both necessary and terrible. Tolkien served in World War I at the Battle of the Somme, where over a million men were killed or wounded in four months. He lost most of his closest friends there. His fiction does not glorify war; it depicts war as the consequence of failures — of pride, greed, miscommunication, and the inability to find a peaceful resolution before it is too late. The Battle of the Five Armies erupts because Thorin refuses to negotiate. The timing of Bilbo’s attempt to prevent it — one hobbit trying to avert a war through a single act of courage — is the novel’s most pointed argument.

Vocabulary

MORAL COURAGE — noun phrase

The willingness to do what is right even when it is costly — especially when the cost is social rather than physical. Not the courage to face a dragon (physical courage), but the courage to disappoint, betray, or anger someone you care about because you believe it is the right thing to do. Bilbo’s giving away of the Arkenstone is an act of moral courage; his fight with the spider was physical courage. Both are real; Tolkien treats the first as harder.

Copia practice:

Original: “Giving away the Arkenstone required more moral courage from Bilbo than any of his encounters with monsters.”

RECONCILIATION — noun

The restoration of a relationship or understanding after a conflict or estrangement. Thorin’s deathbed words to Bilbo — admitting he was wrong about the treasure and that Bilbo acted rightly — are a reconciliation. Tolkien makes it come at the cost of Thorin’s life, which makes it both more painful and more meaningful: Thorin can only reach this understanding after losing everything he hoarded.

Copia practice:

Original: “Thorin’s deathbed reconciliation with Bilbo is the moral resolution the novel has been building toward.”

Comprehension Check
1

Bilbo’s contract with Thorin guaranteed him a fourteenth share of the treasure. By giving the Arkenstone to Bard, he essentially gave away his own share. Does this fact change how you read his decision? Is it easier or harder to admire him for it?

▶ Sample answer
It makes the decision easier to admire precisely because it has a real cost. Bilbo is not acting from a position of safety or surplus; he is giving up something he was contractually owed. This is the difference between moral courage and simply doing what’s convenient — Bilbo’s choice has weight because it costs him personally. That cost is part of what Tolkien is pointing to when Gandalf says “Well done.”
2

Thorin calls Bilbo a traitor. Gandalf implicitly calls him a hero. Can both be right? What does it mean for a single act to be both a betrayal and the right thing to do?
