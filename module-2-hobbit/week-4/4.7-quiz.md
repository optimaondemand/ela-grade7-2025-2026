**Q1.** Bilbo secretly gives the Arkenstone to Bard and Thranduil as a bargaining chip against Thorin. Gandalf, who appears afterward, says "Well done, Mr. Baggins." What does Gandalf's approval reveal about how Tolkien wants the reader to judge this act?

A) Gandalf's approval proves Bilbo made the tactically correct decision, regardless of its ethics.
B) Because Gandalf is established as the wisest character in the novel, his approval signals that Bilbo's decision represents genuine moral courage, not just a clever move.
C) Gandalf is being ironic — he actually disapproves but does not want to discourage Bilbo.
D) Gandalf's approval is irrelevant because Thorin, as the leader, is the one whose judgment matters.

**Answer: B**

---

**Q2.** Thorin calls Bilbo a traitor and throws him out of the mountain when he learns what Bilbo has done. Yet Tolkien does not present Thorin's reaction as simply "wrong." What is Tolkien doing by giving Thorin's anger some legitimacy?

A) He is showing that Thorin is the true hero of the novel and Bilbo overstepped.
B) He is presenting a genuine moral complexity: Bilbo's act is both a betrayal by the standards of loyalty and contract, and the right thing by a higher moral standard. Both can be true.
C) He is setting up Thorin's redemption arc by showing how irrational dragon-sickness makes him.
D) He is suggesting that hobbits and dwarves have fundamentally incompatible values.

**Answer: B**

---

**Q3.** Identify the grammatical error in this sentence and correct it: "Bilbo was not only brave but also showed great wisdom in giving up the stone."

A) The sentence is grammatically correct as written.
B) Faulty parallelism: "not only brave" (adjective) must match "but also" + adjective. Fix: "Bilbo was not only brave but also wise."
C) The sentence needs a semicolon before "but also."
D) "Showed great wisdom" should be changed to "wisely" to correct the sentence.

**Answer: B**

---

**Q4.** The Battle of the Five Armies erupts when the goblins and wargs attack just as the men, elves, and dwarves are about to fight each other. What is Tolkien suggesting by having the threat of a meaningless civil war interrupted by a real enemy?

A) War between races is never truly meaningless — all battles serve a purpose in the larger design.
B) The quarrel over gold is petty; the arrival of a genuine external threat reveals how much energy was being wasted on pride and greed that could have been channeled toward real defense.
C) Tolkien is showing that the goblins and wargs are the ultimate villains of the novel, worse than any of the greed-driven conflicts.
D) The interruption is a plot device to avoid having the author decide who would have won the civil conflict.

**Answer: B**

---

**Q5.** During the battle itself, Bilbo is knocked unconscious by a stone thrown at the beginning of the fighting. He misses nearly all of it and wakes after the eagles and Beorn have turned the tide. What does Bilbo's position — unconscious, at the margin — suggest about his role in the novel's climax?

A) Bilbo's unconsciousness is Tolkien's way of punishing him for giving away the Arkenstone.
B) The hero of this story is not primarily a warrior; Bilbo's greatest contribution came before the battle (the Arkenstone) and his role in the fighting was never the point of his transformation.
C) Bilbo fails as a hero because he is absent when his companions need him most.
D) The scene is realistic — hobbits are physically weaker than men and dwarves, so Bilbo's injury is expected.

**Answer: B**

---

**Q6.** Which of the following sentences uses parallel structure correctly?

A) "Thorin wanted gold, controlling the mountain, and to defeat his enemies."
B) "Thorin wanted gold, control of the mountain, and victory over his enemies."
C) "Thorin wanted gold, to control the mountain, and defeating his enemies."
D) "Thorin wanted gold and also to control the mountain and his enemies defeated."

**Answer: B**

---

**Q7.** On his deathbed, Thorin tells Bilbo: "If more of us valued food and cheer and song above hoarded gold, it would be a merrier world." What does this deathbed statement reveal about Thorin's understanding of what the quest ultimately cost?

A) Thorin is delirious from his wounds and should not be taken at face value.
B) Thorin acknowledges that his dragon-sickness corrupted his values and that Bilbo's Shire-like priorities — comfort, fellowship, ordinary pleasures — were wiser than the obsession with wealth.
C) Thorin is specifically praising hobbits and saying that all dwarves are naturally flawed.
D) The statement is Tolkien's way of ending the novel on a light note after heavy events.

**Answer: B**

---

**Q8.** Bilbo refuses Thorin's offered share of the treasure, taking only a small portion he can carry home. Gandalf notes that Bilbo is a wiser man for it. How does this choice connect to the novel's theme of dragon-sickness?

A) Bilbo is wise to refuse because transporting large amounts of gold would be physically dangerous.
B) By taking only what he can use, Bilbo demonstrates that he is immune to the corrupting influence of accumulated wealth — the trait that destroyed Smaug and nearly destroyed Thorin.
C) Bilbo's refusal shows he is still primarily a Baggins — comfort-seeking and unambitious.
D) Taking a small portion rather than none shows Bilbo has been partially infected by dragon-sickness.

**Answer: B**

---

**Q9.** Choose the most precise subordinating conjunction to join these sentences: "Bilbo gave back the mithril coat Thorin gave him." "He felt he had been given far more than his share already."

A) Although Bilbo gave back the mithril coat, he felt he had been given far more than his share already.
B) Because Bilbo felt he had been given far more than his share already, he considered giving back the mithril coat. (Note: he keeps it — revise the example)
C) Bilbo kept the mithril coat since Thorin had given it before the conflict.
D) Unless Bilbo had kept the mithril coat, he would have had nothing from the adventure.

**Answer: C**

**Q10.** The novel ends with Bilbo back in Bag End — but not quite the same Bag End. He is considered "queer" by his neighbors, his reputation is somewhat tarnished, and he is clearly not entirely comfortable with the ordinary life he once prized. What does this ending suggest about what the journey cost him, and whether it was worth it?

A) The ending suggests the journey was a mistake — Bilbo lost social standing and contentment for no permanent gain.
B) The ending suggests that transformation is irreversible: Bilbo cannot go back to who he was, and the novel treats this as fitting rather than tragic — a person who has seen more cannot un-see it.
C) Tolkien is setting up a sequel by showing Bilbo's dissatisfaction with the Shire.
D) The neighbors' judgment of Bilbo reflects Tolkien's view that adventure is socially disruptive and should be avoided.

**Answer: B**
