7th Grade ELA — Module 2
The Hobbit — Week 5 — Lesson

Lesson 5.1 — The Return

What Bilbo brings back — and what the Shire cannot hold anymore

Objectives
1

Analyze the ending of The Hobbit as a completed hero’s journey — and identify what Tolkien’s ending says about what transformation costs.

2

Compare Bilbo’s return to the Shire with Odysseus’s return to Ithaca, identifying what is similar in the structure of return and what is fundamentally different in what each hero brings back.

Warm-Up 2 min
If you went on an extraordinary adventure — survived real danger, made difficult choices, grew in ways you could not have anticipated — and then came home, do you think your home would feel the same? Do you think you would feel the same in it? What would be different, and who would notice?

Bilbo comes home to find his neighbors have given him up for dead and are auctioning off his belongings. He wins them back at auction. He drinks his tea. He writes his memoirs. On the surface, very little has changed. Tolkien’s ending is deliberately quiet — and that quietness is the point.

Lesson

The ending of The Hobbit is deliberately anti-climactic. Bilbo comes home, recovers his property, and settles back into the Shire — but with a crucial difference: the neighbors consider him “queer,” respectable hobbits no longer quite approve of him, and he is quietly aware that the world is larger than the Shire in a way it was not before. Tolkien does not give Bilbo a heroic homecoming. There is no public recognition, no celebration, no official acknowledgment that he did anything remarkable. What Bilbo brings back is entirely internal: experience, judgment, a broader view, and the scar of Thorin’s death. This is what makes the comparison to Odysseus so revealing. Odysseus’s return is a military reconquest: he slaughters the suitors, reclaims Penelope, re-establishes himself as king. His return is measured in external events. Bilbo’s return is measured in internal ones: he writes poetry, he values good food again, he smokes his pipe by the fire. But he is also slightly out of place. “Not the Bilbo Baggins you knew before,” says Balin on a later visit. Tolkien’s answer to the module’s opening question — whether heroism is something you are or something you become — turns out to be both, and neither fully. Bilbo is not more heroic than ordinary life in the Shire allows for. But he has had an experience of himself that could not have happened in the Shire, and he carries it forward in everything he does. The transformation is real. It is just invisible to his neighbors — which may be exactly where Tolkien thinks the most important transformations live.

Vocabulary

TRANSFORMATION — noun

A fundamental change in character, outlook, or capacity — not just a change of circumstances. Bilbo’s transformation is not that he went on an adventure; it is that the adventure changed what he was capable of seeing, doing, and choosing. A character who returns from an adventure unchanged has not experienced transformation but merely travel.

Copia practice:

Original: “Bilbo’s transformation is visible not in what the Shire sees but in what he knows about himself.”

ANTI-CLIMAX — noun

A conclusion that deliberately falls short of the emotional or dramatic peak the reader might expect — used by skilled writers to create a specific effect rather than a feeling of disappointment. Tolkien’s ending is an intentional anti-climax: after trolls, dragons, battles, and moral crises, Bilbo drinks his tea. The smallness of the ending is the point. Compare this to the end of The Odyssey, which ends with Odysseus firmly back in power — a proper climax.

Copia practice:

Original: “The novel’s quiet, domestic ending is a deliberate anti-climax that makes a point about where heroism actually lives.”

Comprehension Check
1

Tolkien closes the novel with Bilbo smoking his pipe, drinking his tea, and writing his memoirs. Why does this ending feel right for this hero, even though it would feel wrong for Odysseus or Beowulf?

▶ Sample answer
Bilbo’s heroism was always private rather than public — he never fought a named enemy in single combat, never claimed a throne, never sought recognition. His transformation happened inside him, not in the view of a hall or a court. The quiet ending matches the quiet nature of his heroism: the right ending for a character whose arc was about discovering what he was capable of, not about displaying it to an audience. Odysseus’s heroism was always public and martial; a quiet ending for him would feel like a defeat. For Bilbo, it feels like completion.
2

The module began with the question: is heroism something you are or something you become? Based on the full novel, what is Tolkien’s answer? Do you agree?
