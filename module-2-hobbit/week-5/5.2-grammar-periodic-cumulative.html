7th Grade ELA — Module 2
The Hobbit — Week 5 — Grammar Lesson

Lesson 5.2 — Periodic and Cumulative Sentences in Tolkien

How Tolkien builds tension and richness through sentence structure — and how you can too

Objectives
1

Distinguish periodic sentences (main clause withheld until the end) from cumulative sentences (main clause stated first, then elaborated) and identify the effect each creates.

2

Find and analyze examples of both types in Tolkien’s prose, and write one original sentence of each type.

Warm-Up 1 min
Read these two sentences: (A) “Bilbo sat down heavily in his chair, exhausted, dusty, without his pocket-handkerchief, and profoundly relieved to be home.” (B) “Exhausted, dusty, without his pocket-handkerchief, and profoundly relieved to be home, Bilbo sat down heavily in his chair.” Which sentence keeps you waiting longer? Which one gives you the picture before the event?

Sentence A is cumulative: the main clause comes first and the modifiers pile up after. Sentence B is periodic: the modifiers come first and the main clause arrives last, after the reader has been made to wait. Both are from the same raw material; the difference is entirely in the order. The order is the effect.

Lesson

A cumulative sentence (also called a loose sentence) states its main clause first and then adds modifying phrases and clauses afterward. The reader gets the main point immediately and the detail accumulates: “Bilbo walked home slowly, richer than he had left, older in ways he could not quite name, and more aware of what the world held beyond the Shire.” The main clause is “Bilbo walked home slowly”; everything after the comma adds to it.

A periodic sentence withholds the main clause until the end, making the reader wait. The clauses and phrases build toward the main verb: “After a journey of many months, through mountains and forests and under the earth, having survived trolls and goblins and a dragon, Bilbo Baggins finally walked through his own gate.” The main clause — “Bilbo Baggins finally walked through his own gate” — is the last thing the reader reaches.

Tolkien uses both throughout The Hobbit. His action sentences are often cumulative — the event arrives first and the detail piles up fast. His descriptive and atmospheric sentences are often periodic — the setting or the character’s state arrives after the reader has been placed inside the mood.

Examples from The Hobbit

“In a hole in the ground there lived a hobbit.” — Cumulative

The main clause arrives immediately (“there lived a hobbit”); the opening phrase sets the location. Simple, direct, immediate.

“Far over the misty mountains cold, / To dungeons deep and caverns old, / We must away ere break of day, / To seek the pale enchanted gold.” — Periodic (verse)

The goal (“To seek the pale enchanted gold”) arrives last, after the destination, the time constraint, and the journey have been established. The waiting creates the feeling of inevitability.

Practice
1

Identify whether this sentence is periodic or cumulative, and explain what effect the structure creates: “Bilbo reached into his pocket, felt the cold smooth metal, and paused — a ring.”

▶ Answer
Cumulative (with a dash for final emphasis): the action unrolls in real time — reach, feel, pause — and then the noun “a ring” arrives after the dash with extra weight. The dash creates a mini-periodic effect at the end of an otherwise cumulative structure.
2

Write one cumulative sentence and one periodic sentence about Bilbo’s return home. Label each and explain in one phrase why you chose that structure for that content.
