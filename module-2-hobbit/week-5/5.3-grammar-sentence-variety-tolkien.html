The Hobbit — Week 5 — Grammar Lesson

Lesson 5.3 — Sentence Variety in Tolkien’s Prose

The rhythm Tolkien creates — and how to steal it

Objectives
1

Identify how Tolkien varies sentence length to control pacing and emphasis, and name the structural tools he uses.

2

Apply sentence variety techniques to revise a monotonous passage using the full grammar toolkit from Module 2.

Warm-Up 1 min
Read this passage and notice where your reading pace changes: “In the hole in the ground there lived a hobbit — not a nasty, dirty, wet hole, filled with the ends of worms and an oozy smell, nor yet a dry, bare, sandy hole with nothing in it to sit down on or to eat: it was a hobbit-hole, and that means comfort.” Now read this: “He ran. He ran fast. He ran without stopping.” What is different?

The Tolkien passage varies wildly — long and elaborate, with a short punchy ending. The “He ran” passage is monotonous in the other direction: short, short, short. Neither extreme is the goal. The goal is variety, and the grammar toolkit you’ve built this module gives you the tools to achieve it.

Lesson

Tolkien’s prose rhythm follows a recognizable pattern: long elaborative sentences that build atmosphere or detail, followed by short punchy sentences that land the key event or judgment. The long sentences create the world; the short ones puncture it. Look at the opening of The Hobbit: two long sentences describing Bag End and its comfort, then: “and that means comfort.” The short sentence is the point; the long sentences are the preparation. This module has given you all the tools Tolkien uses to vary length:

Compound
Two independent clauses joined by FANBOYS — adds length and shows relationship
Semicolon
Two independent clauses joined without a conjunction — tightens the connection
Complex
Dependent + independent clause — adds condition, cause, or concession
Introductory phrase
Prepositional / participial / adverbial before the main clause — varies the opening and adds context
Periodic
Main clause withheld to the end — builds suspense
Cumulative
Main clause first, detail accumulates — unfolds in time
Dash
Creates emphasis or interruption within a sentence
Short
One main clause, no modifiers — arrives hard. Use sparingly.

The short sentence is the most powerful tool precisely because it is the rarest. A paragraph of all short sentences feels breathless and flat. A paragraph of all long sentences feels dense and airless. One short sentence after several long ones — that is the move.

Practice — Full Module Revision

Rewrite the following monotonous passage about Bilbo’s return, using at least five different sentence structures from the list above. Label each structure type in brackets after the sentence. The content must remain the same.

Original: “Bilbo came home. He was tired. He had lost his reputation. His neighbors thought he was odd. He did not mind. He sat by his fire. He was content. He was not the same as before.”
▶ Sample revision
After months of trolls, dragons, and a battle he had tried to prevent, Bilbo came home. [introductory-prepositional] He had lost his reputation among the respectable hobbits of the Shire; he had gained something they could not see. [semicolon] Although his neighbors thought him queer, Bilbo did not mind their opinion as he once would have. [complex-concession] He sat by his fire, smoking his pipe in the old comfortable way, knowing he was not quite the hobbit who had lived here before — [cumulative + dash] and knowing that was all right. [compound] He was content. [short]
