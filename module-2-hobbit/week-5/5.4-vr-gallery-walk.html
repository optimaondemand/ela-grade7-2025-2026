7th Grade ELA — Module 2
The Hobbit — Week 5 — VR Activity 1

VR 5.4 — Module Gallery Walk

Walking through classmates’ compositions — and responding to what you find

What You’re Doing

Over four weeks, your classmates have been building spatial compositions in persistent Sessions — Shire vs. Road, troll staging, riddle chamber, Smaug’s lair, the Arkenstone threshold, and the Battle of Five Armies map. In this session you will walk through at least two classmates’ compositions from different weeks, read their sticky notes, and respond on a Reply Whiteboard IFX near each composition. Your response must engage with the interpretive claim in the sticky notes — not just describe what you see, but respond to the argument being made.

What You’ll Need
  • Device: VR headset (recommended) or desktop (acceptable)
  • Sessions to visit: ELA7-M2-W1, ELA7-M2-W2, ELA7-M2-W3, or ELA7-M2-W4 (visit at least two different Sessions)
  • Note: These are persistent Sessions — your classmates’ compositions are still there
What to Do, In Order
1

Visit Two Different Compositions

Join two of the previous weeks’ Sessions and find compositions that belong to different classmates. Read their sticky notes and spatial arrangements carefully. Look for: What interpretive claim are they making through their IFX arrangement? What does their sticky note argue? Do you agree with their reading of the scene?

2

Place a Reply Whiteboard IFX Near Each Composition

For each composition you visit, place a Whiteboard IFX nearby (do not disturb the original composition). Title it with your name: [YourName] Reply. On this board, write your response. Your response must include three elements:

1. What the composition argues: State the interpretive claim you see in the staging and sticky note (one sentence).
2. What you agree with or find compelling: Engage with the strongest part of the argument (one or two sentences).
3. Where you see it differently or what the composition is missing: Offer a genuine point of disagreement or a gap — not “good job,” but an actual interpretive pushback (one or two sentences).
3

Take a Snapshot of Each Reply

Take a snapshot showing your reply board (legible) and the composition you are responding to in the same frame. Take one snapshot per composition visited.

What to Submit

Upload both snapshots to this Canvas assignment — one per composition visited. Each must show your legible reply whiteboard and the composition it responds to.

File format: PNG or JPG snapshots (2 files)
Naming: LastName_VR5-4_Reply1 and LastName_VR5-4_Reply2
Time

Approximately 20–25 minutes in-headset: ~10 min visiting and reading each composition, ~10 min writing two reply boards, ~5 min snapshots.

Points

10 points — completion
Two compositions visited (different classmates, different weeks), two reply boards with all three required elements, two legible snapshots submitted.

Facilitator Note

The most common failure mode is generic responses: “Good job, I agree.” The third element — genuine interpretive pushback — is the crucial check. If a student’s reply board does not name a specific disagreement or gap, it does not meet the standard. Prompt: What would the composition look like if you disagreed with it? What would a different student have arranged differently, and why? Walk the room to ensure students are actually reading sticky notes and engaging with claims rather than just looking at the IFX arrangements.
