# Closing Discussion: Is Bilbo a Hero?
## Canvas Discussion — Week 5
## 15 points | Threaded | Require initial post

---

### Setup for teacher (Canvas Discussion settings)

- **Title:** Closing Discussion: Is Bilbo a Hero?
- **Type:** Threaded
- **Require initial post before viewing replies:** Yes
- **Points:** 15
- **Grading type:** Points
- **Rubric:** 15 = meets all requirements with genuine argument; 8 = one required element missing or reply is generic; 0 = not submitted

---

### Discussion prompt (paste into Canvas message field)

**Post your response first, then reply to one classmate whose position differs from yours.**

The module opened with a question: is Bilbo Baggins a hero? You have now read the full novel, built VR compositions staging his key moments, recorded a spatial analysis comparing Chapter 1 Bilbo to Chapter 19 Bilbo, and walked through classmates' interpretations. Answer the question now — not with your first impression, but with the argument you have built.

**Your initial post must include:**

1. **A parallel-structure thesis** as your first or second sentence. Name at least two specific qualities Bilbo demonstrates and use parallel grammatical form. Do not begin with "I think" or "In my opinion."

   *Example of parallel structure:* "Bilbo Baggins is a hero not because he defeats monsters with strength but because he chooses rightly under pressure and gives up what he values most when it matters."

2. **Two specific scenes** — one from the first half of the novel (Chapters 1–10) and one from the second half (Chapters 11–19). For each scene, name the chapter, describe what Bilbo does or chooses, and connect it specifically to your claim about heroism.

3. **One concessive sentence** beginning with "Although..." that grants the strongest objection to your position, then answers it. Your concession must be genuinely strong — not a strawman.

**Reply to one classmate** whose position differs from yours (or who uses a different key scene than you do). Name their specific claim, identify which of their two scenes you find most compelling, and explain where your interpretation of that scene differs from theirs — or what you think it proves that they have not accounted for.

---

### Rubric detail

**15 points:**
- Parallel-structure thesis present and correctly formed (no "I think," parallel grammatical elements)
- Two scenes cited with chapter reference, specific action/choice described, and connected to claim
- Concessive sentence begins "Although," grants a real objection, and answers it
- Reply names classmate's specific claim and engages with their evidence (not just "I agree" or "good point")

**8 points:**
- Thesis present but parallel structure missing or weak
- Only one scene, or scenes described without connection to claim
- Concession missing or generic
- Reply does not engage with classmate's specific argument

**0 points:** Not submitted

---

### Teacher note

The comparison to Odysseus will surface naturally in many posts — students have been primed by the 5.5 VR session. Do not require it in the discussion post (the module project is where that comparison becomes the central argument), but if students bring it up, the most productive response is to push on *what* the comparison reveals: why does it matter that Odysseus's heroism is public and Bilbo's is private? What does each novel think counts as evidence of heroism?

Post 2–3 public replies to the discussion thread to model close engagement with student arguments: name the student's claim, push on one detail, ask one question. These public comments do the relational and pedagogical work that the rubric cannot.
