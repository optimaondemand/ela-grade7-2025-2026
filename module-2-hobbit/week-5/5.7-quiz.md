**Q1.** The module opened with the question: is heroism something you are, or something you become? Based on the full arc of *The Hobbit*, which answer does Tolkien most clearly endorse?

A) Heroism is something you are — Bilbo always had it, and the adventure simply revealed what was already there.
B) Heroism is something you become — Bilbo was not heroic in Chapter 1 and would never have become so without the specific experiences of the journey.
C) Tolkien's answer is both at once: the capacity was always there (the Tookish side), but it required the journey to become actual — heroism is latent potential that must be activated by experience.
D) Tolkien does not answer this question; the novel leaves it open for the reader to decide.

**Answer: C**

---

**Q2.** Tolkien's ending is deliberately anti-climactic: Bilbo comes home, recovers his possessions at auction, and settles back into domestic life, considered "queer" by his neighbors. What does this anti-climactic ending argue about the nature of heroism?

A) Bilbo's heroism was ultimately futile — nothing changed because of what he did.
B) The most important transformations are internal and invisible to the world around the hero; the quiet ending is the point, not a disappointment.
C) Tolkien runs out of dramatic material after the Battle of the Five Armies and wraps up hastily.
D) The ending suggests Bilbo should have refused to go on the adventure, since his reputation suffered.

**Answer: B**

**Q3.** Which of the following sentences is a periodic sentence (main clause withheld until the end)?

A) "Bilbo walked through the gate, tired but content, carrying a small bag of gold and a great deal of experience."
B) "He walked through the gate."
C) "Although he had lost his reputation among the respectable hobbits of the Shire, although he had given away his share of the treasure, and although Thorin had died calling him a traitor, Bilbo Baggins walked through his own gate at last."
D) "Bilbo had survived trolls, goblins, and a dragon; he had lost most of his silver spoons."

**Answer: C**

**Q4.** Compare Bilbo's return to the Shire with Odysseus's return to Ithaca. Which statement most accurately identifies the key difference in what each hero brings home?

A) Odysseus brings home physical wounds and material losses; Bilbo brings home material wealth.
B) Odysseus brings home a tested and externally restored identity (king, husband, warrior); Bilbo brings home an internal transformation that is largely invisible to those around him.
C) Both heroes bring home essentially the same thing: survival, wisdom, and a renewed appreciation for home.
D) Odysseus brings home failure since he lost all his men; Bilbo succeeds since the dragon is dead.

**Answer: B**

**Q5.** Over the five weeks of this module, you practiced eight grammar structures. Which of the following correctly identifies all the structures in this sentence: "After many months of danger and transformation, Bilbo came home — changed in ways his neighbors could not see, and quieter than he had been before."

A) Adverbial clause opening, main clause, dash, cumulative modifiers, compound
B) Prepositional phrase opening, main clause, dash for emphasis, cumulative participial phrase, compound clause
C) Participial phrase opening, periodic main clause, semicolon, parallel structure
D) Periodic structure, passive voice, adverbial clause, dash interruption

**Answer: B**

**Q6.** Tolkien uses the term *eucatastrophe* for a sudden joyful turn that averts disaster at the last moment. Which event from *The Hobbit* most fully fits this definition — not just as a rescue, but as an unexpected reversal at the moment of greatest darkness?

A) Gandalf's first appearance at Bilbo's door.
B) The arrival of the eagles to rescue the company from the treetops as the Wargs' fire closes in below them.
C) The thrush overhearing Bilbo's report about Smaug's bare patch and carrying the information to Bard.
D) Bilbo's decision to give the Arkenstone to Bard.

**Answer: B**

**Q7.** Bilbo's three most significant moments of growth in the novel are often identified as: the riddle game (Chapter 5), naming his sword "Sting" (Chapter 8), and giving away the Arkenstone (Chapter 15). Which of the following correctly ranks these from least to most significant, in terms of what each reveals about Bilbo's developing character?

A) Sting naming < Arkenstone < riddle game — cunning requires least growth, moral sacrifice requires most
B) Riddle game < Sting naming < Arkenstone — the riddle game is partly luck; naming claims identity; giving the Arkenstone costs the most and is chosen freely
C) Arkenstone < riddle game < Sting naming — Sting naming is the turning point because it is the first act of self-definition
D) All three are equally significant and cannot be ranked.

**Answer: B**

**Q8.** A cumulative sentence states its main clause first and then adds modifying phrases and clauses. Which of the following is an example of a correctly formed cumulative sentence about *The Hobbit*?

A) "Having survived trolls, goblins, Gollum, a dragon, and a battle, Bilbo finally reached the Shire."
B) "Bilbo reached the Shire at last, older and quieter than he had left, carrying a small bag of gold and a great many memories he would spend the rest of his life writing down."
C) "Although Bilbo had changed, the Shire had not."
D) "Not the Bilbo Baggins you knew before, the neighbors said of him."

**Answer: B**

**Q9.** The novel's grammar sequence this module included: compound sentences (Week 1), semicolons (Week 1), active/passive voice (Week 2), the em dash (Week 2), introductory phrases (Week 3), sentence openings (Week 3), parallel structure (Week 4), complex sentences (Week 4), periodic sentences (Week 5), and cumulative sentences (Week 5). Which of the following sentences correctly uses THREE different structures from this list?

A) "Bilbo was brave; his courage came from unexpected places — from wit, from luck, and from the willingness to act when no one else would." [semicolon, dash, parallel structure]
B) "Although Bilbo was afraid, but he acted anyway, not only running but also fighting." [complex + compound (faulty) + faulty not-only/but-also]

**Q10.** Gandalf says to Bilbo near the end of the novel: "You are a very fine person, Mr. Baggins, and I am very fond of you; but you are only quite a little fellow in a wide world after all." What does this statement capture about Tolkien's understanding of heroism and proportion?

A) Gandalf is diminishing Bilbo's achievements to keep him humble.
B) Genuine heroism does not require being great in the world's eyes; a very fine person of limited scope who acts rightly in their small sphere has achieved what the world needs from them.
C) Tolkien uses Gandalf's words to signal that Bilbo is not truly a hero — he was only a participant in events larger than himself.
D) The statement is Tolkien's way of saying that hobbits are inherently lesser beings than wizards or kings.

**Answer: B**
