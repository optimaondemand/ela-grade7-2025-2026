## Questions (5 questions — survey mode, no correct answer)

**Question 1** (Essay — the essential question revisited)
At the start of this module you answered: "What can a poem do that prose cannot?" Now that you have read poems from five centuries, staged images and structures in VR, and written your own original poem with a craft analysis, answer the question again. Has your position changed? What specific poem, formal element, or moment in your own writing most changed or confirmed your thinking?

*Write 3–5 sentences. Compare your answer here to what you wrote at the start of the module.*

**Question 2** (Likert scale — confidence rating)
How confident are you that your Module 3 Project poem has a clear central image and that your craft analysis genuinely explains how the poem works (rather than summarizing what it says)?
- 5 — Very confident: the image is specific and the analysis explains my actual formal choices
- 4 — Confident: I have a real image and the analysis covers most of what I intended
- 3 — Somewhat confident: I have a poem and an analysis but I'm not sure they connect well
- 2 — Not very confident: I'm not sure my craft analysis does more than describe the poem
- 1 — Not confident: I don't feel like I understood what a craft analysis was supposed to do

**Question 3** (Essay — what remains unclear)
Name one thing from this module that you still don't fully understand — a concept, a grammar structure, a poem, a formal term, or an idea from the readings. Be specific. "I don't understand poetry" is not useful; "I don't understand how Frost's poem can mean two opposite things at the same time" is.

*This is the most important question on this form. Your teacher reads every response.*

**Question 4** (Multiple Choice — grammar self-assessment)
Which grammar structure from Module 3 do you feel you have mastered well enough to use deliberately in your own writing without being reminded?
- Appositive (a noun phrase that renames a nearby noun)
- Relative clause (who, whom, whose, which, or that + clause)
- Colon (introducing a list, explanation, or quotation after a complete clause)
- Absolute phrase (a noun + participle modifying the whole sentence)
- Noun clause (a clause functioning as a subject, object, or complement)
- Anaphora (deliberate repetition of a word or phrase at the start of successive units)
- Line break analysis (explaining where a line ends and why it matters)
- I'm not confident in any of them yet

**Question 5** (Essay — virtue reflection)
The seven Optima virtues are: courage, courtesy, honesty, perseverance, self-government, service, and responsibility. Which one did this module ask the most from you, personally — not the speaker of any poem, but you as a student and writer? Name the virtue and write 2–3 sentences explaining why. If writing your own poem required a particular kind of courage or honesty, say so specifically.
