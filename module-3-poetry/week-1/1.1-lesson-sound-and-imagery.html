7th Grade ELA — Module 3
Poetry — Week 1 — Lesson 1.1

Sound and Imagery: How Poems Work

What a poem does before it means anything — and why that matters

This Lesson

You will identify sound devices in two short poems and explain how the sounds create meaning — not just decoration.

Opening Question

Think of a line of writing — a sentence, a lyric, a title, anything — that has stayed with you. It can be from a book, a song, a film, a speech. What is it, and why do you think it stuck? Write two or three sentences before you read on.

Lesson

A poem is a made thing. Before it carries a meaning, it carries a sound — a texture of consonants and vowels that arrives in the reader's ear before the mind has processed a single idea. This is why a poem in a foreign language can move you even when you understand none of the words: the arrangement of sounds does something to the body that precedes understanding. The technical terms for the main sound devices are alliteration (the repetition of initial consonant sounds, as in “the wild and wintry waste”), assonance (the repetition of vowel sounds within words, as in “the rain in Spain”), and consonance (the repetition of consonant sounds anywhere in the words, not just at the start). But memorizing those terms is the easy part. The harder and more interesting question is: why does a poet choose these particular sounds here, in this line? Hard plosive sounds — b, d, p, k — create percussive, abrupt effects; soft fricatives — s, f, sh — slow the line down and soften it. A poet in full control of sound is using it the way a composer uses rhythm: to shape how the reader moves through the poem and what they feel while they move.

Imagery is the poem’s second instrument. An image is any word or phrase that calls up a sensory experience — not just what something looks like, but what it sounds like, smells like, feels like, tastes like, or moves like. Strong images do not describe; they transport. When Robert Frost writes “And miles to go before I sleep,” the image of the road at night in winter is not decoration appended to a philosophical idea — it is the idea, made physical and available to the reader’s body. The two instruments — sound and image — work together: sound controls how fast you move through the line; the image controls what you see when you arrive.

Vocabulary — Week 1, Word 1

ALLITERATION

The repetition of the same initial consonant sound in words that are close together in a line. Effect depends on which consonant: hard sounds accelerate and punch; soft sounds lull and slow.

Copia exercise: Robert Frost writes, “The woods are lovely, dark and deep.” Rewrite this line using alliteration on a different consonant. Keep the mood. Change everything else.

Vocabulary — Week 1, Word 2

IMAGERY

Language that creates a sensory experience in the reader’s mind. Not limited to sight. An image can be tactile (“the cold creep of the shadow”), auditory (“the low moan of the wind”), olfactory, or kinesthetic. An effective image transports; a weak image describes.

Copia exercise: Emily Dickinson writes, “I felt a Funeral, in my Brain.” Rewrite using a different physical event or location to carry the same sense of psychological weight. Keep “felt” or an equivalent. Change everything else.

Comprehension Check

1. In Frost’s poem, the final two lines are: “And miles to go before I sleep, / And miles to go before I sleep.” Which of the following best names what this repetition creates?

A) It confuses the reader about how far the speaker must travel.

B) It makes the poem longer without adding meaning.

C) It creates a hypnotic, incantatory effect that makes the repeated words feel inevitable — and slightly ominous.

D) It shows the speaker is forgetful.

Answer: C — Repetition at the end of a poem (anaphora/epistrophe) creates rhythm and weight. The doubling makes “sleep” feel like more than literal sleep.

2. Dickinson ends her poem with: “And then the Windows failed — and then / I could not see to see —” What is unusual about the phrase “could not see to see”?

A) It is a typo — Dickinson meant to write “could not see to read.”

B) It distinguishes between physical sight and some deeper faculty of perception, both of which fail at the moment of death.

C) It means the speaker is blind from birth.

D) It is an example of alliteration.

Answer: B — The redundancy is the point. “See to see” splits perception from consciousness — the eyes stop, then something beyond the eyes stops.
