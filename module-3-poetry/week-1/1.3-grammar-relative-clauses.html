Relative Clauses

Who, which, and that — how to add information without adding sentences

The Structure

A relative clause is a dependent clause that modifies a noun, introduced by a relative pronoun: who, whom, whose, which, or that. It is the most efficient way to add a subordinate idea to a noun without writing a new sentence. Like appositives, relative clauses can be restrictive (no commas) or non-restrictive (commas required).

Pronoun Refers to Example
who / whom People Frost, who grew up in New England, set most of his poems in rural landscapes.
whose Possession (people or things) Dickinson, whose poems were largely unpublished in her lifetime, is now one of the most studied poets in the language.
which Things (non-restrictive) The fly, which buzzes three times in the poem, represents the mundane intrusion of mortality.
that Things (restrictive) The image that stays with most readers is the fly buzzing at the moment of death.

The which/that rule

That introduces a restrictive clause (essential to meaning — no commas): The poem that changed my thinking about death…

Which introduces a non-restrictive clause (added information — commas required): The poem, which Dickinson wrote in 1862, was not published until…

Relative clauses in literary analysis

Combining appositives (from 1.2) with relative clauses gives you a powerful analytic toolkit. You can pack several layers of information into a single sentence:

The final stanza, which withholds the speaker’s actual death from the reader, shifts the perspective to someone who can no longer see — a speaker whose consciousness fades with the windows.

Exercises

Exercise 1 — Combine with a relative clause

Combine the two sentences using a relative clause with the pronoun in brackets. Add commas if the clause is non-restrictive.

  1. Frost wrote about dark New England woods. Those woods appear in several of his most famous poems. [which]
  2. Dickinson rarely left her home in Amherst. Her poems range across the entire universe. [whose]
  3. The fly buzzes at the moment of death. That moment is the poem’s emotional climax. [that]

Sample: (1) Frost wrote about dark New England woods, which appear in several of his most famous poems. (2) Dickinson, whose poems range across the entire universe, rarely left her home in Amherst. (3) The fly buzzes at the moment of death, which is the poem’s emotional climax. — OR: The fly buzzes at the moment that is the poem’s emotional climax.

Exercise 2 — Choose who or whom

Fill in the blank with who or whom. Tip: substitute “he/she” = who; “him/her” = whom.

  1. Emily Dickinson was a poet _____ published almost nothing in her lifetime.
  2. The editor _____ Dickinson trusted most was her friend Thomas Higginson.
  3. The speaker, _____ we never see directly, narrates her own death.

Answers: (1) who (she published)  (2) whom (she trusted him)  (3) whom (we never see her)
