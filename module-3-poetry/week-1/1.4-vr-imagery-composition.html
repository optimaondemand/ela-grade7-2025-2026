7th Grade ELA — Module 3 — VR Assignment
Poetry — Week 1 — VR 1.4

VR 1.4: Imagery in Space

Build a spatial composition that makes a poem’s central image three-dimensional

What You’re Doing

A poem creates images in the reader’s mind. Your job in this session is to take one central image from the Week 1 readings and build it in three-dimensional space using IFX. Then annotate your composition with a sticky note that names the image and explains what it contributes to the poem’s meaning. The question you are answering with your composition: if you could walk into this poem, what would you see?

What You’ll Need
  • Device: VR headset (required for IFX placement and spatial composition)
  • Session ID: ELA7-M3-W1 — Persistent Session
  • Location: Forest Clearing (or equivalent woodland/nature Location)
  • Pre-reading: You should have read both Week 1 poems before entering VR
How to Join

Session type: Persistent (your composition will remain for the gallery walk in Week 5)

Session ID: ELA7-M3-W1

On arrival, look for the Whiteboard IFX at the spawn point labeled “Week 1 Imagery — [Your Name]”. If none exists for you, you will place your own in the first step below.

What to Do
1

Choose your poem and image

Select one of the two Week 1 poems (Frost or Dickinson). Identify the one image you will build. Good choices: the dark woods and traveler (Frost), the fly buzzing in a death chamber (Dickinson). Name your chosen image before you begin placing IFX — write it on a sticky note and place it at your workspace as a title.

2

Build the composition (4–6 IFX)

Place 4–6 IFX from the ENGAGE library to represent the image. Think spatially: where is the dominant element? What is in the foreground vs. background? Scale and positioning carry meaning. For Frost: consider tree IFX (large, surrounding), traveler IFX (small, at a threshold), horse IFX (at the edge). For Dickinson: consider room IFX, window IFX (letting in fading light), fly IFX (placed at center, near the speaker). Lock IFX in place when you are satisfied with their positions.

3

Write the analytical sticky note

Place a sticky note beside your composition. It must include three things: (1) the title of the poem and the image you are representing, (2) one sentence using an appositive that names a sound device in the poem (e.g., “The opening line, an example of alliteration, creates…”), (3) one sentence explaining what the image contributes to the poem’s meaning — not just what it depicts, but what it does.

4

Take a snapshot

Take a snapshot of your complete composition with the sticky note visible. Name it YourLastName_M3_1.4. Submit the snapshot file to this Canvas assignment.

Submission

Upload your snapshot (YourLastName_M3_1.4) to this Canvas assignment. Estimated time: 20–25 minutes in headset.

Facilitator Note

Students are identifying one central image from either Frost or Dickinson and building a spatial composition using 4–6 ENGAGE IFX. The key assessment criterion is the sticky note: does the student’s analytical sentence go beyond describing the image to explaining what it does in the poem? Students who describe well but don’t interpret should be asked: “What does the poet want the reader to feel when this image appears?” The appositive requirement is a grammar integration point — watch for students who use it correctly vs. those who omit it. Compositions are persistent for the Week 5 gallery walk; remind students not to delete their work.
