VR 1.5: Sound Map

Make sound devices visible — then record your analysis aloud

What You’re Doing

You are returning to the same Session as VR 1.4 but working in a different area to create a sound map of one of the two Week 1 poems. A sound map is a visual representation of where and how sound devices operate in a poem. You will use a Whiteboard IFX to write out the poem’s most sonically rich stanza and annotate each sound device you find. Then you will record a 60–90 second spatial recording in which you explain aloud what the sound devices contribute to the poem’s effect.

What You’ll Need
  • Device: VR headset
  • Session ID: ELA7-M3-W1 (same Persistent Session as VR 1.4 — move to a new area away from your 1.4 composition)
  • Location: Forest Clearing
  • Your poem: You may use the same poem as VR 1.4 or switch to the other one
What to Do
1

Choose a stanza and place your Whiteboard IFX

Place a Whiteboard IFX in a new area of the Location. Connect it to your Drawpad. Title it: SOUND MAP — [Poem Title] — [Your Name]. Then write out the stanza (or the two most sonically rich lines, if the poem is short) on the board. You can write directly on the Whiteboard IFX with the virtual pen or use text input.

2

Annotate the sound devices

On the Whiteboard IFX, mark at least three sound devices. Use consistent notation: circle alliteration, underline assonance, draw a wavy line under consonance. Below each mark, write the device’s name. You must find at least two different types of sound devices (not three examples of the same type).

3

Record your analysis (60–90 seconds)

Start a spatial recording. Standing in front of your sound map, explain: (1) which poem and stanza you chose, (2) which sound devices you found and where, (3) — this is the most important part — what the sounds contribute to the poem’s meaning or effect. Do not just read the labels off the board. Say something like: “The repeated /s/ sounds in the third line slow the pace of the reading, which matches the stillness Frost describes in the scene.” Use at least one relative clause (from Grammar 1.3) in your spoken analysis.

4

Submit the spatial recording

End the spatial recording. The file will appear in your ENGAGE recordings. Share the link and paste it into the Canvas submission text box for this assignment. Name it YourLastName_M3_1.5.

Submission

Paste the spatial recording link into the Canvas submission text box. Estimated time: 20–25 minutes in headset.

Facilitator Note

Students are creating a visual annotation of a poem’s sound devices on a Whiteboard IFX and recording a spoken analysis. The key move to look for in the recording is whether students interpret the sound devices (what do they do?) rather than merely identifying them (what are they called?). Students who only label are doing half the work. The grammar integration point: listen for relative clauses in the spoken analysis. If a student’s recording is too short or too thin, ask: “What does that sound device make the reader feel or do?” Graded on completion (submitted recording + annotated board visible in persistent Session).
