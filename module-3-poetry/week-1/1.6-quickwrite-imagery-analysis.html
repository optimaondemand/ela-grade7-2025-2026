7th Grade ELA — Module 3 — Quick Write
Poetry — Week 1 — QW 1.6

QW 1.6: Analyzing Imagery and Sound

5 points — pass/fail — submit as text entry

The Task

Write a paragraph of 5–7 sentences analyzing one image from one of the two Week 1 poems. Your paragraph must include all three of the following labeled elements.

Required elements

[APP] One sentence using an appositive that names either the poet or the poem and adds information about them (from Grammar 1.2).

[REL] One sentence using a relative clause with who, which, whose, or that (from Grammar 1.3).

[SOUND] One sentence identifying a specific sound device in the poem by its correct name, quoting the relevant word(s), and explaining what the sound does in that moment — not just that it exists.

Example Paragraph (on “I Heard a Fly Buzz When I Died”)

Emily Dickinson, a poet who left behind nearly 1,800 poems discovered after her death [APP], opens this poem in a room that feels suspended between life and what comes after. The speakers in Dickinson’s death poems, who are always aware of the reader watching them [REL], narrate their own endings with unsettling calm. Here, the central image is the fly — not a symbol of rot or death as it might appear in another poet’s hands, but a symbol of the ordinary world’s refusal to pause for anyone’s dying. The buzz of the fly, an example of onomatopoeia, forces an ugly, intrusive sound into the poem’s quiet — Dickinson uses it to argue that death is not solemn; it is interrupted [SOUND]. The poem’s most disturbing move is its final image: the windows fail, then the speaker’s ability to perceive fails, in that order. What Dickinson shows is that consciousness does not end dramatically — it dims like a light through glass.

Submission

Type your paragraph directly into the Canvas text entry box. Label each required element in bold brackets: [APP], [REL], [SOUND]. These labels can appear within the sentence; you do not need to list them separately.

Full credit (5 pts): Paragraph contains all three labeled elements, each used correctly, and the analysis explains meaning rather than just identifying devices. No credit: Missing two or more required elements, or elements are present but mislabeled or incorrect.
