**Q1.** Robert Frost's "Stopping by Woods on a Snowy Evening" contains the lines: "The only other sound's the sweep / Of easy wind and downy flake." What sound device is most prominently at work in these lines, and what does it contribute?

A) Onomatopoeia — the words "sweep" and "flake" sound like wind and snow falling.
B) Alliteration and assonance — the soft /w/ and /s/ consonants and the open vowels slow the reading to match the stillness and quiet of the scene.
C) Consonance — the repeated /t/ sound in "The only other" creates a percussive, urgent effect.
D) Rhyme — the perfect rhyme between "sweep" and "sleep" appears later and drives the poem forward.

**Answer: B**

**Q2.** In Emily Dickinson's "I Heard a Fly Buzz When I Died," the fly is described as having "uncertain stumbling Buzz." Which of the following best explains why Dickinson uses the word "stumbling" to describe a fly's buzzing?

A) Dickinson is making a factual error — flies do not stumble.
B) "Stumbling" is an example of transferred epithet: the unsteady, faltering quality of the dying speaker's perception is projected onto the fly's sound, making the fly feel like an extension of the speaker's fading consciousness.
C) "Stumbling" is an example of alliteration with "Buzz."
D) Dickinson uses "stumbling" to suggest the fly is drunk.

**Answer: B**

C) Emily Dickinson was a reclusive poet, she published almost nothing.
D) Emily Dickinson: a reclusive poet who published almost nothing in her lifetime.

**Q4.** The module's essential question is: "What can a poem do that prose cannot?" Based on the Week 1 readings, which of the following best answers this question?

A) A poem can tell a story; prose cannot.
B) A poem can use rhyme; prose never does.
C) A poem can use the arrangement of sound — the texture of consonants and vowels — to shape how a reader moves through a line and what they feel while moving, in ways that prose almost never can.
D) A poem is always shorter than prose, which makes it easier to understand.

**Answer: C**

**Q5.** Which sentence uses a relative clause correctly?

**Q6.** Frost ends his poem with the same line repeated twice: "And miles to go before I sleep." A student argues this is just a printing error or a mistake. What is the strongest counter-argument?

A) The repetition creates a rhyme, which is required in all formal poetry.
B) The repetition is deliberate: it creates an incantatory, hypnotic effect and makes "sleep" carry a weight beyond its literal meaning — the doubling suggests the speaker is aware of a meaning they cannot or will not name directly.
C) Frost repeats the line because the poem is too short without it.
D) Repeated lines are common in all poems and carry no particular significance.

**Answer: B**

**Q7.** In Dickinson's poem, the line "the Eyes around — had wrung them dry —" refers to those watching the speaker die. The dash after "around" and after "dry" is a Dickinson signature. What is the effect of the dash in that line?

A) The dashes indicate that words are missing and the poem is incomplete.
B) The dashes create sudden pauses that break the line into emotional beats, slowing the reader down and giving each phrase the weight of an intake of breath.
C) The dashes are used incorrectly and represent Dickinson's unconventional grammar.
D) The dashes show that the line is a question, not a statement.

**Answer: B**

**Q8.** Which of the following is an example of imagery that is tactile (felt by the body) rather than visual (seen by the eye)?

A) "The woods are lovely, dark and deep."
B) "The only other sound's the sweep / Of easy wind and downy flake." — the word "downy" gives the snow a soft, physical texture.
C) "I heard a Fly buzz — when I Died."
D) "The windows failed."

**Answer: B**

**Q9.** A student writes: "The alliteration in Frost's poem is when words start with the same letter." What is wrong with this definition, and what would a better one say?

A) Nothing is wrong — that is the correct definition of alliteration.
B) The definition is imprecise: alliteration is the repetition of the same initial consonant sound, not necessarily the same letter ("phone" and "for" alliterate; "cat" and "city" do not). A better definition would specify sound, not spelling.
C) The definition is wrong because alliteration applies to vowels, not consonants.
D) The definition is wrong because alliteration only occurs in poetry, not prose.

**Answer: B**

**Q10.** Frost's speaker says he has "promises to keep" and "miles to go before I sleep." Based on the poem as a whole, what is the most defensible interpretation of the tension the speaker feels?

A) The speaker is physically tired and wants to stop walking.
B) The speaker is caught between the pull of rest — possibly permanent rest, possibly death — and the obligations of a life not yet finished; the dark woods represent something attractive precisely because of how completely they would end those obligations.
C) The speaker regrets leaving his horse in the cold and is hurrying home out of concern for the animal.
D) The speaker has forgotten where he is going and pauses to remember.

**Answer: B**
