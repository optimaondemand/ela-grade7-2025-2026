Meter and the Ballad: How Form Carries Story

Why poetry has rhythm — and what ballads were built to do

This Lesson

You will read the ballad “Lord Randall” and a narrative poem by Langston Hughes, scan the metrical pattern of a four-line stanza, and explain how meter and repetition work together to carry a story.

Opening Question

Think of a song you know — not a poem, but a song. If you removed the melody, would the words still carry some kind of rhythm? Try reading the chorus aloud, without singing. Write two sentences about what happens when you remove the music. Does anything of the rhythm survive?

Lesson

Meter is the organized pattern of stressed and unstressed syllables in a line of poetry. When you read a poem aloud and feel that you are naturally emphasizing certain syllables over others — the WOODS are LOVE-ly, DARK and DEEP — you are feeling meter. The most common metrical foot in English is the iamb: one unstressed syllable followed by one stressed syllable (da-DUM). A line of five iambs is called iambic pentameter, the meter Shakespeare used in his sonnets and plays. But meter is not only for formal or elevated poetry. The ballad stanza — the form of folk songs, murder ballads, and sea shanties — is one of the most ancient and durable metrical forms in English: four-line stanzas alternating lines of four stresses (tetrameter) and three stresses (trimeter), with the second and fourth lines rhyming. It is simple enough to memorize and flexible enough to carry almost any story.

The ballad is a narrative poem: a poem that tells a story. The oldest ballads were not written down at all — they were sung by people who could not read and passed from singer to singer through memory. Their formal features (the repeated stanza, the simple rhyme, the refrain) are mnemonic devices: they help the singer remember. The emotional content of ballads is stark because singers needed their listeners to feel the story immediately, without explanation. “Lord Randall,” an anonymous Scottish ballad that may be eight centuries old, tells the story of a son returning home poisoned by his lover through a series of questions and answers between mother and son — and the audience has to piece together what happened from what isn’t said. The poem’s refrain — “for I’m weary wi’ hunting, and fain wad lie down” — shifts from exhaustion to something more final with each repetition. That is what repetition in poetry can do: keep the words constant while the context beneath them changes everything.

Historical Context

Ballads were the mass media of the medieval and early modern world. Before printing presses, they were the primary way stories of disaster, scandal, love, and murder spread across a population. The English tradition of the broadside ballad — a ballad printed on a single sheet and sold in the street — lasted from the 16th to the 19th century. Langston Hughes, writing in Harlem in the 1920s–1940s, deliberately drew on the ballad tradition to give Black American vernacular speech the formal dignity of literary art. His ballads do the same work as “Lord Randall”: they tell a stripped-down story with no wasted words and let the refrain accumulate weight with each repetition.

Vocabulary — Week 2, Word 1

METER

The organized pattern of stressed and unstressed syllables in a line of poetry. The most common foot in English is the iamb (da-DUM). Ballad stanzas alternate lines of four stresses and three stresses.

Copia exercise: Frost writes, “The woods are lovely, dark and deep.” This line has four iambs. Rewrite it with a completely different subject and scene, but preserve the four-stress pattern (da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM). Mark the stresses above your version.

Vocabulary — Week 2, Word 2

REFRAIN

A line or group of lines that repeats at intervals throughout a poem or song, usually at the end of each stanza. In ballads, the refrain seems to stay the same while the story around it changes its meaning. A powerful refrain can mean one thing the first time and something completely different the last time.

Copia exercise: Lord Randall’s refrain is “for I’m weary wi’ hunting, and fain wad lie down.” Write a modern English equivalent that could work as the refrain for a narrative poem about a soldier returning from a long war. The refrain should be able to mean tiredness in the first stanza and something more permanent in the last.

Comprehension Check

1. In “Lord Randall,” the mother asks increasingly specific questions about what her son ate. At what point does the reader understand what has happened to Lord Randall, even though the poem never says it directly?

A) When Lord Randall says he is tired from hunting.

B) When Lord Randall reports that his hounds ate the same food and died — the connection between the food, the dogs’ death, and his own deteriorating condition makes the poisoning unmistakable.

C) When the mother asks where he has been.

D) The poem never makes clear that Lord Randall was poisoned.

Answer: B — The poem relies on inference. The hounds dying is the pivot; from there, the reader completes the logic the poem withholds.

2. In Hughes’s “Ballad of the Landlord,” who speaks the last four lines of the poem, and how does this shift in speaker change the poem’s meaning?

A) The landlord speaks the last four lines, explaining his side of the story.

B) A newspaper headline and a judge’s sentence speak the last four lines — the poem suddenly shifts from personal confrontation to institutional voice, showing how quickly a human story is processed and crushed by impersonal systems.

C) The tenant returns to ask for mercy.

D) Hughes himself speaks, offering a moral to the story.

Answer: B
