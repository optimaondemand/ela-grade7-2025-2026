The Colon

The mark that says: here it comes

What a Colon Does

The colon (:) is the mark of anticipation. It follows a complete independent clause and introduces what the clause has set up: a list, an explanation, a quotation, an example, or a dramatic reveal. Think of it as an arrow: the colon points forward from the statement that precedes it to the material that completes or expands it.

The colon is the most dramatic punctuation mark in English prose — more emphatic than a comma, more forward-looking than a period, and more precise than a dash. In analytical writing, it is the mark you use when you want to announce that a definition, a list, or a piece of evidence is about to arrive.

Three correct uses of the colon

1. To introduce a list

Ballads have three defining features: a four-line stanza, a rhyme on lines two and four, and a repeated refrain.

2. To introduce an explanation or definition

The refrain does something remarkable in ballad poetry: it keeps the words constant while the story beneath them changes everything they mean.

3. To introduce a quotation that the sentence sets up

Dickinson opens the poem with one of the most unusual first lines in American poetry: “I Heard a Fly buzz — when I died.”

The golden rule of the colon

What comes before the colon must be a complete independent clause. This is the most common colon error: placing a colon after a fragment or an incomplete clause.

Wrong: The poem contains: alliteration, assonance, and consonance. (The clause before the colon is incomplete — “The poem contains” is not a standalone thought.)

Right: The poem uses three sound devices: alliteration, assonance, and consonance.

Colon vs. dash

Both the colon and the em dash can introduce a phrase that follows a complete clause. The difference is tone: the colon is formal and deliberate; the dash is emphatic and sometimes abrupt.

Colon (formal): Frost’s poem ends with a difficult admission: he has promises to keep and miles to go before he can rest.

Dash (emphatic): Frost’s poem ends with a difficult admission — promises to keep, miles to go, no rest yet.

Exercises

Exercise 1 — Add a colon or fix the error

Each sentence is either missing a colon or has one placed incorrectly. Add or move the colon, or rewrite the clause before it to make it complete.

  1. The ballad stanza consists of: four lines with the second and fourth rhyming.
  2. Langston Hughes chose the ballad form for one clear reason the form gave Black American speech the authority of a literary tradition centuries old.
  3. Lord Randall answers his mother with increasing weariness in every stanza he says, “for I’m weary wi’ hunting, and fain wad lie down.”

Sample: (1) Rewrite: “The ballad stanza has three defining features: four lines, a rhyme on lines two and four, and a refrain.” (2) “…for one clear reason: the form gave…” (3) “…in every stanza, he says: ‘for I’m weary…’”

Exercise 2 — Write your own

Write two sentences about “Ballad of the Landlord” or “Lord Randall” that each use a colon correctly. Each sentence must use a different colon function (introduction of a list; introduction of an explanation; introduction of a quotation).
