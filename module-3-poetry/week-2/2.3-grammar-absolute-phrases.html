Absolute Phrases

Floating modifiers that belong to the whole sentence — not just one word

What an Absolute Phrase Is

An absolute phrase consists of a noun (or pronoun) plus a participle (or participial phrase) and modifies the entire sentence rather than a single word in it. Unlike a participial phrase, which must modify a specific noun, an absolute phrase floats: it describes circumstances or conditions surrounding the main action without attaching to any particular word in the main clause.

The test: if you can add “with” or “his/her/its/their” before the phrase and it makes sense, you probably have an absolute phrase.

Examples

Absolute phrase at the front:

His voice growing weaker with each stanza, Lord Randall answers his mother’s questions.

“His voice growing weaker with each stanza” is the absolute phrase. “His voice” is the noun; “growing weaker” is the participial phrase. It does not modify “Lord Randall” specifically — it describes the circumstance of the whole action.

Absolute phrase at the end:

The speaker narrates her own death, her consciousness dimming with each stanza like light through failing glass.

Absolute phrase in the middle:

The ballad, its refrain repeating like a ritual, builds dread by accumulation rather than revelation.

Absolute phrase vs. dangling participle

A participial phrase dangles when the noun it should modify is absent from the sentence. An absolute phrase never dangles because it doesn’t attach to a noun — it belongs to the whole sentence.

Dangling: Growing weaker with each stanza, the questions came harder and harder. (The questions are not growing weaker.)

Absolute: His voice growing weaker with each stanza, the questions came harder and harder for Lord Randall to answer.

Exercises

Exercise 1 — Identify the absolute phrase

Underline the absolute phrase in each sentence. Then identify the noun and participial phrase that compose it.

  1. The poem’s refrain echoing through each stanza, the reader feels the weight of repetition build.
  2. Hughes shifts suddenly to a newspaper headline, his narrative voice replaced by institutional language.
  3. Lord Randall answers his mother, his responses growing shorter and more resigned as the poem progresses.

Exercise 2 — Write your own

Write two sentences about one of the Week 2 poems. Each sentence must contain an absolute phrase. Label each absolute phrase with brackets.

Tip: start with the noun that will head your absolute phrase (“his voice,” “her tone,” “the poem’s refrain”), then add a participle that shows what that noun is doing.
