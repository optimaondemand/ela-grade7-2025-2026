QW 2.6: Ballad Analysis

5 points — pass/fail — submit as text entry

The Task

Write a paragraph of 5–7 sentences analyzing how one of the Week 2 ballads uses repetition or meter to build its emotional effect. Your paragraph must include all three labeled elements below.

Required elements

[COL] One sentence using a colon to introduce either a definition, an explanation, or a short list (from Grammar 2.2). The clause before the colon must be complete.

[ABS] One sentence containing an absolute phrase (a noun + participle that modifies the whole sentence, not a single word) (from Grammar 2.3).

[REF] One sentence that quotes the refrain or a repeated line from the poem and explains how its meaning changes between its first and last appearance in the poem.

Example Paragraph (on “Lord Randall”)

The anonymous Scottish ballad “Lord Randall” derives its power from a single formal choice: it withholds the central event entirely, and the audience must infer a poisoning from a series of increasingly ominous domestic details [COL]. His voice growing weaker with each stanza, Lord Randall answers his mother’s questions with shorter and shorter phrases until he can only ask to make his will [ABS]. The poem’s refrain — “for I’m weary wi’ hunting, and fain wad lie down” — means exhaustion from a long day in the first stanza, but by the final stanza, when the audience knows what has happened to Lord Randall’s hounds, “fain wad lie down” has become the request of a dying man [REF]. This transformation is the ballad’s most powerful move: the words do not change, but everything beneath them does. The meter reinforces the effect — the regular alternation of four-stress and three-stress lines creates a rhythm so stable that its very stability becomes sinister as the content darkens.

Submission

Type your paragraph into the Canvas text entry box. Label each required element in bold brackets. Full credit (5 pts): All three elements present, correctly used, and the analysis explains effect rather than just identifying features.
