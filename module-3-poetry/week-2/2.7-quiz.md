**Q1.** The traditional ballad stanza alternates lines of four stresses and three stresses. Which of the following best explains why ballads were built this way rather than in a more complex metrical form?

A) Ballads were composed by people who could not count past four.
B) The alternating four-stress/three-stress pattern is simple enough to memorize, flexible enough to carry any story, and creates a rocking rhythm that aided singers in remembering and audiences in following — it is a mnemonic and emotional tool, not a limitation.
C) The pattern was required by law in medieval Scotland.
D) Complex meters were reserved for prose, so poetry had to use simpler forms.

**Answer: B**

**Q2.** In "Lord Randall," the audience learns that Lord Randall was poisoned not from any direct statement but from inference. At what moment does the inference become unavoidable?

A) When the mother asks where he has been.
B) When Lord Randall mentions his sweetheart for the first time.
C) When Lord Randall reports that his hounds ate the same food and died — the parallel between the dogs' death and his own deteriorating state makes the conclusion unavoidable even though the poem never says it.
D) When Lord Randall asks to make his will in the final stanza.

**Answer: C**

**Q3.** Which sentence uses a colon correctly?

**Q4.** Langston Hughes's "Ballad of the Landlord" ends with a newspaper headline and a judge's sentence. What does this shift in speaker accomplish?

A) It tells us that the tenant won his court case.
B) It shows that the story has a happy ending because the law intervened.
C) It shifts from individual human experience to impersonal institutional language, showing how quickly a personal injustice is processed, categorized, and made invisible by the machinery of the legal system.
D) It suggests that Hughes lost interest in the story and ended the poem abruptly.

**Answer: C**

**Q5.** Which sentence contains an absolute phrase?

**Q6.** The refrain in "Lord Randall" — "for I'm weary wi' hunting, and fain wad lie down" — appears in every stanza but means something different by the end. What is the name for this technique, and what does it achieve?

A) This is called enjambment; it carries the meaning from one stanza to the next without stopping.
B) This is called a volta; it marks the turn in the poem's argument.
C) This is sometimes called incremental repetition or contextual transformation: the words stay identical while the narrative context changes their meaning — by the final stanza, "fain wad lie down" is a dying man's request, not a hunter's complaint.
D) This is called assonance; the repeated vowel sounds in the refrain create the emotional effect.

**Answer: C**

**Q7.** Which of the following correctly scans the meter of this line from a traditional ballad: "Oh where have you been, Lord Randall, my son?"

A) da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM (four iambs — iambic tetrameter)
B) The line cannot be scanned because it is in archaic Scottish dialect.
C) DUM-da DUM-da DUM-da DUM-da (four trochees — trochaic tetrameter)
D) The line has four main stresses (where, been, Ran-, son) with unstressed syllables between them — roughly iambic tetrameter with a feminine ending, which is typical of ballad meter.

**Answer: D**

**Q8.** What does the word "refrain" mean in the context of a ballad?

A) A refrain is the title of the poem.
B) A refrain is a line or group of lines that repeats at intervals, usually at the end of each stanza, and often accumulates meaning with each repetition even when the words remain identical.
C) A refrain is the poem's closing moral or lesson.
D) A refrain is the name for the poet's decision to avoid rhyme.

**Answer: B**

**Q9.** A student argues that "Ballad of the Landlord" is not really a ballad because it has no refrain and the stanza lengths vary. What is the strongest response to this argument?

A) The student is correct — "Ballad of the Landlord" is a free verse poem, not a ballad.
B) The student is partially right: "Ballad of the Landlord" does not use all the conventions of a traditional ballad. But Hughes is deliberately working with the form rather than inside it — the poem uses the ballad's oral directness, vernacular voice, and stripped-down narrative while adapting the structure for a modern political context. "Ballad" names the tradition it draws on, not a rigid set of rules it must obey.
C) The student is wrong — all ballads must have a refrain, and "Ballad of the Landlord" does have one.
D) The student is wrong because Hughes never called the poem a ballad in the title.

**Answer: B**

**Q10.** Why did ballads traditionally withhold key information — like the details of Lord Randall's poisoning — instead of stating it directly?

A) The poets were afraid of censorship from church authorities.
B) Withholding information is a defect of the form — earlier poets were simply less skilled at exposition.
C) Ballads were sung to audiences who needed to feel the story immediately; withholding the central event forces the audience to participate actively in constructing meaning, and the moment of inference is more emotionally powerful than any direct statement could be.
D) The singers who transmitted ballads orally often forgot the key details and improvised around them.

**Answer: C**
