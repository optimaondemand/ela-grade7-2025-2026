Poetry — Week 3 — Lesson 3.1

The Sonnet: The Turn and the Argument

Fourteen lines that have to mean something — and the moment they pivot

This Lesson

You will read Shakespeare’s Sonnet 18 and Sonnet 73, identify the volta (the poem’s argumentative turn), and explain what the sonnet form requires of a poet that free verse does not.

Opening Question

Every argument has a turning point — a moment when the reasoning shifts, complicates, or arrives at a conclusion. Think of an argument you have actually had with someone (not a fight — an actual discussion of a question). Where did the conversation turn? What caused it? Write two sentences identifying the turning point and what changed after it.

Lesson

The sonnet is fourteen lines of iambic pentameter, and it is one of the most demanding forms in the Western literary tradition — not because fourteen lines is hard to write, but because a sonnet has to make a complete argument in those fourteen lines. The form does not allow for sprawl or vagueness. The Shakespearean (or English) sonnet divides into three quatrains (four-line stanzas) and a closing couplet (two rhyming lines), with the rhyme scheme ABAB CDCD EFEF GG. The three quatrains develop a problem or an idea; the couplet resolves it, complicates it, or delivers the final turn. The Italian (or Petrarchan) sonnet divides into an octave (eight lines, ABBAABBA) and a sestet (six lines, variable rhyme), with the turn between them. In both forms, the structural hinge is the volta: the Italian word for “turn,” the moment when the poem’s direction changes. The volta is usually marked by a conjunction — “But,” “Yet,” “And yet,” “So” — but not always. Sometimes the shift is syntactic rather than lexical, detectable by the change in what the poem is doing rather than by a single word.

Shakespeare’s Sonnet 18 is probably the most famous poem in the English language. It begins with a comparison (“Shall I compare thee to a summer’s day?”) and then systematically dismantles the comparison, arguing that summer fades while the beloved does not. The volta arrives in the couplet: “So long as men can breathe, or eyes can see, / So long lives this, and this gives life to thee.” The argument that has been running through twelve lines resolves here — and the resolution is not about the beloved at all, but about the poem itself and what poetry can do. This is the move that makes Sonnet 18 more interesting than it first appears: it is not simply a love poem. It is an argument about the power of verse to defeat time. The beloved is almost beside the point — the subject of the argument has quietly shifted from the person to the poem.

Historical Context

Shakespeare published his sonnets in 1609, but most were probably written in the 1590s, during a plague closure of the London theaters that left him with time for poetry. The sonnet tradition he inherited was Italian — Petrarch had defined the form in the 14th century, and English poets had been adapting it for 60 years by the time Shakespeare wrote. What Shakespeare added was the Elizabethan concern with time, decay, and the power of art to resist both — themes that run through almost all 154 sonnets. Sonnet 73 makes this explicit, using the metaphor of autumn, then twilight, then dying embers to build toward the same argument: what you love will be gone, so love it more now.

Vocabulary — Week 3, Word 1

VOLTA

The “turn” in a sonnet — the moment when the poem’s direction, argument, or tone shifts. In a Shakespearean sonnet, it typically occurs at the couplet; in a Petrarchan sonnet, between the octave and the sestet. The volta is the structural proof that a sonnet makes an argument, not just a description.

Copia exercise: Shakespeare begins Sonnet 18’s couplet with “So long as men can breathe, or eyes can see.” Rewrite the couplet’s turn using a different conditional structure (“As long as…” or “While…”) but preserving the argument that the poem will outlast the beloved’s mortality.

Vocabulary — Week 3, Word 2

EXTENDED METAPHOR

A metaphor that runs through an entire poem (or a substantial portion of it) rather than appearing in a single line. In Sonnet 73, the extended metaphor moves through three stages — autumn, twilight, dying fire — each describing the same thing (aging and coming death) from a different angle. Extended metaphors require the reader to track a comparison across many lines.

Copia exercise: Sonnet 73 opens: “That time of year thou mayst in me behold / When yellow leaves, or none, or few, do hang.” Rewrite this opening quatrain using a different season (or a different time of day) as the extended metaphor for aging. Keep the structure: “That [time] thou mayst in me behold / When…”

Comprehension Check

1. In Sonnet 18, the poem has been arguing that the beloved surpasses summer in twelve lines. What does the couplet add that the rest of the poem does not say?

A) The couplet repeats the comparison between summer and the beloved.

B) The couplet shifts the poem’s claim: the beloved will be preserved not by their own qualities but by the poem itself — “this” (the poem) will give “thee” (the beloved) life after death. The true subject of the poem is the power of verse to defeat time.

C) The couplet apologizes for the comparison by admitting it was imperfect.

D) The couplet introduces a new comparison between the beloved and winter.

Answer: B

2. In Sonnet 73, the three quatrains use three different extended metaphors for the speaker’s aging: autumn, twilight, and dying embers. What does the movement through these three metaphors accomplish that a single metaphor would not?

A) Three metaphors are simply more interesting than one, so the poem holds the reader’s attention longer.

B) The three metaphors build a cumulative argument: each one narrows the time frame (autumn → evening → the last glow of a fire), so by the time the couplet arrives, the speaker’s remaining life feels very short indeed. The metaphors are not synonyms; they escalate.

C) Three metaphors allow the poem to rhyme more easily.

D) The three metaphors represent three different people who loved the speaker.

Answer: B
