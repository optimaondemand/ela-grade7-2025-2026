Noun Clauses

Clauses that do the work of nouns — and why analytical writers need them

What a Noun Clause Does

A noun clause is a dependent clause that functions as a noun inside a larger sentence. It can serve as a subject, a direct object, a subject complement, or an object of a preposition. Noun clauses are introduced by subordinating words like that, what, which, who, whom, whose, when, where, why, whether, how.

For literary analysis, noun clauses are especially valuable because they let you state interpretive claims as complete ideas while integrating them into a larger sentence:

Noun clause functions — examples from the Week 3 poems

As subject:

That the poem will outlast the beloved is the couplet’s central claim.

As direct object:

Shakespeare argues that poetry defeats time more convincingly than the body can.

As subject complement (after a linking verb):

The sonnet’s real subject is what art can preserve that nature cannot.

As object of a preposition:

Shakespeare builds his argument from what he observes about summer’s impermanence.

The “that” question

Noun clauses as direct objects often omit “that” in informal writing: “Shakespeare argues the poem will outlast the beloved.” Both versions are correct. In formal analytical writing, including “that” is usually clearer: “Shakespeare argues that the poem will outlast the beloved.”

Exercises

Exercise 1 — Identify the noun clause and its function

Underline the noun clause in each sentence. Then label its function: subject, direct object, subject complement, or object of a preposition.

  1. What the volta does to the sonnet’s argument is the most important structural question to ask.
  2. Shakespeare claims that the beloved will live forever through verse.
  3. The poem’s argument is that art outlasts the people it celebrates.
  4. The couplet depends on what came before it in the three quatrains.

Answers: (1) “What the volta does to the sonnet’s argument” — subject; (2) “that the beloved will live forever through verse” — direct object; (3) “that art outlasts the people it celebrates” — subject complement; (4) “what came before it in the three quatrains” — object of preposition “on.”

Exercise 2 — Write your own

Write three sentences about Sonnet 18 or Sonnet 73, each using a noun clause in a different function. Label the function in brackets after each sentence.

Tip: start with one of these sentence frames: “What Shakespeare argues in the couplet is…” / “The poem demonstrates that…” / “The turn depends on what…”
