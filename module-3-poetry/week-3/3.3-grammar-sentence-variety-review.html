Combining Structures: Writing Like an Analyst

Putting appositives, relative clauses, absolute phrases, colons, and noun clauses to work together

The Goal

This lesson is a synthesis. You have now learned five sentence structures in Module 3: appositives, relative clauses, absolute phrases, colons, and noun clauses. The goal here is not to learn a new structure but to practice combining them so they do not appear as isolated requirements but as natural parts of an analytical voice. A mature literary analyst does not think “now I will use an appositive” — they write sentences that carry as much information and precision as the analysis requires, and those sentences naturally include these structures.

A model analysis paragraph — annotated

Shakespeare’s Sonnet 18, one of the most anthologized poems in the English language [APPOSITIVE], opens with a question that frames the entire argument: shall the speaker compare the beloved to summer, or is such a comparison ultimately inadequate? [COLON + NOUN CLAUSE]. The first twelve lines systematically dismantle the comparison, each quatrain introducing a new summer flaw [ABSOLUTE PHRASE], until the couplet arrives with the poem’s real claim. What Shakespeare ultimately argues [NOUN CLAUSE as subject] is that the beloved does not need summer’s beauty, which fades and dies [RELATIVE CLAUSE], because the poem will preserve them: “So long as men can breathe, or eyes can see, / So long lives this, and this gives life to thee” [COLON + QUOTATION]. The final couplet, whose two lines contain the poem’s argument in compressed form [RELATIVE CLAUSE with “whose”], is not a flourish but a proof.

Exercise

Analyze the sentences below for what is missing, then revise

The following paragraph analyzes Sonnet 73 but is written with very simple sentence structures — all short, all the same. Revise it into 4–5 sentences by combining ideas using at least three of the five Module 3 structures. Label which structures you used in brackets.

Shakespeare wrote Sonnet 73. It is about aging and death. The speaker uses three metaphors. The first is autumn. The second is twilight. The third is dying embers. Each one is shorter than the last. The couplet is about love. It says that knowing someone will die makes you love them more. This is the poem’s argument.

Your revised version does not need to use all five structures — choose three that genuinely improve the clarity and precision of the analysis. Label each with brackets. There is no single correct revision.
