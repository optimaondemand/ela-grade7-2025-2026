VR 3.5: Three Metaphors in Space

Stage the three extended metaphors of Sonnet 73 as three connected zones — then record your analysis

What You’re Doing

Sonnet 73’s three quatrains use three extended metaphors that escalate toward the same point. In this session, you will stage all three as connected zones in the same area of the Session (continuing in ELA7-M3-W3), moving from the broadest (autumn) to the narrowest (dying embers). A spatial recording of 60–90 seconds will explain how the metaphors escalate and why the progression matters to the argument.

What You’ll Need
  • Device: VR headset
  • Session ID: ELA7-M3-W3 (same Persistent Session as VR 3.4, new area)
  • Poem: Sonnet 73 (this assignment is specifically for Sonnet 73’s three-metaphor structure)
What to Do
1

Stage Metaphor 1: Autumn (Quatrain 1)

Place 2–3 IFX representing late autumn: bare or nearly-bare trees, a cold or fading light. The scale should be large — autumn is the widest metaphor. Place a sticky note with: (1) the quoted opening line of Quatrain 1, (2) what this metaphor says about the speaker’s age/situation. Label this zone M1: Autumn — Yellow Leaves, or None.

2

Stage Metaphor 2: Twilight (Quatrain 2)

Place 2 IFX representing dusk or the transition from day to night: a dimming light source, a horizon or sky IFX. Smaller scale than Autumn. Sticky note with: the twilight line (“In me thou see’st the twilight of such day”) and what the narrowing from season to time-of-day represents (time compressing). Label: M2: Twilight — The Black Night.

3

Stage Metaphor 3: Dying Embers (Quatrain 3)

Place 1–2 IFX representing the last glow of a fire (fireplace IFX, ember IFX, or similar). Smallest scale of all three. Sticky note with: the ember lines and what the narrowing from day-to-night to a single dying fire represents (the final, narrowest measure of remaining life). Label: M3: Dying Embers — Consumed with That Which It Was Nourished By.

4

Record the analysis (60–90 seconds)

Walk through your three zones in the spatial recording, starting with the widest (Autumn) and ending at the narrowest (Embers). Explain: (1) what each metaphor represents, (2) how the scale decreases from metaphor to metaphor and what that compression does emotionally, (3) what the couplet then does with the argument these three metaphors have built. Use at least one noun clause (from Grammar 3.2) in your spoken analysis.

Facilitator Note

The key interpretive point: the three metaphors escalate by compressing the time frame. Autumn = a season; twilight = an hour; dying embers = minutes. Students who arrange three equivalently sized zones have not understood that the escalation is the argument. Encourage spatial shrinkage: Autumn large, Twilight smaller, Embers smallest. The walk-through structure of the spatial recording is important — the student should physically move from zone to zone in the recording so the spatial progression is felt. Noun clause in the recording: listen for “What the poem argues is…” or “What the compression achieves is…”
