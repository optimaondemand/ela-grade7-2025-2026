QW 3.6: Analyzing the Volta

5 points — pass/fail — submit as text entry

The Task

Write a paragraph of 5–7 sentences analyzing the volta in either Sonnet 18 or Sonnet 73. Your paragraph must include all three labeled elements below.

Required elements

[NC] One sentence using a noun clause as the subject or direct object (from Grammar 3.2). The noun clause must contain an interpretive claim about the poem.

[VOLT] One sentence that identifies precisely where the volta occurs, quotes the key word or phrase that signals the turn, and explains what the argument shifts to after the turn.

[MET] One sentence analyzing the extended metaphor or central image of the poem and explaining what it contributes to the argument beyond literal description.

Example Paragraph (on Sonnet 18)

What Sonnet 18 ultimately argues is not that the beloved is beautiful, but that the poem itself will preserve that beauty after the beloved is gone [NC]. The twelve lines that precede the volta establish the comparison between the beloved and summer and then systematically dismantle it: summer is too brief, too rough, too variable. The volta arrives in line 13 with the word “So long” — the argument pivots from what nature cannot preserve to what verse can, shifting the poem’s subject from the beloved to the poem’s own power [VOLT]. The extended metaphor of summer, which the poem builds up only to tear down, is itself the argument: by proving that summer is inferior to the beloved, and then that the poem is superior to both, Shakespeare constructs a three-tiered hierarchy in which art sits highest [MET]. The couplet’s final claim — “this gives life to thee” — is therefore not a compliment to the beloved but a statement of confidence in the survival of verse.

Submission

Type your paragraph into the Canvas text entry box. Label each required element in bold brackets. Full credit (5 pts): All three elements present, correctly used, and the analysis explains argument and effect.
