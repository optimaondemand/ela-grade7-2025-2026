**Q1.** The Shakespearean sonnet has a specific structural shape. Which of the following correctly describes it?

A) Eight lines (octave) followed by six lines (sestet), with the turn between them.
B) Three quatrains (ABAB CDCD EFEF) followed by a rhyming couplet (GG), with the argumentative turn usually arriving at or near the couplet.
C) Fourteen lines of free verse with no required rhyme scheme.
D) Two stanzas of seven lines each, with the turn in the middle of the poem.

**Answer: B**

---

**Q2.** In Sonnet 18, Shakespeare spends twelve lines arguing that the beloved surpasses summer. What does the final couplet add that the previous twelve lines do not say?

A) The couplet explains that the comparison to summer was a mistake.
B) The couplet shifts the poem's claim: the beloved will be immortalized not by their own qualities but by the poem itself — "this" (the verse) will give "thee" life. The true subject of the argument turns out to be poetry's power, not the beloved's beauty.
C) The couplet introduces a new comparison between the beloved and winter.
D) The couplet softens the poem's argument by admitting the speaker may be wrong.

**Answer: B**

**Q3.** Which sentence uses a noun clause correctly as the subject of the sentence?

A) That the poem will outlast the beloved, which Shakespeare argues in the couplet.
B) What Shakespeare argues in the couplet is that art defeats time more reliably than physical beauty can.
C) Shakespeare argues that the poem will outlast the beloved, that this is the couplet's claim.
D) The poem's argument that art defeats time, what the couplet reveals.

**Answer: B**

**Q4.** In Sonnet 73, the three extended metaphors move from autumn to twilight to dying embers. What does the narrowing scale of each metaphor accomplish in the poem's argument?

A) Each metaphor introduces a different speaker, so the poem gains multiple perspectives.
B) The three metaphors are essentially equivalent — they all mean the same thing and could appear in any order.
C) The scale compresses deliberately: autumn = a season, twilight = an hour, dying embers = minutes. By the time the couplet arrives, the reader feels the speaker's remaining time as very short indeed. The escalation is the argument, not just decoration.
D) The narrowing scale shows that the speaker is becoming less poetic with age.

**Answer: C**

**Q5.** What is a volta?

A) A volta is the Italian word for "stanza" — it refers to any four-line unit in a sonnet.
B) A volta is the argumentative turn in a sonnet — the moment when the poem's direction, reasoning, or tone shifts. It is usually marked by a conjunction like "But" or "Yet" and signals that the poem has arrived at a new stage of its argument.
C) A volta is the rhyme scheme of an Italian (Petrarchan) sonnet.
D) A volta is the final couplet of a Shakespearean sonnet, always the last two lines.

**Answer: B**

**Q6.** The line "So long as men can breathe, or eyes can see" appears in the couplet of Sonnet 18. What grammatical structure does this line use, and what does it do in the argument?

A) It is a simple declarative sentence that states a fact about human biology.
B) It is a conditional clause ("so long as...") that sets up the duration of the poem's guarantee — as long as anyone reads, the beloved lives. The structure makes the poem's claim feel as permanent as human existence itself.
C) It is an example of alliteration (the repeated "s" sounds).
D) It is a periodic sentence in which the main clause is withheld until the final word.

**Answer: B**

**Q7.** A student writes: "Shakespeare's Sonnet 18 is a love poem because he says his beloved is beautiful." What is the problem with this reading, and what is a more complete one?

A) There is no problem — the student has correctly identified the poem's subject.
B) The reading is incomplete: while the poem uses love as its occasion, its real argument is about the power of art. Shakespeare is not merely praising the beloved — he is arguing that the poem will defeat time in a way that both summer and the beloved cannot. Reducing the poem to "he says she is beautiful" misses the couplet's shift.
C) The student is wrong because Sonnet 18 is about autumn, not summer.
D) The student is wrong because Shakespeare does not use the word "beautiful" in the poem.

**Answer: B**

**Q8.** What is an extended metaphor?

A) A metaphor that appears only once in a poem but is very elaborate.
B) A metaphor that runs through a substantial portion of a poem, requiring the reader to track a comparison across multiple lines or stanzas. In Sonnet 73, the three quatrains each develop a separate extended metaphor (autumn, twilight, dying fire) for the same subject.
C) A metaphor that compares two things that are not usually compared.
D) A metaphor that uses simile ("like" or "as") rather than direct comparison.

**Answer: B**

---

**Q9.** Sonnet 73 ends: "This thou perceiv'st, which makes thy love more strong, / To love that well which thou must leave ere long." What argument does the couplet make using the three preceding metaphors?

A) The couplet argues that the beloved should leave before the speaker dies.
B) The couplet argues that perceiving the speaker's decline — as the three metaphors have established — makes love stronger, not weaker: knowing that something will be lost intensifies rather than diminishes love for it. The couplet is addressed to the beloved as a conclusion drawn from the evidence the metaphors have built.
C) The couplet argues that the speaker should leave before they become too old.
D) The couplet argues that love is strongest when it is unrequited.

**Answer: B**

---

**Q10.** Which of the following best explains why the sonnet form requires a complete argument rather than simply a description or feeling?

A) The sonnet form requires rhyme, and rhyme forces poets to change their subjects.
B) The sonnet's fixed structure — developing through quatrains and resolving (or turning) in the couplet or sestet — creates an expectation of argumentative movement. A sonnet that describes a scene without arriving at a claim or a turn has failed the form. The volta is the structural proof that the poem is thinking, not just feeling.
C) Sonnets are fourteen lines long, and fourteen lines is always enough for a complete argument.
D) Shakespeare required all English sonnets to make arguments, which became the tradition.

**Answer: B**
