Anaphora and Rhetorical Figures

Repetition with purpose — the rhetorical figures that build momentum

Repetition as a Rhetorical Tool

In grammar lessons, we are usually taught that repetition is a weakness — vary your word choice, avoid repeating the same word. In rhetoric and poetry, repetition is often a strength. The key is purposeful repetition: using the same word or structure not out of laziness but because the repetition does something a varied synonym cannot do. The three major figures of deliberate repetition are anaphora, epistrophe, and antithesis.

Figure Definition Example
Anaphora Repetition at the beginning of consecutive lines/clauses “Does it dry up / like a raisin in the sun? / Or does it fester like a sore— / Or does it crust and sugar over…” — Hughes
Epistrophe Repetition at the end of consecutive lines/clauses “And miles to go before I sleep, / And miles to go before I sleep.” — Frost
Antithesis Placing contrasting ideas in parallel grammatical form “The woods are lovely, dark and deep, / But I have promises to keep.” — the loveliness of the woods contrasted with the duty of promises, both in parallel clauses

What these figures do in analytical writing

These figures are not only for poetry — they appear in speeches, essays, and arguments. In analysis, you can use them intentionally:

Anaphora: Hughes’s similes accumulate by repetition: does it dry up, does it fester, does it crust, does it sag— and finally, does it explode.

Epistrophe: The repeated “before I sleep” gives Frost’s couplet its incantatory, almost hypnotic quality.

Antithesis: The poem sets loveliness against obligation, rest against responsibility — and the tension between them is the poem.

Exercises

Exercise 1 — Identify the figure

Name the rhetorical figure (anaphora, epistrophe, or antithesis) in each example and explain in one sentence what it contributes.

  1. “Because I could not stop for Death — / He kindly stopped for me.” (Dickinson)
  2. “We shall fight on the beaches, we shall fight on the landing grounds, we shall fight in the fields and in the streets.” (Churchill)
  3. “Not that I loved Caesar less, but that I loved Rome more.” (Shakespeare, Julius Caesar)

Answers: (1) Antithesis — the speaker’s inability to stop contrasted with Death’s kindness in stopping. (2) Anaphora — “we shall fight” repeated to build momentum toward resistance. (3) Antithesis — “loved Caesar less” vs. “loved Rome more” in parallel form.

Exercise 2 — Write your own

Write one sentence of literary analysis about any of the Week 4 poems that uses anaphora (repeated parallel phrases, not just a list). Then write a second sentence using antithesis (two contrasting ideas in parallel grammatical form). Label each in brackets.
