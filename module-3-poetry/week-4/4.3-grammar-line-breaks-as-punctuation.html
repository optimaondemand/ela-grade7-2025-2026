Line Breaks as Punctuation

Where the line ends is a choice — and it means something

The Line Break as a Decision

In prose, the line ends when it reaches the right margin. In poetry, the line ends when the poet decides it should end. This is not a small difference. Every line break in a poem is a choice, and that choice is as meaningful as any word in the line. The end of a line creates a pause, an emphasis, an expectation, or a surprise, depending on where the break falls relative to the grammar of the sentence.

Two key terms

End-stopped line

A line that ends at a natural grammatical pause (comma, period, semicolon, or the end of a phrase). The reader pauses at the end of the line because the grammar pauses too.

The woods are lovely, dark and deep, ← comma here = end-stopped
But I have promises to keep, ← end-stopped again

Enjambment

A line that ends in the middle of a grammatical unit, forcing the reader to continue to the next line to complete the thought. The word comes from the French enjamber, “to straddle.” Enjambment creates momentum, surprise, or tension — the end of the line sets up an expectation that the next line may or may not fulfill.

Hughes: “Does it dry up ← enjambment; “dry up” is a verb phrase that needs its simile
like a raisin in the sun?” ← the completion arrives in the next line

What enjambment and end-stops do

End-stopped lines create a sense of completeness, finality, or deliberation. Each line feels like a unit. Frost uses end-stops throughout “Stopping by Woods” because the poem is about a pause — the formal stillness of the end-stopped lines enacts the stillness of the scene.

Enjambment creates momentum, restlessness, or a gap between what the end of a line promises and what the beginning of the next line delivers. Hughes uses enjambment in “A Dream Deferred” to keep the list driving forward — each incomplete line pushes toward the next simile and, ultimately, the final isolated question.

Exercises

Exercise 1 — Label end-stopped and enjambed lines

Read the following stanza from “A Dream Deferred” and label each line as either end-stopped (E) or enjambed (ENJ). Then write one sentence explaining what the mixture of end-stops and enjambment contributes to the stanza’s effect.

Does it dry up
like a raisin in the sun?
Or fester like a sore—
And then run?

Answers: Line 1 (ENJ — “dry up” is incomplete without the simile); Line 2 (E — ends with a complete question); Line 3 (ENJ — the dash is not a full stop, and “run” completes the thought); Line 4 (E — complete verb phrase). Effect: the enjambment keeps the list driving forward; end-stops create brief moments of landing before the next question launches.

Exercise 2 — Write using both techniques

Write a four-line poem (does not need to rhyme) about anything. Use at least two enjambed lines and at least one end-stopped line. Then write one sentence explaining why you placed the line breaks where you did.
