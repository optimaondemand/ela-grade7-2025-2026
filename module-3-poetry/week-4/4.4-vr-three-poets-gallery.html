7th Grade ELA — Module 3 — VR Assignment
Poetry — Week 4 — VR 4.4

VR 4.4: Three Poets, One Theme

Build a comparative gallery of three poems on the theme of time or constraint

What You’re Doing

All three of the Week 4 poems deal with time, constraint, or deferred possibility. In this session you will create a three-station gallery — one station per poem — that represents each poem’s central image. A Whiteboard IFX at each station names the poem and states in one sentence what it says about the shared theme. Then you will walk from station to station in a spatial recording and explain what comparing the three poems reveals that reading each one alone would not.

What You’ll Need
  • Device: VR headset
  • Session ID: ELA7-M3-W4 — Persistent Session
  • Location: Art Gallery or Museum Location
What to Do
1

Build Station 1: Hughes — “A Dream Deferred”

Place 2–3 IFX representing the poem’s central image (consider: a raisin, something festering or drying, a compressed or boxed-in space). Whiteboard IFX: poem title, one sentence stating what the poem says about deferred possibility, and the key line (“Or does it explode?”). Label the station STATION 1: Hughes.

2

Build Station 2: Frost — “The Road Not Taken”

Place 2–3 IFX representing the road-choice image (two path IFX diverging, or a single figure at a fork). Whiteboard IFX: poem title, one sentence on what the poem actually argues (not the misreading), and the key lines. Label STATION 2: Frost.

3

Build Station 3: Dickinson — “Because I Could Not Stop for Death”

Place 2–3 IFX representing the carriage journey (carriage IFX, two figures, a fading horizon or a small house/grave). Whiteboard IFX: poem title, one sentence on what the carriage ride represents, and the key lines from the final stanza. Label STATION 3: Dickinson.

4

Record the comparative walk-through (60–90 seconds)

Walk from Station 1 to Station 3 in the spatial recording. At each station, state what the poem says about time or constraint in one sentence. Then, from Station 3, explain: what does comparing these three poems reveal that reading any one of them alone would not? Use at least one anaphora structure (from Grammar 4.2) in your spoken analysis — “Dickinson argues… Frost argues… Hughes argues…” is an acceptable form.

5

Submit the recording

Paste the spatial recording link into the Canvas text box. Name it YourLastName_M3_4.4.

Submission

Paste spatial recording link. Estimated time: 25–30 minutes in headset.

Facilitator Note

The key analytical move is the final comparative question: “What does comparing these three poems reveal?” Students who answer this question substantively — something like “all three poets agree that time is an adversary, but only Hughes explicitly connects that adversary to political and social systems” — have done strong analytical work. Students who say only “they are all different” need a follow-up question. The anaphora grammar integration: listen for parallel structure (“Dickinson argues… Frost argues… Hughes argues…”) in the recording. Watch for Frost misreadings in the Whiteboard IFX — students who describe Frost’s poem as about celebrating individuality should be redirected to the “really about the same” admission.
