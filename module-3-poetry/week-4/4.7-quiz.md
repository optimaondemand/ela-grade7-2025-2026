**Q1.** The most famous misreading of Frost's "The Road Not Taken" is that it celebrates the speaker's brave individualism in choosing "the road less traveled." What does the poem actually say that complicates this reading?

A) The poem actually says the speaker chose the road that more people had traveled, which is the irony.
B) In the second stanza, the speaker admits that the two roads "had worn them really about the same" — they were not significantly different. The famous final stanza ("and that has made all the difference") is the speaker projecting forward to how they will describe this choice in the future, not a statement of what was true in the moment. The poem is about the stories we tell ourselves about our choices after the fact.
C) The poem's misreading is actually correct — Frost intended it as a celebration of individuality.
D) The speaker chose the wrong road and is expressing regret, which readers mistake for pride.

**Answer: B**

**Q2.** Hughes ends "A Dream Deferred" with "Or does it explode?" on its own line, in italics. Which of the following best explains the formal choice?

A) Hughes ran out of room and had to finish the list on a new line.
B) The isolation and italics give the final question the force of a conclusion — after a series of similes about quiet deterioration (drying, festering, crusting, sagging), the poem suddenly raises the possibility of something violent. The line break and emphasis are not decorative; they perform the explosion the line names.
C) Questions must always be placed on their own lines in poetry.
D) The italics indicate that the question is being spoken by a different speaker.

**Answer: B**

**Q3.** What is anaphora?

A) The repetition of a word or phrase at the end of consecutive lines or clauses (epistrophe is actually this).
B) The repetition of the same word or phrase at the beginning of consecutive lines, clauses, or sentences, creating momentum and cumulative emphasis. In "A Dream Deferred," "Does it" (implied) repeats at the start of each simile.
C) A single word repeated three or more times in the same line.
D) The use of parallel structure in a sentence without any repetition of specific words.

**Answer: B**

**Q4.** Dickinson's dashes in "Because I Could Not Stop for Death" are not punctuation errors. What do they do in the poem?

A) They indicate where words were lost from the original manuscript.
B) They create sudden pauses that break the poem's otherwise gentle rhythm, giving each phrase a weight and finality that a comma or semicolon would not. The dash is Dickinson's signature instrument for making the unsaid visible — the pause holds what the words cannot.
C) They are used to mark the ends of lines, since Dickinson did not use conventional line breaks.
D) They indicate that the lines should be read faster, as a breath is released.

**Answer: B**

**Q5.** What is enjambment?

A) A line of poetry that ends at a natural grammatical pause (period, comma, semicolon).
B) A line of poetry that ends in the middle of a grammatical unit, forcing the reader to continue to the next line to complete the thought. Enjambment creates momentum, restlessness, or a gap between what the end of a line implies and what the next line delivers.
C) The repetition of a word at the end of consecutive lines.
D) The use of the same vowel sound across multiple words in a line.

**Answer: B**

**Q6.** Dickinson's speaker in "Because I Could Not Stop for Death" narrates her own death from a retrospective perspective — she is already dead when the poem begins. What does this narrative position allow Dickinson to do that a living speaker could not?

A) It allows Dickinson to describe what happens to the body after death scientifically.
B) The retrospective position allows the speaker to describe the journey toward death with an eerie calm — she has already arrived at the destination and is reporting back. This calm is more disturbing than grief would be, because it implies that death was not a crisis but a quiet completion. The speaker's serenity is the poem's most unsettling feature.
C) It allows Dickinson to avoid the subject of death by describing it as a pleasant carriage ride.
D) It means the speaker cannot know what she is experiencing, so the poem is unreliable.

**Answer: B**

**Q7.** Which sentence uses antithesis correctly?

A) Hughes argues that dreams are deferred, and Frost argues that roads diverge in woods.
B) While Frost's speaker faces a choice between two paths that are "really about the same," Hughes's speaker faces a denial that is not a choice at all — the difference is between freedom constrained by indecision and possibility foreclosed by injustice.
C) Frost is a poet of rural New England; Hughes is a poet of Harlem.
D) Both Frost and Hughes write about things that are difficult, which makes their poems similar in important ways.

**Answer: B**

**Q8.** In "A Dream Deferred," Hughes uses a series of similes before arriving at the final question. How does the order of the similes contribute to the poem's argument?

A) The similes are in random order — any arrangement would have the same effect.
B) The similes escalate in violence: drying and festering are passive and slow; sagging is heavy and exhausted; the final simile (explosion) is sudden and destructive. The ordering is itself an argument — a deferred dream does not quietly diminish; it builds toward something the poem refuses to name as metaphor.
C) The similes are arranged from most to least violent, showing that a dream eventually calms down.
D) The similes alternate between positive and negative to show that deferral has mixed effects.

**Answer: B**

**Q9.** Hughes, Frost, and Dickinson all deal with time or constraint in their poems. What does comparing the three poets reveal that reading any one of them alone would not?

A) Reading all three shows that American poets always agree on the importance of nature.
B) Comparing the three reveals that "constraint" and "time" are not single experiences — they differ radically depending on the speaker's position. Frost's constraints are self-imposed and retrospective; Dickinson's are metaphysical and serene; Hughes's are political and urgent. The comparison shows that poetry is not universal in content even when it is universal in form.
C) All three poets ultimately argue the same thing, which is that we cannot escape death.
D) Comparing the three shows that the ballad form is superior to free verse.

**Answer: B**

**Q10.** A student argues that "A Dream Deferred" is only about personal disappointment — a person who wanted something and didn't get it. What is the strongest counter-argument?

A) The student is correct — the poem is about personal disappointment, not anything larger.
B) The counter-argument is historical and contextual: Hughes wrote in 1951, during a period when Black Americans were systematically denied access to housing, education, employment, and voting rights. A dream "deferred" by a society — not merely by bad luck or personal failure — is a different experience. The poem's final question ("Or does it explode?") acquires a political meaning in this context that makes it impossible to read as purely personal.
C) The poem is not about disappointment at all — it is about a sleeping dream that has not yet woken up.
D) The student is wrong because Hughes never wrote about personal subjects, only political ones.

**Answer: B**
