Poetry — Week 5 — Lesson 5.1

Free Verse: Form as Argument

Why choosing no fixed form is itself a choice — and what Whitman built with it

This Lesson

You will read two poems — one by Walt Whitman and one more recent — and articulate what free verse can do that formal poetry cannot, and what it sacrifices. You will also begin thinking about the module project: an original poem of your own.

Opening Question

Think of a rule you follow — in writing, in sport, in music, anywhere. Now imagine removing that rule entirely. What would you be able to do that you cannot do with the rule in place? What would you lose? Write two sentences: one about what you gain without the rule, one about what the rule was actually giving you.

Lesson

Free verse is poetry that does not follow a fixed metrical pattern or rhyme scheme. This does not mean it is formless. It means that the poet has taken responsibility for every formal decision individually rather than inheriting a system. In a sonnet, the form tells you where the line ends, when to rhyme, where the volta goes. In free verse, you decide all of this. The freedom is real — but it is also a burden. A weak free verse poem has no formal structure to lean on and nothing to blame its failures on. A strong free verse poem has invented its own form from the inside.

Walt Whitman invented American free verse in Leaves of Grass (1855), a book he revised, expanded, and republished throughout his life. His long, rolling lines borrow from the cadences of the King James Bible, from oratory, from the democratic ambition of including everything — every trade, every body, every landscape — in a single poem. The long line is not accidental. A Whitman line cannot be compressed into iambic pentameter without losing something essential: its sense of abundance, its refusal to leave anything out. When he writes in “Song of Myself” “Do I contradict myself? / Very well then I contradict myself, / (I am large, I contain multitudes.)” — the short lines of the parenthetical afterthought are formally different from the expansive lines that surround them. The shift in line length is the shift in tone: from open declaration to quiet, private acknowledgment.

The question the module has been building toward: what can a poem do that prose cannot? Free verse’s answer is this: it can use the white space around the words, the length of the line, the placement of the line break, and the arrangement of the poem on the page as expressive instruments. A prose sentence ends when the thought ends. A poem line ends when the poet decides — and that decision is itself a form of meaning.

Vocabulary — Week 5, Word 1

FREE VERSE

Poetry that does not follow a fixed metrical pattern or regular rhyme scheme. Free verse is not unformed — it creates its own form through line breaks, white space, repetition, and other devices. The absence of a received form means every formal choice is the poet’s own responsibility.

Copia exercise: Whitman writes: “I am large, I contain multitudes.” Rewrite this as a four-line free verse poem that expands the claim — using line breaks, not prose punctuation, to control the pacing. You are not summarizing Whitman; you are writing something new that the line starts for you.

Vocabulary — Week 5, Word 2

CATALOG

A poetic device in which a long list of people, places, things, or ideas is accumulated in lines or stanzas. Whitman uses catalogs extensively as a democratic gesture: by listing farm workers alongside scholars, he argues that all are equally worthy of the poem’s attention. A catalog is not just a list — its length and variety are themselves the point.

Copia exercise: Write a five-item catalog about your school, community, or daily life. Each item should be specific and concrete (not “my friends” but “Marcus with his headphones in at lunch”). The catalog should feel like it is including things that do not usually appear in poems.

Comprehension Check

1. Whitman writes: “Do I contradict myself? Very well then I contradict myself, (I am large, I contain multitudes.)” The parenthetical “(I am large, I contain multitudes)” is in shorter, more compressed lines than the surrounding poem. What does this formal shift do?

A) The shorter lines indicate that the speaker has lost confidence and is speaking more quietly.

B) The compression signals a private, almost whispered aside — a moment of self-disclosure that is more intimate than the large declarations surrounding it. The parentheses and shorter lines enact the speaker stepping momentarily out of their public voice.

C) Whitman made the lines shorter to fit the page on the original printing.

D) The shorter lines are a mistake — Whitman later revised them to be longer.

Answer: B

2. In Collins’s “Introduction to Poetry,” what does the speaker argue that readers do wrong, and what does the poem suggest they should do instead?

A) Collins argues that readers read too slowly; they should read poems quickly to feel the rhythm.

B) Collins argues that readers try to force poems to confess a hidden meaning — to "torture" the poem for its answer — rather than inhabiting the poem's imagery and sound on its own terms. The poem suggests that reading a poem should feel like moving through it, not interrogating it.

C) Collins argues that readers do not memorize enough poems.

D) Collins argues that free verse is superior to formal poetry because it does not restrict the reader.

Answer: B
