The Grammar of the Page: White Space, Stanza, and Form

How what is not on the page is as expressive as what is

The Expressive Page

Prose fills the page from left margin to right margin. Poetry does not. The white space around a poem’s lines is not empty — it is expressive. A stanza break creates a pause; a longer pause than a line break but shorter than a section break. An isolated line (a single line between two groups of lines) creates emphasis by surrounding that line with silence. A very long line suggests abundance, sweep, or overflow. A very short line commands attention precisely because the surrounding page is so quiet.

Key terms for the grammar of the page

Stanza

A grouped unit of lines, separated from the next group by white space. Stanzas are to poems what paragraphs are to prose: they organize the poem into units of thought. A stanza break signals that one phase of the poem’s argument or development has ended and another is beginning.

Isolated line

A single line that stands apart from surrounding stanzas. Hughes’s “Or does it explode?” is an isolated line: it is the only line in the final “stanza,” which means it is surrounded by silence. The silence is emphasis.

Line length as meaning

A long line suggests expanse, inclusion, overflow, or the inability to stop. Whitman’s long lines perform democratic inclusion — they refuse to leave anything out. A short line suggests arrest, emphasis, compression, or silence. Dickinson’s dashes create pauses within lines that are already short; the combination creates maximum compression.

For your own poem (the module project)

When you write your own poem for the module project, think about these questions before you decide where the lines and stanzas break:

  • Where do I want the reader to pause? (line break or stanza break)
  • Where do I want the reader to move quickly? (enjambment, long line)
  • What is the most important word or phrase? (put it at the end of a line, or on its own line)
  • Is the white space on the page working for me or against me?
Exercise

Rewrite the paragraph as a poem

The following paragraph says something that could be a poem. Rewrite it as a free verse poem by deciding where the lines break, where the stanzas break, and what (if anything) should stand alone. You are not adding words or changing the content — you are deciding how the white space on the page makes meaning. Write one sentence afterward explaining your most important formal choice.

In my grandmother’s kitchen there was a window that always fogged in winter so that the world outside looked like a painting of itself. She would wipe a circle in the glass with her palm, and I would press my face against the cold and look through. The rest of the world went on without knowing I was watching.

There is no single correct version. The question is whether your line breaks serve the content and create the effects you intend. Your explanatory sentence should make an interpretive choice, not just describe what you did: not “I put a line break after ‘painting of itself’” but “I isolated ‘The rest of the world went on’ as a single line because it carries the weight of the whole poem and should be surrounded by silence.”
