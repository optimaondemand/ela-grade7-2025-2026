Module 3 Grammar Review: The Full Toolkit

All seven structures in one place — and how they work together in your poem’s analysis

The Module 3 Grammar Structures
Week Structure What it does
1 Appositive Renames a noun to add information in the same sentence
1 Relative clause Adds information to a noun via who/which/that/whose
2 Colon Introduces a list, explanation, or quotation after a complete clause
2 Absolute phrase Noun + participle that modifies the whole sentence
3 Noun clause Dependent clause functioning as subject, object, or complement
4 Anaphora / rhetorical figures Purposeful repetition that builds momentum or contrast
4–5 Line break / enjambment / end-stop Spatial and temporal control of pace and emphasis in poetry

A model analysis paragraph using all seven structures

Walt Whitman, [APPOSITIVE] the poet who reinvented American verse by refusing its inherited formal constraints, builds “Song of Myself” out of catalogs and long lines [RELATIVE CLAUSE: who reinvented...] that refuse to leave any trade, body, or landscape outside the poem’s embrace. [NOUN CLAUSE] What Whitman argues through this formal choice is that democracy is not a political ideal but a poetic practice: every voice deserves a line. [COLON] The poem makes three kinds of democratic claim: inclusion of all subjects, equality of the long line for all speakers, and refusal of hierarchy between high and low diction. [ABSOLUTE PHRASE] His lines expanding across the page like a man throwing his arms wide, Whitman refuses the compression and order of formal verse because compression would require him to leave something out — and nothing, the poem argues, should be left out. The parenthetical lines [ANAPHORA/CONTRAST] are shorter, quieter, more intimate: one register for the public declaration, another for the private acknowledgment. Whitman’s most famous enjambment — “I am large, I contain” followed by the line break before “multitudes” — suspends the reader for a beat, then delivers the most expansive word in the poem as the first word of the new line. [ENJAMBMENT analyzed]

Preparation for the Module Project

Your module project requires you to write an original poem and a craft analysis. The analysis is where you will use these seven structures. Before you write your poem, answer these questions in writing (these are preparation notes, not a graded submission):

  1. What is the one thing you want your poem to say or do? (Not “I want to write about my dog” — what do you want a reader to feel or understand?)
  2. Will you use a formal structure (rhyme, meter, fixed stanza length) or free verse? Why?
  3. What is the central image your poem will use?
  4. Which of the seven Module 3 grammar structures will appear in your craft analysis? (You need at least three.)
