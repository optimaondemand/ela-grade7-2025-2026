# Week 5 Discussion — Closing: What Can a Poem Do?
# Canvas Discussion — Threaded, Require Initial Post First
# 15 points — Rubric: 15/8/0
# Settings: published, graded discussion

---

## Discussion Prompt

The module opened with the question: **What can a poem do that prose cannot?**

You have now read poems from five centuries: an anonymous Scottish ballad, Shakespeare's sonnets, Emily Dickinson, Robert Frost, Langston Hughes, and Walt Whitman. Your VR compositions have made images three-dimensional. Your quickwrites have practiced the grammar of analysis. Your module project poem asks you to try to do whatever it is you think poems can do.

**Now answer the question.**

Your initial post must include:

1. **A direct answer to the question** in one or two sentences. What *specifically* can a poem do that prose cannot? (Not "express emotions" — that is too vague. Be specific about form, sound, image, white space, or another element from the module.)

2. **One piece of evidence from the module readings** — quote a line or describe a formal choice from one of the poems — that supports your answer. Explain why this example proves your claim.

3. **One sentence about your own poem** — what you tried to do in it that you could not have done in a prose paragraph.

**Then reply to one classmate** whose answer differs from yours. Your reply must:
- Name their claim specifically (do not just say "I agree" or "interesting point")
- Either extend their argument with a second example from the module, or respectfully identify where you think the claim is incomplete

---

## Rubric

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **15** | Initial post directly answers the essential question with a specific formal claim; evidence is quoted or precisely described and genuinely proves the claim; connection to the student's own poem is specific; reply engages with the classmate's argument, not just their conclusion |
| **8** | Initial post answers the question but with a vague claim ("poems use imagery") or evidence that is described but not explained; reply is substantive but brief |
| **0** | Missing initial post, or post does not attempt to answer the essential question |

## Teacher Note

This discussion is the culminating synthesis of Module 3. Students are being asked to make an argument about the nature of poetry based on five weeks of reading, writing, and spatial composition. The common failure mode is abstraction: students who say "poems can make you feel emotions" or "poems express ideas in a unique way" without identifying a specific formal mechanism. Push students toward form: sound, line break, white space, meter, image, refrain, volta, anaphora. The claim should be falsifiable: "a poem can use white space to make silence visible in a way prose cannot" is a specific, arguable claim. "A poem can express feelings" is not.

The connection to the student's own poem is the module's synthesis move: now that you have tried to write one, what do you understand about the form that you did not before? Students who connect their creative experience to their critical argument are doing the highest-level work in the module.

Replies should be substantive. A reply that says "I agree, poems do use imagery well" is not engaging with the classmate's argument. Ask students: "What is their specific claim? What is your response to that specific claim?"
