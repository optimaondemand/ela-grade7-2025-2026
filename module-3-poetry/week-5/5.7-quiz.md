**Q1.** The module opened with the question: "What can a poem do that prose cannot?" Which of the following answers is most specific and best supported by the Module 3 readings?

A) A poem can express emotions more intensely than prose.
B) A poem can use the line break, white space, and sound patterns to make formal choices that are themselves expressive — the arrangement of words on the page, where the line ends, and the texture of consonants and vowels are instruments prose almost never uses as deliberately.
C) A poem is shorter than prose, so it is easier to understand.
D) A poem always rhymes, which makes it more memorable than prose.

**Answer: B**

---

**Q2.** Walt Whitman's long lines are not simply a matter of preference — they are a formal argument. What does the long line argue?

A) Whitman's long lines argue that brevity is overrated in poetry.
B) The long line enacts democratic inclusion: by refusing to cut the line before it has contained everything it needs to contain, Whitman argues that poetry should not select and compress, but include. The expansiveness of the line is the expansiveness of the poem's vision.
C) Whitman uses long lines because he was paid by the word.
D) The long line makes the poem feel more like prose, which Whitman preferred to formal poetry.

**Answer: B**

---

**Q3.** What is a catalog in the Whitman tradition, and what does it do?

A) A catalog is an alphabetical index of topics in a long poem.
B) A catalog is a long, accumulated list of people, places, trades, or things — used as a democratic gesture to argue that everything in the list is equally worthy of the poem's attention. The length and variety of the catalog are themselves the point: the poem refuses to leave anything out.
C) A catalog is a type of meter in which each syllable is equally stressed.
D) A catalog is the title page of a poetry collection.

**Answer: B**

**Q4.** Billy Collins's "Introduction to Poetry" argues that readers do something wrong when they approach a poem. What does he argue they should NOT do?

A) Readers should not memorize poems because memorization reduces them to performance.
B) Readers should not try to "torture" poems into confessing a hidden meaning. Collins argues that readers should inhabit the poem's imagery and sound — move through it rather than interrogate it. The poem itself demonstrates this by being a poem about how to read poetry, not an essay.
C) Readers should not read poems silently — they must always be read aloud.
D) Readers should not enjoy poems because enjoyment interferes with analysis.

**Answer: B**

---

**Q5.** What is the difference between an end-stopped line and an enjambed line?

A) An end-stopped line is shorter; an enjambed line is longer.
B) An end-stopped line ends at a natural grammatical pause, giving the reader a moment of rest; an enjambed line ends in the middle of a grammatical unit, forcing the reader to continue to the next line to complete the thought. Enjambment creates momentum or suspense; end-stops create finality or deliberation.
C) An end-stopped line rhymes with the following line; an enjambed line does not.
D) Enjambment is a technique found only in formal poetry; free verse uses only end-stopped lines.

**Answer: B**

**Q6.** Which of the following best explains what free verse is and what it is not?

A) Free verse is poetry without any rules — the poet does whatever feels right.
B) Free verse is poetry that does not follow a fixed metrical pattern or regular rhyme scheme. It is not formless — it creates its own form through line breaks, white space, sound, and repetition. The absence of a received form means every formal choice is the poet's own responsibility, which requires more deliberateness, not less.
C) Free verse is poetry that chooses not to use imagery or metaphor.
D) Free verse was invented in the 20th century and did not exist before Whitman.

**Answer: B**

**Q7.** Across the five weeks of Module 3, you have studied poems from the 14th century (the ballad) to the 20th century (Dickinson, Frost, Hughes, Collins). What does studying this range of poems reveal about the nature of poetry itself?

A) Studying the range shows that earlier poems are always more formal and later poems always more experimental.
B) The range reveals that the question "what can a poem do?" has been answered differently in every century and every context — ballads used meter and refrain for memory and oral transmission; Shakespeare used the sonnet's structure for argument; Dickinson used compression and dashes for psychological precision; Hughes used anaphora and escalation for political urgency; Whitman used the long line for democratic inclusion. Form is always in service of purpose.
C) The range shows that poetry has gotten progressively better over time.
D) The range shows that rhyme and meter are the only tools that have survived across all centuries of poetry.

**Answer: B**

**Q8.** You are writing your own poem for the module project. Which of the following best describes what a "craft analysis" should do?

A) A craft analysis summarizes the poem's content so that readers who did not read the poem can understand it.
B) A craft analysis explains the formal choices the poet made — where the lines break and why, what the central image is and what it does, how a specific grammatical structure shapes meaning — and argues that these choices serve the poem's purpose. The analysis does not describe the poem; it explains how the poem works.
C) A craft analysis lists the vocabulary words used in the poem.
D) A craft analysis is a biography of the poet explaining why they wrote the poem.

**Answer: B**

**Q9.** In Dickinson's "Because I Could Not Stop for Death," the speaker describes the carriage passing a school, fields of grain, and a house that looks like a swelling of the ground. What does the sequence of images reveal?

A) The sequence is random — Dickinson is describing the New England countryside she knew.
B) The sequence tracks the stages of human life from childhood (the school) through labor and maturity (the grain fields) to death and burial (the house that is a grave). The carriage is not moving through a landscape; it is moving through the speaker's entire life at once, compressed into a single journey. The sequence is the argument.
C) The sequence represents the four seasons of the year.
D) The images represent three different people the speaker loved who died before her.

**Answer: B**

**Q10.** A student argues: "The best poems have a clear, single message that the reader can state in one sentence." Using what you learned in Module 3, what is the strongest counter-argument to this claim?

A) There is no counter-argument — all great poems do have a single clear message.
B) The strongest counter-argument is that ambiguity, tension, and the poem's resistance to a single paraphrase are often what make a poem worth reading. Frost's poem resists the misreading of "the road less traveled" precisely because the poem holds two truths at once: the speaker believes their choice mattered, and the poem shows that the choice may not have been as decisive as they think. Dickinson's "could not see to see" cannot be reduced to one sentence without losing what makes it interesting. The best poems mean more than a paraphrase can hold — that is the point.
C) The counter-argument is that poems should not have messages at all — only feelings.
D) The counter-argument is that modern poems intentionally have no meaning and are therefore impossible to paraphrase.

**Answer: B**
