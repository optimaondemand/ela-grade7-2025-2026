7th Grade ELA — Module 4
Roll of Thunder, Hear My Cry — Module Project

Module 4 Project: Literary Analysis Essay

400–500 words — 100 points — Word document submitted to Canvas

The Assignment

Write a 400–500 word literary analysis essay arguing what a specific formal choice Mildred D. Taylor made in Roll of Thunder, Hear My Cry does for the novel’s argument. Not what the novel is about — how it works.

Your essay is not a book report. It is an argument about craft. Your thesis names the formal choice and states specifically what that choice does. Your body paragraphs use specific evidence from the novel to show the claim in action. Your conclusion returns to the essential question and states what you now understand that you didn’t at the module’s start.

Choose One Focus
Option A: The Child Narrator

Argue what Taylor achieves by using Cassie — a child who cannot fully understand what she experiences — as the novel’s narrator. Your thesis should name this choice and argue specifically what it does for the novel’s argument, not just what it is.

Option B: The Land as Symbol

Argue what the Logan family’s 400 acres symbolize and how Taylor uses the land’s recurring presence to carry the novel’s argument about holding on. Your thesis should name the land as a symbol and argue what it symbolizes and why that symbol matters to the novel’s argument.

Option C: Setting as Argument

Argue how Taylor uses the novel’s setting — Mississippi in 1933, the specific physical spaces, the geography of the county — to enforce the novel’s argument about power and resistance. Your thesis should name setting as a formal choice and argue what that choice does for the novel’s larger argument.

Required Essay Structure
1
Introduction (50–75 words): Open with the rhetorical question or observation that drives your argument. State your thesis in the last sentence of the introduction. Thesis = named formal choice + what it does for the novel’s argument.
2
Body Paragraph 1 (100–125 words): Present your strongest piece of evidence. Introduce it with an analytical setup sentence, provide or reference the evidence, and explain what it demonstrates about your thesis claim. Include at least one participial phrase [PART] and one adverbial clause [ADV].
3
Body Paragraph 2 (100–125 words): Present a second piece of evidence OR develop the first further with a related detail. This paragraph must include at least one compound-complex sentence [CC] and must acknowledge the most significant complication to your argument before countering it (concessive structure [CON]).
4
Conclusion (75–100 words): Return to the essential question — How do people hold on to what is theirs when the world tries to take it from them? — and state what you now understand about it that you didn’t fully understand before reading this novel. Do not summarize your essay. Add something new: what does Taylor’s formal choice reveal about this question that another novel might not?
Required Tags (label in your essay)
  • [THESIS] — Your thesis sentence in the introduction
  • [PART] — One participial phrase (any body paragraph)
  • [ADV] — One adverbial clause (any body paragraph)
  • [CC] — One compound-complex sentence (Body Paragraph 2)
  • [CON] — One concessive sentence (Body Paragraph 2)
Rubric
Criterion 25 pts — Strong 18 pts — Developing 10 pts — Beginning
Thesis and Argument Thesis names the formal choice and makes a specific claim about what it does. The essay pursues that claim across all paragraphs without drifting into plot summary. Thesis is present but names the choice without fully arguing what it does. Some drift toward summary in body paragraphs. No clear thesis, or thesis only names a topic rather than making a claim. Essay is primarily plot summary.
Use of Evidence Evidence is specific (named scenes, specific details, quoted phrases). Each piece of evidence is introduced and explained analytically — not dropped in and left to speak for itself. Evidence is present but general, or evidence is introduced without explanation of what it demonstrates. Little or no evidence from the novel, or evidence is used only as plot summary.
Grammar Structures All five required structures [THESIS], [PART], [ADV], [CC], [CON] are present, correctly formed, and labeled. Structures add analytical precision rather than feeling forced. Three or four required structures present and labeled. Minor errors in formation that do not obscure meaning. Fewer than three required structures present, or structures are present but incorrectly formed or not labeled.
Conclusion and Essential Question Conclusion returns to the essential question and adds something new — a genuine extension of the essay’s argument to the larger question. Does not merely restate the thesis. Conclusion mentions the essential question but primarily restates the thesis or summarizes the essay. No conclusion, or conclusion ignores the essential question entirely.
