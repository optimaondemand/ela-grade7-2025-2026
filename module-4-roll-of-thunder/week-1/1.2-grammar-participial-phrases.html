Participial Phrases

A verb form that acts as an adjective — and a key tool in analytical writing

What It Is

A participial phrase is a group of words built around a verb form (ending in -ing, -ed, or -en) that acts as an adjective — it modifies a noun. Unlike a verb, it describes rather than predicates. Unlike a plain adjective, it carries the sense of action or state.

Examples from Taylor-style prose:

Frightened by the noise, Little Man pressed against the wall.  (modifies: Little Man)
Knowing the night riders were near, Papa put out the lantern.  (modifies: Papa)
Stamped and labeled by white hands, the textbook had been used up before it ever reached Black children.  (modifies: the textbook)

When a participial phrase comes at the beginning of a sentence, place a comma after it. The noun being modified must immediately follow — or the phrase dangles (see below).

Participial Phrase vs. Absolute Phrase

In Module 3, you learned absolute phrases. These two structures look similar but work differently:

Structure Form What it modifies Example
Participial phrase verb form (-ing/-ed/-en) + extras A specific noun in the sentence Frightened by the gun, Cassie froze.
Absolute phrase noun + verb form + extras The whole sentence His voice steady, Papa faced the men.

The key test: Does the phrase have its own noun? If yes → absolute phrase. If no, it needs to borrow the subject from the main clause → participial phrase.

The Dangling Participle

A dangling participle occurs when the participial phrase’s implied subject does not match the grammatical subject of the sentence. The result is absurd.

✗ Dangling

Running down the road, the trees blurred past.

(The trees are not running.)

✓ Fixed

Running down the road, Cassie let the trees blur past.

(Cassie is running.)

Exercise 1: Identify the Participial Phrase

In each sentence, underline the participial phrase and circle the noun it modifies.

  1. Carrying the burned textbook back to the teacher, Little Man kept his chin up.
  2. Surrounded by cotton fields, the Logan house stood apart from its neighbors in ownership if not in appearance.
  3. Gripping the reins, Big Ma guided the wagon without looking at Cassie.
  4. Warned by her mother, Cassie kept her thoughts to herself on the walk home.
Exercise 2: Combine into One Sentence

Combine each pair of sentences into one sentence with an opening participial phrase. Make sure the phrase’s implied subject matches the sentence’s grammatical subject.

  1. Papa was working the railroad far from home. He sent money back every month to keep the mortgage paid.
  2. Mama was disturbed by what had happened to the textbooks. She wrote a letter of complaint to the county.
  3. Stacey was embarrassed by T.J.’s taunting. He refused to look at Cassie after school.
