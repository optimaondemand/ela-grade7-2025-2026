Opening Modifiers

Three structures, one position — and how to choose between them

The Big Picture

You now know three kinds of opening modifiers — phrases that go at the beginning of a sentence, followed by a comma, before the main clause. Each one front-loads a different kind of information. Here they are together:

Type Module What it tells us Example
Prepositional phrase Module 2 Where or when In the cotton fields, the work never stopped.
Absolute phrase Module 3 A bodily or emotional state (has its own noun) Her face hard with anger, Big Ma said nothing.
Participial phrase Module 4 An action or condition of the subject Reaching for the burned book, Little Man began to cry.
How to Choose

All three follow the same punctuation rule: opening modifier + comma + main clause. The choice depends on what you want to say first:

  • Use a prepositional phrase when location or time sets up the main action: On the road to Strawberry, Cassie saw the town for the first time.
  • Use an absolute phrase when a character’s body or expression reveals their inner state before the main action: His hands tight on the reins, Papa kept his voice calm.
  • Use a participial phrase when a character’s action directly leads to or accompanies the main clause: Knowing what the Grangers wanted, Papa refused to sell.
Exercise 1: Identify the Type

Label each opening modifier as PP (prepositional phrase), ABS (absolute phrase), or PART (participial phrase).

  1. Surrounded by darkness, the family waited for morning.
  2. Her voice barely above a whisper, Mama explained what the land meant to the family.
  3. In the middle of the night, they heard horses on the road.
  4. Staring at the boys, Cassie felt something she couldn’t name.
  5. His eyes fixed on the tree line, Papa said nothing.
  6. After the night riders passed, the house was very quiet.
Exercise 2: Move the Modifier to the Front

Rewrite each sentence so that the underlined information becomes an opening modifier (use the type indicated in brackets). Add a comma.

  1. Cassie was gripping her books tightly as she walked into the schoolroom. [PART]
  2. Big Ma was sitting with her back very straight while she listened to Mama. [ABS]
  3. They sat together at the edge of the property and talked about what to do next. [PP]
