7th Grade ELA — Module 4
Roll of Thunder, Hear My Cry — Week 2 — Lesson 2.1

Strawberry: Setting as Power

Chapters 4–7: how Taylor uses place to make her argument about injustice visible

Warm-Up

Think of a place where you have felt like you belonged — and a place where you have felt like you did not. What was the difference? Was it something visible and explicit, or something unspoken and enforced? Write 2–3 sentences before reading.

The Lesson

The Strawberry chapters are the pivot of the novel. Before them, Cassie knows her family faces injustice but hasn’t encountered it head-on herself. The trip to Strawberry changes that. In Mr. Barnett’s store, Cassie learns that the town has a social order enforced through space: who gets served first, who waits, who calls whom by what title, who steps aside on the sidewalk. When Cassie refuses to step aside for Lillian Jean Simms — refuses to accept the spatial grammar of the town — Mr. Simms physically places her in the gutter and tells her where she belongs. Big Ma watches and says nothing.

Taylor uses Strawberry not just as a location but as an argument. The town is organized to enforce an order, and every detail — who gets served first, who uses the front entrance, who must yield — is evidence for that argument. Cassie’s violation of the order is not rudeness; it is a refusal to accept the map the town is built on. And Big Ma’s silence in that moment is one of the most painful scenes in the novel. She does not validate the Simms’s claim, but she does not resist it either — and Cassie does not understand why. Taylor leaves it there, uncommented upon. The reader has to sit with it.

Alongside the Strawberry scene, the boycott begins. Mama organizes the community to stop shopping at the Wallace store, which is both a response to specific harm and a statement: we can refuse, we have somewhere else to go, our spending is power. The boycott costs the family. It also costs the Wallaces. That is the point.

Reading

Read Chapters 4–7 of Roll of Thunder, Hear My Cry.

As you read, mark: (1) every moment where the town’s social order is enforced through space or speech, (2) Big Ma’s silence in the Strawberry scene and your reaction to it, (3) what the boycott requires of the Logans and their neighbors.

Vocabulary

Setting

The time, place, and social conditions in which a story takes place — not just where, but the rules that govern that where. Setting in a serious novel is never merely backdrop. Taylor’s Strawberry is not just a town; it is a system of power made visible through space. The setting of a scene tells us who belongs and who is made to feel they don’t.

Copia Exercise

Example: “The setting of Mr. Barnett’s store reveals the social order of Strawberry: who is served first and who waits is not random, but enforced.”

Rewrite this sentence using the word setting, applying it to a different scene or location in Chapters 4–7.

Symbol

An object, place, or event that carries a meaning beyond its literal self. The Logan land is a symbol of dignity and ownership. Strawberry is a symbol of the system that denies both. A good symbol in a novel does not just stand for one thing — it gathers meaning from each scene it appears in.

Copia Exercise

Example: “The sidewalk in Strawberry functions as a symbol of who the system decides belongs in public space.”

Rewrite this sentence using the word symbol, applying it to a different object or place from the Strawberry chapters.

Comprehension Check

1. What happens in Mr. Barnett’s store, and what does it reveal about the social order of Strawberry?

  • A) Mr. Barnett runs out of supplies and apologizes to all customers equally.
  • B) Mr. Barnett ignores the Logans to serve white customers first, then reacts with hostility when Cassie speaks up — revealing that the store operates by a racial hierarchy that Cassie refuses to accept and that the adults in her family cannot openly challenge.
  • C) Big Ma argues with Mr. Barnett and demands equal service.
  • D) Stacey fights with another boy in the store and the family is asked to leave.

2. Why does Big Ma stay silent when Mr. Simms forces Cassie into the gutter?

Write 2–3 sentences. Do not simply say “she was afraid.” Consider what would have happened if she had spoken, and what she might have been protecting.

3. What is the goal of the boycott Mama organizes? What does it cost the family, and what does it accomplish?

Write 2–3 sentences in your own words.
