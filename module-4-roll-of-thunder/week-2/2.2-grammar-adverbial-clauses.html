Adverbial Clauses

Subordinate clauses that tell why, when, how, and under what conditions

What It Is

An adverbial clause is a subordinate clause that functions like an adverb — it modifies a verb, adjective, or another adverb, telling when, where, why, how, or under what conditions something happens. It begins with a subordinating conjunction.

Examples from Taylor-style prose:

Because the Wallaces owned the store, the Logans’ boycott cost them business.  (why)
Although Big Ma said nothing, Cassie felt her silence as betrayal.  (contrast)
Since the Logans owned their land, they could not be evicted as easily as sharecroppers.  (reason/condition)
The Logan children walked quietly when they passed the Wallace store.  (when — clause second, no comma)

Punctuation rule:

  • Clause FIRST: adverbial clause + comma + main clause → Because it was dangerous, Papa went anyway.
  • Clause SECOND: main clause + adverbial clause → Papa went anyway because it was dangerous. (no comma)
Common Subordinating Conjunctions

Cause

because • since • as

Contrast

although • even though • while • whereas

Time

when • while • after • before • until • as soon as

Condition

if • unless

Concession

even though • though • while

Exercise 1: Identify the Conjunction and Its Function

Underline the adverbial clause. Identify the subordinating conjunction and what it tells us (cause, contrast, time, condition, concession).

  1. Because Mr. Barnett ignored the Logan children, Cassie spoke up — and that moment changed everything.
  2. Although T.J. was Stacey’s best friend, he continued to take advantage of Stacey’s loyalty.
  3. Since the Logans owned their land, they had the standing to organize a boycott without fearing eviction.
  4. Mama started the boycott after the school night-burning.
  5. Unless the community refused to shop at the Wallace store, the Wallaces faced no consequences for what they had done.
Exercise 2: Combine Using an Adverbial Clause

Combine each pair into one sentence using an adverbial clause. Choose the subordinating conjunction that best captures the logical relationship. Add correct punctuation.

  1. Mr. Simms humiliated Cassie on the sidewalk. Big Ma stayed silent. (The relationship is contrast: Big Ma’s silence despite witnessing the humiliation.)
  2. The family shopped in Vicksburg instead. They lost the convenience of the local store. (The relationship is cause: why the loss occurred.)
  3. The boycott put pressure on the Wallaces. The community held together. (Use a conditional: the boycott works only if something is true.)
