VR 2.5: Big Ma’s Silence

Stage what is NOT said — and record your interpretation of the most painful silence in the novel

What You’re Doing

Still in Session ELA7-M4-W2, you will stage the moment Big Ma does not defend Cassie in Strawberry — and then record your interpretation. This is a scene of silence, not action. The composition must show what is NOT said. Then you will write on a Whiteboard IFX and record yourself explaining your interpretation of Big Ma’s silence and what Taylor is doing by writing it this way.

What You’ll Need
  • Device: VR headset
  • Session ID: ELA7-M4-W2 — Persistent Session (same as VR 2.4 — move to a new area)
What to Do
1

Stage the silence (3–5 IFX)

Place IFX to represent the Strawberry sidewalk moment — specifically the space where Big Ma says nothing. Think about: Who is present? What are the forces at work on Big Ma in this moment? What does she see that Cassie doesn’t? What does Cassie want to happen that doesn’t? You are staging an absence — the words that are not spoken — so your composition needs to show both the people present and the pressure they are under.

2

Place a Whiteboard IFX

Write three sentences on the Whiteboard:
• Sentence 1: What Big Ma does NOT say (and could have said).
• Sentence 2: What Cassie understands in the moment.
• Sentence 3: What the reader understands that Cassie does not (dramatic irony). This sentence must use an adverbial clause (underline and label [ADV]) and the word SYMBOL (underline and label [VOCAB]).

3

Record: interpretation (60–90 seconds)

Start a spatial recording in front of your composition and Whiteboard. Answer three questions aloud: (1) Why does Taylor write Big Ma’s silence without explaining or judging it? (2) What is Big Ma protecting in this moment? (3) What does Cassie’s failure to understand the silence cost her, and what does it cost Big Ma?

4

Submit the recording

Paste the spatial recording link into Canvas as YourLastName_M4_2.5.

Submission

Paste spatial recording link into Canvas. Estimated time: 25–30 minutes in headset.

Facilitator Note

Big Ma’s silence is the module’s most morally complex moment. Students often want to judge it simply (she was wrong not to defend Cassie, or she was right to protect her). Push them toward complexity: “What would have happened if Big Ma had argued? What is she choosing between?” The Whiteboard’s three-part structure forces them to separate Cassie’s understanding from the reader’s — that gap IS dramatic irony. In the recording, listen for students who are genuinely grappling with the complexity rather than just summarizing the scene. The students who say “Big Ma was protecting Cassie from a worse outcome” are closer to the novel’s argument than those who say “Big Ma was afraid.”
