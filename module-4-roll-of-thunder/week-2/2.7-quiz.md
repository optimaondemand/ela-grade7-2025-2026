**Q1.** In Mr. Barnett's store, what does the order in which customers are served reveal about Strawberry's social order?

A) Mr. Barnett prefers to help customers who arrived earliest, regardless of race.
B) Mr. Barnett is simply too busy and disorganized to serve everyone in order.
C) The store operates by a racial hierarchy — white customers are served first regardless of when they arrived, and this is not a mistake but a rule that Black customers are expected to accept without protest.
D) Mr. Barnett recognizes the Logans from their land dispute and refuses to serve them as personal revenge.

**Answer: C**

---

**Q2.** When Mr. Simms forces Cassie off the sidewalk, Big Ma says nothing. What is the most complete and honest interpretation of her silence?

A) Big Ma agrees with Mr. Simms and thinks Cassie was wrong to stand her ground.
B) Big Ma is so frightened that she forgets how to speak.
C) Big Ma's silence is strategic — speaking up would put Cassie and herself in greater danger, but the silence costs Big Ma something too, and Taylor writes it without explanation precisely because it is too painful and complicated to reduce to a simple judgment.
D) Big Ma doesn't notice what is happening because she is shopping.

**Answer: C**

---

**Q3.** What is the goal of the boycott Mama organizes, and what makes it a meaningful act of resistance rather than a futile gesture?

A) The boycott is purely symbolic — it has no real economic impact and Mama knows this.
B) The boycott targets the Wallace store specifically because the Wallaces are responsible for real harm and economic pressure is the one form of resistance the community can exercise without direct physical confrontation. Its power depends on collective participation.
C) The boycott is a religious protest organized by the church against immoral business practices.
D) The boycott is primarily about saving money by shopping in Vicksburg instead of locally.

**Answer: B**

---

**Q4.** Which sentence correctly uses an adverbial clause?

A) Because Cassie refused to step aside, showing the town she would not accept its social order.
B) Although the Logans faced real danger, but they continued to organize the boycott.
C) Although the trip to Strawberry was meant to be routine, it became the moment Cassie first encountered the town's racial hierarchy directly.
D) Since the setting, the social order of Strawberry was visible.

**Answer: C**

---

**Q5.** What is the difference between SETTING as a literary term and simply "where a story takes place"?

A) There is no difference — setting just means location.
B) Setting includes not only the physical place but the time, social conditions, and rules that govern that place — it is the system the characters live inside, not just the backdrop they stand in front of.
C) Setting refers only to the historical period, not the location.
D) Setting is the author's description of the landscape and weather.

**Answer: B**

---

**Q6.** Identify the adverbial clause and its subordinating conjunction in this sentence: "Since the Logans owned their land outright, they could not be evicted the way sharecroppers could."

A) The adverbial clause is "the way sharecroppers could" and the conjunction is "way."
B) The adverbial clause is "the Logans owned their land outright" and the conjunction is "since" — it tells us why they could not be evicted.
C) There is no adverbial clause in this sentence.
D) The adverbial clause is "they could not be evicted" and the conjunction is "since."

**Answer: B**

---

**Q7.** In the Strawberry chapters, the sidewalk where Cassie is forced to step into the gutter functions as a symbol. What does it symbolize?

A) The poverty of Strawberry as a town — the streets are not well-maintained.
B) The distance between Cassie's home and the town.
C) The physical enforcement of a social order that determines who belongs in public space and who must yield — the sidewalk is the town's hierarchy made literal in stone.
D) The passage of time — Cassie walks the same sidewalk as a child that her parents walked.

**Answer: C**

---

**Q8.** Why does Taylor write Big Ma's silence in the Strawberry scene without explaining or judging it?

A) Taylor forgot to include Big Ma's inner thoughts and didn't revise the scene.
B) Big Ma's thoughts are not important to the novel's argument.
C) Cassie is the narrator, and she cannot access Big Ma's thoughts — which is exactly the point: the dramatic irony of this scene depends on Cassie's inability to understand what Big Ma was choosing between.
D) Taylor wants readers to believe Big Ma was simply afraid and leave it at that.

**Answer: C**

---

**Q9.** Which subordinating conjunction BEST completes this sentence to make a concessive argument: "_____ the boycott created hardship for the Logan family, it was a form of resistance the community could exercise together."

A) Because
B) When
C) Unless
D) Although

**Answer: D**

**Q10.** What does T.J.'s behavior in Chapters 4–7 — getting Stacey punished, taking credit for things Stacey did — begin to suggest about his character?

A) T.J. is jealous of Stacey's grades and wants to sabotage him academically.
B) T.J. is simply mischievous in the way all young boys are and means no real harm.
C) T.J.'s choices in these chapters show a pattern of using people for his own advantage without considering the cost to them — a pattern Taylor is setting up to matter in the novel's final act.
D) T.J. dislikes the Logan family and has always tried to hurt them.

**Answer: C**
