Compound-Complex Sentences

The most capable sentence type — built for moral complexity

A Review of Sentence Types
Type Structure Example
Simple 1 independent clause Papa held his ground.
Compound 2+ independent clauses joined by FANBOYS Papa held his ground, and Hammer wanted to do more.
Complex 1 independent + 1 subordinate clause Although Hammer wanted to confront the Simms, Papa held him back.
Compound-Complex 2+ independent + 1+ subordinate clause Although Hammer wanted to confront the Simms, Papa held him back, and the night passed without violence.
When to Use a Compound-Complex Sentence

Use a compound-complex sentence when the situation you are describing is genuinely complex — when it has:

  • More than one cause
  • More than one consequence
  • An action and its complication
  • A concession and a counter-argument

The foil of Hammer and Papa is exactly this kind of situation: two people, one shared value, two strategies, one night, two outcomes. A compound-complex sentence can hold all of that.

Examples

Pattern 1 [Sub], [Ind], [conj] [Ind]:
“Although Uncle Hammer wanted to confront the Wallaces, Papa held him back, and the night passed without violence that would have destroyed the family.”

Pattern 2 [Ind] [conj] [Ind], [Sub]:
“T.J. wanted things the Logans had, and even the things he gained cost him something, because every choice he made with the Simms brothers moved him further from the people who actually cared about him.”

     = subordinate clause         = independent clause

Exercise 1: Label the Clauses

Label each underlined clause as S (subordinate) or I (independent). Then name the sentence type: simple, compound, complex, or compound-complex.

  1. Although Big Ma stayed silent in Strawberry, she was not agreeing with Mr. Simms, and she was not abandoning Cassie.
  2. Papa worked the railroad because the farm income alone could not pay the mortgage.
  3. T.J. lost Stacey’s coat, but he never apologized.
  4. Because the boycott required courage, many families joined only reluctantly, and the Wallaces put pressure on those who wavered.
Exercise 2: Combine Three Short Sentences

Combine the three short sentences into one compound-complex sentence. The result should capture the moral complexity of the situation — not just list the facts.

  1. Uncle Hammer was furious. Papa was careful. The night passed without confrontation. (Use a subordinating conjunction + a coordinating conjunction.)
  2. T.J. wanted a coat. Stacey gave him the coat. T.J. showed it off in a way that got Stacey in trouble. (Capture the full irony of the situation in one sentence.)
  3. Cassie did not understand Big Ma’s silence. She remembered it. It stayed with her for a long time. (Show the temporal relationship using when or until.)
