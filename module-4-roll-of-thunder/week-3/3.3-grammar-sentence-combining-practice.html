Sentence Combining: All Three Module 4 Structures

Using participial phrases, adverbial clauses, and compound-complex sentences to write precisely about complex situations

Why Sentence Structure Matters in Analysis

Each sentence structure serves a different analytical purpose. Before you combine, ask: what is the logical relationship I’m expressing?

Structure Best for Example from novel analysis
Participial phrase Showing an action or state that leads into the main claim Knowing the danger he faced, Papa chose patience over confrontation.
Adverbial clause Expressing cause, contrast, condition, or time Although Hammer’s anger was honest, Papa’s patience was strategic.
Compound-complex Capturing moral complexity with multiple clauses Although Hammer wanted to act, Papa held back, and the family survived the night.
Model: Choppy Writing vs. Precise Analysis

✗ Choppy and Weak

T.J. was friends with the Simms brothers. He wanted a gun. He thought they could help him get it. The Logans tried to warn Stacey. Stacey did not want to see it. T.J. made his own choices.

✓ Precise and Analytical

Drawn to the Simms brothers by what they could offer him, T.J. made choices that cost him the friendship and protection of the Logans, although the Logans tried to warn Stacey about what was happening and Stacey chose not to see it.

Exercise 1: From Choppy to Precise

Combine each group into one or two precise sentences using the structure indicated in brackets.

  1. Uncle Hammer came home for Christmas. He saw the Logans were struggling. He wanted to help immediately. Papa was more cautious. [compound-complex]
  2. The family needed the mortgage money. Papa worked on the railroad all year. He was away from home. [participial phrase + adverbial clause]
  3. T.J. told the Simms about the gun in the store window. He thought they would help him get it. He didn’t understand what he was getting into. [adverbial clause + compound-complex]
