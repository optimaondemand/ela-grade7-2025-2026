VR 3.5: T.J. and the Logans

Stage T.J.’s drift — what he wants, what he gives up, and what Taylor is saying through his choices

What You’re Doing

Still in Session ELA7-M4-W3, you will stage T.J.’s drift away from the Logans toward the Simms brothers. T.J. is the novel’s most tragic figure — not its villain, though he does villainous things. He wants things he sees others have, and he is willing to compromise himself to get them. Your composition and recording should help you think through what Taylor is arguing through T.J.

What You’ll Need
  • Device: VR headset
  • Session ID: ELA7-M4-W3 — Persistent Session (same as VR 3.4 — move to a new area)
What to Do
1

Stage T.J.’s situation (4–6 IFX)

Place IFX to show what T.J. wants and what he gives up in order to get it. The things T.J. wants are mostly material — the gun, status, the Simms brothers’ approval — while what he gives up is less visible: Stacey’s loyalty, the Logans’ protection, his own better instincts. Show both sides.

2

Place a Whiteboard IFX

Write two sentences: (1) what T.J. and Stacey share (what they have in common), (2) what divides them (the choice T.J. makes that Stacey wouldn’t). The second sentence must use an adverbial clause (underline and label [ADV]) and the word CHARACTERIZATION used analytically (underline and label [VOCAB]).

3

Record: your interpretation of T.J. (60–90 seconds)

Start a spatial recording. Standing in front of your composition, answer these questions aloud: Is T.J. a villain, a victim, or both — and what specific evidence from Chapters 8–10 supports your claim? What would Taylor want readers to feel about T.J. at this point in the novel?

Submit the recording

Facilitator Note

T.J. is the test of students’ moral thinking. Prompt: “If T.J. grew up with the Logans’ family, their land, their solidarity — would he have made the same choices?” This question is one Taylor seems to want readers to ask. Students who answer it too quickly in either direction (“yes, he’s just bad” or “no, it’s all the system’s fault”) are not doing the novel’s work. Push toward both: T.J. made real choices, and the world he lived in made those choices almost inevitable. The Whiteboard’s first sentence (“what T.J. and Stacey share”) is important: T.J. is not simply different from Stacey, and recognizing their commonality is what makes T.J. tragic rather than merely bad.
