7th Grade ELA — Module 4
Roll of Thunder, Hear My Cry — Week 4 — Lesson 4.1

The Fire and the Novel’s Argument

Chapters 11–12: sacrifice, strategy, and the cost of holding on

Warm-Up

Think of something you worked hard for and then had to give up — or someone who chose to sacrifice something valuable to protect something else. What did that sacrifice say about what the person valued most? Write 2–3 sentences before reading.

The Lesson

T.J. Avery is arrested at the end of Chapter 11. A white man is dead. T.J. was present at the robbery, though he did not kill anyone. In the white community of Strawberry, the question of whether T.J. killed the man is beside the point — what matters is that a Black teenager was there, and the community is moving toward a lynching. The Logan men — Papa and Uncle Hammer — are there, and they see what is coming.

Papa sets his own cotton field on fire. This act is Taylor’s boldest move in the novel. The land — the thing the entire book has been about, the thing the Logans have sacrificed and struggled and been threatened for — Papa destroys it himself. Not all of it, but enough to redirect the white community’s attention from the lynching to the fire. Fighting a fire that threatens everyone’s crops is more urgent, in the moment, than carrying out a lynching.

T.J. is not saved from the legal consequences — he will almost certainly go to jail and the novel does not lie about this. But the lynching does not happen. Papa bought him that much. And he bought it at the price of the land he has been protecting for the entire novel.

What does this mean? If land = dignity = the ability to hold something the world wants to take — then burning the land is not defeat. It is Papa choosing, with full awareness of the cost, to spend some of what he has been holding in order to act with dignity. The Logans’ value is not the land itself. It is what the land makes possible — the freedom to choose, to refuse, and in this final moment, to sacrifice. That is the novel’s argument: holding on is not hoarding. It is knowing when spending what you have is the right thing to do.

Reading

Read Chapters 11–12 of Roll of Thunder, Hear My Cry.

Mark: (1) the moment when Papa decides to set the fire — what does he see that makes him choose this?, (2) the final scene — Cassie crying and not knowing quite why, (3) anything the novel leaves unresolved.

Vocabulary

Theme

The central argument or insight a work of literature offers about human experience. Not a topic (racial injustice, family, land) but a specific claim about the topic: what the novel argues about it. Taylor’s theme is not simply “injustice is wrong.” It is something more specific: something about how people hold on to dignity when the world tries to take it, what sacrifice means, what ownership really is. Finding the theme means finding the specific claim.

Copia Exercise

Example: “The theme of Roll of Thunder is not simply that racial injustice is wrong, but that holding on to dignity sometimes requires knowing when to spend what you have been protecting.”

Rewrite this sentence using the word theme, making a slightly different claim about what the novel’s central argument is.

Motif

A recurring image, object, or idea that reinforces a theme throughout a work. The Logan land is both a symbol (at specific moments) and a motif (throughout the novel): it appears again and again, and each time it carries the same weight of meaning. A motif gathers significance across the novel; by the final chapter, when the land burns, readers feel the full weight of everything it has meant.

Copia Exercise

Example: “The land functions as a motif in Roll of Thunder because it appears at every crucial moment — when the family faces threat, when they choose resistance, and when Papa burns it — gathering more meaning each time.”

Rewrite this sentence using the word motif, applying it to a different recurring element in the novel (fire, the road, silence, night, or another element you notice).

Comprehension Check

1. Why does Papa set his own cotton field on fire? What is he trying to prevent, and what does he sacrifice to prevent it?

Write 3 sentences. Be specific about what the fire prevents and what it costs.

2. T.J. is not saved from all consequences — the novel ends with him almost certainly facing jail. Why does Taylor end the novel this way? What does it tell us about what the novel is arguing?

Write 2–3 sentences. Avoid easy answers.

3. The novel ends with Cassie crying and not knowing exactly why. What do you think she is mourning? What does the reader understand about her tears that she does not?

Write 2–3 sentences.
