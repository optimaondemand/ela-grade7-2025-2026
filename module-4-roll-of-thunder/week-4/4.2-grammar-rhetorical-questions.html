Rhetorical Questions

Questions asked for effect, not information — and how to use them in analytical writing

What a Rhetorical Question Does

A rhetorical question is a question asked for effect, not to get an answer. The answer is either implied or obvious — and by not stating it, the writer forces the reader to supply it, which makes it more powerful than if the writer had just said it directly.

Examples (novel context):

“What does land mean to a family who has never been allowed to own anything?” — The answer (everything) is more powerful unstated.
“If you had watched your neighbors burned out and your children humiliated, would you have done nothing?” — Forces the reader into the moral situation.
“When Papa burns his own cotton, is he losing or winning?” — Opens the question the essay will answer.

In analytical writing, rhetorical questions work best at the beginning or end of an argument: to open a question your essay will answer, or to close with the implication you want the reader to feel.

Genuine Question vs. Rhetorical Question
Genuine question Rhetorical question
“How many acres does the Logan family own?” “What does 400 acres mean to a family the system was designed to dispossess?”
“Does T.J. go to jail at the end?” “If T.J. had grown up with the Logans’ family, their land, their solidarity — would he have made the same choices?”

How to tell the difference: A genuine question asks for information you don’t have. A rhetorical question already contains the answer — or the point is that the answer can’t be given cleanly, and the question itself is the argument.

Exercise 1: Genuine or Rhetorical?

For each question, identify whether it is genuine (G) or rhetorical (R), and explain your reasoning in one sentence.

  1. “What chapter does the fire occur in?”
  2. “If Big Ma had spoken up in Strawberry, what would have happened to her and Cassie?”
  3. “What was the name of the store the Logan family boycotted?”
  4. “How can you hold on to something by setting it on fire?”
  5. “Who are the members of the Logan family?”
Exercise 2: Revise to a Rhetorical Question

Rewrite each flat declarative statement as a rhetorical question that makes the same point more forcefully.

  1. “The land is the most important thing in the novel.”
  2. “Papa’s decision to burn the cotton shows how much he values T.J.’s life.”
  3. “It is unfair that T.J. faces jail when the Simms brothers are responsible.”
