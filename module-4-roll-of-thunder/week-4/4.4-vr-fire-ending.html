VR 4.4: The Fire

Stage what is destroyed and what is preserved — a composition about sacrifice, not defeat

What You’re Doing

In this session you will stage the novel’s climax: Papa’s fire. This is a composition about sacrifice, not destruction. The fire destroys part of the land the Logan family has held and fought for throughout the entire novel — and it preserves something else. Your IFX arrangement and analytical sticky note should argue what is burning and what survives.

What You’ll Need
  • Device: VR headset
  • Session ID: ELA7-M4-W4 — Persistent Session
  • Location: Art Gallery or Museum
  • Reading: Chapters 11–12 completed
What to Do
1

Stage the fire (4–6 IFX)

Place IFX to represent both what the fire destroys and what it preserves or demonstrates. The fire burns cotton — part of the land that has been the novel’s central symbol. But it also stops a lynching. What does stopping the lynching demonstrate about what the Logans value? Let your IFX show both sides: the loss and the choice. The composition should feel like a sacrifice, not a defeat.

2

Place your name sticky note

[Your Name] — “The Fire” — Module 4 Week 4

3

Place an analytical sticky note

Write two sentences: one rhetorical question (label it [RQ]) about what the fire means, and one concessive sentence (label it [CON]) that acknowledges what is lost and then states what is preserved or demonstrated. Together, these two sentences should argue your interpretation of the fire’s meaning in the novel.

Facilitator Note

The key question: why doesn’t the fire feel like defeat? Ask students: “What does the fire protect that the fire also destroys? How does the fire demonstrate rather than contradict the Logan family’s value of the land?” A strong composition shows both the loss and the choice. Students who only stage the destruction (fire, burning, loss) miss the point; students who stage both the burning cotton and something preserved (T.J.’s life, the family’s agency, the dignity of choosing) are closer to the novel’s argument. The analytical sticky note’s rhetorical question and concessive sentence structure forces students to hold both sides simultaneously.
