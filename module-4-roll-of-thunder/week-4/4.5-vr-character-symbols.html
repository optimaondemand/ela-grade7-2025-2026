7th Grade ELA — Module 4 — VR Assignment
Roll of Thunder, Hear My Cry — Week 4 — VR 4.5

VR 4.5: Character Symbol Gallery

Four stations, four characters — what each holds, protects, and risks by the novel’s end

What You’re Doing

Still in Session ELA7-M4-W4, you will create a four-station gallery — one station for each of four major characters — representing what each character values, protects, and risks or loses by the novel’s end. Then you will connect all four stations with a single Whiteboard sentence that argues what the four together reveal about the novel’s central theme.

What You’ll Need
  • Device: VR headset
  • Session ID: ELA7-M4-W4 — Persistent Session (same as VR 4.4 — move to a new area)
  • Characters: Papa (David Logan), Mama (Mary Logan), Cassie, T.J.
What to Do
1

Create four stations (2–3 IFX each)

Place four distinct areas, one per character. For each, use 2–3 IFX to represent: (1) what that character most values or protects, (2) what they risk or lose. The stations should be distinct but arranged so a viewer understands they belong to the same gallery — same session, different stations.

2

Place a sticky note at each station

Each station gets a sticky note: character name + one sentence using MOTIF or THEME analytically (underline and label [VOCAB]).

3

Place a Whiteboard IFX

Write one compound-complex sentence (underline and label [CC]) that connects all four stations and argues what they together reveal about the novel’s central theme. This is your gallery’s thesis.

4

Take a snapshot and submit

Take a snapshot that shows as many of the four stations and the Whiteboard as possible. Name it YourLastName_M4_4.5. Upload to Canvas.

Submission

Upload snapshot to Canvas. Estimated time: 25–30 minutes in headset.

Facilitator Note

The T.J. station is the most revealing. Students who only represent what T.J. wants (material things) without representing what he gives up (Stacey’s loyalty, the Logans’ protection) are reading T.J. as a villain rather than as a tragedy. Prompt: “If T.J.’s station is only what he wanted, what does your arrangement argue about him? What does it leave out?” The Whiteboard compound-complex sentence is the integration move — it should connect all four characters to the same theme rather than just summarizing each. Watch for Whiteboard sentences that are really four separate statements joined with “and” — these are not compound-complex, they are just compound. Prompt: “Where is the subordinate clause? What is the dependent relationship you want to express?”
