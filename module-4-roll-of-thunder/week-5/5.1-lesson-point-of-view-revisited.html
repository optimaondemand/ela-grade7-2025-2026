7th Grade ELA — Module 4
Roll of Thunder, Hear My Cry — Week 5 — Lesson 5.1

Point of View Revisited: What Cassie Finally Understands

The novel as a whole: returning to the essential question with the full book in view

The Module Question, Revisited
How do people hold on to what is theirs when the world tries to take it from them?
Warm-Up

Think of a book you’ve read that didn’t end the way you expected — one that left something unresolved. What do you think the author wanted you to feel or understand at the end? Write 2–3 sentences.

The Lesson

At the end of the novel, Cassie cries. She doesn’t fully know why. She is crying, she thinks, for T.J. — but she is also crying for something she can’t yet name: a world that should be different and isn’t, a loss that is too large and too ongoing to be resolved in one novel, the weight of everything she has come to understand over twelve chapters about what the people she loves face every day.

Taylor chose a child narrator for this ending with great care. An adult narrator might explain too much — might name the grief, give it context, place it in a larger historical argument, tell us what to feel. Cassie cannot do any of that. She can only feel. And so the reader is left holding two things at once: Cassie’s authentic, inarticulate grief, and our own larger understanding of what it is grief for. That is the novel’s final, most powerful use of dramatic irony.

The novel ends not with triumph or total defeat but with ambiguity: the land is still there, T.J. is in jail, the fire is out, the family is intact. Taylor refuses to resolve the situation cleanly because it is not resolvable cleanly — not in 1933, not in the years that follow. The novel ends where the history continues. Cassie’s tears are not the end of something. They are the beginning of understanding.

Now you are writing your essay. You have the full novel, five weeks of VR work, grammar practice, and quickwrites as preparation. The essay asks you to take one formal choice Taylor made — her use of a child narrator, her use of the land as a symbol, or her use of setting to enforce the novel’s argument — and argue specifically what that choice does. Not what the novel is about, but how it works.

Full-Novel Comprehension Check

1. Why does the novel end with Cassie crying rather than with a clear statement of what has been won or lost?

Write 2–3 sentences. This is an analytical question, not a plot question.

2. How has Cassie’s understanding of the world changed from Chapter 1 to Chapter 12? Name one specific thing she knows now that she didn’t fully grasp at the start.

Write 2 sentences.

3. If you were writing your essay tomorrow, what would your thesis be? Write a one-sentence thesis that names the formal choice AND what it does for the novel’s argument.

One sentence. This is practice for VR 5.5.
