Grammar 5.2: Module 4 Structures Review

All four Module 4 structures working together in one paragraph

The Four Structures at a Glance
Structure What It Is Key Signal Words Best Used For
Participial Phrase
[PART]		 A phrase beginning with an -ing or -ed/-en verb form that modifies a noun burned, holding, knowing, torn, acting Describing a character or object while the main action continues; economical detail
Adverbial Clause
[ADV]		 A subordinate clause beginning with a subordinating conjunction that modifies the main verb because, although, even though, since, when, unless Expressing cause, contrast, concession, condition, or time relationship between two events
Compound-Complex
[CC]		 Two or more independent clauses joined by a coordinating conjunction, plus at least one subordinate clause for, and, but, yet, so — combined with because, although, when, etc. Showing that two results share a common cause, or that a concession applies to a complex situation
Rhetorical Question /
Concessive Sentence
[RQ] / [CON]		 [RQ] A question asked for effect, not answered directly. [CON] A sentence that grants an objection, then restates the argument stronger [RQ]: What does…? If…, what…? [CON]: Even though…, … / Granted that…, … Opening an analytical argument, pivoting to a counter-argument, acknowledging complexity without abandoning a claim
Model Paragraph: All Four Labeled

Read the analytical paragraph below. Each sentence uses one of the four Module 4 structures. Notice how the structures are not just decorative — each one does specific analytical work.

[RQ] If Taylor wanted to argue that the Logan family’s land means freedom, why does she make them risk losing it rather than simply keeping it safe?

[PART] Knowing that their 400 acres are the physical proof of their independence, the Logans treat every threat to the land as a threat to something deeper than property.

[CON] Even though Papa burns part of the very cotton that funds the mortgage payment, the fire is not a defeat, because the choice to act — to spend the land on saving a life — is itself the expression of the freedom the land was meant to protect.

[CC] Taylor refuses to resolve T.J.’s fate cleanly, and she leaves Cassie’s grief unnamed, because the injustice the novel depicts did not resolve cleanly in 1933.

[RQ] Rhetorical question opens the argument
[PART] Participial phrase, underlined
[CON] Concessive sentence
[CC] Compound-complex, subordinate clause underlined
Exercise 1: Identify the Structure

Each sentence below uses one Module 4 structure. Label it [PART], [ADV], [CC], [RQ], or [CON]. Then underline the key structural element (the participial phrase, the subordinate clause, or the concessive opener).

  1. "Watching the cotton fields burn from the porch, Cassie felt something she couldn’t name yet."
  2. "If the Logans had only been protecting the land itself, why would they burn it to save someone else?"
  3. "Even though Big Ma forces Cassie to apologize to Lillian Jean, the apology demonstrates the social order Big Ma has had to navigate her entire life, not Cassie’s genuine guilt."
  4. "T.J. wanted the coat, but he gave up Stacey’s loyalty to get it, because he mistook material things for the kind of status that actually protects you."
  5. "Hammered by his brother’s caution, Uncle Hammer drove away from Strawberry without the confrontation that would have cost the family everything."

You may use the model above as a guide for how the structures can connect to each other — but the content should be your own argument, not a restatement of the model.
