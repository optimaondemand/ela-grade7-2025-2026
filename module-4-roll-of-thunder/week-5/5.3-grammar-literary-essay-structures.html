Grammar 5.3: Literary Essay Sentence Structures

Three moves every analytical paragraph needs: introducing evidence, explaining evidence, conceding and countering

Why Essay Sentences Are Different

A literary analysis essay doesn’t just state an argument — it walks a reader through a text to show how the argument is supported. That requires three distinct sentence moves, each with its own shape. When an essay paragraph is weak, it’s usually because one of these three moves is missing or blurred together with another.

The three moves are: introducing evidence (contextualizing what you’re about to cite), explaining evidence (saying what the evidence actually demonstrates), and conceding and countering (acknowledging what the opposing reading gets right before restating your claim stronger). You have already practiced all three using Module 4 structures. This lesson names them formally and shows how they connect inside a single paragraph.

Move 1: Introducing Evidence

Before you drop in a piece of evidence from the novel, you need to set it up. This is not "In chapter 12, Papa burns the cotton" — that is a plot summary. This is a sentence that gives the evidence a context and a direction: tells the reader what to notice about it before they read it.

Useful introductory structures:

Structure Example
[PART] phrase at opening Facing the threat of a lynching, Papa makes the choice that demonstrates what holding the land has always meant.
[ADV] clause (cause) Because Taylor gives Cassie no adult narrator to explain what she sees, the reader must supply the larger understanding that Cassie cannot.
Author-action phrase Taylor uses the detail of the 400 acres — introduced in the novel’s first pages and returned to in the last — to anchor her argument about what holding on requires.
Avoid: "In chapter 12…" or "The book says…" or "When I read the part where…" These tell the reader where you found the evidence, not what it means. Your introduction should move the reader toward a specific analytical point.
Move 2: Explaining Evidence

After you introduce your evidence (a quotation, a scene reference, or a specific detail), you must explain what it demonstrates. This is the most commonly skipped step in student essays, and it is the most important one. Evidence does not speak for itself. You speak for the evidence.

Useful explanatory structures:

Structure Example
This [noun] demonstrates / reveals / shows This choice demonstrates that the land’s value is not simply economic — it is the material form of the family’s refusal to be erased.
[CC] sentence connecting two effects The fire costs the Logans a real portion of their harvest, but it preserves something the harvest was meant to protect, because the ability to choose what to sacrifice is itself what the land represented.
In other words, / What this means is In other words, Taylor argues that holding on does not mean hoarding — it means knowing when spending what you have is the only act of dignity available.
Avoid: Stopping at "This shows that Papa loves his family." That is an observation, not an explanation. Push one step further: what does it show about how the novel argues? What does Taylor want the reader to understand from this?
Move 3: Conceding and Countering

A strong analytical essay acknowledges what the other side gets right. A concession is not a surrender — it is proof that you have read carefully enough to know where your argument could be challenged. The concessive sentence structure you practiced in Lesson 4.3 is the tool for this move.

Pattern: Even though [what the opposing reading correctly observes], [your claim] because [your reasoning].

Example: Even though Papa’s fire destroys a portion of the land that the entire novel has treated as the family’s most precious possession, the act is not a defeat, because Taylor has already shown us that the land’s value lies in what it makes possible — and what it made possible here was the choice to act.

Notice that the concession is specific: it names what the opposing reading actually gets right (the fire does destroy land), not a straw man (the fire is obviously great and only bad readers would think otherwise). The stronger you make the concession, the stronger your counter-argument looks.

Exercise 1: Identify the Move

Label each sentence below as INTRO (introducing evidence), EXPLAIN (explaining evidence), or CONCEDE/COUNTER.

  1. "Even though a child narrator limits what the reader can know directly, this limitation is itself the point: Cassie’s incomplete understanding performs the novel’s argument about what racism costs its youngest victims."
  2. "Holding the book upside down at the novel’s opening, Little Man refuses the damaged copy in a gesture that Taylor uses to introduce the novel’s central question of dignity."
  3. "This refusal demonstrates that even a child can identify when an object is being used to communicate contempt, and that identifying it — refusing it — is its own act of resistance."
  4. "Knowing the land has been paid for in suffering and sacrifice, Papa treats any threat to it as a threat to the memory of everyone who died for it."
  5. "What this scene reveals is that the land is not just property — it is the accumulated proof that the Logan family cannot be erased."
