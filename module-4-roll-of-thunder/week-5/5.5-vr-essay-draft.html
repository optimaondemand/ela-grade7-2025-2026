VR 5.5: Stage Your Essay Argument

Your thesis, your evidence, your voice — in spatial form before you commit it to paper

What You’re Doing

This is your essay in spatial form before you write it out as prose. You will stage the argument — your thesis, the evidence you are using, and the connection between them — using IFX and a Whiteboard, then record yourself explaining the argument aloud. The recording is the submission. The essay itself is the module project.

What You’ll Need
  • Device: VR headset
  • Session ID: ELA7-M4-W5 — Persistent Session (your own session from this week)
  • Bring with you: Your essay focus (child narrator, land as symbol, or setting as argument) and at least two specific pieces of evidence from the novel
  • Materials from the module: Your VR compositions from Weeks 1–4 are in persistent sessions — you may refer to them while building this one, but do not copy them. This session is your argument, not your archive.
What to Do
1

Stage your evidence (4–6 IFX)

Choose 4–6 IFX that represent the specific evidence your essay will use — not the general novel, but your particular argument’s evidence. If you are writing about the land as symbol, what IFX captures what the land means in the scenes you are analyzing? If you are writing about the child narrator, what IFX captures what Cassie can and cannot see? The composition should reflect your specific thesis, not just the novel in general.

2

Place a Whiteboard IFX: your thesis and structure

Write three things on the Whiteboard (connect it to your Drawpad): (1) your one-sentence thesis — named formal choice + what it does for the novel’s argument (label [THESIS]); (2) two or three pieces of specific evidence from the novel you are using, each labeled [EV1], [EV2], [EV3]; (3) your concession — the strongest objection to your thesis and your counter (label [CON]). The Whiteboard is your essay outline in spatial form.

3

Record your argument (2–3 minutes)

Make a spatial recording while standing in your composition. In the recording, speak your essay argument aloud: state your thesis, walk through your evidence and what it demonstrates, and state your concession and counter. This should sound like an argument, not a summary of the novel. Aim for 2–3 minutes. You do not need to be perfect. You need to test whether your argument makes sense out loud — what does not make sense in the recording is what you will fix in the essay draft.

4

Take a snapshot and submit with the recording link

Name your snapshot YourLastName_M4_5.5. Upload the snapshot to Canvas. In the Canvas text box, paste the recording link and write 2 sentences: What is your thesis? What did you realize while recording that you need to fix before writing the essay?

Submission

Upload snapshot + paste recording link + 2 sentences to Canvas. Estimated time: 25–35 minutes in headset.

The Three Essay Focus Options

Your essay argues about one of the following formal choices Taylor made. Your Whiteboard thesis should name the choice AND state what it does for the novel’s argument — not just what the novel is about.

Option A: The Child Narrator
Argue what Taylor achieves by using Cassie — a child who cannot fully understand what she is experiencing — as the novel’s narrator. What does this choice do that an adult narrator could not?
Option B: The Land as Symbol
Argue what the Logan family’s 400 acres symbolize throughout the novel, and how Taylor uses the land’s recurring presence to carry the novel’s argument about holding on.
Option C: Setting as Argument
Argue how Taylor uses the novel’s setting — Mississippi in 1933, the physical geography of the county, the specific spaces like the store and the road — to enforce the novel’s argument about power and resistance.
Facilitator Note

The most useful feedback at this stage is on the thesis line. A weak thesis names the formal choice but not what it does (“Taylor uses a child narrator”). A strong thesis names both the choice and its function (“Taylor uses a child narrator to create a gap between what Cassie can see and what the reader understands, turning Cassie’s limited knowledge into the novel’s most powerful form of dramatic irony”). When reviewing Whiteboard content, focus on: Is the thesis a claim, not just an observation? Are the evidence items specific (a scene, a detail, a character action) rather than general (“the land appears in many scenes”)? Does the concession engage the strongest version of the opposing reading? Students whose recording reveals that they don’t yet understand what they want to argue — who restate the plot rather than analyze the craft — need the essay delayed one day to allow a revision to the Whiteboard first.
