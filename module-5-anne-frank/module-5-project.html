7th Grade ELA — Module 5 — Project
The Diary of a Young Girl — Module Project

Module 5 Project: Literary Analysis Essay

A focused argument about craft and identity in Anne Frank’s diary

The Assignment

Write a 400–500 word literary analysis essay arguing how one formal or rhetorical choice Anne Frank makes in The Diary of a Young Girl contributes to the diary’s argument about identity and resistance. This is not a biography of Anne Frank and not a summary of the diary. It is an argument about what the diary does — and how Anne Frank made it do that.

Choose One Focus
Option A: Anne’s Voice
Argue what Anne’s distinctive voice does for the diary’s argument about identity. Your thesis must name the voice as a formal choice and state what it does. Don’t argue that Anne has a distinctive voice — argue what that voice argues.
Option B: The Dash as Expression
Argue what Anne’s use of the em dash — specifically its role in foregrounding asides, pivots, and moments of self-correction — does for the diary’s construction of an immediate, unguarded self.
Option C: Audience
Argue how Anne’s construction of an imagined future audience changed the diary from private record to public document — and what that change means for how the diary argues about identity and resistance.
Option D: Identity Claims
Argue how Anne’s practice of writing explicit identity claims — declarative statements about who she is and what she values — functions as an act of resistance rather than just self-expression.
Required Essay Structure
Introduction (50–75 words)
Open with a claim about the diary, not with "Anne Frank was born in 1929." Include a [PERIODIC] sentence somewhere in the introduction. End with your thesis — one sentence that names the formal choice and states what it does.
Body Paragraph 1 (100–125 words)
First piece of evidence. Use [Q][SIGNAL] to introduce your quotation. Use [DASH] somewhere in your explanation. Your explanation must add something not already in the quotation — it must interpret, not restate.
Body Paragraph 2 (100–125 words)
Second piece of evidence. Use [Q][SIGNAL] to introduce your quotation. Include a [PARA] sentence in your explanation. Acknowledge the strongest objection to your thesis (concession) and counter it.
Conclusion (75–100 words)
Return to the essential question: How do people preserve who they are when everything around them tries to erase them? Your conclusion should state what the diary taught you that you couldn’t have answered the same way before reading it. Do not restate your thesis word for word — extend it.
Required Tags in Your Essay

Label each required element in brackets within your essay text. These tags let your reader (and your teacher) confirm that the required structures are present. Do not remove them before submitting.

[THESIS] — your one-sentence thesis in the introduction
[PERIODIC] — your periodic sentence in the introduction

[DASH] — your em dash used in explanation
[PARA] — your parallel structure sentence

Grading Rubric (100 points)
Criterion 25 pts 18 pts 10 pts 0 pts
Thesis and Argument Thesis names a formal choice AND argues its specific effect. Argument is consistent throughout. Thesis names a choice but the effect is vague or inconsistently supported. Thesis is present but observational rather than argumentative ("Anne uses voice"). No discernible thesis or the essay is a summary.
Evidence and Explanation Both quotations directly support the thesis. Explanation sentences interpret — they add something the quotation does not already say. Quotations are related to the thesis. At least one explanation sentence moves beyond restatement. Quotations are present but explanation sentences mostly restate the quotation. No textual evidence, or evidence is unrelated to the thesis.
Grammar Structures All required structures ([PERIODIC], [Q][SIGNAL], [DASH], [PARA], [CON]) are present and used correctly. Tags are labeled. Most structures present and labeled. One structure missing or used incorrectly. Two or more structures missing or unlabeled. Required structures not attempted.
Conclusion and Essential Question Conclusion returns to the essential question and states something new — what the diary taught this reader that they couldn’t have said before reading it. Conclusion addresses the essential question but stays close to the thesis restatement. Conclusion is present but only restates the thesis without returning to the essential question. No conclusion, or conclusion is unrelated to the essay’s argument.
