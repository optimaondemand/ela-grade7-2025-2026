7th Grade ELA — Module 5
The Diary of a Young Girl — Week 1 — Lesson 1.1

Into Hiding: Anne Frank Begins Her Diary

What a diary is for, and why this one matters

The Module Question
How do people preserve who they are when everything around them tries to erase them?
Warm-Up

Have you ever kept a diary or journal — or wanted to? What would you want a diary to do that talking to another person cannot? Write 2–3 sentences.

Historical Context
Amsterdam, 1942. Anne Frank was thirteen years old when she received a diary as a birthday gift. Five weeks later, her family went into hiding in a concealed apartment above her father’s office — a space known as the Secret Annex. They were Jewish, and Nazi Germany had occupied the Netherlands. Jews faced deportation to concentration camps. For two years and one month, eight people lived in the Secret Annex, sustained by non-Jewish friends who brought them food and news. Anne wrote nearly every day. On August 4, 1944, the hiding place was discovered. All eight residents were arrested. Anne died in Bergen-Belsen concentration camp in February or March 1945, weeks before it was liberated. She was fifteen. Her father Otto, the only Secret Annex resident to survive, returned to Amsterdam after the war and found Anne’s diary. He arranged for its publication in 1947.
The Lesson

Anne begins her diary by explaining something important: she is writing to a friend she has invented, named Kitty. “I want to write,” she says early on, “but more than that, I want to bring out all kinds of things that lie buried deep in my heart.” The diary, in other words, is not just a record of events — it is the place where Anne can be fully herself, without performing for anyone, without protecting anyone’s feelings, without adjusting her voice to suit a listener. The diary is where Anne is most completely Anne.

This matters for how you read the text. Anne is not trying to write literature. She is trying to survive — and writing is part of how she survives. She uses the diary to think through her feelings about her family, her situation, herself, and the world outside she cannot reach. When the entries are funny, it is because Anne was funny. When they are angry, it is because Anne was genuinely angry. When they are wise beyond what you would expect from a fifteen-year-old, it is because hiding — confinement, fear, uncertainty, time to think — forced a particular kind of growth.

The literary vocabulary for this module begins with two terms. The first is VOICE: the distinctive quality of a writer’s language — the word choices, rhythms, asides, and attitudes that make a piece of writing sound like a specific person and no one else. Anne’s voice is her most powerful characteristic as a writer. The second is THE DASH: the punctuation mark Anne uses constantly, and which you will study as a grammar structure this week. The dash in Anne’s writing is often the mark of her voice in its most unguarded form — the aside, the afterthought, the thing she couldn’t help adding.

Vocabulary

VOICE — the distinctive quality of a writer’s language that makes it sound like a specific person: their word choices, rhythms, humor, directness, and way of seeing the world.

Example from the diary: “I don’t want to set down a series of bald facts in a diary as most people do, but I want this diary itself to be my friend.”

Copia exercise: “I don’t want to play the same sport every week as most people do, but I want the sport itself to be a place I think.” Rewrite this sentence keeping the word WANT and the structure “I don’t want to X as most people do, but I want Y itself to be Z.” Use your own subject.

Anne’s first entries introduce the people around her: her parents, her older sister Margot, their friends the van Pels, and the dentist Fritz Pfeffer, who will join them in the Annex later. But she also introduces herself — her opinions, her quarrels, her likes and dislikes — with unusual frankness. By the time you reach the first pages, you already know a great deal about who Anne Frank is. That is the power of an unguarded voice.

Comprehension Check

1. Anne says she is writing to an imaginary friend named Kitty rather than to a real person. What does this tell you about what she needs the diary to be?

Write 2 sentences. This is an analytical question, not a factual one.

2. Name one thing about Anne’s voice that comes through in the first week of entries. Give a specific example.

Write 1–2 sentences.

3. Why does it matter that Anne began her diary before going into hiding rather than after?

Write 2 sentences. Think about what we know about Anne before everything changes.
