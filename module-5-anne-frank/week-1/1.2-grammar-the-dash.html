Grammar 1.2: The Em Dash — Voice in Punctuation

The punctuation mark that signals an unguarded mind

What Is an Em Dash?

The em dash (—) is longer than a hyphen (-) and different from an en dash (–). It is one of the most expressive punctuation marks in English because it can do several different jobs — and because it signals a particular kind of thinking: associative, digressive, willing to interrupt itself.

In formal writing, the dash is used sparingly. Anne Frank, writing in a diary, uses it constantly — and it is central to the quality that makes her voice feel so immediate. When you see a dash in Anne’s writing, you are usually seeing the place where she couldn’t stop herself from adding one more thing.

Four Uses of the Em Dash
Use What it does Example
1. The aside Interrupts the sentence to add an observation or aside, then continues "We go to the van Pels for dinner — which is never as good as at home — and then back to our little room."
2. The afterthought Adds something at the end of the sentence that feels like a second thought "I want to go on living even after my death — and therefore I am grateful to God for giving me this gift."
3. The pivot Signals a turn in the sentence — from setup to conclusion, from problem to realization "We have been here for three weeks — and I still cannot believe this is our life now."
4. The emphasis break Isolates a word or phrase for emphasis by surrounding it with dashes "The thing I want most — the thing I need — is someone to talk to who actually listens."
Dash vs. Comma vs. Parentheses

All three can set off additional information inside a sentence, but they create different tones:

Commas (neutral): "We ate dinner, which was late as usual, and then went to bed."
Dashes (emphatic, voice-forward): "We ate dinner — which was late as usual, because someone forgot to cook the potatoes — and then went to bed."
Parentheses (whispered aside, de-emphasized): "We ate dinner (which was late as usual) and then went to bed."

Anne uses dashes because she is not de-emphasizing her asides — she is emphasizing them. The parenthetical thought is often the most important part of what she wants to say.

Exercise 1: Identify the Use

Label each dash as: ASIDE, AFTERTHOUGHT, PIVOT, or EMPHASIS BREAK. Then name what the dash adds to the sentence — what would be lost if you removed it?

  1. "The hiding place felt smaller every week — and yet somehow we filled it with arguments and plans and even laughter."
  2. "Peter — who never says much in the evenings — surprised everyone by asking a question during dinner."
  3. "I have always wanted to be a writer — not just someone who writes, but someone whose words matter."
  4. "The one thing we could not hear — the thing we all pretended not to miss — was the sound of children playing outside."
  5. "Mother and I disagree about almost everything — or perhaps we agree about everything and disagree about how to say it."
Exercise 2: Write with the Dash

Write three sentences about a small space you know well (a bedroom, a car, a classroom). Use a different dash technique in each sentence. Label each in brackets: [ASIDE], [AFTERTHOUGHT], [PIVOT], or [EMPHASIS BREAK]. The sentences should reveal something about the space through the dash — not just describe it.

This exercise practices voice. What you add after the dash is where your voice lives.
