7th Grade ELA — Module 5 — VR Assignment
The Diary of a Young Girl — Week 1 — VR 1.5

VR 1.5: Anne vs. the Diary — Two Compositions

What Anne puts into the diary and what the diary gives back

What You’re Doing

Still in Session ELA7-M5-W1, you will create two small compositions side by side: one representing what Anne brings to the diary (her experiences, her feelings, her observations, her self), and one representing what the diary gives back to her (the thing she cannot get anywhere else in the Annex). Then you will connect them with a Whiteboard sentence that argues what the relationship between Anne and her diary reveals about the module question.

What You’ll Need
  • Device: VR headset
  • Session ID: ELA7-M5-W1 — Persistent Session (same as VR 1.4 — move to a new area)
What to Do
1

Create two compositions (2–3 IFX each)

Composition A: What Anne brings to the diary — the raw material she gives it. Composition B: What the diary gives back to Anne that nothing else in the Annex can. Place them side by side. A sticky note on each: label it [A] or [B] and write one sentence about what your IFX choices argue. Each sticky note should use a dash [DASH].

2

Place a Whiteboard IFX between the two compositions

Write one sentence on the Whiteboard that connects both compositions and answers: how does the relationship between Anne and her diary help answer the module question? Use VOICE [VOCAB] in the sentence and a dash [DASH]. Label the sentence [WB].

3

Make a 60–90 second recording

Walk between the two compositions and explain: what is the most important difference between what Anne gives the diary and what it gives back to her? Why does that difference matter for understanding what the diary is actually doing for her?

4

Take a snapshot and submit

Name it YourLastName_M5_1.5. Upload snapshot + paste recording link to Canvas.

Facilitator Note

The key distinction this assignment is trying to surface: the diary is not just a record of Anne’s life — it is a tool for constructing her identity. What Anne gives the diary is experience; what it gives back is a self that is more fully articulated than she could achieve through conversation alone. Students who argue the diary "gives back" comfort or companionship are on the surface; students who argue it gives back selfhood, authorship, or the ability to make sense of what is happening to her are at the depth this question is after. The recording is where this distinction usually becomes clearest — students who understand it speak more precisely about the diary as a tool, not just as a journal.
