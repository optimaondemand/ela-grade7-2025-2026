7th Grade ELA — Module 5
The Diary of a Young Girl — Week 2 — Lesson 2.1

Daily Life in Hiding: What Confinement Does

Eight people, one building, two years — and what happens to human beings under pressure

Warm-Up

Think of a time you were stuck somewhere — a long car trip, a sick day inside, a situation you couldn’t leave. What did it do to your mood? Did it change how you saw the people around you? Write 2–3 sentences.

The Lesson

One of the things the diary captures with unusual honesty is the texture of daily life in hiding. Anne writes about arguments over food, annoyance at her mother, longing for friends, boredom, frustration with the adults around her. These entries are sometimes funny and sometimes painful — and they matter because they resist the temptation to make the Secret Annex sound romantic or heroic. Living in hiding was not an adventure. It was confinement, with its full weight of irritability and proximity and noise.

But something else happens alongside the daily friction. Anne begins, in these middle entries, to think more deeply about who she is. She watches herself have reactions — to her mother, to Peter van Pels, to the tension in the Annex — and she writes about what she notices. This is one of the most unusual qualities of her diary: she is not just recording what happens, she is analyzing herself as a character in the story of her own life. She is doing, on the page, what a careful literary reader does: she is noticing patterns, questioning motives, and trying to understand a complex inner world.

The second grammar structure for this module is PARALLEL STRUCTURE: the technique of writing items in a series or a list so that each item has the same grammatical form. Parallel structure creates rhythm, clarity, and emphasis. When Anne lists the things she misses — friends, school, the outdoors, the freedom to be loud — the list has force because each item is grammatically the same shape. When the shapes are uneven, the list loses its punch.

Vocabulary

PARALLEL STRUCTURE — the technique of expressing related items in the same grammatical form, so that a list or comparison moves with even rhythm and clarity.

Example: “She wanted to read, to write, and to be heard.” (three parallel infinitives)

Copia exercise: “She wanted to eat, to sleep, and to forget.” Rewrite this sentence keeping the three parallel infinitives, but change all three verbs to verbs that describe something a student does before a big test. Keep the exact same sentence frame.

Anne’s relationship with her mother is one of the most honest and complicated threads in the diary. Anne criticizes her mother sharply, sometimes unfairly; she later reflects on this and acknowledges her own harshness. This back-and-forth — observation, reaction, self-correction — is the shape of Anne’s thinking throughout the diary. It is what makes her not just a diarist but a genuinely rigorous self-observer.

Comprehension Check

1. Anne writes often about her frustrations with the people she lives with — especially her mother. What does this honesty tell you about what the diary is doing for her?

Write 2 sentences. This is an analytical question.

2. Name one parallel structure Anne uses in the diary (a list, a series, or a comparison). Explain what it emphasizes.

Write 1–2 sentences. Quote or closely paraphrase the example.

3. Anne analyzes herself as well as others. Find one moment where she catches herself reacting and then questions or complicates her own reaction. What does this self-analysis reveal about her character?

Write 2 sentences.
