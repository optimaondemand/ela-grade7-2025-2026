Grammar 2.2: Parallel Structure

How rhythm and grammar form work together in a list

What Is Parallel Structure?

Parallel structure means that items in a series, a list, or a comparison use the same grammatical form. When the forms are uneven — when one item is a noun, the next is an infinitive, and the third is a gerund — the sentence stumbles. When the forms are parallel, the sentence moves with clarity and force.

The parallel structure in rhetoric goes back to ancient Greek orators. When you read lists in speeches, prayers, laws, and powerful prose, the parallel structure is not accidental — it is what gives the list the feeling that nothing could be added or removed without damaging the whole.

Common Parallel Forms
Form Parallel Example Broken Example
Nouns "She missed sunlight, friends, and freedom." "She missed sunlight, her friends, and to be free."
Infinitives "She wanted to read, to write, and to be heard." "She wanted to read, writing, and being heard."
Gerunds (-ing) "Reading, writing, and arguing kept the Annex alive." "Reading, to write, and arguments kept the Annex alive."
Adjectives "The Annex was small, cold, and crowded." "The Annex was small, very cold, and people crowded in."
Clauses "She knew what she felt, what she thought, and what she feared." "She knew what she felt, what her thoughts were, and fear."
Parallelism and Emphasis

Parallel structure doesn’t just create grammatical correctness — it creates emphasis through repetition of form. The more items in the list, the more insistent the rhythm. Writers use this to build toward a final, most important item, or to convey that all items are equally weighted and none can be removed.

Compare: "She was afraid of the dark, of noise, and of silence."  vs.  "She was afraid of the dark, of the noise outside, and of what the silence might mean."

The second version has parallel structure AND builds — each item is more complex and more charged than the last. The list moves.
