Grammar 2.3: Parallelism in Analytical Writing

Using parallel structure to make a literary argument feel inevitable

From Lists to Arguments

Parallel structure is not just a correction rule — it is a rhetorical tool. When you use it in analytical writing, it does more than make your lists grammatical. It signals that the items you are listing share a relationship that the reader should notice. It makes your argument feel organized, weighty, and inevitable.

In a literary essay, parallelism often appears in two places: in the thesis (listing what you claim about a text) and in body paragraphs (listing the effects of a formal choice). Both uses work the same way — the parallel structure of the list argues that the items belong together and carry the same weight.

Parallelism in Thesis Statements
Weak (broken parallelism): "Anne Frank's diary preserves her voice, reveals her character, and being an important historical document."
Strong (parallel): "Anne Frank's diary preserves her voice, reveals her character, and argues for the irreducibility of human identity even under the most extreme erasure."

Notice that in the strong version, all three verbs are in the same form (third-person present: preserves, reveals, argues) and the third item is the most analytical — it doesn’t just describe the diary but makes a claim about what the diary does.

Combining Dash and Parallel Structure

Anne Frank’s diary often uses both — a parallel list interrupted by a dash for emphasis. This is one of the most expressive sentence structures in personal and analytical prose:

"She preserved what the Nazis could not confiscate — her humor, her anger, her hope, and the stubborn particularity of her own mind."

The dash isolates the list, and the parallel nouns (humor, anger, hope, mind) give the list its force. The final item — the most abstract and specific — lands with weight because the three before it have prepared the reader for it.
