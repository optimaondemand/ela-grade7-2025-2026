7th Grade ELA — Module 5
The Diary of a Young Girl — Week 3 — Lesson 3.1

Anne the Writer: Identity, Ambition, and the Question of Audience

The moment Anne decides the diary matters beyond herself

Warm-Up

If you knew that everything you wrote would eventually be read by thousands of people, would you write differently? What would you add? What would you remove? Write 2–3 sentences.

The Lesson

In the spring of 1944, Anne heard a Dutch radio broadcast in which a government minister said that after the war, diaries and letters written during the occupation should be collected and preserved. Anne heard this and made a decision: she began revising her diary. She went back through her earlier entries and rewrote many of them, imagining a future reader — not just Kitty, but an actual audience who would one day read her account of what it was like to live in hiding during the Nazi occupation.

This is one of the most important and often overlooked facts about the diary: what we read is not only a spontaneous record. It is, in significant part, a revised, shaped, and consciously crafted text. Anne was not just a diarist — she was a writer who took her own work seriously enough to go back and improve it. She wrote fictional short stories, she wrote essays, she worked on a book about the Annex under a pseudonym. She had a writerly ambition that the diary both recorded and fed.

This week’s grammar structure is DIRECT QUOTATION INTEGRATION: how to introduce, punctuate, and explain a direct quotation in analytical writing. When you write about a text — especially a text like the diary, where the author’s specific words are the evidence — you need to handle quotations correctly. A dropped quotation with no introduction or explanation is one of the most common weaknesses in student essays.

Vocabulary

AUDIENCE — the intended or implied reader of a piece of writing; the person or group the writer imagines reading their work. Every writer writes with some sense of audience, whether explicit or not, and the implied audience shapes the choices the writer makes.

Example: "When Anne began revising the diary for a future reader, she shifted her audience from an imaginary friend to a public witness — a change that transformed the diary from private confession to historical document."

Copia exercise: Rewrite the example sentence keeping the word AUDIENCE and the dash structure. Change the subject: write about a different type of writer (a blogger, a poet, a journalist) who shifts their audience mid-work and what that shift does to the writing.

The diary Anne revised is the one we read. She calls it “Het Achterhuis” (The Secret Annex) in her notes — she was already thinking of it as a title. She was fifteen years old. The distance between the girl who wrote to Kitty for companionship and the writer who revised her diary for a future public is the distance Anne Frank traveled inside the Annex — and it is the distance this week’s lessons are about.

Comprehension Check

1. Why is it significant that Anne revised her diary after hearing the radio broadcast? What does this tell you about how she saw herself?

Write 2 sentences.

2. How does knowing that Anne revised the diary change how you read it? Does it feel more or less authentic, or does authenticity work differently here?

Write 2 sentences. This is an analytical question — there is no single right answer.

3. Anne identifies as a writer. Name one piece of evidence from the diary that supports this claim — something she says or does that shows writerly ambition rather than just the desire to express her feelings.

Write 1–2 sentences.
