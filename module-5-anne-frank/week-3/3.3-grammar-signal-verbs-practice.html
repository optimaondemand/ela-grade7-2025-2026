Grammar 3.3: Signal Verbs and Explanation Sentences

Making sure every quotation earns its place in your paragraph

The Signal Verb Is a Claim

The signal verb you choose before a quotation is not neutral. When you write “Anne says,” you are telling the reader nothing about how to interpret what follows. When you write “Anne insists,” you are already making an argument: that this statement carries conviction, that it is not tentative, that Anne believes it strongly enough to repeat or emphasize it. The signal verb is where the analytical work begins.

This means choosing the wrong signal verb is a kind of error — not a grammatical one, but an interpretive one. If Anne is expressing doubt and you write “Anne declares,” you have misread the passage. If she is making an unusually forceful statement and you write “Anne notes,” you have undersold it.

Signal Verb Categories
Category Verbs Use when the writer is…
Confident argues, insists, declares, maintains, contends making a strong, certain claim
Observing notes, observes, records, describes making a factual or perceptual claim without strong emotion
Questioning wonders, questions, asks, reflects uncertain, searching, or thinking through a problem
Admitting admits, acknowledges, concedes, confesses acknowledging something difficult or contrary to their main argument
Hoping / Desiring longs, hopes, desires, yearns (use sparingly) expressing wish or aspiration
The Explanation Sentence

After the quotation, the explanation sentence must do real analytical work. It should not restate the quotation in different words (“In other words, Anne says she wants to live after death”). It should say what the quotation reveals, argues, or demonstrates about your thesis claim.

Weak explanation: "This shows that Anne wants to live after her death by writing."
Strong explanation: "This reveals that Anne has begun to understand writing not as expression but as survival — not of her body, which she knows is in danger, but of her selfhood, which she is determined to preserve beyond the reach of what is trying to erase her."
Exercise 1: Choose the Right Signal Verb

For each quotation, choose the best signal verb and explain in one sentence why you chose it over the others offered.

  1. "I don't want to have lived in vain like most people. I want to be useful or bring enjoyment to all people, even those I've never met." Options: says / insists / wonders
  2. "Sometimes I think God is trying to test me, both now and in the future... I'll just have to be good." Options: argues / reflects / declares
  3. "I know what I want, I have a goal, I have opinions, I have a religion and love. Let me be myself and then I am satisfied." Options: notes / insists / wonders
