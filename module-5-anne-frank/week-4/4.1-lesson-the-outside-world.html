7th Grade ELA — Module 5
The Diary of a Young Girl — Week 4 — Lesson 4.1

The Outside World: Fear, Hope, and the Last Entries

Anne’s growing awareness of the world beyond the Annex, and what the final silence means

Warm-Up

When you can’t know what’s happening around you — when you’re waiting for news, or when the situation is out of your control — what do you hold on to? What makes it possible to stay hopeful without being dishonest about the danger? Write 2–3 sentences.

The Lesson

The later entries of the diary carry a double awareness that is unlike anything in the earlier entries. Anne knows more now — she has heard more news about what is happening to Jews in the occupied territories, she has been in hiding long enough to understand that this is not a temporary inconvenience but a reality that may not end the way she hopes. And yet the final entries are also marked by a kind of fierce, deliberate hope — not naive optimism, but something harder: the refusal to let what she knows about the world determine who she will be inside it.

The last entry in the diary is dated August 1, 1944. Three days later, the hiding place was discovered. The diary ends without ceremony — there is no final entry that knows it is the final entry. Anne was writing as someone who expected to write again. The silence after the last entry is not part of the text; it is imposed on it from outside. And this is perhaps the most powerful thing about the diary as a document: the ending is not an ending Anne chose. It is the interruption of a life still in motion.

This week’s grammar structure is the PERIODIC SENTENCE: a sentence in which the main clause is delayed to the end, so that everything before it builds toward the final statement. Periodic sentences are among the most powerful in the literary toolkit because they make the reader wait — they create suspense, weight, and inevitability. Anne uses periodic sentence structures in her most serious and reflective entries.

Vocabulary

PERIODIC SENTENCE — a sentence in which the main clause (the part that makes the central statement) is withheld until the end, after a series of subordinate clauses, phrases, or other elements that build context and anticipation.

Example: "Despite two years of confinement, despite the fear that came with every sound on the stairs, despite knowing what was happening to people like her in the world beyond the Annex, Anne Frank did not stop believing in the goodness she wanted to find."

Copia exercise: Rewrite this sentence keeping the three "despite" phrases and the PERIODIC SENTENCE structure. Change Anne Frank to a different subject and find a different final claim. The three "despite" phrases should build: each one harder to dismiss than the last.

One of the most famous lines in the diary comes from an entry in July 1944, less than a month before the arrest: “It’s difficult in times like these: ideals, dreams and cherished hopes rise within us, only to be met by the horrible truth and be shattered. It’s a wonder I haven’t abandoned all my ideals, they seem so absurd and impractical. Yet I cling to them because I still believe, in spite of everything, that people are truly good at heart.” Anne knows this may not be rational. She holds it anyway. That is not naivety — it is a choice, made deliberately, about who she will be.

Comprehension Check

1. The last diary entry is dated August 1, 1944. Anne does not know it is the last entry. How does knowing this as a reader change the way you experience that entry?

Write 2 sentences. This is a literary question about dramatic irony.

2. Anne writes that she still believes "people are truly good at heart" even after everything she has experienced and witnessed. How do you interpret this claim? Is it hope, denial, or something else?

Write 2 sentences. There is no single right answer — argue your interpretation.

3. Name one periodic sentence structure in the diary (you may write one inspired by Anne’s style if you can’t find an exact one). What does the delay of the main clause do?

Write 1–2 sentences.
