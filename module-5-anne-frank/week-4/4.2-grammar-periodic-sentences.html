Grammar 4.2: The Periodic Sentence

How to make a reader wait — and why the wait matters

Periodic vs. Cumulative Sentences

Most sentences are cumulative: the main clause comes first, and additional information is added after it. This is the natural English word order, and it is clear and efficient. But it is also the least emphatic sentence form available — because the most important information comes first, before the reader has been prepared for it.

A periodic sentence reverses this: all the subordinate material — the context, the concessions, the complications — comes first, and the main clause arrives at the end. The reader must hold everything in suspension until the final statement lands. Done well, periodic sentences feel inevitable — as though everything before the main clause was a preparation the sentence required.

Type Structure Example
Cumulative Main clause first, details after "Anne did not stop writing, even when the danger grew closer, even when the news from outside was terrible, even when hope seemed irrational."
Periodic Details first, main clause at end "Even when the danger grew closer, even when the news from outside was terrible, even when hope seemed irrational, Anne did not stop writing."
Building a Periodic Sentence

The most common patterns for building the opening of a periodic sentence:

Repeated prepositional phrases: "In the silence of the Annex, in the fear of discovery, in the longing for an ordinary life, Anne wrote."
Repeated subordinate clauses: "Although she knew the danger, although she had heard the rumors, although she could not pretend the world was safe, she still believed."
Repeated participial phrases: "Having survived two years in hiding, having watched her family diminish under pressure, having written herself into existence on page after page, Anne arrived at the last entry still in motion."
The rule: The opening elements must build — each one a little more charged than the last. A periodic sentence where all three openers feel equally weighted is weak. The third opener should feel like the one that makes the wait worth it.
Exercise 1: Identify and Convert

Each sentence below is cumulative. Convert it to a periodic sentence by moving the main clause to the end. Then write one sentence explaining what changes — what the periodic version does that the cumulative version cannot.
