The Diary of a Young Girl — Week 4 — VR 4.5

VR 4.5: The Diary as Argument

What does the diary, as a whole, argue? Stage your answer.

What You’re Doing

Still in Session ELA7-M5-W4, you will stage your argument about what the diary, taken as a whole, claims or demonstrates. Not what it is about — what it argues. This is preparation for your essay thesis. The Whiteboard sentence is your thesis statement. The composition is your evidence in spatial form.

What You’ll Need
  • Device: VR headset
  • Session ID: ELA7-M5-W4 — Persistent Session (move to a new area)
What to Do
1

Stage your thesis in IFX (4–6 IFX)

Choose IFX that represent the argument you are making in your essay — not the diary in general, but your specific claim about what the diary does. If your essay argues about Anne’s voice as an act of resistance, what does that look like spatially? If it argues about the diary as a tool for preserving identity, what IFX make that argument?

2

Place a Whiteboard IFX: your thesis

Write your thesis sentence on the Whiteboard. Requirements: it must name a formal or rhetorical choice Anne made (her use of voice, her use of audience, her use of dash/direct address, her use of parallel structure in the diary’s reflective entries) AND state what that choice does for the diary’s argument. Label [THESIS]. Add two or three pieces of evidence below the thesis on the Whiteboard: quotations or moments from the diary that support your claim. Label [EV1], [EV2], [EV3].

3

Make a 2-minute recording

State your thesis, walk through your evidence, and explain how each piece of evidence supports your claim. This is your essay spoken aloud. What doesn’t make sense in the recording is what you fix before writing the essay.

4

Take a snapshot and submit

Name it YourLastName_M5_4.5. Upload snapshot + paste recording link to Canvas. Include your one-sentence thesis in the Canvas text box.

Submission

Upload snapshot + paste recording link + one-sentence thesis to Canvas. Estimated time: 25–30 minutes in headset.

Facilitator Note

The Whiteboard thesis is the main assessment here. A weak thesis: "Anne Frank's diary is about her life in hiding." A developing thesis: "Anne Frank's diary preserves her voice and identity." A strong thesis: "By writing to an imagined future audience and revising her diary to be read as a historical document, Anne Frank transforms private expression into a public act of resistance — using the formal structure of the diary to argue that the self cannot be erased as long as it can write." The recording is where you find out whether the student actually understands their own argument: students who can say it clearly in 2 minutes have the essay; students who keep slipping into plot summary need more time before writing.
