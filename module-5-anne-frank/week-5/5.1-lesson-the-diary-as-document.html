7th Grade ELA — Module 5
The Diary of a Young Girl — Week 5 — Lesson 5.1

The Diary as Document: What Survived and Why It Matters

Looking at the diary as a whole: what it is, what it does, what it asks of us

The Module Question, Revisited
How do people preserve who they are when everything around them tries to erase them?
Warm-Up

If you knew that something you wrote would be read by millions of people long after you were gone, would you be proud of it? Embarrassed? Surprised? Write 2–3 sentences about what it would feel like to know your most private writing had become a public document.

The Lesson

Otto Frank survived Auschwitz and returned to Amsterdam in June 1945. When he arrived, Miep Gies — one of the non-Jewish friends who had sheltered the Annex residents and brought them food — handed him a bundle of papers she had rescued from the floor of the Annex the day of the arrest. She had saved the diary without reading it, intending to return it to Anne when the war ended. When Otto learned that Anne had not survived, he began reading it for the first time.

Otto Frank spent two years arranging for the diary to be published. He edited it, combining Anne’s two versions (the spontaneous original and the revised version) and removing some passages he felt were too private or too harsh. The book was published in Dutch in 1947, translated into German and French, and eventually into dozens of languages. It has sold tens of millions of copies. The Anne Frank House in Amsterdam, where the Secret Annex is preserved, receives more than a million visitors a year.

This is the context in which you have been reading. You are not one of the first readers; you are one of millions. And yet the diary does not feel like a document written for millions — it feels written for you, specifically, which is the most powerful quality of Anne’s voice. She imagined a reader she could not know, and every reader who comes to the diary feels like the one she imagined.

Now you are writing your essay. You have the full diary, five weeks of VR work, grammar practice, and quickwrites as preparation. The essay asks you to take one rhetorical or formal choice Anne made — her use of voice, her use of the dash as an expressive tool, her use of parallel structure in her most serious entries, or her relationship to audience — and argue specifically what that choice does for the diary’s argument about identity and resistance.

Full-Diary Comprehension Check

1. Otto Frank edited the diary before publication, removing some passages he felt were too private. Does knowing this change how you read it? What does it mean that the diary we have is itself a product of choices?

Write 2 sentences. This is an analytical question with no single right answer.

2. Anne imagined a future audience for her diary. You are that audience. Name one moment in the diary where you felt directly addressed — where Anne’s voice seemed to know you would be reading. What did she do to create that effect?

Write 2 sentences.

3. Write your one-sentence essay thesis: name a formal or rhetorical choice Anne made AND state what that choice does for the diary’s argument about preserving identity.

One sentence. This is practice for VR 5.5.
