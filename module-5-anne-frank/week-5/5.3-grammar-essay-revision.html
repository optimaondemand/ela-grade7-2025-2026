Grammar 5.3: Revising for Analytical Precision

What to look for when you revise a literary analysis essay

Revision Is Not Proofreading

Proofreading catches errors: wrong punctuation, misspelled words, missing quotation marks. Revision is larger: it asks whether your argument actually works. You can fix every comma in a paragraph and still have a paragraph that doesn’t say anything. Revision is where you find out if the paragraph says something.

The four questions below are the revision checklist for your Module 5 essay. Work through each one on every paragraph you have written.

Four Revision Questions
1. Does my thesis name the choice AND what it does?

"Anne Frank uses a distinctive voice" names the choice but not what it does. "Anne Frank’s use of the dash to insert emphatic asides into her sentences constructs a voice so immediate and unguarded that the reader cannot experience the diary as a historical artifact — only as a living person still in mid-thought" names the choice AND argues its effect.

2. Does my evidence actually support my thesis, or is it just a scene I like?

Every quotation or scene reference should connect directly to the thesis claim. If you can’t write one sentence explaining how this specific quotation demonstrates your thesis, the quotation probably doesn’t belong in this essay. It may belong in a different essay — save it.

3. Does my explanation sentence do real work, or does it just restate the quotation?

"In other words, Anne is saying she wants to live after death through her writing" is restating. "This reveals that Anne understood the diary as a form of testimony — a document designed to exist independently of whether she survived to see it read" is explaining. Test: does your explanation sentence contain a word or idea that was not already in the quotation? If not, it’s restating.

4. Does my conclusion add something new, or does it just repeat the thesis?

A conclusion that only restates the thesis is a summary. A conclusion that returns to the essential question and states what the diary taught you that you didn’t know before is a conclusion. The essential question is: How do people preserve who they are when everything around them tries to erase them? What does the diary argue about this question that you couldn’t have answered the same way before you read it?

Exercise: Self-Revision

Take the paragraph you wrote in Grammar 5.2 Exercise 2. Apply all four revision questions to it. For each question, write one sentence labeled [R1], [R2], [R3], [R4] that either confirms the paragraph meets the criterion or identifies what needs to change. Then revise one sentence in the paragraph based on what you found — label the revision [REV] and explain in a sentence what you changed and why.

A paragraph that survives all four revision questions is ready to go into your essay.
