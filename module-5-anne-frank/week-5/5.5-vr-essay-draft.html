The Diary of a Young Girl — Week 5 — VR 5.5

VR 5.5: Stage Your Essay Argument

Your thesis, your evidence, your voice — before you commit it to prose

What You’re Doing

Stage your essay argument in VR before you write the prose version. This is a spatial draft: your thesis on a Whiteboard, your evidence in IFX, your argument in a 2–3 minute recording. What doesn’t make sense in the recording is what you fix before you write.

What You’ll Need
  • Device: VR headset
  • Session ID: ELA7-M5-W5 — Persistent Session
  • Bring: Your thesis (from Grammar 5.2 or Lesson 5.1) and two or three specific quotations you plan to use
What to Do
1

Stage your evidence (4–6 IFX)

Choose IFX representing the specific evidence your essay will use. Not the diary in general — your particular argument’s evidence. If your essay is about voice, what IFX captures the quality of voice you are arguing about? If it is about audience, what IFX represents the relationship between Anne and her imagined reader?

2

Place a Whiteboard IFX: thesis + evidence + concession

Write on the Whiteboard: [THESIS] your one-sentence thesis (formal choice + what it does); [EV1] [EV2] your two or three quotations or evidence items; [CON] your concession — the strongest objection to your thesis and your counter.

3

Record your argument (2–3 minutes)

State your thesis, walk through your evidence and what it demonstrates, state your concession and counter. This is your essay spoken aloud. If you catch yourself summarizing the diary instead of arguing about craft, stop and restart.

4

Take a snapshot and submit

Name it YourLastName_M5_5.5. Upload snapshot + recording link. In the Canvas text box: your one-sentence thesis + one sentence about what you realized while recording that you need to fix.

The Four Essay Focus Options
Option A: Anne’s Voice
Argue what Anne’s distinctive voice does for the diary’s argument about identity. Thesis must name the voice as a formal choice AND state what it does.
Option B: The Dash as Expression
Argue what Anne’s use of the em dash — specifically its role in foregrounding asides and pivots — does for the diary’s construction of an immediate, unguarded self.
Option C: Audience
Argue how Anne’s construction of an imagined future audience changed the diary from private record to public document, and what that change means for how the diary argues about identity and resistance.
Option D: Identity Claims
Argue how Anne’s practice of writing explicit identity claims — declarative statements about who she is and what she values — functions as an act of resistance rather than just self-expression.
Submission

Upload snapshot + paste recording link + thesis + fix sentence to Canvas. Estimated time: 25–35 minutes in headset.

Facilitator Note

The one-sentence thesis submitted in the Canvas text box is the main assessment item here. A strong thesis: "By addressing her diary to a future audience she could not know and revising her entries to be read as a historical document, Anne Frank transforms the act of private writing into a form of public resistance — insisting, through the formal choices of her voice, that her selfhood was worth preserving beyond the reach of what was trying to erase it." A developing thesis: "Anne Frank's use of voice in her diary helps preserve her identity." Students who submit a developing thesis need a brief teacher comment before they write the essay: one question that pushes the thesis from observation to argument.
