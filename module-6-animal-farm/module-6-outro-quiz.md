## Quiz Questions

**Question 1** — Essay (required)
*The Essential Question, final answer*

At the start of this module, you answered this question based on what you already knew or believed. Now answer it again — based on what you have read, argued, and written across five weeks of Animal Farm.

**How does language shape who holds power, and who loses it?**

Your answer should be 3–4 sentences. Reference at least one specific moment from the novella — a commandment, a speech, a slogan, a scene — and name what Orwell did formally (not just what happened in the plot) to make that moment do its work.

*(Essay response — ungraded, teacher compares to intro answer to track growth)*

**Question 2** — Multiple Choice (Likert)
*Confidence check — teacher early-warning signal*

How confident do you feel that you understand the central argument of Animal Farm — not the plot, but what the novella argues about language and power, and how its formal choices make that argument?

A. Very confident — I could explain it to someone who hasn't read the book and make them see what I see.
B. Mostly confident — I understand it, but I am not sure I could explain every piece of it clearly.
C. Somewhat confident — I understand parts of it, but some of the formal argument still feels murky.
D. Not very confident — I followed the plot but the argument about language and power still feels unclear to me.

**Question 5** — Essay
*Virtue reflection*

Animal Farm is a book about what happens when courage fails — when the animals who could resist don't, and when language is used to make resistance feel impossible.

Name one moment in the novella when an animal had the chance to resist and didn't — or almost did and couldn't. Then answer: what would have needed to be different — in the language of Animal Farm's world, not in the plot — for resistance to have been possible?

You don't need to write more than 3–4 sentences. This is a reflection, not an essay argument.

*(Essay response — ungraded, teacher reads for insight into student engagement with the module's central ethical question)*
