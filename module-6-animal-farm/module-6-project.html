Animal Farm — Module Project

Module 6 Project: Literary Analysis Essay

How language and power work in Animal Farm — argued through one formal choice

The Assignment

Write a 400–500 word literary analysis essay arguing how one formal or rhetorical choice Orwell makes in Animal Farm contributes to the novella’s argument about language and power. This is not a plot summary and not a biography of Orwell. It is an argument about what the novella does — and how Orwell made it do that.

Choose One Focus
Option A: The Passive Voice as Political Tool
Argue how Orwell's use of passive voice constructions in Squealer's speeches makes an argument about how propaganda works to hide agency and normalize power. Your thesis must name the passive voice as a formal choice AND argue what it does.
Option B: The Changing Commandments
Argue how Orwell's technique of incremental revision — adding qualifying phrases to absolute laws — makes a specific argument about the relationship between written law and power, and about what language cannot protect on its own.
Option C: Anaphora in Old Major’s Speech
Argue how Old Major's use of anaphora, and the novella's subsequent reduction of revolutionary language to a two-word slogan, represents Orwell's argument about how language can be captured and simplified by those who inherit the power a revolution generates.
Option D: The Final Commandment as Irony
Argue how the grammatical impossibility of "All animals are equal, but some animals are more equal than others" works as the novella's central ironic device — and what it argues about the relationship between language, law, and the legitimation of power.
Required Essay Structure
Introduction (50–75 words)
Open with a claim about the novella, not with "George Orwell was born in 1903." Use [ANAPHORA] or [WHEREAS] somewhere in the introduction. End with your thesis — one sentence naming the formal choice and what it does, labeled [THESIS].
Body Paragraph 1 (100–125 words)
First piece of evidence. Use [Q][SIGNAL] to introduce your quotation. Use [APP] to identify the character or element's allegorical role. Your explanation must add something not already in the quotation.
Body Paragraph 2 (100–125 words)
Second piece of evidence. Use [Q][SIGNAL] to introduce your quotation. Use [WHEREAS] or [ALTHOUGH] somewhere in your explanation to name the gap between appearance and reality. Acknowledge the strongest objection to your thesis [CON] and counter it.
Conclusion (75–100 words)
Return to the essential question: How does language shape who holds power, and who loses it? Your conclusion should state what the novella taught you that you couldn't have answered the same way before reading it. Do not restate your thesis word for word — extend it.
Required Tags in Your Essay
[THESIS] — your one-sentence thesis
[ANAPHORA] or [WHEREAS] — in the introduction
[Q][SIGNAL] — before each quotation
[APP] — your appositive phrase
[WHEREAS] or [ALTHOUGH] — in Body 2
[CON] — your concession in Body 2
Grading Rubric (100 points)
Criterion 25 pts 18 pts 10 pts 0 pts
Thesis and Argument Thesis names a formal choice AND argues its specific effect. Argument is consistent throughout. Thesis names a choice but the effect is vague or inconsistently supported. Thesis observes rather than argues ("Orwell uses passive voice"). No thesis or essay is a plot summary.
Evidence and Explanation Quotations directly demonstrate the formal claim. Explanation sentences interpret — they add something the quotation does not say. Quotations relate to the thesis. At least one explanation moves beyond restatement. Quotations present but explanation mostly restates. No textual evidence.
Grammar Structures All required structures present, correctly used, and labeled. Most structures present and labeled. One missing or incorrect. Two or more structures missing or unlabeled. Required structures not attempted.
Conclusion and Essential Question Conclusion answers the essential question using the novella's formal argument in a way that adds something new. Conclusion addresses the essential question but stays close to the thesis restatement. Conclusion only restates the thesis. No conclusion.
