7th Grade ELA — Module 6 — Week 1
Animal Farm — Lesson 1.1

1.1 Lesson: The Rebellion

Chapters 1–3 — How a dream becomes a doctrine

Essential Question — Module 6

How does language shape who holds power, and who loses it?

Historical Context

George Orwell published Animal Farm in 1945, after years of trying to find a publisher willing to print it — most rejected it because, during World War II, the Soviet Union was an Allied power and the book was obviously a criticism of Stalin's regime. Orwell had fought in the Spanish Civil War and watched socialist ideals be crushed by political corruption firsthand. The novella takes place on a farm in England, but every character, event, and slogan corresponds to something in Soviet history: Old Major is Karl Marx, Napoleon is Stalin, Snowball is Trotsky, and the Seven Commandments mirror the distortion of revolutionary ideology over time. Knowing this context is useful — but the novella works even without it, because Orwell was writing about something larger than one country's history.

Warm-Up

Think of a rule — in your home, school, or a sport — that started for a good reason but has since been bent or quietly changed. What happened to the original reason? Write two sentences.

Lesson

Animal Farm is an allegory — a story in which characters, settings, and events stand for something beyond themselves. Every element of the farm has a double life: the animals represent classes of people, the pigs represent political leaders, and the farm itself represents any society in which one group seizes power and uses language to justify keeping it. What makes Orwell's allegory devastating is not the obvious parallels to Soviet history but the precision with which he shows how an idea that begins in genuine injustice can be hollowed out, word by word, until the original meaning is entirely gone. Old Major's speech in Chapter 1 is the seed of everything that follows: it names the animals' suffering accurately and proposes a remedy. But the speech is also, already, a piece of rhetoric — it uses repetition, emotional appeal, and a simple binary ("man vs. animal") to do its work. By Chapter 3, the pigs have reduced Old Major's complex vision to a single slogan: "Four legs good, two legs bad." This module asks you to watch how that reduction happens, who benefits from it, and what tools — specifically, what language tools — make it possible.

Vocabulary: ALLEGORY

A narrative in which characters and events represent abstract ideas or historical figures, so that the story operates simultaneously on a literal level (a farm, some animals) and a symbolic level (a society, its leaders, its people).

Example from the text: "Old Major, a prize Middle White boar, had a strange dream and called the animals of the farm together to share it."

Copia exercise: Rewrite the sentence above, keeping "Old Major" and the structure (an animal called others together to share something), but change the content so it represents something in your own world — a person, a setting, a message.

Reading

Read Chapters 1–3 of Animal Farm. Pay particular attention to Old Major's speech in Chapter 1 and to the Seven Commandments in Chapter 2. Notice the moment in Chapter 3 when the Seven Commandments are reduced to a single slogan. That reduction is the first act of the allegory.

Comprehension Check

1. What does Old Major claim is the source of all the animals' suffering?

Write one sentence. Then name one specific phrase from his speech that makes this claim.

2. The Seven Commandments are eventually summarized as "Four legs good, two legs bad." What is lost in that reduction?

Name one specific thing the commandments said that the slogan does not say.

3. By the end of Chapter 3, the pigs have separated themselves from the other animals in one practical way. What is it, and what does it suggest about the rebellion's ideals?

One to two sentences.
