Grammar 1.2: Appositive Phrases

Defining, identifying, and characterizing in one move

What an Appositive Is

An appositive is a noun or noun phrase placed beside another noun to identify, define, or describe it. It renames or clarifies the noun it follows. An appositive phrase is the appositive plus any words that modify it.

Example from Animal Farm:

"Old Major, a prize Middle White boar, was twelve years old and had lately grown rather stout."

The underlined phrase is the appositive. It renames "Old Major" and tells us something about him — not through a separate sentence, but inside the sentence itself. The commas signal that this information could be removed without breaking the sentence.

Analytical example:

"The Seven Commandments, the founding laws of Animal Farm, are changed so gradually that most animals do not notice when they disappear."

Here the appositive "the founding laws of Animal Farm" is doing analytical work — it tells the reader what the Seven Commandments meant, which makes the claim that they are quietly erased more powerful.

Two Types of Appositives
Type Tag Punctuation When to use it
Nonrestrictive [APP-NR] Set off by commas The noun is already identified; the appositive adds extra information. Remove it and the sentence still makes sense.
Restrictive [APP-R] No commas The appositive is essential to identify which noun you mean. Remove it and the sentence loses a necessary distinction.
Nonrestrictive: "Squealer, the pigs' chief propagandist, assured the animals that Snowball had always been a traitor." — We already know who Squealer is; "the pigs' chief propagandist" adds description.
Restrictive: "The commandment 'All animals are equal' was the last to be altered." — The appositive identifies which commandment; without it, we don't know which one.
Exercise 1: Identify and Classify

Underline the appositive phrase in each sentence. Label it [APP-NR] or [APP-R]. Then write one sentence explaining what work the appositive is doing — what does it add to the meaning of the sentence?

1. Napoleon, the largest of the pigs, stepped forward and took control of the Sunday meetings.
2. The slogan "Four legs good, two legs bad" was the only rule Boxer could remember.
3. Boxer, the strongest animal on the farm, worked longer than any of the others but received no more food.
4. The windmill, the pigs' great project, was blown down by a storm — and the pigs blamed Snowball.
Exercise 2: Write Your Own

Write two sentences about Animal Farm using appositive phrases, labeled [APP-NR] or [APP-R] in brackets.

  1. One sentence that introduces a character using a nonrestrictive appositive that makes an analytical observation about their role in the allegory.
  2. One sentence that uses a restrictive appositive to identify which commandment, rule, or event you are referring to.
Your appositive should do more than just rename — it should add something a reader needs in order to understand your analytical point.
