VR 1.5: Build an Allegory Map

Name what each character represents — and argue what the pairing tells us

What You’re Doing

Remain in the same Session from VR 1.4. Move to a new area of the Location. You are building a spatial allegory map — a spatial arrangement showing which characters represent which real-world figures or groups, with a key claim about what each pairing argues. The goal is not just identification (“Napoleon = Stalin”) but interpretation: what does it mean that Orwell chose these animals to represent these figures?

What You’ll Need
  • Device: VR headset
  • Session ID: ELA7-M6-W1 — same Persistent Session as VR 1.4
  • Bring: Your knowledge of the Chapters 1–3 characters and your historical context notes
What to Do
1

Place a Whiteboard IFX: the allegory map

On the Whiteboard, write the following structure for at least three characters. Use appositive form: [Character], [what they represent in the allegory], [one-sentence claim about what Orwell's choice of this animal reveals]. You must include Old Major, Napoleon, and one animal of your choice (Boxer, Squealer, the sheep, the dogs, Benjamin, or Mollie).

2

Place 1–2 IFX for each character on the map

Arrange IFX near each Whiteboard entry to represent the character. The IFX should suggest something about their role — Old Major as visionary, Napoleon as authority, your chosen character as what they represent. The spatial arrangement should argue something: characters in opposition, characters in hierarchy, or characters in proximity that reveals a relationship.

3

Record a 60–90 second explanation

Walk your viewer through the map. For each character, state the appositive (who they represent), then state one claim about what Orwell's choice of animal reveals about the real-world figure. Do not just name — argue.

Submission

Upload snapshot YourLastName_M6_1.5 + paste recording link to Canvas. Estimated time: 25–30 minutes in headset.

Facilitator Note

The claim sentence is the main assessment item here. "Napoleon = Stalin" is identification. "Napoleon, Orwell's portrait of Stalin, is a pig because pigs are associated with intelligence, self-interest, and appetite — qualities Orwell treats not as exceptional but as predictable in anyone who accumulates power" is interpretation. Students who struggle with the claim sentence may need a teacher prompt: "Why did Orwell make this character a pig/horse/sheep specifically, rather than some other animal?"
