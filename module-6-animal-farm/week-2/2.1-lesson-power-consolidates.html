7th Grade ELA — Module 6 — Week 2
Animal Farm — Lesson 2.1

2.1 Lesson: Power Consolidates

Chapters 4–6 — How Squealer uses language to erase what happened

Warm-Up

There is a difference between "The window was broken" and "I broke the window." Both sentences describe the same event, but one of them names who did it and one of them doesn't. When would you choose the first version over the second? Write two sentences.

Lesson

By Chapter 6, Napoleon has ended the Sunday meetings, assumed full decision-making authority, and expelled Snowball from the farm — then declared that Snowball was a traitor all along. How does he do this without open revolt? Through Squealer. Squealer, the pigs' propagandist, is the character through whom Orwell explores one of the novella's central arguments: that language is not just a tool for communicating what happened, but for determining what is believed to have happened. Squealer does not lie clumsily; he lies structurally. He uses the passive voice to erase agency (“It has been decided” rather than “Napoleon has decided”), selective statistics to make the farm seem more productive than it is, and the threat of Jones's return as a trump card that shuts down questioning. The most important lesson Chapters 4–6 teach is not that Napoleon is cruel — that is obvious — but that cruelty requires a language system to normalize it. Squealer is that system.

Vocabulary: PASSIVE VOICE

A grammatical construction in which the subject of the sentence receives the action rather than performing it, allowing the agent (who did the action) to be omitted or moved to a secondary position. In active voice: "Napoleon abolished the Sunday meetings." In passive voice: "The Sunday meetings were abolished." Who abolished them? The passive voice does not say.

Example from the text: "It was announced that from now on the Sunday morning Meetings would come to an end."

Copia exercise: Rewrite the sentence above in active voice. Then write a sentence about your own school using the passive voice to hide who made a decision.

Reading

Read Chapters 4–6 of Animal Farm. Find at least two places where Squealer appears and mark them. Notice whether Orwell uses active or passive voice in Squealer's speeches, and compare it to how the narration describes events.

Comprehension Check

1. Napoleon ends the Sunday meetings and begins making all decisions himself. How does Squealer convince the other animals this is actually better for them?

Name one specific argument Squealer makes.

2. After Snowball is expelled, Squealer claims that Snowball was never truly a hero of the Battle of the Cowshed — that he was, in fact, a traitor from the beginning. What is the animals' response, and what does their response reveal?

One to two sentences.

3. In Chapter 6, the pigs begin sleeping in the farmhouse and using the beds — which violates one of the original commandments. How does Squealer resolve this contradiction?

One sentence. Then identify what this tells you about the function of the Seven Commandments at this point in the story.
