Grammar 2.2: Active and Passive Voice

How the choice of voice reveals — or conceals — who is responsible

The Difference
Voice Tag Structure Effect
Active [ACTIVE] Subject performs the action: Napoleon abolished the meetings. Clear agency: we know who did it.
Passive [PASSIVE] Subject receives the action: The meetings were abolished. Hidden agency: who did it is absent or moved to a "by" phrase.

The passive voice is not always wrong. It is a deliberate rhetorical choice. The question a careful reader asks is: who benefits from hiding the agent? In Animal Farm, the pigs benefit every time the passive voice is used, because it hides the fact that it is always the pigs making the decisions.

How to Form the Passive Voice

Passive voice = a form of to be + the past participle of the main verb.

"The commandments were changed." (to be: were + past participle: changed)
"It has been decided that the pigs shall sleep in beds." (to be: has been + past participle: decided)
"Snowball was declared a traitor." (to be: was + past participle: declared)
Squealer’s Passive Voice Toolkit

Notice how often Squealer's speeches (and the narration of Napoleonic decisions) use the passive voice:

"It was announced that from now on the Sunday morning Meetings would come to an end." — Who announced it? The passive voice doesn't say.
"It had been found necessary to make certain readjustments." — Who found it necessary? Who made the readjustments?
"It was put to the vote." — Who put it to the vote? Were all animals actually included?

Each of these passive constructions erases Napoleon's name while keeping his power intact.

Exercise 1: Identify and Convert

Each sentence below is in passive voice. Label it [PASSIVE], identify who the hidden agent is (or could be, based on the story), and rewrite it in active voice labeled [ACTIVE]. Then write one sentence explaining what changes when you name the agent.

1. It was decided that Animal Farm should trade with neighboring farms.
2. Snowball was declared a traitor and an enemy of Animal Farm.
3. The milk and windfalls were to be reserved for the pigs alone.
Exercise 2: Write Your Own Squealer

Write three sentences in Squealer's voice announcing a new unfair policy. Use the passive voice deliberately to hide who made the decision. Label each sentence [PASSIVE] and underline the passive construction. Then write one sentence of analysis, labeled [ACTIVE], that names Napoleon as the actual agent.

If your Squealer sentences sound reasonable even though they describe something unfair, you have done the exercise correctly.
