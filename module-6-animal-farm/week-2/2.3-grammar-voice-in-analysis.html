Grammar 2.3: Voice as a Rhetorical Choice

When to use active or passive voice — and how to analyze the choice in literature

When Passive Voice Is the Right Choice

Passive voice is not always wrong. Good writers use it deliberately. Three situations where the passive voice is the better choice:

1. The agent is unknown: "The commandment was altered sometime during the night." (We don't know exactly who did it — or Orwell wants us to feel that uncertainty.)
2. The agent is obvious or unimportant: "The harvest was gathered in record time." (All the animals gathered it — naming each one is unnecessary.)
3. The writer wants to emphasize the action over the agent: "Boxer was sent to the knacker's." (Orwell puts "Boxer" at the front to emphasize what happens to him, not who did it — the reader already feels who did it.)
How to Analyze Voice in Literary Analysis

When you notice a passive construction in a text you are analyzing, ask three questions:

  1. Who is the hidden agent — who is not named?
  2. Who benefits from the agent being unnamed?
  3. What would the sentence reveal if it were in active voice?
Model analysis sentence:
"When Orwell writes [PASSIVE] 'It was announced that the Sunday Meetings would come to an end,' he uses the passive voice to perform the novel's central argument: [ACTIVE] Napoleon has made all the decisions, but the passive construction allows those decisions to appear as though they emerged from no particular person — as though power acts on its own, without a hand to guide it."
Exercise 1: Analyze a Voice Choice

Read each sentence from Animal Farm below. For each: (1) label it [ACTIVE] or [PASSIVE], (2) write one sentence answering the question "who benefits from this choice of voice?" and (3) write the sentence in the other voice to show what changes.

1. "It had been found necessary to make certain readjustments in the rations."
2. "Napoleon led them back to the store-shed and served out a double ration of corn to everybody."
3. "The pigs did not actually work, but directed and supervised the others."
Exercise 2: Write an Analytical Paragraph (Four Sentences)

Write a four-sentence analytical paragraph arguing that Orwell uses the passive voice strategically in Animal Farm to represent how propaganda works. Use the following structure and label each sentence:

  1. [APP] — introduce Squealer using an appositive that names his allegorical role
  2. [Q][SIGNAL] — quote one passive-voice sentence from Chapters 4–6 using a signal verb
  3. [PASSIVE] / [ACTIVE] analysis — explain what the passive construction conceals and what naming the agent in active voice would reveal
  4. [DASH] — end with a sentence using a dash that connects this to the essential question
A strong analysis sentence names not just what the passive voice does, but why Orwell chose it — what argument it makes about power and language.
