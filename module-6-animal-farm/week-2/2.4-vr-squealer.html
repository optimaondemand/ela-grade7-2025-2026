VR 2.4: Stage Squealer’s Argument

How does propaganda use language to hide who is responsible?

What You’re Doing

Stage one of Squealer's propaganda speeches from Chapters 4–6 as a spatial composition. Your job is to represent both what Squealer says and what he is actually doing — the gap between his words and reality. Use IFX and sticky notes to make that gap visible.

What You’ll Need
  • Device: VR headset
  • Session ID: ELA7-M6-W2 — Persistent Session
  • Location: Civic / Political Building or Meeting Hall
  • Bring: A quotation from one of Squealer's speeches (Chapters 4–6) that uses passive voice
What to Do
1

Stage two sides of one moment (4–6 IFX total)

Divide your composition into two areas: What Squealer says (use IFX that represent the surface of his argument — safety, fairness, the threat of Jones) and What is actually happening (use IFX that represent the reality he is concealing). The two areas should be in visible tension. You are making the gap visible in space.

2

Place a sticky note with the passive-voice quotation and its active-voice conversion

On your sticky note: write [PASSIVE] + the quotation from Squealer's speech. Then write [ACTIVE] + the same sentence rewritten to name Napoleon. Then write one sentence labeled [ANALYSIS] explaining what changes when you name the agent.

3

Take a snapshot and submit

Name it YourLastName_M6_2.4. In the Canvas text box: one sentence describing what your composition argues about the relationship between passive voice and propaganda.

Submission

Upload snapshot YourLastName_M6_2.4 + one-sentence Canvas text. Estimated time: 20–25 minutes in headset.

Facilitator Note

The strongest compositions will make the gap between surface argument and concealed reality spatially obvious — two IFX clusters that don't match. The key analytical move is the [ACTIVE] rewrite: a student who can convert "It was decided" into "Napoleon decided" and explain what changes is understanding how propaganda works grammatically. Check that [ANALYSIS] sentences address why Orwell made this choice — not just what the passive voice does.
