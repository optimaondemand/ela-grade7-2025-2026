VR 2.5: Stage the Power Structure

How does hierarchy reveal itself spatially — before it announces itself openly?

What You’re Doing

Remain in the same Session from VR 2.4, in a new area. By the end of Chapter 6, Animal Farm has a clear power hierarchy — though it has not been officially declared. Stage that hierarchy spatially: who is at the top, who is in the middle, who is at the bottom, and what the spatial arrangement reveals about who benefits from the rebellion and who does not.

What You’ll Need
  • Device: VR headset
  • Session ID: ELA7-M6-W2 — same Persistent Session as VR 2.4
  • Bring: Your understanding of the farm's hierarchy by Chapter 6
What to Do
1

Build the hierarchy in IFX (6–8 IFX)

Arrange IFX to represent at least four characters or groups (Napoleon/pigs, Boxer/working animals, Squealer, the sheep/dogs). The spatial placement should argue something about the hierarchy — not just list it. Think about height, distance, proximity, and orientation. The arrangement itself should make an analytical claim.

2

Place a Whiteboard IFX with a thesis

On the Whiteboard, write: [APP] label for one character + [PASSIVE] one passive-voice sentence from Chapters 4–6 that helped establish this hierarchy + [THESIS] one sentence arguing what the power structure reveals about the allegory's argument about how power actually works.

3

Record a 60–90 second explanation and submit

Walk through the hierarchy composition. For each level, name who is there and use one sentence (labeled [APP] or [PASSIVE]) to explain how language helped place them there. Submit snapshot YourLastName_M6_2.5 + recording link.

Submission

Upload snapshot YourLastName_M6_2.5 + paste recording link to Canvas. Estimated time: 25–30 minutes in headset.

Facilitator Note

The [THESIS] sentence on the Whiteboard is the primary assessment item. Strong thesis: "The power structure of Animal Farm by Chapter 6 reveals that the rebellion changed who holds power without changing the structure of power itself — the pigs replaced Jones without replacing the hierarchy Jones represented." Developing thesis: "The pigs are at the top because they are smarter than the other animals." Help students push from description to argument by asking: "What does this hierarchy argue about power — not just who has it but how it works?"
