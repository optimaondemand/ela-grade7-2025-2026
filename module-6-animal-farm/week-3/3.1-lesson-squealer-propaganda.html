Animal Farm — Lesson 3.1

3.1 Lesson: The Language of Terror

Chapters 7–8 — Executions, revised history, and the final function of language

Warm-Up

Is there a difference between a lie and a half-truth? Between a story told incorrectly and a story told selectively? Write two sentences explaining what you think the difference is.

Lesson

Chapters 7 and 8 are the darkest section of the novella. Napoleon holds public executions — animals confess to crimes they did not commit and are immediately killed by the dogs — and Squealer revises the official account of the Battle of the Cowshed a second time, this time awarding Napoleon a medal he did not earn. The commandments change again, quietly, to accommodate each new violation. By the end of Chapter 8, the animals have accepted a version of history so distorted that the original Seven Commandments are unrecognizable. Orwell's argument in these chapters is not just that Napoleon is cruel but that cruelty requires a specific kind of language infrastructure: the animals confess, which means they participate in their own condemnation; the commandments change in writing, which means the law is always technically being followed; and Squealer provides an explanation for everything, which means there is never a silence in which the animals could hear themselves think. PROPAGANDA, as Orwell uses it here, is not just lying — it is a system that leaves no room for alternatives.

Vocabulary: PROPAGANDA

Information, especially biased or misleading information, used to promote a particular political cause or point of view and to shape what an audience believes rather than to inform them accurately. In Animal Farm, propaganda is not a single lie but an ongoing system of distortion that replaces critical thinking with conditioned response.

Example from the text: "The animals were shaken and miserable. They did not know which was more shocking — the treachery of the animals who had leagued themselves with Snowball, or the cruel retribution they had just witnessed."

Copia exercise: Rewrite the sentence above, keeping the structure of two things that shake the animals, but change the content to describe a situation in which two explanations for the same event are offered, only one of which is true.

Reading

Read Chapters 7–8 of Animal Farm. Mark every moment where Squealer appears and every change to the commandments. Pay attention to what the animals do and do not say after the executions.

Comprehension Check

1. The animals confess to crimes before being executed. Why do you think Orwell included confessions rather than simply having Napoleon execute the animals without explanation?

One to two sentences. Think about what the confession does for Napoleon's authority.

2. After the executions, Clover and the other animals feel horror but cannot put it into words. What does their silence suggest about the relationship between language and resistance?

Two sentences.

3. By the end of Chapter 8, the commandment about alcohol has been changed to say that no animal shall drink alcohol "to excess." How does Orwell use the phrase "to excess" to make an argument about how language enables corruption?

One to two sentences.
