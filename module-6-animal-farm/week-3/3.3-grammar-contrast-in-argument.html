Grammar 3.3: Using Contrast in Literary Analysis

Combining contrast clauses with appositives, passive voice, and dashes to build analytical sentences

The Contrast Clause as Thesis Structure

A contrast clause can anchor a thesis, a topic sentence, or a closing claim. The strongest analytical theses about Animal Farm name a gap — between stated ideals and actual practice, between Squealer's rhetoric and narrative reality, between Old Major's vision and Napoleon's farm. The contrast clause is built for exactly this.

Weak thesis (no contrast): "In Animal Farm, Napoleon uses language to control the other animals." — True but obvious. No gap, no argument.
Strong thesis (contrast clause): "[WHEREAS] Whereas the commandments were presented as the permanent ethical foundation of Animal Farm, Orwell shows that written law is only as stable as the willingness of those in power to honor it — and that propaganda, not force, is what makes the alterations invisible to those who suffer from them."
A Model Analytical Paragraph — All Structures Labeled

[WHEREAS] Whereas the original commandment declared that no animal would kill any other animal, Orwell shows that the revised version — "without cause" added by paint in the night — does not merely bend the rule but eliminates the principle the rule was meant to protect.

[Q][SIGNAL] Squealer insists, following the executions, that the animals remember things "incorrectly" and that documentary evidence would prove Napoleon's innocence.

[PASSIVE] But no documentary evidence is produced — [APP] Squealer, the novel's embodiment of state propaganda, simply asserts the existence of evidence that confirms what Napoleon has already decided to be true.

[DASH] This is not a failure of fact-checking — it is an argument that propaganda does not need facts to succeed, only the absence of alternatives.

Exercise 1: Revise Toward Contrast

Each sentence below is an accurate observation but not yet an analytical argument. Revise it using a contrast clause labeled [WHEREAS], [WHILE], or [ALTHOUGH] to name the gap that makes the observation significant.

1. The pigs live in the farmhouse and sleep in beds.
2. Squealer tells the animals that production has increased.
3. Napoleon uses the dogs to threaten animals who question his decisions.
Exercise 2: Write Your Module 6 Thesis

Write a one-sentence thesis for your Module 6 essay. It must use a contrast clause ([WHEREAS], [WHILE], or [ALTHOUGH]) and name a formal or rhetorical choice Orwell makes — not just what happens in the story. Label the contrast clause in brackets. Then write one sentence labeled [FIX] explaining what you would need to strengthen the thesis further.

The [FIX] sentence is the most useful thing you can write right now. A thesis that knows its own weakness is a thesis that can be improved.
