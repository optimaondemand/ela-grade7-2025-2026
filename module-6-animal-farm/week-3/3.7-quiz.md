**Question 1** (5 pts)
What is propaganda, as Orwell uses the concept in Animal Farm?

A. An outright lie told by a political leader to gain support for a war.
B. An ongoing system of distortion — using language, selective facts, and rewritten history — that shapes what an audience believes and removes the alternatives they would need to think differently.
C. A formal speech given by a leader in which statistics are used to misrepresent economic conditions.
D. The use of physical violence to enforce obedience among a population.

**Correct: B**

**Question 2** (5 pts)
After the executions in Chapter 7, Clover stands at the barn wall and feels horror — but cannot put her feelings into words. What does Orwell argue through her silence?

A. That Clover is less intelligent than the pigs and therefore unable to articulate complex emotions.
B. That the animals are too afraid to speak, even when no one is watching.
C. That propaganda has removed the language the animals would need to name what they feel — their resistance is paralyzed not by force but by the absence of the words that would make resistance possible.
D. That Clover has forgotten the original commandments and therefore cannot remember what has been violated.

**Correct: C**

**Question 3** (5 pts)

A. Napoleon used propaganda to control the animals, and Squealer helped him.
B. The pigs slept in beds, which was against the original commandments, but Squealer explained it.

D. Although propaganda is used by Napoleon, the animals on Animal Farm do not notice it is happening.

**Question 6** (5 pts)

**Question 8** (5 pts)

A. "In Animal Farm, Orwell uses language to show how propaganda works in a totalitarian society."
B. "Whereas the original Seven Commandments were meant to ensure that no animal would exploit another, Orwell shows that language — specifically the passive voice and incremental revision — is the primary mechanism by which those protections are dismantled without the protections ever appearing to be violated."

B. It is a grammatical error — Orwell meant to write "in excess" rather than "to excess."
