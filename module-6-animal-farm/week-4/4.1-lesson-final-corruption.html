7th Grade ELA — Module 6 — Week 4
Animal Farm — Lesson 4.1

4.1 Lesson: The Final Corruption

Chapters 9–10 — When the revolution eats itself completely

Warm-Up

Is it possible for a story to be both satirical and tragic? Can something that makes you laugh also break your heart? Think of an example — from a book, film, or story you know. Write two sentences.

Lesson

Chapter 9 ends with Boxer's death — sold to the knacker's by the pigs who then use the money to buy whiskey — and Squealer's explanation that the van with "Horse Slaughterer" painted on its side was, in fact, a veterinary vehicle. Chapter 10 completes the circle. The pigs walk upright, wear clothes, carry whips, and trade with the human farmers. The single surviving commandment reads: "All animals are equal, but some animals are more equal than others." The neighboring farmers look in through the farmhouse window at the pigs playing cards with the humans, and they can no longer tell the difference between pig and man. This is the final image of the novella — and it is Orwell's most devastating use of IRONY. The revolution promised liberation from exactly the hierarchy that has been restored. The slogan that remains is a grammatical impossibility that declares itself to be law. And the animals outside the window looking in are doing exactly what they were doing at the beginning of the novel — looking at their oppressors from the outside, unable to speak.

Vocabulary: IRONY (Dramatic and Verbal)

Verbal irony: The speaker says the opposite of what they mean. When Squealer says the van was a veterinary vehicle, he knows it was not. Dramatic irony: The reader knows something the characters do not. We know the pigs are becoming what they expelled Jones for being — the animals outside the farmhouse window do not. Structural irony: The entire narrative is built on a gap between what is claimed and what is true — the story's structure makes that gap visible from the first page to the last.

Example from the text: "All animals are equal, but some animals are more equal than others."

Copia exercise: Write a sentence using the same contradictory structure ("All X are Y, but some X are more Y than others") about a situation in your own world. Then write one sentence of analysis explaining what the contradiction reveals.

Reading

Read Chapters 9–10 of Animal Farm. Pay particular attention to Boxer's end in Chapter 9 and the final scene in Chapter 10. Notice the moment when the animals outside the window can no longer distinguish pig from man. That is the novel's closing image — and its final argument.

Comprehension Check

1. Boxer's motto throughout the novel has been "I will work harder" and "Napoleon is always right." What does his death reveal about those mottos — and about the allegory's argument about loyalty?

One to two sentences.

2. The final surviving commandment reads: "All animals are equal, but some animals are more equal than others." This is grammatically impossible. Why does Orwell make it grammatically impossible rather than simply saying "the pigs are in charge now"?

Two sentences.

3. The final image: the animals look in through the farmhouse window and cannot distinguish pig from man. What has changed since the novel's opening, and what has stayed exactly the same?

Two sentences.
