Grammar 4.2: Anaphora

Repetition at the beginning — why it works, and how it can be used against you

What Anaphora Is

Anaphora is the repetition of the same word or phrase at the beginning of successive clauses or sentences. It creates rhythm, emphasis, and emotional momentum. It is one of the oldest rhetorical devices in human language — and one of the most effective tools of propaganda.

From Animal Farm — Old Major’s speech:

"Is it not crystal clear, then, comrades, that all the evils of this life of ours spring from the tyranny of human beings?
Only get rid of Man, and the produce of our labour would be our own.
Only get rid of Man, and the produce of our labour would be our own.
Almost overnight we could become rich and free. What then must we do?"

The repetition of "Only get rid of Man" creates emotional momentum that sweeps the listener past the question of whether getting rid of Man would actually solve all their problems.

The slogan as compressed anaphora:

"Four legs good, two legs bad."

Not quite anaphora in the full sense, but the parallel structure and repetition of "[X] legs" create the same effect: a rhythm that bypasses critical thought. The sheep who bleat it for hours are not thinking — they are participating in a sound pattern.

How Anaphora Works Rhetorically

Anaphora creates three effects that propagandists and genuine orators both rely on:

1. Rhythm and momentum. Repeated openings create a beat that pulls the listener forward. By the third repetition, the listener is expecting a fourth — and is emotionally prepared to accept the conclusion.
2. Emphasis through accumulation. The thing repeated at the beginning gains weight each time it appears. Old Major's "Only get rid of Man" becomes more absolute with each repetition.
3. Bypass of critical analysis. The rhythm of anaphora can suppress the listener's urge to ask "but wait" between clauses. The sheep bleat because the slogan has a rhythm — not because they have evaluated its logic.
Exercise 1: Identify and Analyze

Read each example below. Underline the anaphora, label it [ANAPHORA], and write one sentence explaining what emotional or rhetorical effect the repetition creates.

1. "And now, comrades, I will tell you about my dream of last night. I cannot describe that dream to you. It was a dream of the earth as it will be when Man has vanished."
2. "No animal in England is free. No animal in England knows the meaning of happiness or leisure after he is a year old."
3. "Four legs good, two legs bad. Four legs good, two legs bad. Four legs good, two legs bad." (the sheep, bleating)
Exercise 2: Write Anaphora in Two Registers

Write anaphora twice on the same topic, using the same opening phrase, but for different purposes:

  1. Write three sentences labeled [ANAPHORA-PROPAGANDA] that Squealer could use to convince the animals that trading with humans is in their interest. The anaphora should create momentum that suppresses questions.
  2. Write three sentences labeled [ANAPHORA-ANALYSIS] that you could use in a literary analysis essay about Animal Farm. The anaphora should create cumulative analytical force — each sentence adding to a building argument about the novel.
Notice that anaphora in propaganda works by rhythm; anaphora in analysis works by accumulation. Both use the same structure — but to do different things.
