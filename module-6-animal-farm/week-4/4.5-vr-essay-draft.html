VR 4.5: Stage Your Essay Argument

Your thesis, your evidence, your voice — in spatial form before you commit it to prose

What You’re Doing

Remain in the same Session from VR 4.4, in a new area. Stage your essay argument before you write it in prose. Use IFX for your evidence, a Whiteboard for your thesis structure, and a short recording to argue your case. What does not make sense in the recording is what you fix before you write the essay.

The Four Essay Focus Options
Option A: The Passive Voice as Political Tool
Argue how Orwell's use of passive voice constructions — in Squealer's speeches and in the narration of Napoleon's decisions — makes an argument about how propaganda works to hide agency and normalize power.
Option B: The Changing Commandments
Argue how Orwell's technique of incremental revision — adding qualifying phrases to absolute laws — makes a specific argument about the relationship between written law and power, and about what language cannot protect on its own.
Option C: Anaphora in Old Major’s Speech
Argue how Old Major's use of anaphora in his speech (and the novella's subsequent reduction of that speech to a two-word slogan) represents Orwell's argument about how revolutionary language works and how it can be captured and simplified by those in power.
Option D: The Final Commandment as Irony
Argue how the grammatical impossibility of "All animals are equal, but some animals are more equal than others" works as the novel's central ironic device — and what it argues about the relationship between language, law, and the legitimation of power.
What to Do
1

Stage your evidence (4–6 IFX)

Choose IFX representing the specific evidence your essay will use — not Animal Farm in general, but your particular argument's evidence. If your essay is about passive voice, what IFX captures the hiding of agency? If it is about the commandments, what IFX represents the before and after?

2

Place a Whiteboard IFX: thesis + evidence + concession

[THESIS] your one-sentence thesis (formal choice + what it does); [EV1] [EV2] your quotations or evidence items; [CON] the strongest objection to your thesis and your counter.

3

Record your argument (2–3 minutes) and submit

State your thesis, walk through evidence, state concession and counter. Submit snapshot YourLastName_M6_4.5 + recording link + one-sentence thesis + one sentence about what you realized during recording that you need to fix.

Submission

Upload snapshot YourLastName_M6_4.5 + recording link + thesis sentence + fix sentence to Canvas. Estimated time: 25–30 minutes in headset.

Facilitator Note

The one-sentence thesis is the primary assessment item here. A strong thesis: "By using the passive voice in Squealer's speeches and in the narration of Napoleon's decisions, Orwell makes an argument that propaganda does not merely distort reality — it restructures the grammar of what reality can say about itself." A developing thesis: "Orwell uses passive voice to show how Napoleon hides what he is doing from the animals." Students who submit a developing thesis need one teacher question before writing: "What does the passive voice specifically do that naming Napoleon directly could not?"
