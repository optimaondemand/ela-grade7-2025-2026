7th Grade ELA — Module 6 — Week 5
Animal Farm — Lesson 5.1

5.1 Lesson: Animal Farm as Argument

The novella as a whole — what it proves, and what it leaves open

Warm-Up

Is Animal Farm pessimistic? Does Orwell believe things can get better? Or is he saying that this is simply how power always works? Write two sentences with your opinion.

Lesson

Orwell once wrote that Animal Farm was "the first book in which I tried, with full consciousness of what I was doing, to fuse political purpose and artistic purpose into one whole." The key phrase is "fuse" — the novella is not political analysis dressed up in animal costumes; it is a work of art whose formal choices (allegory, irony, passive voice, anaphora, the gradual revision of the commandments) are themselves the argument. When Orwell uses the passive voice in "It was announced that the Sunday Meetings would come to an end," he is not just reporting a plot event — he is showing the reader exactly how propaganda operates by performing it grammatically. When the commandment changes from an absolute to one with a qualifier, the reader experiences the revision rather than merely being told about it. This is what literature can do that a political essay cannot. The argument does not sit on top of the story; it lives in the way the story is told.

This week you will write your Module 6 essay. Before you do, take a moment to ask what the novella finally argues — not what it summarizes, but what it has demonstrated through form. A text that shows you something through its structure has argued more powerfully than a text that tells you the same thing directly. Orwell knew this. Your essay should argue the same way: not just that he does something, but what that formal choice demonstrates that cannot be demonstrated any other way.

One thing the novella explicitly leaves open: it does not tell us what the animals should have done. It diagnoses. The question of what literacy, resistance, or solidarity would have required is left for the reader.

You have finished Animal Farm. This week focuses on essay preparation, grammar review, peer response, and writing. If you want to revisit a specific passage, go back to the chapter where you first encountered the evidence you plan to use in your essay.

Comprehension Check

1. Orwell says his goal was to "fuse political purpose and artistic purpose into one whole." Name one formal choice in Animal Farm — a specific technique, not a plot event — and explain in one sentence how it performs the political argument rather than just illustrating it.

This will probably become part of your essay. Write it carefully.

2. What does Animal Farm argue that Orwell could not have argued as effectively in a straightforward political essay about Stalin?

One to two sentences. Think about what the allegory and the form allow that direct argument cannot.

3. Write a practice one-sentence thesis for your essay. It must name a formal choice AND argue what that choice does. Label it [THESIS].

You will refine this in Grammar 5.2 and VR 5.5. Write the best version you can right now.
