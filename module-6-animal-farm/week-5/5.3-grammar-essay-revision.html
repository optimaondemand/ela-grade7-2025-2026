Grammar 5.3: Revising for Argument

Four revision questions for the Module 6 analytical essay

Revision Is Argument-Testing

The four questions below test whether your essay is arguing or only reporting. You can have correct grammar, labeled structures, and accurate evidence — and still not be arguing. Use these questions on every paragraph before you submit.

Four Revision Questions
1. Does my thesis name a formal choice AND what it does?

"Orwell uses passive voice in Animal Farm" names a choice but not what it does. "Orwell's consistent use of the passive voice in Squealer's speeches enacts the novel's argument that propaganda works not by asserting power directly but by removing from the grammar of public life any sentence in which power can be named as a subject" names the choice AND argues its specific effect.

2. Does my evidence actually demonstrate my thesis, or does it just relate to the topic?

Every quotation should connect directly to your specific formal claim. If you're arguing about passive voice, the quotation should be in passive voice. If you're arguing about anaphora, the quotation should use anaphora. A related quotation is not the same as a demonstrating quotation.

3. Does my explanation sentence name something the quotation doesn't say?

"In other words, Orwell is showing that the pigs hide who made the decision" is restating. "This construction enacts what it describes: by removing Napoleon from the sentence, the passive voice performs the disappearance of agency that propaganda requires" is explaining. Test: is there a word or idea in your explanation that wasn't already in the quotation? If not, it's a restatement.

4. Does my conclusion connect back to the essential question in a way that adds something new?

The essential question is: How does language shape who holds power, and who loses it? A conclusion that restates your thesis is a summary. A conclusion that uses Animal Farm's formal argument to answer this question in a way you couldn't have answered it before reading the novel is a conclusion.

Exercise: Self-Revision

Take the paragraph you wrote in Grammar 5.2, Exercise 2. Apply all four revision questions. For each, write one sentence labeled [R1], [R2], [R3], [R4] that either confirms the paragraph meets the criterion or identifies what needs to change. Then revise one sentence based on what you found — label the revision [REV] and explain in a sentence what you changed and why.

A paragraph that passes all four questions is ready for the essay.
