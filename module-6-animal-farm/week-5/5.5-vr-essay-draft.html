VR 5.5: Stage Your Final Essay Argument

Your thesis, your evidence, your voice — before you commit it to prose

What You’re Doing

Stage your final essay argument in VR before you write the prose version. This is your last chance to test your argument spatially. What doesn’t make sense in the recording is what you fix before you write. Come with your refined thesis from Grammar 5.2 and the evidence you have been building across four weeks of VR compositions.

What You’ll Need
  • Device: VR headset
  • Session ID: ELA7-M6-W5 — Persistent Session
  • Bring: Your refined thesis (from Grammar 5.2) and two or three specific quotations you plan to use in your essay
What to Do
1

Stage your evidence (4–6 IFX)

Place IFX representing the specific evidence your essay will use — not Animal Farm in general, but your argument's particular evidence. If your essay is about passive voice, what IFX represents the hiding of agency? If it is about the commandments, what represents their original form and their revision?

2

Place a Whiteboard IFX: thesis + evidence + concession

[THESIS] your one-sentence thesis; [EV1] [EV2] your quotations or evidence items; [CON] the strongest objection to your thesis and your counter.

3

Record your argument (2–3 minutes) and submit

State your thesis, walk through your evidence, state your concession and counter. If you catch yourself summarizing the plot, stop and restart. Submit snapshot YourLastName_M6_5.5 + recording link + thesis + fix sentence.

Submission

Upload snapshot + paste recording link + thesis + fix sentence to Canvas. Estimated time: 25–35 minutes in headset.

Facilitator Note

The one-sentence thesis is the primary assessment item. Strong thesis: "By constructing Squealer's speeches in the passive voice — 'It was decided,' 'It has been found necessary' — Orwell does not merely describe how propaganda works; he performs it, giving the reader the experience of receiving decisions without being able to identify who made them." Developing thesis: "Orwell uses passive voice to show that the pigs hide what they are doing." Students with a developing thesis need one question before writing: "What does the passive voice do to the reader that simply telling us Napoleon lied could not?"
