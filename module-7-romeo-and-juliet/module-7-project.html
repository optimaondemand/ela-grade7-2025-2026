Module 7 Essay: How Shakespeare’s Language Argues

100 points — Submit as Word document via OneDrive

Essential Question

How does Shakespeare use the structure of language — meter, metaphor, and dramatic irony — to argue that some experiences cannot be expressed in ordinary words?

Overview

You will write a four-paragraph analytical essay arguing that Shakespeare uses a specific formal choice in Romeo and Juliet to make an argument that the plot alone cannot make. Your essay should move from a specific observation about language to a claim about what that language argues — about love, fate, time, or the limits of expression.

Your essay must use the required M7 structures you have practiced throughout the module. These are not decorative labels — they mark the analytical moves your essay makes.

Choose Your Essay Focus

Select one of the four focus options below. Your essay argues one formal claim; it uses all four required M7 structures. All four options can produce strong essays — choose the one whose evidence you know best from your reading and VR work.

Option A: Shared Meter and the Form of Union

Argue that the shared iambic pentameter [METER-SHARED] — the moments when Romeo and Juliet complete each other’s lines and together form a single poem — makes an argument about their love that no declaration either character could make alone would accomplish. Key moments: the first meeting sonnet (1.5), possible shared lines in the balcony scene. Key question: what does it mean that the form enacts what the words cannot say?

Option B: The Light/Dark Extended Metaphor

Argue that the light/dark extended metaphor [METAPHOR] — running from “It is the East, and Juliet is the sun” through lightning, stars, and the candle Romeo carries into the tomb — embeds the play’s argument about the brevity of love inside the vehicle itself, before Romeo can articulate the danger. Key question: what does the vehicle (light) add to the tenor (love) that changes how we understand what Romeo is saying?

Option C: Dramatic Irony from Prologue to Tomb

Argue that the dramatic irony [IRONY-D] — the knowledge given to the audience in the prologue and carried through every joyful scene to the tomb — is not a device but an argument: that the gap between what the audience knows and what the characters know is itself Shakespeare’s claim about the nature of tragedy. Key moments: the prologue, the wedding, Act 3 Scene 5 (Juliet’s double meanings), Romeo in the tomb. Key question: what does the audience feel that no character can feel, and what does that feeling argue?

Option D: Mercutio as Foil and the Cost of the Private Choice

Argue that Mercutio’s function as Romeo’s foil [FOIL] — his contrast with Romeo in wit, honor, and loyalty to the social world — makes an argument that the plot alone cannot make: that Romeo’s private choice (the marriage) destroys a person who had nothing to do with it, and that the feud costs most those who try to stand outside it. Key moments: Act 1 Queen Mab speech, Act 3 Scene 1 death, “A plague on both your houses.” Key question: what does Mercutio’s death clarify about Romeo that Romeo could not clarify about himself?

Essay Structure

Your essay has four paragraphs. Each paragraph has a required structure. Keep each paragraph focused — one idea, one argument, the evidence that proves it.

Paragraph 1: Introduction

Open with a statement about what the play’s formal choice does that the plot cannot. Introduce Romeo and Juliet and Shakespeare by name. End with your [THESIS] — a specific, arguable claim that names the formal choice and what it argues. Use [METAPHOR] or [IRONY-D] or [METER-SHARED] or [FOIL] to label your first analytical move.

Paragraph 2: First Body Paragraph

Introduce your first piece of evidence. Use [Q] to introduce the quotation, [SIGNAL] to explain what it shows, and [APP] to identify Shakespeare’s or a character’s function in a phrase following a noun. Label your M7 structure ([METAPHOR], [IRONY-D], [METER-SHARED], or [FOIL]) in the analytical sentence. Connect to your thesis.

Paragraph 3: Second Body Paragraph

Introduce your second piece of evidence — from a different moment in the play than Paragraph 2. Use [Q] and [SIGNAL]. Use a contrast clause: [WHEREAS], [WHILE], or [ALTHOUGH] to name what the evidence complicates or deepens. End with [CON] — a sentence connecting this evidence to the thesis.

Paragraph 4: Conclusion

Restate your thesis in different words — not a copy, a revision. Answer the essential question directly: how does Shakespeare use the structure of language to argue that some experiences cannot be expressed in ordinary words? Your final sentence should name what the play leaves the audience with — not a summary, but a judgment.

Required Tags Checklist

Before submitting, confirm your essay includes every tag below. Tags appear in brackets in your essay text; they are not deleted before submission. They show your teacher which analytical move each sentence makes.

[THESIS] — your arguable claim (Introduction)
[METAPHOR] or [IRONY-D] or [METER-SHARED] or [FOIL] — your focus structure (at least twice)
[Q] — before each quotation
[SIGNAL] — before each analytical explanation of a quotation
[APP] — at least one appositive phrase
[WHEREAS], [WHILE], or [ALTHOUGH] — at least one contrast clause
[CON] — at least one connecting sentence closing a body paragraph
