Romeo and Juliet — Lesson 1.1

The Prologue and Act 1: Two Households

Why Shakespeare tells you everything before the play begins — and what that does to the audience

Objectives

  • Identify the prologue as a Shakespearean sonnet and explain what its form does to the audience before the action begins
  • Define iambic pentameter and locate its pattern in the prologue and Act 1 speeches
  • Explain how the feud between the Montagues and Capulets functions as the force that makes the lovers’ story inevitable

Warm-Up

Think of a story you already know the ending to — a movie you’ve seen twice, a book where you knew from the start someone was going to die. Did knowing the ending make the story boring? Or did it change how you paid attention to what happened? Write two or three sentences.

Lesson

Romeo and Juliet begins with one of the strangest choices in all of drama: before the first scene, a character called the Chorus steps forward and tells the audience exactly what is going to happen — two young people from feuding families will fall in love, and they will die. There is no suspense about the outcome. Shakespeare doesn’t do this by accident. The prologue is written as a sonnet — a fourteen-line poem in iambic pentameter, the metrical pattern that goes da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM, five pairs of syllables per line, the pattern that Shakespeare uses for virtually all of his verse. By choosing this form for the prologue, Shakespeare signals from the first breath that this is not simply a story about two teenagers who were unlucky; it is an argument about what happens when people inherit a hatred they did not choose, and when language — the language of insult, of ancient grievance, of family name — has more power than individual love. The audience knows the ending so they can watch the whole play asking a different question: not what will happen, but why, and whether it could have been otherwise. That shift — from suspense to understanding — is what tragedy does, and Romeo and Juliet teaches you from its first fourteen lines exactly how to watch it.

Historical Context

Shakespeare wrote Romeo and Juliet around 1594–1596, early in his career. The Globe Theatre — open-air, no electric light, audiences standing in the yard — meant plays had to work with language alone: there were no realistic sets, no recorded sound effects, no close-ups. When Shakespeare writes that Juliet’s room is lit by the moon, the audience has to hear that and see it in their minds. This makes the language of the play do double duty: it tells you what is happening and creates the visual world in which it happens.

Vocabulary: Iambic Pentameter

Iambic pentameter is a metrical pattern used in English poetry: each line contains five (penta) pairs of syllables called iambs, where the first syllable is unstressed and the second is stressed (da-DUM). Read this line from the prologue aloud and tap your finger on the stressed syllables:

“Two house-holds, both a-like in dig-ni-ty”
da-DUM / da-DUM / da-DUM / da-DUM / da-DUM

Copia exercise: Take this line from the prologue — “From ancient grudge break to new mutiny” — and rewrite it in plain modern prose. Then try writing one line of your own in iambic pentameter about anything. It doesn’t have to rhyme. Tap out the beats as you go.

Reading: Romeo and Juliet — Prologue and Act 1

Read the Prologue and Act 1 (all five scenes). Pay attention to:

  • Who speaks in verse (iambic pentameter) and who speaks in prose — the Capulet servants in 1.1 speak in prose; Romeo and Juliet speak in verse
  • The shared sonnet in 1.5: Romeo and Juliet’s first fourteen lines of dialogue are a complete sonnet, with Romeo speaking the first quatrain, Juliet the second, and both sharing the couplet
  • What the feud has cost the city by Act 1 — two street brawls before the first scene is over

Comprehension Check

1. The prologue tells the audience that the lovers will die before the play begins. What effect does this create for the audience watching the play?

A. It creates suspense because the audience is waiting to see if the prologue is telling the truth.
B. It removes suspense and replaces it with dramatic irony — the audience watches knowing what the characters do not, which makes every joyful moment feel weighted with what is coming.
C. It makes the play boring because there is no reason to watch if you know the ending.
D. It allows Shakespeare to skip the rising action and begin with the climax.

✓ B

2. In Act 1 Scene 5, Romeo and Juliet’s first exchange of dialogue forms a complete sonnet. What does this formal choice argue about their connection?

A. It shows that Romeo has been rehearsing his lines and is not being sincere.
B. It suggests their connection is artificial and constructed rather than genuine.
C. It argues that their love is not accidental — even their language fits together perfectly, as if they complete each other’s sentences because they are completing a single poetic form.
D. It shows that Juliet has studied poetry and is testing Romeo to see if he is educated.

✓ C

3. In Act 1, who speaks in prose (not verse), and what does this distinction signal about social class and dramatic function?

A. The nobility speak in prose; the servants speak in verse, because Shakespeare wants to show that ordinary people have more to say.
B. Romeo speaks in prose when he is in love with Rosaline, and switches to verse when he meets Juliet, showing that Juliet elevates his language.
C. The servants and lower-class characters generally speak in prose, while nobles and lovers speak in verse — though Shakespeare occasionally breaks this pattern deliberately to create dramatic effect.
D. All characters speak in verse in Act 1; prose does not appear until Act 3.

✓ C
