Romeo and Juliet — Grammar 1.2

Grammar: Iambic Pentameter and Verse vs. Prose

Hearing the beat — and learning to write about when Shakespeare breaks it

The Pattern

An iamb is a pair of syllables: one unstressed, one stressed — da-DUM. Pentameter means five. Iambic pentameter = five iambs per line = ten syllables, alternating unstressed and stressed.

“But soft, what light through yon-der win-dow breaks?”

but SOFT / what LIGHT / through YON / der WIN / dow BREAKS
da-DUM   da-DUM   da-DUM   da-DUM   da-DUM

Most of Shakespeare’s noble characters speak in blank verse — iambic pentameter that does not rhyme. Servants, clowns, and comic characters often speak in prose — ordinary sentences without meter. When a noble character suddenly drops into prose, or when two characters share a single line of verse, Shakespeare is making a deliberate dramatic choice.

Feature Blank Verse Prose
Form Iambic pentameter, no rhyme Ordinary sentences, no meter
Who uses it Nobles, lovers, serious moments Servants, comic scenes, low characters
When broken A break signals emotion, disorder, or irony A shift to verse signals gravity or formality
Label for analysis [METER] [PROSE]

Shared Lines — A Special Technique

Sometimes two characters split a single line of iambic pentameter between them. This is called a shared line (or split line). In Act 1 Scene 5, Romeo and Juliet share a line:

Romeo: “Have not saints lips, and holy palmers too?”
Juliet: “Ay, pilgrim, lips that they must use in prayer.”

Romeo’s line ends mid-thought; Juliet’s line completes the iambic pentameter. They are, quite literally, completing each other’s rhythm.

A shared line creates dramatic intimacy — it argues that two characters are so attuned to each other that their speech flows as one. When you see one in an essay, use the label [METER-SHARED] and explain what the shared rhythm argues about the relationship.

Exercise 1 — Identify the Pattern

For each passage below, identify whether it is blank verse [METER] or prose [PROSE]. Then explain in one sentence what this choice signals.

1. “What, art thou drawn among these heartless hinds? Turn thee, Benvolio, look upon thy death.” — Tybalt, 1.1

Verse or prose? What does this choice signal about Tybalt’s character or the moment?

2. “I will bite my thumb at them; which is a disgrace to them, if they bear it.” — Sampson, 1.1

Verse or prose? What does this tell you about Sampson’s social position?

3. “Did my heart love till now? Forswear it, sight! / For I ne’er saw true beauty till this night.” — Romeo, 1.5

Verse or prose? What does the rhymed couplet here add beyond the pattern of blank verse?

Exercise 2 — Write with the Label

Write two sentences about any moment in Act 1 where the choice between verse and prose matters. Use [METER] or [PROSE] to label the structure you are discussing, and explain what that formal choice argues — not just what it is.

Model:

“When the Capulet servants open Act 1 in [PROSE], Shakespeare signals that the feud has infected the lowest level of the social order — even people with no personal stake in the quarrel have absorbed its logic of insult and violence. The shift to [METER] when Tybalt enters argues that the nobility of the house brings not dignity but a more formal, more dangerous commitment to the fight.”
