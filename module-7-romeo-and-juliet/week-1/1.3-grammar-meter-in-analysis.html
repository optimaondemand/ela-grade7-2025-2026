Romeo and Juliet — Grammar 1.3

Grammar: Using Meter as Evidence in Literary Analysis

Moving from “this is verse” to “this verse does something”

The Problem: Identifying vs. Arguing

Identifying meter is the first step. But in a literary analysis essay, naming the structure is not enough — you have to argue what it does. Compare these two approaches:

Weak — just identifies:

“Shakespeare uses iambic pentameter in the balcony scene. This is the standard meter for Shakespeare’s plays.”

Strong — argues what the meter does:

“[METER-SHARED] When Romeo and Juliet complete each other’s lines of iambic pentameter in Act 1 Scene 5, Shakespeare argues that their love is not impulsive infatuation but something structurally inevitable — their very speech is already one continuous breath.”

The strong version names the structure, cites a specific moment, and then answers the question What does this argue? — not What does this mean in a general sense? but what claim about the play’s argument follows from this particular formal choice.

Three Questions for Analyzing Meter

1. Who speaks in verse here, and who does not?

The contrast between verse and prose is often the argument. A character who drops from verse to prose is losing control, becoming familiar, or shifting emotional register.

2. Is the meter regular or broken?

A line that has too many or too few syllables (called a metrical variation) breaks the pattern. These breaks are deliberate. Look for them around moments of violence, shock, or grief.

3. Do two characters share a line?

A shared line argues connection, completion, or harmony. The absence of shared lines — characters who finish their own lines without the other — can argue isolation.

Model Analysis Sentence

Read this model analytical sentence and notice how all four Module 7 structures are already beginning to appear together (even though you have only studied one so far):

“[METER-SHARED] Romeo and Juliet’s shared lines in Act 1 Scene 5 — [APP] their first meeting, a moment that seals both their love and their doom — argue that the lovers do not choose each other so much as complete each other: [WHEREAS] whereas the feud has made their two families incapable of sharing a street without bloodshed, these two share a single breath of verse, and Shakespeare makes the contrast felt before either character knows the other’s name.”

Labels visible: [METER-SHARED] (meter structure), [APP] (appositive from M6), [WHEREAS] (contrast clause from M6). These structures from earlier modules will carry into your M7 essay.

Exercise 1 — From Identification to Argument

Each sentence below only identifies a feature. Revise it so that it makes an argument about what that feature does. Use the label and add the analytical claim.

1. “The Capulet servants in Act 1 Scene 1 speak in prose.”

Revise: use [PROSE] and argue what this choice does.

2. “The prologue is written in iambic pentameter.”

Revise: use [METER] and argue what the choice of iambic pentameter for the prologue does that plain prose could not.

3. “Romeo and Juliet share lines of verse in Act 1 Scene 5.”

Revise: use [METER-SHARED] and argue what the shared verse argues about the nature of their connection.

Exercise 2 — Write Your Own

Write a four-sentence analytical paragraph about Act 1 that uses all of the following:

  • [METER] or [METER-SHARED] or [PROSE] — to analyze a specific verse or prose choice
  • [Q][SIGNAL] — to introduce a quotation from Act 1
  • [APP] — an appositive phrase identifying a character’s dramatic function
  • A sentence explaining what the quoted passage argues (not just what it says)
