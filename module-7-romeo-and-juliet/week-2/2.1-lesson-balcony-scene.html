7th Grade ELA — Module 7 — Week 2
Romeo and Juliet — Lesson 2.1

Act 2: The Balcony Scene and the Language of Light

How Romeo turns Juliet into the sun — and what that extended metaphor argues

Objectives

  • Define extended metaphor and trace it across the balcony scene (Act 2 Scene 2)
  • Explain what the light/dark pattern argues about Romeo and Juliet’s situation — not just how it describes their feelings
  • Identify apostrophe (addressing someone or something absent) as a formal choice and explain how Shakespeare uses it in Juliet’s soliloquy

Warm-Up

Think about a time you tried to describe a feeling that was too large for ordinary words — grief, joy, fear, love. Did you reach for a comparison? Did you say it was like something? Write two or three sentences about why human beings compare feelings to objects and natural phenomena when the feelings themselves are too abstract to hold.

Lesson

Act 2 Scene 2 — the scene we call the “balcony scene” — is where Shakespeare’s poetry is doing its most extraordinary work. Romeo, hidden in the Capulet orchard, watches Juliet appear at her window and immediately begins an extended metaphor: Juliet is the sun; the moon she outshines is the pale thing that Rosaline was; her eyes are stars. The metaphor is not decoration. Romeo is not simply saying that Juliet is beautiful. He is saying that she is the source of light in his world — that she makes other things visible, including himself. This is a claim about dependency, about how completely she has reoriented his sense of what matters. The danger built into the metaphor, which Romeo does not see but Shakespeare’s audience cannot miss, is that the sun rises and sets; light is temporary. In Act 2 the metaphors are all brightness and speed — “too like the lightning” is Juliet’s own word for their love — and Shakespeare uses this to argue that the speed of their connection is both its beauty and its peril. A love that moves like lightning does not have time to survive the world it is born into.

Historical Context

The Globe Theatre performed during the day with no artificial lighting. When Romeo says “But soft, what light through yonder window breaks? / It is the East, and Juliet is the sun,” the audience is standing in full daylight watching an actor in a wooden theater describe something they cannot see staged around them. The entire scene is a triumph of language creating reality — the night, the orchard, the stars, the window — out of nothing but words and the imagination of the audience. This is part of Shakespeare’s argument: language can build a world. And it can destroy one.

Vocabulary: Extended Metaphor and Apostrophe

An extended metaphor [METAPHOR] is a comparison that is developed across multiple lines or passages, adding new terms and implications as it unfolds. A simple metaphor says “she is the sun.” An extended metaphor follows: the sun rises, so she is his morning; the sun gives light, so she is his sight; to lose the sun is to lose one’s world. Each new element extends the argument of the original comparison. Apostrophe [APOSTROPHE] is the rhetorical device of addressing someone or something that is absent, unable to hear, or inanimate — “O Romeo, Romeo, wherefore art thou Romeo?” Juliet is not asking where Romeo physically is; she is addressing the name itself, the word that makes him her enemy, and asking why that word should have such power.

Copia exercise: Take Juliet’s line — “What’s in a name? That which we call a rose / By any other name would smell as sweet” — and rewrite her argument in plain modern prose. Then write one sentence arguing whether you agree with Juliet or not, with a specific reason.

Reading: Romeo and Juliet — Act 2

Comprehension Check

1. Romeo opens Act 2 Scene 2 with the line “But soft, what light through yonder window breaks? / It is the East, and Juliet is the sun.” What does describing Juliet as the sun argue, beyond the obvious compliment?

A. That Romeo is a skilled poet who knows how to compliment women effectively.
B. That Juliet is fair-skinned and her appearance in the window is dramatically bright.
C. That Juliet is Romeo’s source of light and orientation — without her, he moves in darkness; with her, he can see the world and himself; and implicitly, that the sun sets, which is a formal foreshadowing Shakespeare embeds in the metaphor.
D. That Romeo is comparing Juliet to the Greek goddess of the sun, which is a classical allusion.

✓ C

2. Juliet says their love is “too rash, too sudden, / Too like the lightning, which doth cease to be / Ere one can say it lightens.” What does this self-awareness argue about Juliet as a character and about the play’s argument?

A. It shows that Juliet is timid and does not actually love Romeo as much as he loves her.
B. It shows that Juliet, unlike Romeo, is capable of seeing the danger in their speed — and that she proceeds anyway, which argues that the love is strong enough to override the warning her own language delivers.
C. It shows that Juliet is quoting a proverb she heard from the Nurse and is not expressing her own view.
D. It shows that Juliet is experienced in love and knows how to protect herself from heartbreak.

✓ B

3. Juliet says “What’s in a name? That which we call a rose / By any other name would smell as sweet.” What is wrong with Juliet’s argument — and what does Shakespeare want the audience to understand that Juliet does not?

A. Nothing is wrong with it; Juliet is correct that names do not determine character.
B. Juliet’s argument is logically sound but emotionally wrong — a rose might smell as sweet by any name, but names in Verona are not neutral labels; they are inherited allegiances, and the name “Montague” will get Romeo killed. Shakespeare knows what Juliet does not yet know: that names in this play have the power of swords.
C. Juliet is wrong because Romeo’s name is the best thing about him, and she should embrace rather than reject it.
D. Juliet’s argument assumes that only roses have names, which is a logical error.

✓ B
