Romeo and Juliet — Grammar 2.2

Grammar: Extended Metaphor and the Tag System

How to track a metaphor across a passage — and argue what it does

What an Extended Metaphor Does

A simple metaphor names a comparison once: “she is the sun.” An extended metaphor [METAPHOR] develops the comparison through multiple terms, adding new dimensions as it goes. The extended metaphor is doing more than decorating — it is building an argument. Each new element extends or complicates the original comparison, often in ways that produce unexpected implications.

How the Light Metaphor Works in Act 2 Scene 2

Term of metaphor What it claims Hidden implication
Juliet is the sun She gives light, she warms, she is the source The sun sets; this is temporary
Her eyes are stars She brightens what would be dark Stars are distant and untouchable
Love is lightning Beautiful, sudden, intensely bright Lightning ends before you can say it flashed
The moon (= Rosaline?) is envious Old feelings are dim by comparison The moon is borrowed light; it reflects the sun

The metaphor does not just describe love as bright. It builds a world in which Juliet is the center of Romeo’s light system — and in which everything built on light is subject to darkness. The extended metaphor is Orwell’s argument about language (from Module 6) applied to love: language does not just describe; it creates the terms in which we understand what we feel. Romeo can only understand Juliet through the light metaphor — and the light metaphor already contains the warning he will not hear.

The Tag: [METAPHOR]

When you analyze an extended metaphor in your essay, use the tag [METAPHOR] to mark where you are analyzing figurative language. Use it immediately before or after the quotation you are discussing.

Weak — just identifies the metaphor:

“[METAPHOR] Romeo calls Juliet the sun in Act 2 Scene 2. This metaphor compares Juliet to the sun.”

Strong — argues what the metaphor does:

“[METAPHOR] When Romeo, [APP] the Montague son who has reoriented his entire world around a girl he met three hours ago, declares ‘It is the East, and Juliet is the sun,’ Shakespeare is not simply saying that Juliet is beautiful; he is arguing that Romeo’s love has made Juliet his source of orientation — and that a love this total, built on a source that sets, has darkness built into it from the first declaration.”

Exercise 1 — Track the Metaphor

Find three more light/dark images in Act 2 Scene 2 beyond the ones in the table above. For each, write one sentence using [METAPHOR] that argues what that specific term adds to the extended metaphor — what new implication it introduces or complicates.

Format: [METAPHOR] + quotation + one sentence arguing what this specific term adds or implies beyond the original “Juliet is the sun” claim.

Exercise 2 — Write the Analysis

Write three sentences that together build an argument about what the light/dark extended metaphor does in Act 2 Scene 2. Your three sentences must include:

  • Sentence 1: [METAPHOR] + quotation (from act 2) + what this opening term of the metaphor claims
  • Sentence 2: [WHEREAS] + a contrast clause naming what the light metaphor promises vs. what the play’s world will actually deliver
  • Sentence 3: A conclusion sentence naming what the extended metaphor argues that a plain statement about love could not argue

This three-sentence structure is a body paragraph in miniature. The same structure will appear in your Module 7 essay.
