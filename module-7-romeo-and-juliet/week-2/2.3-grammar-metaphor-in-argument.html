Romeo and Juliet — Grammar 2.3

Grammar: Using Metaphor as the Basis of a Literary Argument

Building a thesis from figurative language — what the metaphor argues that the story cannot say directly

The Literary Analysis Problem with Metaphor

When students write about figurative language, they often stop at identification: “Romeo uses a light metaphor.” The harder move is to ask: What does this metaphor argue that could not be argued without it? What does the vehicle (light) add to the tenor (love) that changes how we understand the tenor?

Three questions that drive from identification to argument:

1. What does the vehicle bring that the tenor lacks?

Love is abstract; light is concrete, measurable, temporal. The metaphor gives love a physics: it can be bright or dim, it can set, it can blind. These are properties of love that the word “love” alone does not carry.

2. Does the metaphor contain an argument the speaker does not intend?

Romeo does not mean to argue that his love will end by calling Juliet the sun. But he has said it, and Shakespeare has put the argument in the metaphor for the audience to hear. The vehicle carries more than the speaker knows.

3. How does the metaphor connect to the play’s larger argument?

The light/dark metaphor runs through the entire play. Analyzing it in one moment only makes sense if you can show how it connects to the pattern. A body paragraph about the balcony scene should point toward the tomb scene where the light finally goes out.

Model Analytical Paragraph (All Four M7 Structures)

Read this model paragraph and identify the labeled structures:

“[METAPHOR] Romeo’s declaration ‘It is the East, and Juliet is the sun’ does not simply praise Juliet’s beauty; it constructs a cosmos in which she is the source of all light and Romeo, [APP] the Montague son who has spent the first act in the wrong love, is finally oriented. [WHEREAS] whereas the sun metaphor promises warmth and permanence, the vehicle carries a physics Shakespeare intends but Romeo does not: the sun sets, light is borrowed, and what begins in the East must travel toward the West. [METER] The iambic pentameter that carries this declaration moves with the ease of inevitability, five beats completing themselves before the audience can process the implication — which is exactly what the metaphor argues about love itself: it arrives complete, with its ending already embedded in its grammar.”

[METAPHOR] — extended metaphor analysis
[APP] — appositive (carried from M6)
[WHEREAS] — contrast clause (carried from M6)
[METER] — iambic pentameter analysis

Exercise 1 — Build the Thesis

A strong thesis for an essay about Act 2’s extended metaphor must: (a) name the formal choice [METAPHOR]; (b) name the specific vehicle (light/dark, sun, lightning); (c) argue what the metaphor does that a direct statement could not. Write two thesis versions for the same essay and label them:

[DEVELOPING] version:

Write a thesis that names the metaphor but makes a vague claim about its effect.

[STRONG] version:

Revise your thesis so it names the metaphor, the specific vehicle, the specific effect, and what the metaphor argues that the story could not argue without it.

Exercise 2 — Full Paragraph

Write a 5–6 sentence analytical paragraph about the light/dark extended metaphor in Act 2. Use all four of the following:

  • [METAPHOR] + a specific quotation from Act 2 Scene 2
  • [APP] identifying Romeo or Juliet’s dramatic function in the play
  • [WHEREAS] or [ALTHOUGH] naming the gap between what the metaphor promises and what the play will deliver
  • [METER] or [METER-SHARED] at least once

End your paragraph with a sentence beginning with [FIX] identifying what your weakest sentence is and what you would do to strengthen it.
