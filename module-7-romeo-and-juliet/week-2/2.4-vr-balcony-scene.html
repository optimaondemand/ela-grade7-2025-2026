Romeo and Juliet — Week 2 — VR 2.4

VR 2.4: Stage the Balcony Scene

What does the extended metaphor look like spatially? Build the light vs. dark argument.

What You’re Doing

Stage the balcony scene’s extended metaphor — not the scene itself (don’t try to create Romeo below and Juliet above) but the argument the metaphor makes. What does the light/dark extended metaphor look like when you separate its two halves spatially? Place IFX that represent: what the light side of the metaphor promises vs. what the darkness side implies. This composition argues; it does not illustrate.

What You’ll Need
  • Device: VR headset
  • Session ID: ELA7-M7-W2 — Persistent Session
  • Location: Italian/Renaissance City or Courtyard setting
  • Bring: Your three [METAPHOR] sentences from Grammar 2.2 Exercise 1; your Act 2 text
What to Do
1

Two-zone composition: promise vs. implication (6–8 IFX)

Divide your Location space into two zones. In one zone, place IFX representing what the light/sun metaphor promises — warmth, brightness, love, sight. In the other zone, place IFX representing what the light metaphor implies but does not say — the sun sets, lightning is brief, stars are untouchable. Label each zone with a sticky note. The arrangement itself is the argument.

2

Whiteboard IFX: [METAPHOR] + [WHEREAS] sentence

On the Whiteboard IFX, write: [METAPHOR] — one specific quotation from Act 2 Scene 2 + one sentence arguing what that term adds to the metaphor; then [WHEREAS] — a contrast clause naming what the metaphor promises vs. what it implies.

3

Submit: snapshot + Canvas text

Take snapshot YourLastName_M7_2.4. In the Canvas text box, type your Whiteboard IFX sentences and add: in one sentence, what does your spatial arrangement argue that the Whiteboard IFX sentences don’t say explicitly?

Submission

Upload snapshot YourLastName_M7_2.4 + Canvas text. Estimated time: 20–25 minutes in headset.

Facilitator Note

The key assessment question: can the student explain what the light metaphor implies that it does not say? Students who stage two piles of cheerful vs. sad IFX without analytical connection to the metaphor need the question: “Why does calling her the sun carry a warning Romeo does not intend?” Strong [WHEREAS] sentence: “Whereas Romeo’s sun metaphor declares that Juliet is his source of light and life, the same vehicle carries the knowledge that the sun sets — and Shakespeare uses this embedded implication to argue that Romeo has already spoken his own foreshadowing.”
