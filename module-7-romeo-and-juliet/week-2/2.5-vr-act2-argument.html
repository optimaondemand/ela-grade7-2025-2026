VR 2.5: Map the Speed of Act 2

They meet at night, agree to marry at dawn, and are wed by afternoon — stage what this speed argues

What You’re Doing

Act 2 unfolds in about eighteen hours of story time: Romeo and Juliet meet Monday evening, exchange vows by Monday night, send messages Tuesday morning, and marry Tuesday afternoon. Stage this timeline spatially and then build a thesis from it. The speed of Act 2 is itself a formal argument about the nature of the love — and about why the world cannot contain it.

What You’ll Need
  • Device: VR headset
  • Session ID: ELA7-M7-W2 — Persistent Session (same as VR 2.4)
  • Location: Italian/Renaissance City or Courtyard setting
  • Bring: Your [STRONG] thesis from Grammar 2.3 Exercise 1 and your Act 2 text
What to Do
1

Timeline composition: four Act 2 moments (4–6 IFX)

Place four IFX representing the four key moments of Act 2 in sequence: (1) the meeting / shared sonnet; (2) the balcony scene vows; (3) the Nurse as messenger; (4) the marriage. Arrange them left to right or in a line. For each, place a sticky note naming the moment and one phrase from the text that captures what is happening to the language at that moment — is it careful, rushed, ecstatic, practical?

2

Whiteboard IFX: thesis + evidence

Write your [THESIS] from Grammar 2.3 (or a revision of it). Then: [EV1] and [EV2] — two specific quotations from Act 2 that you will use in your essay to support the thesis. Label each with one sentence of analytical explanation.

3

Record your argument (60–90 seconds) and submit

State your thesis, walk the timeline, explain how the speed of Act 2 relates to your thesis about the extended metaphor. Submit snapshot YourLastName_M7_2.5 + recording link + thesis sentence.

Submission

Upload snapshot YourLastName_M7_2.5 + paste recording link + thesis sentence to Canvas. Estimated time: 20–25 minutes in headset.

Facilitator Note

The thesis is the primary assessment item. Students should arrive with it from Grammar 2.3. The VR work here is building evidence for the essay argument, not exploring the play in general. If a student does not have a thesis yet, redirect them to Grammar 2.3 before they enter VR. Strong thesis example: “By extending the sun/light metaphor across all of Act 2 — beginning with divinity and ending with lightning — Shakespeare argues that this love is too total and too brief to survive the world it was born into, and embeds the argument in the vehicle before Romeo can articulate the danger.”
