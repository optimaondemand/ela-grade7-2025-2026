Romeo and Juliet — Grammar 3.2

Grammar: Dramatic Irony — Three Types, One Tag

Naming the gap between what the audience knows and what the character knows — and arguing what that gap does

The Three Types of Irony — Precisely Named

Students often use irony to mean surprising or coincidental. In literary analysis, irony is a specific technique with three distinct forms. You need to name the right one.

Type Definition Example from R&J Tag
Verbal irony A character says one thing but means another (the speaker controls the gap) Juliet's double-meaning speeches to Lady Capulet in Act 3 Scene 5 [IRONY-V]
Dramatic irony The audience knows something a character does not (Shakespeare controls the gap) The audience has known since the prologue that the lovers will die; every joy in Acts 1-2 plays against this knowledge [IRONY-D]
Situational irony The outcome is the opposite of what was expected or intended (the plot produces the gap) Friar Lawrence's plan to save the lovers' marriage is what causes their deaths [IRONY-S]

How to Write About Irony in an Essay

Three steps: (1) name the type and cite the specific moment; (2) define the gap -- what does the audience know that the character does not?; (3) argue what this gap does -- what emotional or intellectual effect does the gap produce for the audience?

Weak -- names the technique, no argument:

"[IRONY-D] There is dramatic irony in Act 3 because the audience knows the lovers will die."

Strong -- names the technique, defines the gap, argues the effect:

"[IRONY-D] When Romeo, [APP] the Montague son who does not yet know his marriage will cost his best friend's life, steps between Tybalt and Mercutio to stop the fight, Shakespeare gives the audience a knowledge Romeo cannot have: they know from the prologue that the attempt will fail, that the feud is stronger than the love, and that this gesture of peace will produce a death. The dramatic irony does not merely provide information -- it makes the audience into helpless witnesses, watching a disaster they cannot prevent, which is exactly the emotional experience Shakespeare wants to produce: not surprise at the tragedy, but recognition."

The Foil Structure: [FOIL] and Contrast Clauses

When you analyze a foil character, you always need to name both halves of the contrast -- what quality the foil has vs. what quality the primary character has. The contrast clause structures from Module 6 ([WHEREAS], [WHILE], [ALTHOUGH]) are natural fits here.

"[FOIL] [WHEREAS] Mercutio, [APP] Romeo's foil and the play's most purely social creature, is loyal to the world as it is -- to honor, to wit, to the obligations of male friendship -- Romeo has abandoned all of this for a private ideal that the world cannot accommodate. Mercutio's death is the bill Romeo pays for that choice, presented in Act 3 before he has any chance to explain why the ideal was worth it."

Exercise 1 -- Name the Type

For each moment below, identify the type of irony ([IRONY-V], [IRONY-D], or [IRONY-S]) and write one sentence arguing what the ironic gap does for the audience.

1. In Act 3 Scene 5, Juliet says to her mother "Would none but I might venge my cousin's death!" Lady Capulet believes she is expressing grief for Tybalt; Juliet means grief for Romeo's banishment.

Type? What does this gap argue about Juliet's position in her family?

2. The audience knows from the prologue that the lovers are "star-crossed" and will die. Romeo and Juliet marry in Act 2, believing they have found a way to be together.

Type? What does the audience's knowledge do to the joy of the marriage scene?

3. Friar Lawrence sends a letter to Romeo explaining Juliet's fake death plan. The letter fails to arrive. Romeo hears only that Juliet is dead, and the plan to save the lovers causes their deaths.

Type? What does this moment argue about intentions and outcomes in the play?

Exercise 2 -- Write with [FOIL] and [IRONY]

Write three sentences analyzing Mercutio as Romeo's foil and the dramatic irony of his death. Your three sentences must use:

  • [FOIL] + [WHEREAS] -- naming the contrast between Mercutio and Romeo
  • [IRONY-D] -- naming what the audience knows that Mercutio does not
  • [APP] -- an appositive identifying Mercutio's dramatic function in one phrase
