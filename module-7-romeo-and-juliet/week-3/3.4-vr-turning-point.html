VR 3.4: Stage the Turning Point

Act 3 Scene 1 -- the moment the play becomes something else

What You're Doing

Stage Act 3 Scene 1 as a before/after argument. Before Mercutio dies, the play is moving toward comedy (union, marriage, joy). After Mercutio dies, the play is moving toward tragedy (separation, banishment, death). Your composition argues what changes -- not what happens, but what the genre of the play becomes and what that shift means.

What You'll Need
  • Device: VR headset
  • Session ID: ELA7-M7-W3 -- Persistent Session
  • Location: Verona Street or City Square setting
  • Bring: Your [FOIL]/[WHEREAS] sentences from Grammar 3.2 Exercise 2; your Act 3 text
What to Do
1

Before/after composition (6-8 IFX)

Divide your space: one half represents the play before Mercutio's death (comedic movement -- union, possibility, joy). The other half represents the play after (tragic movement -- separation, death, inevitability). Use IFX that show the contrast. Place a dividing element -- a line of IFX, a barrier -- in the center representing the moment itself. Mercutio's death is the hinge; stage the hinge.

2

Whiteboard IFX: [FOIL] + [IRONY-D]

Write: [FOIL] + [WHEREAS] -- your Mercutio/Romeo contrast sentence from Grammar 3.2; then [IRONY-D] -- one sentence naming what the audience knows that Romeo does not know when he steps between the fighters.

3

Submit: snapshot + Canvas text

Snapshot YourLastName_M7_3.4. In the Canvas text box, type your Whiteboard IFX sentences and add one sentence: what does your spatial arrangement argue about the turning point that the Whiteboard IFX sentences don't say?

Submission

Upload snapshot YourLastName_M7_3.4 + Canvas text. Estimated time: 20-25 minutes in headset.

Facilitator Note

The before/after structure is demanding -- students must think about genre as well as plot. The question to push: "Why is it significant that it LOOKS like a comedy until Act 3 Scene 1? What does that generic expectation do to the audience when it's violated?" Strong [IRONY-D] sentence: "When Romeo steps between Tybalt and Mercutio to stop the fight, the audience -- armed with the prologue's knowledge that the lovers are star-crossed -- understands what Romeo cannot: that his gesture of peace, born of a marriage he cannot reveal, is the exact action that will make the play's ending irreversible."
