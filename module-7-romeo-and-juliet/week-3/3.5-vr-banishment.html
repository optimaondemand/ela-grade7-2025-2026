VR 3.5: Stage the Banishment

Everything Act 2 built, Act 3 undoes — stage the structural reversal and build your essay thesis

What You’re Doing

Romeo’s banishment is the structural reversal of the play: in Act 2, every event moved upward — toward the marriage, toward the reconciliation of the houses, toward a future. In Act 3, Romeo’s banishment undoes all of that in a single sentence from the Prince. Stage what was built in Act 2 alongside what is destroyed in Act 3. Then use the spatial arrangement to anchor your developing essay thesis: what does Shakespeare argue through this reversal?

What You’ll Need
  • Device: VR headset
  • Session ID: ELA7-M7-W3 — Persistent Session (same as VR 3.4)
  • Location: Marketplace / Public Square setting (or return to Courtyard)
  • Bring: Your [THESIS] from Grammar 3.3 Exercise 2 (or a draft of it); your Act 2 and Act 3 text; your [EV1] and [EV2] quotations
What to Do
1

Before-and-after composition (4–6 IFX)

Place IFX representing the three things Act 2 built: (1) the love / the shared sonnet meeting; (2) the balcony vows; (3) the marriage. Then place IFX representing what Act 3 destroys each of these with: (1) the death of Mercutio and Tybalt; (2) the banishment that breaks the vows’ geography; (3) the marriage rendered secret and dangerous. For each destruction IFX, attach a sticky note with the specific line or event from Act 3 that undoes what Act 2 built. Use the spatial arrangement to argue something: the IFX for what is lost should be smaller, darker, or displaced.

2

Whiteboard IFX: thesis + evidence

Write your developing [THESIS] about the play — this can be a revision of your thesis from Grammar 3.3, or a new direction your thinking has taken. Then: [EV1] and [EV2] — two specific quotations from Act 2 or Act 3 that you will use in your essay to support the thesis. Label each with a one-sentence explanation of how it supports your claim.

3

Record your argument (60–90 seconds) and submit

Stand near your composition and record a 60–90-second spatial recording. State your thesis. Walk through the before-and-after composition and explain how the structural reversal from Act 2 to Act 3 supports your argument. Reference at least one of your evidence quotations by memory or by reading it from your sticky note.

Submission

Upload snapshot YourLastName_M7_3.5 + paste recording link + thesis sentence to Canvas. Estimated time: 20–25 minutes in headset.

Facilitator Note

The thesis is the primary assessment item. Students should be developing a claim about what Shakespeare argues — not just what happens, but why the form of the play (the structural reversal, the dramatic irony, the meter) makes that argument. The recording should show a student who can articulate a thesis verbally and connect it to spatial evidence. If a student arrives without a thesis, redirect them to Grammar 3.3 before they begin the composition. Strong thesis example: “By using dramatic irony to let the audience watch Act 2’s love built on information the other characters lack, Shakespeare argues that the tragedy is not a failure of feeling but a failure of communication — the marriage could not survive the world’s ignorance of it.”
