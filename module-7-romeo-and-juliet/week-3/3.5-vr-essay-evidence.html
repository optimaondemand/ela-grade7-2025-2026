VR 3.5: Stage Your Essay Evidence

Your thesis, your three pieces of evidence, your concession -- in space, before prose

What You're Doing

By now you have a thesis from Grammar 3.3. This VR session stages your full essay argument -- thesis, evidence, concession -- before you write the prose version. What you cannot explain clearly in the recording is what you fix before writing. Come with your [STRONG] thesis and at least two quotations you plan to use as evidence.

What You'll Need
  • Device: VR headset
  • Session ID: ELA7-M7-W3 -- Persistent Session (same as VR 3.4)
  • Location: Verona Street or City Square setting
  • Bring: Your [STRONG] thesis from Grammar 3.3; two quotations with analytical sentences
What to Do
1

Stage your evidence (4-6 IFX)

Place IFX representing the specific formal choices your essay will argue about -- not Romeo and Juliet in general, but your argument's particular evidence. Label each with a sticky note: one phrase naming what the IFX represents and one phrase of your analytical claim about it. If your essay is about dramatic irony, what IFX represents the gap between audience knowledge and character knowledge? If it is about extended metaphor, what represents the light metaphor's hidden implication?

2

Whiteboard IFX: [THESIS] + [EV1] + [EV2] + [CON]

[THESIS] your one-sentence thesis; [EV1] and [EV2] your two quotations with one-sentence analytical explanation each; [CON] the strongest objection to your thesis and your counter in one sentence.

3

Record your argument (60-90 seconds) and submit

State your thesis, walk through your evidence, state your concession and counter. If you catch yourself summarizing the plot, stop and restart. Submit snapshot YourLastName_M7_3.5 + recording link + thesis sentence + [FIX] sentence.

Submission

Upload snapshot YourLastName_M7_3.5 + paste recording link + thesis + [FIX] sentence to Canvas. Estimated time: 25-30 minutes in headset.

Facilitator Note

The thesis sentence is the primary assessment item. Strong thesis: "By sustaining dramatic irony from the prologue through Act 5 -- giving the audience knowledge the lovers cannot have -- Shakespeare argues that tragedy is not a failure of love but a failure of the world to accommodate what love requires." Developing thesis: "Shakespeare uses dramatic irony to make the audience feel sad about the ending." Students with a developing thesis need one question before writing: "What does the audience's advance knowledge do to the audience that simply watching the plot unfold could not?"
