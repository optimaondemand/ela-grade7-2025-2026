7th Grade ELA -- Module 7 -- Week 4
Romeo and Juliet -- Lesson 4.1

Acts 4-5: The Tomb and the Argument of Tragedy

What the ending argues -- and why Shakespeare makes you watch it happen

Objectives

  • Explain what the tomb scene argues about language, time, and the failure of the world to accommodate love
  • Identify the structural irony of the play's resolution -- the families reconcile only after both children are dead
  • Define tragic form and catharsis and explain what Shakespeare is arguing about the audience's emotional experience

Warm-Up

Think about a story that ended sadly but felt right -- not happy, but complete. What made the sad ending feel satisfying rather than just depressing? Write two or three sentences about what a sad ending can do that a happy ending cannot.

Lesson

Acts 4 and 5 complete the argument Shakespeare has been building since the prologue. Juliet takes the Friar's potion and appears dead; the letter that would have explained this to Romeo fails to arrive; Romeo learns only that Juliet is dead, buys poison, goes to the tomb, and kills himself beside her. Juliet wakes, finds him dead, and kills herself. The families find them and reconcile -- but only after the cost has been paid in full. This structure is not accidental. The tragedy argues that the world as it is -- organized by feuds, by names, by inherited hatred -- cannot accommodate what Romeo and Juliet are. Not because the love is foolish or impulsive, but because the world will not make room for it. The ending is not a punishment for bad choices; it is the play's proof of its thesis. The prologue said the lovers would die; now we know why, and the why is the argument: language -- the name Montague, the name Capulet, the word "banished" -- is more powerful than love, unless love breaks the language by ending the lives that bear the names. The families reconcile in the final scene, building golden statues -- and the audience recognizes that this too is the play's irony. The statues will be built. The children are dead.

Historical Context

Aristotle, writing about tragedy in the Poetics, argued that great tragedy produces catharsis -- a purging or release of pity and fear in the audience. The audience watches something terrible happen to people who did not deserve it, and the experience of watching -- with full emotional engagement -- somehow clarifies and purifies those emotions rather than simply depressing the audience. Shakespeare's Elizabethan audience would have understood Romeo and Juliet in this tradition: you are meant to cry, to feel helpless, to feel the waste of it -- and to leave the theatre somehow cleaner for the experience. The catharsis is the argument.

Vocabulary: Tragic Form and Catharsis

Tragic form [TRAGIC] refers to the structural shape of a tragedy: the protagonist (or protagonists) are brought from joy to ruin through a combination of circumstance and the world's limitations. The tragic hero does not have to make a fatal flaw -- sometimes the world is simply incapable of containing what the hero is. Catharsis [CATHARSIS] is the emotional purification the audience experiences at the end of a well-made tragedy: not just sadness, but a clarified, illuminated sadness that feels like understanding. Shakespeare argues through the tragic form of Romeo and Juliet that certain kinds of love -- total, fast, private -- cannot survive in the social world, and the audience's catharsis is the proof that the argument landed.

Comprehension Check

1. Romeo's final speech in the tomb includes the line "Eyes, look your last! / Arms, take your last embrace!" What does this return to imperative direct address (apostrophe to his own body parts) argue about Romeo's state of mind?

A. It shows that Romeo has gone insane from grief and is no longer able to think clearly.
B. Romeo is speaking in prose here, which signals his breakdown.
C. The apostrophe to his own body parts argues that Romeo is fully present, fully in control of his final choice -- commanding himself with the same formal language he used to declare his love, which makes the death not impulsive but deliberate and complete.
D. Romeo uses apostrophe here because he cannot address Juliet directly since she appears to be dead.

✓ C

2. At the end of Act 5, both families agree to build golden statues of the lovers and reconcile. What does this resolution argue about the cost of the feud?

A. It suggests that the deaths were not in vain -- the reconciliation proves the feud can end and the sacrifice had meaning.
B. It is the play's final irony: the feud ends only when there is no one left to hate, and the golden statues -- commemorations of children who died so the families could stop fighting -- are an argument that no language of honor or grudge was worth what it cost.
C. It shows that Shakespeare believed in the power of love to transform social conflict.
D. It provides a happy ending that balances the tragedy, following Elizabethan theatrical convention.

✓ B

3. In Act 5, Romeo says of Juliet's face: "Beauty's ensign yet / Is crimson in thy lips and in thy cheeks, / And death's pale flag is not advanced there." What does the return of the light/color metaphor in the tomb argue?

A. Romeo is confused and thinks Juliet is still alive, which is an error in his reasoning.
B. The return of the color and light metaphor in the tomb argues that the beauty metaphor has followed them here -- that the language of love and the language of death now occupy the same space, and Romeo is using the vocabulary of life to greet what he believes is death.
C. Shakespeare is giving Romeo a final moment of happiness before the tragedy ends.
D. The crimson and pale imagery shows that Romeo has studied medicine and is making a clinical observation about Juliet's skin color.

✓ B
