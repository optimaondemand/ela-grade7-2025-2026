Romeo and Juliet — Lesson 4.1

The Final Acts: Recognition, Reversal, and the Form of Tragedy

What Aristotle said tragedy does to an audience — and whether Shakespeare agrees

Objectives

  • Define catharsis, recognition, and reversal as Aristotle describes them and explain how each appears in Acts 4 and 5
  • Analyze the tomb scene as the culmination of the play’s dramatic irony and its argument about language and fate
  • Define elegy and explain what it means that the Prince’s final lines treat the lovers’ story as already a story worth telling

Warm-Up

The word catharsis comes from the Greek word for cleansing or purging. Aristotle argued that tragedy produces catharsis: watching great suffering on stage does something to an audience — it moves through them and leaves them, in some way, cleansed. Before you read the lesson, write two or three sentences about what you think that means. When you watch something deeply sad — a film, a story, a piece of news — does it ever feel like something is released rather than added? What do you think tragedy does to the person watching it?

Lesson

In his Poetics, written in the fourth century BCE, Aristotle described the form that great tragedy takes: a person of noble character falls through a combination of external fate and an internal flaw called hamartia — not a moral defect but a tragic error, a quality that in one context is a virtue and in another becomes the instrument of destruction. The fall moves through two pivotal moments: recognition (anagnorisis), the moment when the hero finally understands what has happened and why, and reversal (peripeteia), the moment when the action turns from good fortune to catastrophe, or vice versa. These two movements together produce in the audience catharsis — an emotional purging, a release of the pity and fear the play has built, leaving the audience not destroyed but, somehow, clarified. Romeo and Juliet maps precisely onto this structure: Romeo’s hamartia is his impulsiveness, his refusal to reason when feeling is overwhelming him, and the reversal is the sequence in Acts 3 through 5 in which every fortunate event converts to disaster. The recognition comes in the tomb: Romeo sees Juliet and cannot reason — she appears dead, he has no information, and he acts entirely on feeling. This is the moment the hamartia completes its work. He drinks the poison. She wakes. She understands. She dies. The feud ends not because the families chose reconciliation but because they have consumed what they were destroying from the beginning — the children who were their future. The Prince’s final lines — “For never was a story of more woe / Than this of Juliet and her Romeo” — enact the third function of tragedy: they transform an event into a story, arguing that the telling of the tragedy is itself a form of meaning, a way of making permanent what love and violence and careless fate have destroyed.

Historical Context

Elizabethan attitudes toward fate and free will were shaped by both classical philosophy and Christian theology, and they were not entirely consistent with each other. The Protestant Reformation, still recent in Shakespeare’s lifetime, had intensified debates about predestination — whether human outcomes were fixed by God in advance, or whether individuals bore genuine moral responsibility. Shakespeare does not resolve this debate in Romeo and Juliet; he dramatizes it. Romeo and Juliet act with full agency and genuine love, and yet the prologue has told the audience they are “star-crossed” from the beginning. The play holds both: they chose, and they were fated, and the tragedy is that these two things are not contradictory.

Vocabulary: Tragic Form

Tragic form, as Aristotle defines it, requires: (1) a protagonist of noble character who is not simply good or bad; (2) hamartia — a tragic flaw or error of judgment, not a moral failing; (3) reversal (peripeteia) — the turn from fortune to catastrophe; (4) recognition (anagnorisis) — the moment of understanding; and (5) catharsis — the emotional purging the audience undergoes. Friar Lawrence delivers the play’s most concentrated recognition speech in Act 5, summarizing the whole story to the Prince. His final lines — “For never was a story of more woe / Than this of Juliet and her Romeo” — belong to the Prince.

Copia exercise: Rewrite Friar Lawrence’s account of the whole story (Act 5 Scene 3, his long speech beginning “I will be brief”) as a modern prose paragraph of 4–5 sentences. Do not summarize lazily; try to capture what he sees as the cause of everything that happened, in your own language. What does Friar Lawrence think went wrong?

Vocabulary: Elegy

An elegy is a formal expression of mourning for the dead, typically a poem or speech that honors the deceased and, in honoring them, seeks to transform grief into something lasting. The Prince’s final couplet is elegiac: it commemorates the lovers by naming their story as a story, converting tragedy into monument.

Reading: Romeo and Juliet — Acts 4 and 5

Read Acts 4 and 5 (all scenes). Pay attention to:

  • Friar Lawrence’s plan in Act 4: what does he believe will happen? What does the audience know that makes his confidence ironic?
  • Juliet’s soliloquy before drinking the potion (Act 4 Scene 3): how does she reason through her fear? Is her reasoning clear or desperate?
  • Romeo’s speech in the tomb (Act 5 Scene 3) before he drinks the poison: what does he see, and what does he fail to see? Where does the hamartia show itself most clearly?
  • The Prince’s final couplet: what does it mean to end a tragedy by naming it as a story?

Comprehension Check

1. Aristotle defines catharsis as the emotional purging an audience undergoes at the end of a great tragedy. Based on the lesson, which answer best explains how catharsis is different from simply feeling sad?

A. Catharsis is the moment when the audience laughs at the tragedy because it is so extreme.
B. Catharsis is an intellectual response, not an emotional one — the audience understands the tragedy without feeling it.
C. Catharsis involves pity (feeling for the suffering character) and fear (recognizing that the same forces could work on any of us); the purging releases these accumulated emotions, leaving the audience clarified rather than simply distressed.
D. Catharsis only occurs when the tragic hero has committed a moral wrong that deserves punishment, and the audience feels justice has been served.

✓ C

2. In the tomb scene, Romeo sees Juliet and drinks poison, believing her dead. Where does his hamartia (tragic flaw) show itself most clearly in this action?

A. Romeo shows cowardice in choosing death rather than finding another way out of his situation.
B. Romeo acts before he can reason — he sees, he feels, he acts, without pausing to verify what is actually true; his impulsiveness, which in Act 1 and 2 appeared as the speed and intensity of his love, is here the quality that kills him.
C. Romeo shows excessive pride by refusing to wait for Friar Lawrence, who he knows is coming to the tomb.
D. Romeo’s decision to die is entirely rational, given that he has no other options after his banishment.

✓ B

3. The Prince ends the play with a rhyming couplet: “For never was a story of more woe / Than this of Juliet and her Romeo.” What does this formal choice — ending in a rhyming couplet that calls it a “story” — argue?

A. The rhyme signals that the play is a comedy after all, because Shakespeare uses rhyming couplets primarily in his comedies.
B. The Prince is being sarcastic — he thinks the story is overwrought and wants the audience to agree.
C. The rhyme creates false closure, arguing that language cannot actually contain the grief of the moment.
D. By calling it a “story” in a formal rhyming couplet, the Prince transforms the event into a monument — the tragedy is named, elegized, and made permanent; the telling becomes the survivors’ only remaining act of meaning, arguing that what cannot be undone can at least be remembered in verse.

✓ D
