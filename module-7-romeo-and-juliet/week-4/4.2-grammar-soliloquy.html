Grammar: The Soliloquy -- Direct Address and Dramatic Structure

How Shakespeare uses a character speaking alone to make the audience part of the argument

What a Soliloquy Does

A soliloquy [SOLO] is a speech in which a character speaks alone on stage -- no other characters hear them. But the audience does. This formal choice creates a unique relationship: the audience is given access to a character's inner life that no other character can have. In Romeo and Juliet, both major characters have soliloquies at key moments: Romeo in the balcony scene, Juliet before taking the potion. Each time, Shakespeare uses the soliloquy to give the audience something that cannot be communicated through action or dialogue alone.

The soliloquy is different from ordinary speech because it is simultaneously private (no character hears it) and public (the audience hears it). This double quality makes it the perfect vehicle for dramatic irony: the audience is positioned inside the character's thinking, and that positioning is itself a formal argument about what the play wants us to understand.

Juliet's Soliloquy Before the Potion (4.3)

Juliet's soliloquy in Act 4 Scene 3 is structurally the most ironic in the play. She imagines all the things that could go wrong with the Friar's plan -- what if it does not work? what if it is a real poison? what if she wakes in the tomb too early? -- and then she takes the potion anyway. The audience watches knowing two things Juliet does not: (a) that the plan will go wrong, and (b) that it will go wrong in a way she did not imagine. The soliloquy makes the audience Juliet's intimate at the exact moment they cannot help her.

"[SOLO] Juliet's Act 4 Scene 3 soliloquy, [APP] the most intimately ironic speech in the play, gives the audience her real fears -- the plan will fail, the Friar cannot be trusted, she will wake too early -- and then she drinks anyway. [IRONY-D] The audience knows from the play's structure that her worst fear (waking to find Romeo dead) is exactly what will happen, but in a form she did not imagine; [WHEREAS] whereas Juliet's fears are specific and contingent, the reality the play has prepared will be simpler and more absolute."
