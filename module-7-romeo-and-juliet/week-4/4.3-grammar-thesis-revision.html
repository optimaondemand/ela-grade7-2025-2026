Grammar: Drafting and Revising Your Module 7 Thesis

From working thesis to strong thesis -- the four revision questions

The Four Revision Questions for a Literary Analysis Thesis

R1: Does my thesis name a specific formal or rhetorical choice?

"Shakespeare uses language" is not specific. "Shakespeare uses dramatic irony sustained from the prologue through Act 5" is specific. The formal choice must be nameable.

R2: Does my thesis argue what the formal choice DOES -- not just what it is?

"Shakespeare uses dramatic irony to create tension" is identifying an effect but not arguing a claim. "Shakespeare uses dramatic irony to argue that tragedy is recognition, not surprise" is an argument about what the play claims.

R3: Could my thesis be written in a sentence a non-reader could have written?

If your thesis does not require reading the play, it is probably too general. "Romeo and Juliet argues that love is powerful" requires no reading. "By sustaining the light/dark metaphor from Act 2's balcony to Act 5's tomb, Shakespeare argues that love and death inhabit the same language" requires reading the text.

R4: Does my thesis connect to the essential question?

The essential question is: How does Shakespeare use the structure of language to argue that some experiences cannot be expressed in ordinary words? Your thesis should show how the formal choice you are analyzing is part of Shakespeare's answer.

Module 7 Essay Focus Options

Option A: The Shared Sonnet and Meter

Argue how the shared sonnet in Act 1 Scene 5 and the iambic pentameter that runs through the play make an argument about the nature of Romeo and Juliet's connection that the plot alone cannot make.

Option B: The Light/Dark Extended Metaphor

Argue how the light/dark extended metaphor from Act 2 through Act 5 makes an argument about love and time that a direct statement of the theme could not.

Option C: Dramatic Irony from Prologue to Tomb

Argue how sustaining dramatic irony from the prologue through Act 5 makes an argument about the nature of tragedy -- specifically, that the audience's experience of recognition rather than surprise is itself the play's thesis.

Option D: Mercutio as Foil and the Cost of Romeo's Choice

Argue how Mercutio's function as Romeo's foil -- and the specific terms of the contrast -- makes an argument about what private love costs in a social world organized by inherited hatred.

Exercise 1 -- Apply the Four Revision Questions

Take your working thesis from Grammar 3.3. Apply all four revision questions to it, labeling your response [R1], [R2], [R3], [R4]. Then write your revised thesis labeled [THESIS].

Exercise 2 -- Essay Opening Paragraph

Write your essay introduction (50-75 words). Requirements:

  • Open with a claim about the play -- not "Shakespeare was born in 1564"
  • Use [METAPHOR] or [IRONY-D] somewhere in the body of the introduction
  • End with your [THESIS] -- one sentence naming the formal choice and what it argues

Add a [FIX] sentence: what is the one thing your introduction still needs to do that it currently does not?
