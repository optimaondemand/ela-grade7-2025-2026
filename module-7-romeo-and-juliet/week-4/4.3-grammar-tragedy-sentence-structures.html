The Four M7 Structures in the Final Acts

By Week 4, you have four analytical tools in regular use. Each one is particularly powerful in writing about the tomb scene and the play’s conclusion, and each one makes a different kind of argument:

[IRONY]

Use for the gap between expectation and outcome — most powerfully in the tomb scene, where Romeo arrives with wrong information and the audience watches him act on it. [IRONY] in the final acts is argument about what the audience experiences: not just pity but the anguish of watching someone make the wrong choice for the last time, with full knowledge and no power to intervene.

[METAPHOR]

The light/dark extended metaphor culminates in the tomb. The torch Romeo carries is the only light in the dark vault; Juliet’s face is the only warmth. When Romeo praises her beauty — “her beauty makes / This vault a feasting presence full of light” — the metaphor that began with her as the sun in Act 2 ends here: she is still the light, but now in a tomb. This is the metaphor completing its argument: light cannot live in the dark, and neither can they.

[METER]

Watch for shifts in verse near the death scenes. Romeo’s final speech before drinking the poison is in iambic pentameter, controlled and formal even in extremity — the meter holds until it doesn’t. Juliet’s final lines are shorter, more fractured: “O happy dagger! / This is thy sheath; there rust, and let me die.” The broken line argues through form that her world has lost the shape that makes sustained verse possible.

[FOIL]

The tomb reveals what the foil relationships argued all along. Paris is there with flowers; Romeo with poison. Mercutio is absent, having died for the gap between Romeo’s new knowledge and the old world’s ignorance. The Nurse is absent; the Friar is late; everyone who might have helped has been removed or delayed. The ending reveals each foil pair’s argument in its final, irreversible form.

Model Analytical Paragraph — All Four Structures Labeled

Read this paragraph twice: once to follow the argument, and once to notice how each labeled structure does a different kind of analytical work.

[IRONY] When Romeo enters the Capulet tomb and pronounces Juliet “dead,” the audience knows what he cannot know — that she is sleeping, not dead, and that Friar Lawrence’s message never arrived — and this gap makes his entire final speech a form of tragic irony: every word of love and farewell lands on the audience as a word spoken in ignorance of the truth that would save him. [METAPHOR] Even as he speaks, he praises her beauty in the language of light: “her beauty makes / This vault a feasting presence full of light,” and in doing so completes the play’s central extended metaphor — Juliet remains the sun, the light, the warmth; but the sun is now in a tomb, and Romeo does not understand what this means. [METER] His final speech before the poison is in full iambic pentameter, his language controlled and formal — the meter does not break, which argues that Romeo goes to his death in full command of his eloquence, and that his eloquence itself cannot save him; language, even at its most ordered, cannot close the gap between what he knows and what is true. [FOIL] Paris, who came to the tomb with flowers and conventional grief, is now dead as well — killed by Romeo, the foil pair resolved violently at its endpoint; what their contrast argued throughout the play (proper love vs. total love) concludes here, with both men dead for Juliet, and Shakespeare arguing through the deaths that the social world’s version of love and the play’s version of love are not compatible, and never were.

Exercise 1 — Tomb Scene Sentences

Write three analytical sentences about the tomb scene. Each sentence must use the labeled structure indicated and make an argument about what Shakespeare achieves in the scene through that structure.

Sentence 1: [IRONY] + [WHEREAS]

Analyze the gap between what Romeo believes when he enters the tomb and what the audience knows. Use [WHEREAS] to name the contrast in one clause. Make an argument about what this gap argues — not just that it is sad, but what Shakespeare claims about language, knowledge, or fate through it.

Sentence 2: [METAPHOR] + [WHEREAS]

Analyze Romeo’s use of light/dark imagery in the tomb. What does it mean that Juliet is still described as light even here? Use [WHEREAS] to contrast what the metaphor argues in Act 2 with what it argues in Act 5.

Sentence 3: [IRONY] + [WHEREAS]

Analyze Juliet’s moment of waking in a different register: what does she find when she wakes, and how does her experience of the moment complete the play’s structure of recognition and reversal?

Exercise 2 — Essay Introduction Paragraph (50–75 words)

Write your essay introduction paragraph in 50–75 words. It should open with a broad claim about tragedy, Shakespeare, or the essential question (use [WHEREAS] or [ALTHOUGH] to set up a contrast), then narrow to your specific [THESIS] about the play. Label both structures. This paragraph will become the opening of your Module 7 essay.

Model opening (do not copy):

“[WHEREAS] Whereas tragedy has always been understood as a form that produces grief, Shakespeare’s Romeo and Juliet uses dramatic irony to argue that grief is not what the audience primarily experiences — what they experience is the anguish of knowing. [THESIS] By building the play so that the audience always knows more than the characters, Shakespeare argues that some experiences — love at this intensity, loss at this speed — cannot be represented in ordinary words, only held in the gap between what we watch and what we cannot prevent.”
