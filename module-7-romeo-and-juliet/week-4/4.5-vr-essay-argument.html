VR 4.5: Stage Your Essay Argument

Choose your essay focus, build its evidence spatially, and record your argument

What You’re Doing

You have been developing an essay argument across all four weeks of Module 7. In this VR session, you will choose one of four essay focus options, build its evidence spatially in the Tomb/Crypt setting, record a 2–3 minute argument, and finalize your thesis and fix sentence. This is the final preparation before you write your Module 7 essay in Week 5.

What You’ll Need
  • Device: VR headset
  • Session ID: ELA7-M7-W4 — Persistent Session (same as VR 4.4)
  • Location: Tomb / Crypt or Dark Interior setting
  • Bring: Your [THESIS] from Grammar 4.3 Exercise 2; your [EV1] and [EV2] quotations from across the module; your [FIX] revision sentence from Grammar 4.3

Choose Your Essay Focus

Select one of the four options below. Your entire spatial composition, Whiteboard IFX, and recording should be organized around the claim of your chosen focus.

Option A — Iambic Pentameter as the Play’s Form of Fate

Argue that iambic pentameter functions in the play the way fate functions in the plot: a structure that the characters move within, that gives their speech its dignity, and that shapes their experience whether they choose it or not. Evidence should span at least three acts.

Option B — The Light/Dark Extended Metaphor as Structural Argument

Argue that the light/dark metaphor is not decorative but structural: it tracks the play’s movement from possibility to extinction, beginning with Juliet as the sun and ending with her as light in a tomb. Evidence should trace the metaphor from Act 2 through Act 5.

Option C — Dramatic Irony in Acts 4–5 as the Audience’s Tragedy

Argue that the tragedy of Acts 4 and 5 is primarily the audience’s, not the characters’: through dramatic irony, the audience experiences the death of the lovers in advance of the characters, watching in helpless knowledge. What does this argue about what tragedy does to an audience?

Option D — Mercutio as Foil: What His Death Reveals About Romeo’s Love

Argue that Mercutio functions throughout the play as the foil who makes Romeo’s love legible — his cynicism is the standard against which Romeo’s idealism is measured — and that his death in Act 3 removes the play’s last protection against Romeo’s extremity. What does the play argue, through this foil relationship, about what love of this kind costs?

What to Do
1

Evidence composition (4–6 IFX)

Place 4–6 IFX representing the key moments from across the module that support your chosen essay focus. Each IFX should be labeled with a sticky note naming the act/scene, the specific quotation or moment, and one phrase about what it contributes to your thesis. Arrange them in an order that shows a logical argument, not just a chronological sequence.

2

Whiteboard IFX: [THESIS][EV1][EV2][CON]

Write your [THESIS] from Grammar 4.3, or a revised version. Then: [EV1] and [EV2] — your two best quotations, each with a one-sentence analytical explanation. Then: [CON] — your concession sentence, which names the strongest counterargument to your thesis and briefly acknowledges what it gets right before your thesis reasserts itself.

3

Record your argument (2–3 minutes) and submit

Walk through your composition and record a 2–3 minute argument. State your thesis first. Walk through your evidence IFX in the order that builds your argument. Deliver your concession and explain why it doesn’t undermine your thesis. End with your [FIX] revision sentence — read it aloud and explain what the revision improves.

Submission

Upload snapshot YourLastName_M7_4.5 + paste recording link + thesis sentence + [FIX] revision sentence to Canvas. Estimated time: 25–30 minutes in headset.

Facilitator Note

This is the culminating VR preparation for the module essay. The thesis and recording are the primary assessment items. The four focus options are designed so that each one is tractable with the evidence students have gathered across the module — redirect students who try to argue all four at once. A strong recording will sound like a student who has a claim, knows their evidence, and can explain how the evidence builds the claim. The [FIX] sentence submission is an indicator of whether students can self-assess their writing quality; look for revisions that make claims sharper or sentences more precise, not just longer.
