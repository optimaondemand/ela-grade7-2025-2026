VR 4.5: Stage Your Full Essay Argument

Your thesis, your evidence, your concession -- before you write the prose

What You're Doing

Stage your complete essay argument in VR before you write the prose version. Come with your revised [THESIS] from Grammar 4.3, two specific quotations with analytical sentences, and your concession. What you cannot explain in the recording is what you fix before you write.

What You'll Need
  • Device: VR headset
  • Session ID: ELA7-M7-W4 -- Persistent Session (same as VR 4.4)
  • Location: Crypt/Tomb interior or Stone Cathedral setting
  • Bring: Revised [THESIS] from Grammar 4.3; two quotations with analytical sentences; your essay focus option
What to Do
1

Stage your evidence (4-6 IFX)

Place IFX representing your two pieces of evidence -- what formally does each represent? Label each with a sticky note: the quotation (abbreviated) + one phrase of your analytical claim. The IFX should represent the formal choice, not the character or event.

2

Whiteboard IFX: [THESIS] + [EV1] + [EV2] + [CON]

[THESIS] your one-sentence thesis; [EV1] and [EV2] your two quotations with one-sentence analytical explanation each; [CON] the strongest objection to your thesis and your counter in one sentence.

3

Record your argument (2-3 minutes) and submit

State your thesis, walk through evidence, state concession and counter. If you are summarizing the plot rather than arguing about a formal choice, stop and restart. Submit snapshot YourLastName_M7_4.5 + recording link + thesis + [FIX] sentence.

Submission

Upload snapshot YourLastName_M7_4.5 + paste recording link + thesis + [FIX] sentence to Canvas. Estimated time: 25-30 minutes in headset.

Facilitator Note

Thesis is the primary assessment item. Strong thesis examples by option: Option A: "The shared sonnet in Act 1 Scene 5, where Romeo and Juliet complete each other's iambic pentameter before knowing each other's names, argues that their love is not a choice but a structural inevitability -- and the tragic irony is that the same form that argues for their union also carries, in the prologue's fourteen identical lines, the announcement of their deaths." Option C: "By announcing the lovers' deaths in the prologue and sustaining dramatic irony across all five acts, Shakespeare argues that tragedy is not a surprise but a recognition -- the audience does not discover the ending in Act 5; they arrive at it."
