7th Grade ELA -- Module 7 -- Week 5
Romeo and Juliet -- Lesson 5.1

Romeo and Juliet as Argument

What Shakespeare argued -- and how the formal choices ARE the argument

Objectives

  • Articulate what Romeo and Juliet argues as a complete work -- not its plot but its thesis about language, love, and the social world
  • Explain how the four formal choices studied in this module (meter/sonnet, extended metaphor, dramatic irony, foil/soliloquy) work together to perform the play's argument
  • Apply the four essay revision questions to your own draft before submitting

Warm-Up

Before you began reading Romeo and Juliet, you would have answered the essential question one way: "How does Shakespeare use the structure of language to argue that some experiences cannot be expressed in ordinary words?" Write two or three sentences about how your answer is different now. What do you know now that you didn't know at the start?

Lesson

By the end of Romeo and Juliet, Shakespeare has made an argument that goes beyond the story of two teenagers in Verona. The play argues that there are forms of love -- total, sudden, structurally necessary -- that the social world as it is organized cannot accommodate; and that language, which ought to be the medium through which love is expressed and protected, is also the weapon that destroys it. The name "Montague" kills Romeo. The word "banished" separates the lovers more completely than any physical wall. The failed letter -- a piece of language that never arrives -- is the final mechanism of the tragedy. And yet the play also argues the reverse: that language, structured into verse, into sonnet, into shared iambic pentameter, can embody what it cannot explain. When Romeo and Juliet complete each other's lines in Act 1 Scene 5, they are not just falling in love; they are becoming, formally, one argument. The tragedy is not that they loved too much. It is that the world they lived in had older language -- of grudge, of honor, of name -- and that language was more powerful than theirs. Shakespeare's final argument, delivered not in a speech but in the whole shape of the play, is that some experiences are too large for ordinary words -- and that the only way to argue this is to build, in verse, a world in which it becomes visible.

How the Four Formal Choices Work Together

[METER] / Sonnet: The shared sonnet argues that the love is inevitable and complete; the prologue sonnet argues that the ending is, too. The same form performs opposite arguments.
[METAPHOR]: The light/dark extended metaphor builds the world the lovers inhabit -- and embeds, in the physics of light, the argument that what is brightest sets first.
[IRONY-D]: Dramatic irony turns the audience into witnesses rather than participants -- we know; we cannot help; and our helplessness is the argument that the world will not change for the lovers.
[FOIL] / [SOLO]: Mercutio's death and the soliloquies give us the private cost of the public conflict -- what individuals pay when the inherited language of hatred is more powerful than the chosen language of love.

Comprehension Check

1. The prologue and the final couplet both use the sonnet form. What does using the same form for both the opening and closing of the play argue?

A. Shakespeare ran out of ideas and simply repeated the opening form at the close.
B. The shared form argues that the play is a complete and closed argument -- the prologue announces the verdict; the final couplet delivers it; the five acts in between are the evidence. The sonnet form frames the tragedy the way a judge's gavel frames a trial.
C. The sonnet form was required by theatrical convention for both opening and closing speeches.
D. The use of the same form signals that nothing has changed, which is a pessimistic argument about the futility of love.

✓ B

2. A student argues: "The real tragedy in Romeo and Juliet is not that the lovers die but that the language of the feud -- name, honor, grudge -- is more powerful than the language of the love." Is this a strong thesis, a developing thesis, or an observation?

A. It is an observation -- it describes something about the play without naming a specific formal choice.
B. It is a developing thesis -- it makes a claim about language and power but does not name the specific formal choice through which Shakespeare makes this argument.
C. It is a strong thesis -- it names the formal choice (language of feud vs. language of love) and argues what happens when they collide.
D. It is a summary of the plot rather than a literary analysis argument.

✓ B

3. Your essay must answer the essential question in the conclusion: How does Shakespeare use the structure of language to argue that some experiences cannot be expressed in ordinary words? What does a strong conclusion do that a simple thesis restatement does not?

A. A strong conclusion is longer than a thesis restatement, which makes it more complete.
B. A strong conclusion restates the thesis in different words so the reader is not bored by repetition.
C. A strong conclusion extends the thesis -- it says what the play taught you that you couldn't have said before reading it; it answers the essential question with the knowledge the essay has built, not just the knowledge you started with.
D. A strong conclusion summarizes the main events of the play so the reader remembers the plot.

✓ C
