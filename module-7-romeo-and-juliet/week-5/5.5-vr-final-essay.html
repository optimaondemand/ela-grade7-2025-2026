VR 5.5: Stage Your Final Essay Argument

Your thesis, your evidence, your voice -- before you commit it to prose

What You're Doing

Stage your final essay argument in VR before you write the prose version. This is your last chance to test the argument spatially. What does not make sense in the recording is what you fix before you write. Come with your revised thesis, two or three specific quotations, and your concession.

What You'll Need
  • Device: VR headset
  • Session ID: ELA7-M7-W5 -- Persistent Session
  • Location: Your choice -- choose a Location that fits the formal choices in your essay
  • Bring: Your revised [THESIS]; two or three quotations with analytical sentences; your [CON]
What to Do
1

Stage your evidence (4-6 IFX)

Place IFX representing the specific formal choices your essay argues about. Label each with a sticky note: the quotation (abbreviated) + one phrase of your analytical claim. The IFX should represent the formal choice, not the character or scene.

2

Whiteboard IFX: [THESIS] + [EV1] + [EV2] + [CON]

[THESIS] your one-sentence thesis; [EV1] and [EV2] your two quotations with one-sentence analytical explanation each; [CON] the strongest objection to your thesis and your counter.

3

Record your argument (2-3 minutes) and submit

State your thesis, walk through your evidence, state your concession and counter. If you catch yourself summarizing the plot, stop and restart. Submit snapshot YourLastName_M7_5.5 + recording link + thesis + [FIX] sentence.

Submission

Upload snapshot YourLastName_M7_5.5 + paste recording link + thesis + [FIX] sentence to Canvas. Estimated time: 25-35 minutes in headset.

Facilitator Note

The thesis is the primary assessment item. Developing thesis: "Shakespeare uses dramatic irony to make the audience sad about the ending." Strong thesis: "By announcing the lovers' deaths in the prologue and sustaining dramatic irony across all five acts, Shakespeare argues that tragedy is not a surprise imposed on the characters but a recognition delivered to the audience -- we do not learn the ending in Act 5; we confirm it." Students with developing theses need one question before writing: "What does the audience's advance knowledge do to the audience that simply watching the plot could not?"
