Rhetoric Capstone — Lesson 1.1

What Is Rhetoric? Language That Wants Something

Ethos, pathos, logos — and why every writer is trying to persuade you of something

Objectives

  • Define rhetoric and explain why all discourse — speeches, poems, essays, advertisements, arguments between friends — makes rhetorical choices
  • Identify the three classical appeals: ethos (credibility), pathos (emotion), and logos (logic and evidence)
  • Analyze how Patrick Henry deploys all three appeals in “Give Me Liberty or Give Me Death” and explain which appeal does the heaviest work in the speech

Warm-Up

Think of the most persuasive thing anyone has ever said to you — a parent, a friend, a teacher, an advertisement, a speech you heard or read. What made it persuasive? Was it who said it, how it made you feel, or whether the reasoning was sound? Write two or three sentences about what worked and why.

Lesson

Rhetoric is the art of using language to persuade. Every text you encounter — a political speech, a poem, a social media post, a legal argument, a love letter — is making choices about how to move its reader. The ancient Greeks organized these choices into three categories, which remain the most useful analytical vocabulary available: ethos, the appeal to the speaker’s credibility and character; pathos, the appeal to the audience’s emotions; and logos, the appeal to reason, evidence, and logic. No real text uses only one. Patrick Henry’s 1775 speech to the Virginia Convention — “Give Me Liberty or Give Me Death” — is one of the most studied examples in English of all three working together. Henry is trying to persuade a reluctant colonial legislature to commit to war with Britain. He cannot order them; he can only argue. And so he deploys his credibility as a known figure, the emotional weight of slavery and liberty, and the logical chain of evidence that Britain has already broken every promise it ever made. The speech works because none of the three appeals is absent and none is excessive. In Module 8, you will practice reading any text the way you would read Henry’s speech — asking not just what it says but what it wants, and how it uses the structure of language to get it.

Historical Context

Patrick Henry delivered this speech on March 23, 1775 — six weeks before the first shots of the American Revolution at Lexington and Concord. The Virginia Convention was debating whether to send Virginia troops to support the war effort. Many delegates still hoped for reconciliation with Britain. Henry was speaking to persuade men who were afraid of what commitment to war would cost them. He had one speech. The text we have is a reconstruction published forty years after the fact, but it preserves the argumentative structure Henry used — and the three appeals are as clearly visible as if they had been labeled.

Vocabulary: Ethos, Pathos, Logos

Ethos [ETHOS] is the appeal to the speaker’s credibility, character, and authority. It answers the question: why should the audience trust this speaker? Ethos can be established directly (“I have studied this for twenty years”) or indirectly (through the precision of the language, the fairness of the argument, the willingness to acknowledge opposing views). Pathos [PATHOS] is the appeal to the audience’s emotions — their fear, hope, grief, pride, or love. It answers the question: how does this text make the audience feel, and what does that feeling make them want to do? Logos [LOGOS] is the appeal to reason and evidence — facts, statistics, logical deductions, analogies, precedents. It answers the question: what reasoning does the text offer, and does it hold?

Copia exercise: Find one sentence from Henry’s speech that uses [PATHOS]. Rewrite it in plain, unemotional language that preserves the same factual content. Then write one sentence explaining what the emotional version does that your plain version cannot.

Comprehension Check

1. Henry opens his speech by addressing the Convention respectfully and acknowledging that other speakers have made arguments he disagrees with. What rhetorical appeal does this opening move primarily serve?

A. Logos — he is offering evidence that opposing arguments exist.
B. Pathos — he is trying to make the audience feel guilty for their timidity.
C. Ethos — he establishes his credibility and fairness before making his argument, so the audience trusts him before he challenges them.
D. None of the three — the opening is purely conventional and has no rhetorical function.

✓ C

2. Henry argues: “We have petitioned; we have remonstrated; we have supplicated; we have prostrated ourselves before the throne… Our petitions have been slighted; our remonstrances have produced additional violence and insult.” Which appeal does this passage primarily use, and why?

A. Ethos — it demonstrates that Henry has personally submitted petitions to the king.
B. Logos — it provides a logical chain of evidence showing that every peaceful avenue has already been tried and failed, which makes war the only remaining option.
C. Pathos — it uses emotional language like “prostrated ourselves” to make the audience feel ashamed of their timidity.
D. None — it is simply a summary of recent political history with no persuasive function.

✓ B

3. Henry ends with: “I know not what course others may take; but as for me, give me liberty, or give me death!” What does this closing do that a summary of his logical argument could not do?

A. It introduces new evidence that Henry has not yet presented, making the logical case stronger.
B. It signals that Henry is finished speaking and the audience should vote.
C. It converts the abstract argument into a personal declaration — Henry stakes his own life on the claim, which is the ultimate appeal to pathos: if the speaker will die for this, the audience must ask whether they are willing to do the same.
D. It uses logos to explain what “liberty” and “death” mean in the colonial context.

✓ C
