Grammar 1.2: Writing the Three Appeals

Using [ETHOS], [PATHOS], and [LOGOS] as analytical tags and as writing tools

The Three Appeals as Tags

In Module 8, [ETHOS], [PATHOS], and [LOGOS] are both analytical tags and writing tags. When you use them in an analytical sentence, you are naming which appeal a passage deploys and arguing what that appeal accomplishes. The tag without the argument is incomplete.

Tag What it names The analytical question it answers Weak use Strong use
[ETHOS] The appeal to credibility and character Why should the audience trust this speaker? “[ETHOS] Henry uses ethos in his opening.” “[ETHOS] By acknowledging his opponents’ arguments before refuting them, Henry argues that he is fair-minded, which makes the audience more willing to trust his reasoning when it becomes radical.”
[PATHOS] The appeal to emotion What does this text make the audience feel, and what does that feeling make them want to do? “[PATHOS] Henry uses emotional language to persuade.” “[PATHOS] When Henry declares ‘give me liberty, or give me death,’ he converts the abstract argument into a personal stake — the audience is not being asked to reason their way to war but to ask whether they share the feeling Henry has made physically present by offering his own life.”
[LOGOS] The appeal to reason and evidence What reasoning does the text offer, and does it hold? “[LOGOS] Henry uses facts and logic.” “[LOGOS] Henry’s enumeration of failed petitions — ‘we have petitioned, remonstrated, supplicated’ — is a logical sequence arguing by elimination: if every peaceful option has been exhausted, war is not a choice but the only remaining path.”

Exercise 1: Identify and Argue the Appeal

For each passage from “Give Me Liberty or Give Me Death,” (1) identify the primary appeal with the correct tag, and (2) write one sentence arguing what that appeal accomplishes — not just what it is.

Passage A

“I have but one lamp by which my feet are guided, and that is the lamp of experience. I know of no way of judging of the future but by the past.”

Tag with [ETHOS], [PATHOS], or [LOGOS]. Then write one sentence arguing what the appeal does for Henry’s argument.

Passage B

“Is life so dear, or peace so sweet, as to be purchased at the price of chains and slavery?”

Tag with [ETHOS], [PATHOS], or [LOGOS]. Then write one sentence arguing what the appeal does for Henry’s argument.

Passage C

“Are fleets and armies necessary to a work of love and reconciliation? Have we shown ourselves so unwilling to be reconciled that force must be called in to win back our love?”

Tag with [ETHOS], [PATHOS], or [LOGOS]. Then write one sentence arguing what the appeal does for Henry’s argument.

Exercise 2: Write with All Three Appeals

Choose a position you actually hold about something in school, your community, or the wider world — something you would genuinely argue for. Write three sentences, each using a different appeal. Label each with the correct tag.

Model (on a different topic):

[ETHOS] As someone who has read every environmental report published by our state agency in the last three years, I can say with confidence that the data supports immediate action. [LOGOS] The evidence is unambiguous: average temperatures have risen 1.3 degrees in our county in the past decade, and the trend line shows no sign of flattening. [PATHOS] What we decide in this room will determine what world our children inherit — and we will have to look them in the eye and explain what we chose.

Write your three sentences below, each on a separate line, each tagged. The position must be one you actually believe. The rhetoric must serve a real argument.
