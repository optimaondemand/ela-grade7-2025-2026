Grammar 1.3: Appeals in Argument — From Observation to Thesis

Building a rhetorical thesis that argues what an appeal does, not just what it is

Three Questions for Any Rhetorical Appeal

Identifying an appeal is the first step. Arguing what it accomplishes is the work. Use these three questions to move from observation to analysis:

Question 1: What does the appeal want from the audience?

Every appeal is trying to produce a response. Ethos wants trust. Pathos wants a feeling that leads to action. Logos wants the audience to accept the reasoning. Name the specific response this appeal is designed to produce.

Question 2: What does this appeal do that the others cannot?

Henry’s closing — “give me liberty, or give me death” — cannot be replaced with a list of facts. The facts are already there. The personal stake is what the logos cannot supply. Name what this specific appeal contributes that a different appeal would miss.

Question 3: Does the appeal hold? Does it earn the response it wants?

Not every appeal succeeds. An ethos appeal made by someone with no relevant experience backfires. A logos appeal with faulty reasoning loses the audience. A pathos appeal that manipulates can be recognized and rejected. Evaluate whether the appeal earns what it asks for.

Model Paragraph: All Three M8 Week 1 Structures

Read this model paragraph. Each required structure is labeled and color-coded.

[LOGOS] Henry’s central argument is structural: he enumerates every peaceful option the colonies have tried — petition, remonstrance, supplication — and argues by elimination that war is not a choice but the only remaining path. [APP] Henry, a lawyer trained in the art of building arguments from evidence, understands that logos alone will not move a frightened legislature, [WHEREAS] whereas his closing declaration — “give me liberty, or give me death” — converts the logical conclusion into a physical choice the audience cannot reason their way out of. [PATHOS] The pathos of the closing works because the logos has already done the work of eliminating every alternative: the audience has been persuaded by reason that no peaceful option remains, and the emotional declaration makes them feel the weight of what their reason has just accepted.
[LOGOS] — appeal to reason, argument by elimination
[APP] — appositive identifying the speaker’s function
[WHEREAS] — contrast clause naming what logos cannot do
[PATHOS] — appeal to emotion, argued as completing logos

Exercise: Develop a Thesis for Your Text

You will spend this module analyzing a text of your own choosing (a speech, editorial, poem, or song — teacher-approved). This week, you are getting your first look at that text through the lens of the three appeals.

Step 1

Read your chosen text once through. Mark every passage where you see [ETHOS], [PATHOS], or [LOGOS] at work.

Step 2

Answer the three analytical questions for the appeal that does the most work in your text: What does it want from the audience? What does it do that the others cannot? Does it earn what it asks for?

Step 3

Write a [DEVELOPING] thesis and a [STRONG] thesis for your capstone essay. The [DEVELOPING] thesis names the appeal and the text. The [STRONG] thesis names what the appeal argues — what claim it is making on behalf of the speaker. Label both with [DEVELOPING] and [STRONG]. Then write one [FIX] sentence explaining what you changed and why.
