VR 1.4: Stage the Three Appeals

Build the rhetorical structure of Henry’s speech as a spatial argument

What You’re Doing

Stage the rhetorical structure of “Give Me Liberty or Give Me Death” spatially: not the content of the speech, but the argument made by the three appeals working together. Where does logos establish the foundation? Where does ethos earn the audience’s trust? Where does pathos convert the logical conclusion into a felt obligation? The arrangement of IFX is the argument.

What You’ll Need
  • Device: VR headset
  • Session ID: ELA7-M8-W1 — Persistent Session
  • Location: Colonial Assembly Hall or Council Chamber setting
  • Bring: Your annotated copy of “Give Me Liberty or Give Me Death” with [ETHOS], [PATHOS], [LOGOS] marked; your three sentences from Grammar 1.2 Exercise 2
What to Do
1

Three-zone composition: ethos / logos / pathos (6–9 IFX)

Divide your space into three zones. In the ethos zone, place IFX representing what makes Henry trustworthy — his authority, his fairness, his willingness to name the stakes. In the logos zone, place IFX representing the chain of evidence — the failed petitions, the logical elimination of every alternative. In the pathos zone, place IFX representing what the speech makes the audience feel — the choice between liberty and death as a lived reality, not an abstraction. Label each zone with a sticky note naming the appeal and one phrase from the speech that best represents it.

2

Whiteboard IFX: your analytical sentence + [WHEREAS]

On the Whiteboard IFX, write: [LOGOS] or [ETHOS] or [PATHOS] — whichever appeal does the most work in Henry’s speech — followed by one sentence arguing what it accomplishes that the others cannot. Then write a [WHEREAS] clause naming what the dominant appeal depends on or completes. Use [APP] to identify Henry’s role in a phrase following his name.

3

Submit: snapshot + Canvas text

Take snapshot YourLastName_M8_1.4. In the Canvas text box, type your Whiteboard IFX sentences. Then add one sentence: what does the spatial separation of the three zones argue that a single list of examples could not?

Submission

Upload snapshot YourLastName_M8_1.4 + Canvas text. Estimated time: 20–25 minutes in headset.

Facilitator Note

The key assessment: can the student argue which appeal does the most work, and why? Students who place IFX representing “fear” in the pathos zone without connecting it to the argument Henry is trying to make need the question: “What does the fear make the audience do?” Strong Whiteboard example: “[LOGOS] Henry’s argument by elimination — enumerating every peaceful option that has failed — is the foundation that makes the pathos of ‘give me liberty, or give me death’ work: the audience has already accepted the reasoning, and the declaration converts the conclusion into a choice they must make with their lives.”
