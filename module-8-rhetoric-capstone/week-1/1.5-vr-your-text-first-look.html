Rhetoric Capstone — Week 1 — VR 1.5

VR 1.5: Your Text — First Look

Stage the rhetorical structure of your chosen text; build your first thesis

What You’re Doing

Stage the rhetorical structure of your chosen text the same way you staged Henry’s speech — with three zones for the three appeals. Then build the first thesis for your capstone essay from what you see in the spatial arrangement. The three-zone composition is a thinking tool: it shows you which appeal dominates and what the others are doing in relation to it.

What You’ll Need
  • Device: VR headset
  • Session ID: ELA7-M8-W1 — Persistent Session (same as VR 1.4)
  • Location: Colonial Assembly Hall or Council Chamber setting
  • Bring: Your chosen text with appeal annotations; your [DEVELOPING] and [STRONG] thesis from Grammar 1.3
What to Do
1

Three-zone composition for your text (4–6 IFX)

In a new area of the Location (separate from your VR 1.4 composition), build a three-zone spatial argument for your chosen text. Place IFX in each zone representing the ethos, pathos, and logos of your text. For each zone, add a sticky note with: (1) the appeal name, (2) one phrase from your text that represents it, and (3) one word describing what that appeal is doing — “earning trust,” “eliminating alternatives,” “producing urgency.”

2

Whiteboard IFX: [THESIS] + [EV1] + [EV2]

Write your [THESIS] from Grammar 1.3 (or a revision of it). Then: [EV1] — a specific quotation from your text + one sentence explaining what it does rhetorically; [EV2] — a second quotation from a different moment + one sentence of analysis. Both evidence sentences must use at least one appeal tag ([ETHOS], [PATHOS], or [LOGOS]).

3

Record your argument (60–90 seconds) and submit

Name your text and its author. State your thesis. Walk your three-zone composition and explain which appeal dominates and why. Submit snapshot YourLastName_M8_1.5 + recording link + thesis sentence.

Submission

Upload snapshot YourLastName_M8_1.5 + paste recording link + thesis sentence to Canvas. Estimated time: 20–25 minutes in headset.

Facilitator Note

The thesis is the primary assessment item. Students who have not completed Grammar 1.3 will not have a thesis to bring to this session — redirect them to Grammar 1.3 first. Students whose thesis still describes rather than argues (e.g., “This speech uses pathos”) should be asked: “What does the pathos argue about its subject?” Strong thesis example: “By grounding its emotional appeal in a specific moment of personal decision rather than in abstract principle, [author]’s [text] argues that the audience cannot remain uninvolved spectators — the pathos converts the argument into a choice they must make with their own lives.”
