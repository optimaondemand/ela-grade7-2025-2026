**Question 2** (5 pts)
Patrick Henry opens "Give Me Liberty or Give Me Death" by acknowledging the arguments of delegates who favor peace and reconciliation before challenging them. What rhetorical purpose does this opening serve?

A. It establishes logos by presenting the opposing side's evidence before refuting it with his own facts.
B. It establishes pathos by making the audience feel guilty for their reluctance to commit to war.
C. It establishes ethos by demonstrating that Henry is fair-minded and has considered opposing arguments — which makes the audience more willing to trust his reasoning when it becomes more radical.
D. It is purely conventional and serves no rhetorical function — colonial-era speeches always began with a acknowledgment of previous speakers.

**Correct: C**

**Question 3** (5 pts)
Henry argues: "We have petitioned; we have remonstrated; we have supplicated; we have prostrated ourselves before the throne... Our petitions have been slighted; our remonstrances have produced additional violence and insult." Which appeal does this passage primarily use?

A. Pathos — the word "prostrated" is emotionally charged and designed to make the audience feel humiliated.
B. Ethos — Henry is establishing his personal credibility by describing what he himself has suffered.
C. Logos — it constructs an argument by elimination: every peaceful option has been tried and has failed, therefore war is the only remaining path.
D. None of the three — it is simply a historical summary with no persuasive function.

**Correct: C**

**Question 4** (5 pts)
Henry asks: "Is life so dear, or peace so sweet, as to be purchased at the price of chains and slavery?" This is a rhetorical question — one that does not expect a direct answer. What appeal does it primarily make?

A. Logos — it presents the logical trade-off between peace and liberty in a way the audience must calculate.
B. Ethos — it establishes Henry's credibility by showing that he has personally weighed the costs of war.
C. Pathos — it converts the abstract choice between war and peace into an emotionally loaded image (chains and slavery) that the audience must feel, not just reason through.
D. None — rhetorical questions are a stylistic device, not a rhetorical appeal.

**Correct: C**

**Question 6** (5 pts)
Henry closes with: "I know not what course others may take; but as for me, give me liberty, or give me death!" What does this closing accomplish that a logical summary of his argument could not accomplish?

A. It introduces new evidence that his previous argument lacked, strengthening the logos of the speech.
B. It signals to the audience that the speech is over and they should vote.
C. It converts the abstract argument into a personal stake — Henry offers his own life as the proof of his conviction, which is an appeal to pathos that asks the audience to match his commitment or admit they value their lives more than liberty.

**Question 7** (5 pts)
A student writes: "In 'Give Me Liberty or Give Me Death,' [PATHOS] Henry's closing declaration converts the logical conclusion of the speech into a physical choice the audience cannot reason their way out of, [WHEREAS] whereas the logos has already done the work of eliminating every alternative — which means the pathos works only because the audience has been persuaded by evidence first." What does the [WHEREAS] clause accomplish in this sentence?

A. It introduces a contrasting character or opposing voice that the student is arguing against.
B. It names the relationship between the pathos and the logos — arguing that they work together, and explaining precisely how, rather than treating them as separate and independent.
C. It signals that the student has identified a contrast between Henry's emotional and logical appeals, which is required for all rhetorical analyses.
D. It provides additional background on the historical context of the speech so the reader understands why logos was necessary.

**Correct: B**
