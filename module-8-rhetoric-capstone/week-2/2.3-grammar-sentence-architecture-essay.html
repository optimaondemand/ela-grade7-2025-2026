Using [PERIODIC] and [CUMUL] to build analytical arguments, not just describe them

Using Sentence Architecture in Rhetorical Analysis

When you analyze a [PERIODIC] or [CUMUL] sentence in a text, you are not just naming a feature — you are arguing that the writer made a structural choice that serves the argument. The analytical question is always: what does this sentence architecture do that a different structure could not?

You can also use these structures in your own analytical writing. A [PERIODIC] analytical sentence builds toward a strong conclusion, holding the reader in suspense before the claim resolves. A [CUMUL] analytical sentence states the claim and extends it with evidence and qualification. Both make your analysis feel more deliberate and more powerful than simple subject-verb-object statements.

Model paragraph with all four M8 Week 2 structures labeled:

[LOGOS] Henry’s central logos appeal builds through accumulation — each failed petition adds to the chain of evidence that war is not a choice but a necessity. [CUMUL] The sentence “we have petitioned, remonstrated, supplicated, prostrated ourselves before the throne” is deliberately cumulative: the claim (we have tried peace) is stated first and extended by each addition, so the logos builds by weight rather than by logical step. [PERIODIC] If the audience accepts the first petition, and the second, and the third — if they accept that each attempt was sincere and each was met with violence — then by the time Henry says “we must fight,” [APP] Henry, the lawyer who knows that frightened men need to feel they have no alternative before they can act, has closed every exit the audience might have taken.
[LOGOS] — the appeal being analyzed
[CUMUL] — sentence architecture building logos by weight
[PERIODIC] — used in the analytical sentence itself, not just identified in Henry
[APP] — appositive identifying Henry’s rhetorical function

Exercise: Develop Your Thesis Using Sentence Architecture

Return to your thesis from Grammar 1.3 and revise it using what you now know about sentence architecture.

Step 1

Find one [PERIODIC] or [CUMUL] sentence in your chosen text. Write a two-sentence analysis: the first sentence uses [PERIODIC] or [CUMUL] to label the structure; the second sentence argues what the architecture does for the text’s dominant appeal ([ETHOS], [PATHOS], or [LOGOS]).

Step 2

Write a revised [THESIS] that includes both your dominant appeal and your sentence architecture observation. The thesis should argue what the text’s structural choices do together — not just what they are.

Step 3

Write one [FIX] sentence explaining what you changed in the thesis and why. What did adding the sentence architecture observation allow you to argue that you could not argue before?
