VR 2.4: Stage the Architecture of Persuasion

Build the sentence-level structure of Henry’s speech as a spatial composition

What You’re Doing

Stage the sentence architecture of “Give Me Liberty or Give Me Death” spatially: where does Henry hold the audience in suspension (periodic) and where does he release them into accumulation (cumulative)? Build a composition that argues the difference between delay and momentum as rhetorical tools — not just labels on a chart, but a spatial argument about what each does.

What You’ll Need
  • Device: VR headset
  • Session ID: ELA7-M8-W2 — Persistent Session
  • Location: Colonial Assembly Hall or Council Chamber setting
  • Bring: Your annotated copy of the speech with [PERIODIC] and [CUMUL] marked; your Exercise 2 sentences from Grammar 2.2
What to Do
1

Two-zone composition: suspension vs. accumulation (6–8 IFX)

Divide your space into two zones. In the periodic zone, place IFX representing what the suspended sentence creates — the held breath, the stacked conditions, the moment of resolution when “we must fight” finally arrives. In the cumulative zone, place IFX representing what the accumulative sentence creates — the forward momentum, the weight of each addition, the logos built through sequence. Label each zone with a sticky note quoting the passage it represents and naming what the structure accomplishes.

2

Whiteboard IFX: [PERIODIC] or [CUMUL] + appeal tag + [WHEREAS]

On the Whiteboard IFX, write: [PERIODIC] or [CUMUL] — your choice of the sentence type that does more work in Henry’s speech — followed by one sentence arguing what that architecture accomplishes. Then write a [WHEREAS] clause naming what the other sentence type provides in contrast. Include at least one appeal tag ([ETHOS], [PATHOS], or [LOGOS]) and one [APP] identifying Henry’s role.

3

Submit: snapshot + Canvas text

Take snapshot YourLastName_M8_2.4. In the Canvas text box, type your Whiteboard IFX sentences and one additional sentence: what does staging the two sentence types spatially reveal about how Henry structures the speech that listing examples on a page could not reveal?

Submission

Upload snapshot YourLastName_M8_2.4 + Canvas text. Estimated time: 20–25 minutes in headset.

Facilitator Note

Students who can name a periodic or cumulative sentence but cannot argue what it does need the question: “If Henry had said ‘we must fight, because we have tried peace and it has not worked’ instead, what would be different?” This comparison usually unlocks the argument about what the periodic structure does that simple sequencing cannot. Strong Whiteboard example: “[PERIODIC] By stacking every condition for war — every failed petition, every broken treaty, every ignored plea — before the word ‘fight,’ Henry, [APP] a lawyer who knew that frightened men need to feel they have exhausted every alternative, makes the conclusion feel not like his choice but like the only one remaining.”
