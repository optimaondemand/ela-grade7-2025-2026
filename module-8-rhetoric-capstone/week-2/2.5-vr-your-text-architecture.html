VR 2.5: Your Text — Sentence Architecture

Stage how your chosen text uses sentence structure to build its argument

What You’re Doing

Apply the sentence architecture analysis to your chosen text. Find where it uses periodic or cumulative structure, stage those moments spatially, and build your thesis to include this observation. The goal is to arrive at the end of this session with a thesis that names the dominant appeal, the sentence structure that serves it, and what together they argue.

What You’ll Need
  • Device: VR headset
  • Session ID: ELA7-M8-W2 — Persistent Session (same as VR 2.4)
  • Location: Colonial Assembly Hall or Council Chamber setting
  • Bring: Your chosen text with [PERIODIC] and [CUMUL] marked; your revised [THESIS] from Grammar 2.3
What to Do
1

Sentence architecture composition for your text (4–6 IFX)

In a new area of the Location, build a composition representing the sentence architecture of your chosen text. If your text uses periodic structures, stage what the delay produces. If it uses cumulative structures, stage what the accumulation builds. Label each IFX cluster with a sticky note: the sentence type, the quotation from your text, and what the structure does for the dominant appeal.

2

Whiteboard IFX: [THESIS] + [EV1] + [EV2]

Write your revised [THESIS] from Grammar 2.3 (or a further revision). Then: [EV1] — quotation + analysis using an appeal tag and a sentence architecture tag; [EV2] — a second quotation from a different moment + analysis. Both evidence sentences must use at least one tag from {[ETHOS], [PATHOS], [LOGOS]} and at least one from {[PERIODIC], [CUMUL]}.

3

Record your argument (60–90 seconds) and submit

Name your text. State your thesis. Walk the composition and explain how the sentence architecture serves the dominant appeal. Submit snapshot YourLastName_M8_2.5 + recording link + thesis sentence.

Submission

Upload snapshot YourLastName_M8_2.5 + paste recording link + thesis sentence to Canvas. Estimated time: 20–25 minutes in headset.

Facilitator Note

The primary assessment is the revised thesis. It should now name the dominant appeal and at least one sentence structure — and argue what together they accomplish. A thesis that only names (“uses pathos and periodic sentences”) without arguing is still [DEVELOPING]. Strong example: “By building its logos appeal through cumulative sentences that add evidence one by one, [author]’s [text] argues that the case for [position] is not a single irrefutable fact but an accumulated weight — and the cumulative structure makes that weight felt rather than stated.”
