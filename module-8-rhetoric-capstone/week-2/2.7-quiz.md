**Question 1** (5 pts)
A periodic sentence is best defined as:

A. A sentence that uses repetition at the beginning of each clause to build emphasis.
B. A sentence that states its main claim at the very beginning and then extends it with qualifications, examples, and additions.
C. A sentence that delays its main clause to the end — all preceding elements are subordinate or conditional, and the reader is held in suspension until the main claim resolves.
D. A sentence that uses all three rhetorical appeals (ethos, pathos, logos) in a single construction.

**Correct: C**

**Question 2** (5 pts)
Henry writes: "If we wish to be free — if we mean to preserve inviolate those inestimable privileges for which we have been so long contending — if we mean not basely to abandon the noble struggle in which we have been so long engaged — we must fight!" What type of sentence is this, and what does it accomplish rhetorically?

A. Cumulative — it states "we must fight" first and then extends the claim with reasons.
B. Periodic — by stacking three conditional clauses before the main claim, Henry forces the audience to accept every premise before they can refuse the conclusion, making "we must fight" feel necessary rather than arguable.
C. Periodic — the sentence is structured this way because colonial-era rhetorical convention required all conditions to precede any main claim in formal speechmaking.
D. Cumulative — Henry lists several things the colonists wish to preserve, and the list builds cumulatively toward the final declaration.

**Correct: B**
