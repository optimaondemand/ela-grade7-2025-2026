Repetition as Argument: Anaphora and Parallelism

How repeating a structure can argue something that a single statement cannot

Objectives

  • Define anaphora (the repetition of a word or phrase at the beginning of successive clauses) and parallelism (the use of the same grammatical structure across related elements)
  • Explain why repetition is an argument and not merely a stylistic decoration — what the repeated structure insists upon that a varied structure would not
  • Identify both figures in Henry’s speech and in your chosen text, and use both deliberately in your own analytical writing

Warm-Up

Martin Luther King Jr. said “I have a dream” eight times in one speech. Why? Write two or three sentences: what does repeating those four words do that saying them once could not do? Do not say “it is more memorable” — say what the repetition argues.

Lesson

Anaphora is the repetition of a word or phrase at the beginning of successive clauses or sentences. When Henry writes “we have petitioned; we have remonstrated; we have supplicated,” the repeated “we have” is anaphora. But anaphora is not decoration — it is an argument about quantity and completeness. The repetition says: this happened not once but again and again; the list is not incidental but exhaustive; there is nothing more that peaceful people could have done. Each repetition is another brick in a wall that the audience cannot see over. Parallelism is the use of the same grammatical structure across related elements. Parallel structure argues equivalence: if two things are expressed in the same grammatical form, the form insists they are equal in kind, weight, and importance. Henry’s “give me liberty, or give me death” is parallel: the two imperatives (“give me”) placed side by side argue that liberty and death are the only two outcomes that exist — no middle ground, no negotiation, no comfort. The parallel structure closes the door on every alternative. Both figures work by creating formal pressure. The audience feels the repetition as inevitability. They feel the parallel structure as a locked binary. And these feelings are arguments.

Historical Context

Anaphora and parallelism are among the oldest rhetorical figures in the classical tradition, named and defined by Aristotle in his Rhetoric and taught as tools of public persuasion for more than two thousand years. The classical orators understood that audiences listening to a speech (rather than reading it) needed formal patterns — repeated sounds, parallel structures — to hold complex ideas in memory. The figures are therefore simultaneously mnemonic devices and arguments: they make the content easier to remember and harder to resist.

Vocabulary: Anaphora and Parallelism

Anaphora [ANAPHORA] repeats a word or phrase at the beginning of successive clauses. The effect is accumulation, insistence, and the sense that the list is complete — nothing has been left out. Parallelism [PARALLEL] uses identical grammatical structures for related elements. The effect is equivalence, balance, and the sense that the parallel elements belong to the same category with the same weight.

Copia exercise: Take Henry’s anaphora: “we have petitioned; we have remonstrated; we have supplicated.” Rewrite it without anaphora, using varied sentence structure and different verbs. Then write one sentence explaining what the anaphoric version argues that your version does not.

Comprehension Check

1. Anaphora repeats a word or phrase at the beginning of successive clauses. What does this repetition argue beyond making the phrase more memorable?

A. It argues that the speaker has run out of new ideas and is repeating the same point to fill time.
B. It argues completeness and insistence — the repetition says that this thing happened not once but again and again, that the list is exhaustive, and that no peaceful or reasonable person could have done more.
C. It argues that the repeated phrase is more important than the other phrases in the speech, which is why the speaker chose to repeat it.
D. It argues that the audience already agrees with the speaker, which is why they do not need a new argument — only reinforcement.

✓ B

2. Henry’s closing — “give me liberty, or give me death” — is a parallel structure. What does the grammatical equivalence of the two imperatives argue?

A. It argues that liberty and death are equally bad outcomes, and Henry is neutral about which one he receives.
B. It argues that the speech is coming to a close, since parallel closings were a conventional signal in colonial oratory.
C. The parallel structure argues that liberty and death are the only two options that exist — no middle ground, no negotiation, no alternative — and the identical grammatical weight of the two imperatives insists that Henry means both with the same seriousness.
D. It argues that Henry is being dramatic and should not be taken literally — no one actually wants to die, so the audience understands this as rhetorical exaggeration.

✓ C

3. Why might a speaker choose anaphora to serve a logos appeal specifically?

A. Because anaphora is inherently emotional and logos is inherently logical, combining them creates a balance.
B. Because the formal repetition of anaphora makes the list of evidence feel exhaustive — the audience does not count the items; they feel that everything possible has been tried — which is the logos argument the speaker needs to make.
C. Because speakers who use anaphora are considered more credible than those who vary their sentence structure, which serves ethos rather than logos.
D. Because anaphora is only appropriate for logos appeals — pathos appeals require different figures and ethos appeals require none.

✓ B
