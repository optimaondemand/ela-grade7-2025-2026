Grammar 3.2: [ANAPHORA] and [PARALLEL] as Analytical Tags

Identifying the figures and arguing what they do for the appeal they serve

The Tags

TagWhat it marksThe analytical questionStrong use
[ANAPHORA] Repeated word or phrase at the start of successive clauses What does the repetition insist upon? What does it argue about completeness, inevitability, or accumulation? “[ANAPHORA] Henry’s threefold ‘we have’ does not merely list actions — it argues that the list is complete, that every peaceful avenue has been taken, and that the audience cannot name one thing Henry’s side has not tried.”
[PARALLEL] Identical grammatical structure across related elements What does the formal equivalence argue? What does the identical structure insist the parallel elements share in weight, kind, or status? “[PARALLEL] The identical imperative structure of ‘give me liberty, or give me death’ argues that there are exactly two outcomes — the parallel form closes every exit and makes the binary feel not like Henry’s preference but like objective fact.”

Exercise 1: Find and Argue the Figure

For each task, (1) find the figure in your chosen text, (2) quote it, (3) tag it with [ANAPHORA] or [PARALLEL], and (4) write one sentence arguing what it does for the appeal it serves.

Task A

Find one instance of [ANAPHORA] in your chosen text. Quote it, tag it, and write one sentence arguing what the repetition does for the appeal it serves.

Task B

Find one instance of [PARALLEL] in your chosen text. Quote it, tag it, and write one sentence arguing what the grammatical equivalence insists the parallel elements share.

Task C

If your chosen text does not have both figures, find the missing one in “Give Me Liberty or Give Me Death” and complete the same exercise with Henry’s text. Note that you will need to account for this in your essay.

Exercise 2: Write with Both Figures

Write two sentences analyzing your chosen text. The first must use [ANAPHORA] in the analysis itself (not just identify it). The second must use [PARALLEL] in the analysis itself. Then combine them into one analytical paragraph that also includes your appeal tag, [APP], and [WHEREAS]. Label all five structures.

Model (on Henry):

[LOGOS] [ANAPHORA] Henry’s anaphora builds the logos appeal through insistence: “we have petitioned, we have remonstrated, we have supplicated” argues not that three things were tried but that everything was tried — the repetition closes the list before it begins. [APP] Henry, a lawyer who understood that frightened audiences need exhausted alternatives before they can act on a radical conclusion, then deploys [PARALLEL] the parallel structure of “give me liberty, or give me death” to close the exit the logos opened: [WHEREAS] whereas the anaphora argues that the past has run out of peaceful options, the parallel structure argues that the future has only two.
