Grammar 3.3: Figures in Your Essay Argument

Using all four M8 structures together to build a complete analytical body paragraph

All Four M8 Structures Together

You now have four analytical structures for the capstone essay. Each has a tag. Each names a different level of the rhetorical argument. When they work together in a single paragraph, they produce an analysis that moves from the sentence level (architecture) through the figure level (anaphora/parallelism) to the appeal level (ethos/pathos/logos) and then to the argument about what together they accomplish.

TagLevelWhat it argues
[ETHOS] [PATHOS] [LOGOS]Appeal levelWhat the text wants from the audience and how it tries to get it
[PERIODIC] [CUMUL]Sentence architecture levelHow the shape of the sentence creates suspense or momentum
[ANAPHORA] [PARALLEL]Figure levelHow repetition argues completeness or equivalence
[APP] [WHEREAS]Sentence structure levelAppositive identifies function; contrast clause names what one thing does that another cannot

Exercise 1: Write a Complete Body Paragraph

Write a complete body paragraph analyzing your chosen text. The paragraph must include all of the following, in a logical sequence. Label each structure with its tag.

  • A topic sentence that names the appeal and the figure you are analyzing
  • [APP] — an appositive identifying the author’s rhetorical function
  • [ANAPHORA] or [PARALLEL] — quotation + argument about what the figure does for the appeal
  • [PERIODIC] or [CUMUL] — quotation + argument about what the sentence architecture does
  • [WHEREAS] — a contrast clause naming what one structural choice does that the other cannot
  • A concluding sentence connecting the paragraph to your thesis

Exercise 2: Thesis Revision

Apply the four revision questions to your current thesis:

[R1] Does my thesis name the text and the formal choices it makes?
[R2] Does my thesis argue what those choices do, not just what they are?
[R3] Does my thesis make a claim that a reasonable reader could disagree with?
[R4] Does my thesis answer the essential question: how does this writer use the structure of language to make an argument?

Write your revised [THESIS] with all four questions answered. Then write a [FIX] sentence explaining your most significant revision.
