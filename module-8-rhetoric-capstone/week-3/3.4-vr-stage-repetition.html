VR 3.4: Stage Repetition as Argument

Build a spatial argument for how anaphora and parallelism argue in “Give Me Liberty or Give Me Death”

What You’re Doing

Stage the figures of repetition in Henry’s speech as a spatial composition: where does anaphora build the logos appeal through accumulation, and where does parallelism close the argument by making the binary feel absolute? The composition should argue the difference between what repetition-as-accumulation does versus what equivalence-as-structure does.

What You’ll Need
  • Device: VR headset
  • Session ID: ELA7-M8-W3 — Persistent Session
  • Location: Colonial Assembly Hall or Council Chamber setting
  • Bring: Your annotated speech with [ANAPHORA] and [PARALLEL] marked; your body paragraph from Grammar 3.3 Exercise 1
What to Do
1

Two-zone composition: accumulation vs. equivalence (6–8 IFX)

Zone 1: [ANAPHORA] — stage what accumulation through repetition does. IFX should represent the building pressure of “we have petitioned, we have remonstrated, we have supplicated.” Zone 2: [PARALLEL] — stage what grammatical equivalence does. IFX should represent the locked binary of “give me liberty, or give me death.” Label each zone with a sticky note quoting the passage and naming the argument the figure makes.

2

Whiteboard IFX: [ANAPHORA] + [PARALLEL] + appeal tag + [WHEREAS]

Write the core analytical sentence from your Grammar 3.3 body paragraph on the Whiteboard IFX, with all tags visible. Then add one [WHEREAS] clause connecting the two figures: what does the anaphora do that the parallelism cannot, and vice versa?

3

Submit: snapshot + Canvas text

Take snapshot YourLastName_M8_3.4. In Canvas text box, type your Whiteboard IFX content. Add one sentence: what does staging the figures spatially reveal about how they work in sequence in the speech?

Submission

Facilitator Note

Students who separate the figures into “list” and “quote” without arguing what each does need the question: “If Henry had varied the sentence structure — ‘we tried petitioning, and remonstrance was attempted, and there were supplications too’ — what would be different about the argument?” This comparison usually unlocks what anaphora does that variation cannot.
