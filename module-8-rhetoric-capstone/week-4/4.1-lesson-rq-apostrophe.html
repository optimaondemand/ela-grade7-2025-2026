Questions That Are Not Questions: Rhetorical Question and Apostrophe

Two figures that address an audience or an absence — and why that matters

Objectives

  • Define rhetorical question (a question posed for effect rather than information, whose answer is implied) and apostrophe (addressing something absent, abstract, or unable to respond)
  • Explain what each figure does to the relationship between the speaker and the audience — how they position the audience and what kind of response they are designed to produce
  • Identify both figures in Henry’s speech and your chosen text and use them in your own analytical writing

Warm-Up

When a parent says “Do you really think that was a good idea?” they are not asking a question. They already know the answer and they want you to know that they know. Write two or three sentences: what does a question-that-is-not-really-a-question do to the person being addressed? What does it demand of them that a direct statement would not?

Lesson

A rhetorical question is a question that does not expect a direct answer because the answer is already implied. When Henry asks “Is life so dear, or peace so sweet, as to be purchased at the price of chains and slavery?” he knows what the audience will answer and he knows they know it too. The power of the rhetorical question is that it makes the audience supply the conclusion themselves: instead of Henry saying “you should not accept chains,” the audience arrives at that conclusion by answering his question, which makes it feel like their own. Apostrophe is the figure of addressing something absent, abstract, or incapable of responding — addressing liberty itself, death, God, justice, a dead person, or an abstract principle as if it could hear. Apostrophe does something to the speaker’s relationship with the audience: by turning away from the audience to address something else, the speaker either draws the audience into that address (they are meant to feel the speaker’s relationship with liberty, or justice, or the dead) or creates a moment of theatrical intensity that makes the abstract feel present. Both figures reshape the relationship between speaker and audience in ways that direct statement cannot. The rhetorical question makes the audience the agent of the argument’s conclusion. The apostrophe gives presence to what is absent and makes the audience feel its weight.

Historical Context

Rhetorical questions and apostrophe were among the most studied figures in classical rhetoric. Quintilian, in his Institutio Oratoria, discussed the rhetorical question as a tool for making the audience participate in the argument rather than passively receive it — which increases both persuasive force and emotional investment. Apostrophe was considered the highest figure of emotional intensity, used for moments when the speaker’s feeling is too great to be expressed in ordinary address. Both figures signal that ordinary communication has reached its limit and a different kind of speech is required.

Vocabulary: Rhetorical Question and Apostrophe

Rhetorical question [RQ] poses a question whose answer is already implied, making the audience supply the conclusion themselves. The effect is participation: the audience arrives at the argument rather than receiving it. Apostrophe [APOS] addresses something absent, abstract, or incapable of responding. The effect is presence and intensity: the abstract is made concrete, the absent is made felt.

Comprehension Check

1. Henry asks: “Are fleets and armies necessary to a work of love and reconciliation?” What makes this a rhetorical question, and what does it do to the audience?

A. It is a rhetorical question because it uses emotional language (“love”) rather than factual language, which makes it a pathos appeal rather than a real question.
B. It is a rhetorical question because the answer is already obvious — no, fleets and armies are not necessary for love and reconciliation — and by making the audience supply this answer, Henry makes them the agents of his argument rather than passive recipients of it.
C. It is a rhetorical question because Henry does not wait for the audience to answer it before continuing with his speech.
D. It is a rhetorical question because it addresses the king directly, which is an instance of apostrophe rather than a genuine inquiry.

✓ B

2. What does apostrophe accomplish that direct address cannot?

A. It allows the speaker to say things they could not say directly to the audience, by pretending to address someone or something else.
B. Apostrophe gives presence to what is absent — by addressing liberty, death, justice, or a person who cannot respond, the speaker makes the abstract feel concrete and the audience feels the speaker’s relationship with that absence as if it were a real, felt encounter.
C. Apostrophe is purely decorative and serves no argumentative function — it is a theatrical gesture that signals the speaker is approaching a climax.
D. Apostrophe allows the speaker to avoid making a direct argument, which protects them from being challenged on specific factual claims.

✓ B

3. Why might a speaker use a rhetorical question to serve a pathos appeal specifically?

A. Because rhetorical questions are inherently emotional and cannot be used for logos or ethos appeals.
B. Because making the audience supply the conclusion means they feel the argument as their own feeling rather than receiving it as the speaker’s argument — which is how pathos works most powerfully: the audience is not told what to feel but arrives at the feeling themselves.
C. Because speakers who use rhetorical questions are considered more credible than those who make direct statements, which serves ethos.
D. Because rhetorical questions are shorter than direct statements and therefore more efficient, which helps the speaker cover more ground in less time.

✓ B
