Grammar 4.2: [RQ] and [APOS] as Analytical Tags

Identifying the figures and arguing what they do for the speaker-audience relationship

The Tags

TagWhat it marksThe analytical questionStrong use
[RQ] A question posed for effect, not information — the answer is already implied What is the implied answer? How does making the audience supply it rather than stating it directly change the argument? “[RQ] When Henry asks ‘Are fleets and armies necessary to a work of love?’ the implied answer (no) is not Henry’s conclusion but the audience’s — by forcing them to supply it, the question makes the pathos of the argument feel like the audience’s own feeling rather than the speaker’s.”
[APOS] Address to something absent, abstract, or incapable of responding What is being addressed and why? What does giving presence to this absence do for the argument? “[APOS] By addressing liberty directly — as if it could hear and be claimed or lost — the speaker makes the abstract concrete: liberty is no longer a political concept to be reasoned about but a presence the audience must choose to keep or surrender.”

Exercise 1: Find and Argue the Figure in Your Text

Task A

Find one [RQ] in your chosen text. Quote it. Identify the implied answer. Write one sentence arguing what the rhetorical question does to the audience’s relationship to the argument that a direct statement would not do.

Task B

Find one [APOS] in your chosen text — or in “Give Me Liberty or Give Me Death” if your text does not use it. Quote it. Name what is being addressed (liberty, death, the nation, a person). Write one sentence arguing what giving presence to this absence does for the argument.

Task C

Write one sentence connecting the [RQ] or [APOS] you found to your dominant appeal ([ETHOS], [PATHOS], or [LOGOS]). The sentence must argue what the figure does for the appeal, not just identify both.

Exercise 2: Complete Body Paragraph with All Six M8 Tags

Write a complete body paragraph for your capstone essay. This paragraph should incorporate all six Module 8 analytical tags. Label each one. The paragraph should read as a coherent argument, not a list of labeled observations.
