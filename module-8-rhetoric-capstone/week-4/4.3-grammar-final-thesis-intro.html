Grammar 4.3: Final Thesis Revision and Essay Introduction

The thesis is ready; now build the introduction that frames it

Four Revision Questions — Final Check

[R1] Does my thesis name the text, the author, and the dominant rhetorical choice I am arguing about?
[R2] Does my thesis argue what the rhetorical choices do, not just what they are? (Not “uses pathos” but “the pathos of X argues Y.”)
[R3] Does my thesis make a claim that a reasonable, careful reader could disagree with?
[R4] Does my thesis answer the essential question: how does this writer use the structure of language to make an argument?

What a Strong Introduction Does

Your introduction has three jobs: (1) orient the reader to the text and the rhetorical situation; (2) establish what makes the formal choices worth analyzing; (3) deliver the thesis as a claim the essay will prove. A strong introduction does not summarize the text or the argument — it sets up the analytical frame so the reader knows exactly what to expect.

Model introduction (on Henry):

On March 23, 1775, Patrick Henry addressed a colonial legislature most of whom hoped for reconciliation with Britain. He had one speech and a single, urgent argument: war was not an option but the only option remaining. [PATHOS] The speech works not by overwhelming the audience with emotion but by making them supply the emotion themselves — through rhetorical questions whose answers are already obvious, through parallel structures that leave no exit, and through a closing declaration that turns a logical conclusion into a personal stake. [APP] Henry, a lawyer who understood that frightened men need to feel they have no alternative before they can act on a radical one, [THESIS] argues through the structure of the speech itself — the cumulative anaphora that closes the past and the parallel binary that closes the future — that by the time the audience hears “give me liberty, or give me death,” there is nothing left to choose.

Exercise: Write Your Introduction

Write your essay introduction for the capstone. It must include all of the following. Label each with its tag.

  • One or two sentences orienting the reader to the text, author, and rhetorical situation
  • One sentence naming what formal choices you will analyze (at least two from your M8 toolkit)
  • [APP] — an appositive identifying the author’s rhetorical function
  • [THESIS] — your final, revised, arguable claim

Then write one [FIX] sentence: what is the one thing in this introduction you are least confident about, and what would you need to change to fix it?
