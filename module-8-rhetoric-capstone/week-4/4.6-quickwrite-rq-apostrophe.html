4.6 — Quickwrite: Rhetorical Question and Apostrophe
5 points · Canvas text box · ~10 minutes
Your Prompt

Choose either [RQ] or [APOS] from your selected text. In 5–7 sentences, identify the moment, explain what appeal it serves, and argue whether it earns that response from the audience. Use all four required tags exactly once each.

Required Tags (use each once)
[RQ] or [APOS] Label the figure you chose — quote the exact words from your text
[ETHOS], [PATHOS], or [LOGOS] Name the rhetorical appeal this figure is serving
[APP] Explain what the figure is doing — why the speaker chose this form rather than a direct statement
[EQ] End with a genuine question this moment raises for you — something you're not sure how to answer yet
Model (Henry's Speech)

[RQ] Henry closes his appeal with a cascade of questions: "Is life so dear, or peace so sweet, as to be purchased at the price of chains and slavery?" Rather than telling the delegates their lives are not worth submission, he makes them arrive at that conclusion themselves. [PATHOS] The questions reach past reason directly into the audience's sense of dignity — no man who hears them can easily admit he would choose chains. [APP] The rhetorical question does what a logical argument cannot: it makes the audience feel the answer before they think it, so by the time they reason through it, the emotional conclusion is already in place. [EQ] But I wonder whether this strategy could backfire — if a listener resents being maneuvered, does the rhetorical question push them toward Henry's position or harden their resistance to it?
