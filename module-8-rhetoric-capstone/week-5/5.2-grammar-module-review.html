7th Grade ELA · Module 8 · Week 5
5.2 — Grammar: All Six Structures Together
Grammar & Writing · ~25 minutes

Before you revise your essay, this lesson asks you to identify all six M8 structures in a single passage — not to prove you can find them, but to practice seeing how they combine. Then you will write a sentence that layers at least two structures at once.

Exercise 1 — Read and Label the Model

Each sentence below comes from a student essay on Henry's speech. In your notebook, copy each bracketed tag and write one sentence explaining what that structure is doing at that moment — not what the tag means in general, but what it is accomplishing here.

[THESIS] Henry's speech conquers resistance through accumulation: each appeal, each sentence structure, and each figure of repetition piles on the last until the delegates cannot imagine any conclusion but war. [CUMUL] He begins with ethos — establishing that he has spent years watching British promises dissolve — and adds, in rapid succession, examples of petitions ignored, armies mobilized, and fleets anchored in American harbors, each addition making the next seem more inevitable. [LOGOS] The catalog of British actions is not emotional manipulation; it is evidence, arranged to be irrefutable. [PERIODIC] When Henry finally reaches his most famous declaration — holding back the resolution through clause after subordinate clause — the word "death" arrives not as a shock but as the only possible end of a sentence whose structure has already prepared us for it. [PATHOS] He does not tell the delegates they should want liberty; [ANAPHORA] he asks, and asks again, and asks once more, whether they have yet found a single reason to believe Britain will ever grant it. [APP] The anaphora does not merely emphasize — it mimics the structure of hope repeatedly disappointed, so the audience feels the exhaustion of waiting before they consciously articulate it. [WHEREAS] While a lesser speaker might have named the emotional weight directly, Henry lets the repetition produce it, trusting his audience to complete the feeling themselves. [PARALLEL] He offers them not an argument and a plea and a warning but a single compound truth: liberty and life are the same, and chains and death are the same. [RQ] The rhetorical question — "Is life so dear, or peace so sweet, as to be purchased at the price of chains and slavery?" — makes the delegates the authors of their own answer. [CON] Henry's genius is not that he is the most eloquent man in the room; it is that he makes every other man in the room feel the answer is already inside them, waiting to be spoken.

Copy all ten tags into your notebook. For each one, write: [TAG]: Here, this structure does __________ because __________.

Exercise 2 — Write a Layered Sentence

Write one sentence about your chosen text that uses at least two of the six M8 structures simultaneously. Label both tags. Then write one sentence explaining why the combination is stronger than either structure alone would be.

Model

[PERIODIC] [PATHOS] Only when Henry has spent twenty lines cataloging every broken treaty, every ignored petition, every British cannon aimed at an American shore does he finally grant his audience the word they have been dreading: war.

The periodic structure makes the audience wait for a conclusion they already sense coming; the pathos attaches an emotional cost to that waiting, so the final word carries the weight of everything that preceded it. Neither structure alone would produce the same effect — the delay without the emotional stakes would be mere suspense, and the pathos without the delay would be mere feeling.

Your turn: Choose any two structures from the six. Write the layered sentence, label both tags, then explain in a second sentence why the combination does something neither structure could do alone. Write in your notebook — you may use this sentence in your essay.

Exercise 3 — Scan Your Own Essay Draft

Open your essay draft and answer these four questions in your notebook. You will use these answers when you revise in 5.3.

  1. How many of the six M8 structures appear in your draft? List each one by tag.
  2. Is there any moment where two structures arrive together? If yes, where? If no, which two could you layer in your strongest body paragraph?
  3. Does your [APP] sentence explain what the formal choice does to the audience, or does it only describe what the choice is? Revise if needed.
  4. Does your [CON] paragraph do something more than restate your thesis? Name the additional move it makes — or plan one.
